    *   **A** is a high-mass, high-luminosity star on the upper main sequence.

    *   **C** is a main-sequence star with a mass roughly similar to that of the Sun.

2.  **Recall the dominant fusion processes:**
    *   In stars, hydrogen fusion occurs primarily via two pathways: the **proton-proton (p-p) chain** and the **CNO cycle**.
    *   The **p-p chain** is the dominant process in stars with masses less than or equal to the Sun's mass (up to about $1.3 M_{\odot}$). In these stars, lower core temperatures favor the p-p chain.
    *   The **CNO cycle** becomes increasingly dominant in stars more massive than the Sun. Because the CNO cycle is highly temperature-dependent, it takes over as the primary energy source in high-mass stars (like star A).

3.  **Conclusion:**
    *   Star **A** relies heavily on the CNO cycle due to its high core temperature.
    *   Star **C** lies on the main sequence in the region representing stars similar to our Sun. In stars of this mass, the core temperature is cool enough that the **proton-proton chain reaction** is the primary mechanism for hydrogen fusion.
    *   Star **B** (a giant) has already moved off the main sequence and may be fusing helium or hydrogen in a shell.
    *   Star **D** (a white dwarf) is no longer undergoing nuclear fusion.
