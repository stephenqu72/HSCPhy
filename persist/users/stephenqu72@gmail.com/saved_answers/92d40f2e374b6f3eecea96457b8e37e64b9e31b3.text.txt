### Explanation
To solve this problem, we apply the principles of orbital mechanics regarding energy and conservation of angular momentum.

1.  **Initial State:** The satellite is in a stable circular orbit. In a circular orbit, the gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy are constant. The speed of a satellite in a circular orbit at radius $r$ is given by $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$.
2.  **The Impulse:** The problem states the satellite fires its thrusters to **reduce** its orbital speed at point $X$. 
3.  **Orbital Mechanics:**
    *   When the satellite slows down, it no longer has enough kinetic energy to maintain its current circular orbit radius at its original speed.
    *   According to Kepler's Laws and the conservation of mechanical energy, if a satellite loses speed at a specific point in its orbit, that point becomes the **apogee** (the highest point) of a new, elliptical orbit. 
    *   Since the speed is reduced at point $X$, the satellite will "fall" toward the planet, reaching a lower altitude on the opposite side of its orbit (the perigee).
4.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **Option A:** Shows the satellite moving into a new elliptical orbit where point $X$ is the highest point (apogee). This is consistent with a decrease in orbital speed.
    *   **Option B:** Shows the satellite moving into an orbit where point $X$ is the lowest point (perigee). This would require an *increase* in speed.
    *   **Options C and D:** Show circular orbits. Changing the speed at a single point cannot result in a new circular orbit; it must result in an elliptical orbit because the total energy of the system has changed.

Therefore, since the satellite loses kinetic energy at $X$, its total mechanical energy decreases, and it must drop into a lower path, making $X$ the point furthest from the center of the planet.
