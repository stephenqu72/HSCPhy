### Answer and Explanation

To solve this problem, we apply the **de Broglie hypothesis**, which relates the momentum of a particle to its wavelength:
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$
where $\lambda$ is wavelength, $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$), $m$ is mass, and $v$ is velocity (speed).

Rearranging this to match the graph (plotting inverse wavelength $\frac{1}{\lambda}$ vs speed $v$):
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = \left( \frac{m}{h} \right) v$$
This equation is in the form of a linear relationship $y = mx_{slope}x + c$, where the gradient (slope) of the graph is $\frac{m}{h}$.

#### Part (a): Plotting the results
To plot the graph:
1. **Axes:** Place "Particle speed ($10^6 \text{ m s}^{-1}$)" on the $x$-axis and "Inverse of the wavelength ($\text{pm}^{-1}$)" on the $y$-axis.
2. **Scale:** Choose a scale that utilizes the grid space effectively (e.g., $x$-axis: $0$ to $8 \times 10^6$; $y$-axis: $0$ to $0.12 \text{ pm}^{-1}$).
3. **Data Points:** Plot the pairs: $(1.4, 0.017), (2.9, 0.039), (4.1, 0.051), (6.8, 0.089), (7.3, 0.117)$.
4. **Line of Best Fit:** Draw a straight line through the origin $(0,0)$, as the theory predicts $\frac{1}{\lambda} \propto v$.

#### Part (b): Determining the mass
1. **Calculate the Gradient:** Pick two points on your line of best fit (ideally far apart). Using the data points $(1.0, 0.012)$ (extrapolated) and $(7.0, 0.085)$:
   $$\text{Gradient} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{0.085 - 0.012}{7.0 \times 10^6 - 1.0 \times 10^6} \approx 1.217 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s m}^{-1} \text{ pm}^{-1}$$
2. **Unit Conversion:** The wavelength is in $\text{pm}^{-1}$ ($10^{-12} \text{ m}^{-1}$). We must convert to $\text{m}^{-1}$ to use SI units.
   $$\text{Gradient in SI units} = 1.217 \times 10^{-8} \times 10^{12} = 1.217 \times 10^4 \text{ s/m}^2$$
3. **Solve for Mass:** Since $\text{Gradient} = \frac{m}{h}$:
   $$m = \text{Gradient} \times h$$
   $$m = (1.217 \times 10^4) \times (6.626 \times 10^{-34})$$
   $$m \approx 8.06 \times 10^{-30} \text{ kg}$$
