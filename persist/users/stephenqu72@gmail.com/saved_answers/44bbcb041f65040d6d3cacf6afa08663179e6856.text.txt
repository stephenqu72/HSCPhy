To answer this question effectively, we must apply the principles of the **Doppler Effect** for light and understand how rotational velocity affects spectral line broadening.

### Analysis and Answer

**1. Motion regarding Earth (Doppler Effect):**
By comparing the positions of the spectral lines of the stars to the laboratory hydrogen spectrum, we can determine their radial velocity:
*   **Star A:** The spectral lines are shifted toward longer wavelengths (to the right) compared to the laboratory spectrum. This is a **redshift**, indicating that Star A is moving **away** from Earth.
*   **Star B:** The spectral lines are also redshifted (further to the right than Star A), indicating that Star B is moving **away** from Earth at a greater velocity than Star A.

**2. Rotational Motion (Spectral Line Broadening):**
The question states that the spectral lines for Star B are wider than those for Star A. In stellar spectroscopy, this phenomenon is known as **Doppler broadening**.
*   This widening is caused by the rotation of the star. As the star rotates, one side moves toward us (blueshift) and the other side moves away (redshift).
*   The light from the entire stellar disk is integrated, resulting in a blurred or "broadened" spectral line.
*   **Conclusion:** Since the lines of Star B are wider than those of Star A, **Star B has a faster rate of rotation** than Star A.
