The correct answer is **B. Atoms emit discrete spectral lines.**

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the historical progression of atomic models and their specific limitations.

1.  **Rutherford's Model (The Planetary Model):** Following his gold foil experiment, Ernest Rutherford proposed that an atom consisted of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons orbiting at various distances.
2.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **A, C, and D:** These are the primary findings of the Geiger-Marsden experiment (the gold foil experiment). Rutherford’s model was specifically designed to explain these observations: the fact that most alpha particles passed through (proving the atom is mostly empty space) and that some were deflected or bounced back (proving the existence of a dense, concentrated positive charge/nucleus).
    *   **B: Atoms emit discrete spectral lines:** This is the correct answer because Rutherford's model had a major flaw regarding electromagnetic theory. According to classical physics, an electron orbiting the nucleus is an accelerating charge. An accelerating charge must continuously radiate energy as electromagnetic waves. If an electron were constantly losing energy, it would spiral inward and collapse into the nucleus. Furthermore, as the electron spiraled inward, its orbital frequency would change continuously, which should produce a **continuous spectrum** rather than the **discrete spectral lines** observed experimentally in gases.

Because Rutherford’s model could not account for the stability of the atom or the specific quantized nature of spectral emissions, Niels Bohr later improved upon this model by introducing quantized orbits where electrons do not radiate energy.
