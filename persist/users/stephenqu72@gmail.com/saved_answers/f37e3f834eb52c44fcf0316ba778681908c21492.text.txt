To solve these problems, we must bridge the gap between linear motion and rotational motion.

### Part (a)
**Given:** 
- Radius ($r$) = $1\text{ m}$
- Torque ($\tau$) = $180\text{ N m}$
- Rotational speed = $2500\text{ rpm}$

**1. Calculate Force ($F$):**
Torque is defined as $\tau = F \cdot r$. Given $r = 1\text{ m}$, then:
$$F = \frac{\tau}{r} = \frac{180\text{ N m}}{1\text{ m}} = 180\text{ N}$$

**2. Calculate Velocity ($v$):**
Velocity $v$ is the tangential speed of the rotation. First, find angular velocity $\omega$ in radians per second:
$$\omega = 2500 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} \approx 261.80\text{ rad/s}$$
Since $v = \omega \cdot r$:
$$v = 261.80\text{ rad/s} \times 1\text{ m} = 261.80\text{ m/s}$$

**3. Calculate Power ($P$):**
Using the provided formula $P = F \cdot v$:
$$P = 180\text{ N} \times 261.80\text{ m/s} \approx 47,124\text{ W} \text{ (or } 47.12\text{ kW)}$$

### Part (b)
**Given:**
- Torque ($\tau$) = $12\text{ N m}$
- Rotations ($n$) = $15$

**Concept:** 
Work ($W$) in rotational systems is $W = \tau \cdot \theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle in radians.

**1. Calculate $\theta$:**
One rotation is $2\pi$ radians. For $15$ rotations:
$$\theta = 15 \times 2\pi = 30\pi \approx 94.25\text{ radians}$$

**2. Calculate Work:**
$$W = 12\text{ N m} \times 94.25\text{ rad}$$
$$W \approx 1,131\text{ J}$$

---

### Teacher's Tip for Success:
In HSC Physics, always check your units. When dealing with rotational motion, convert "rpm" to "radians per second" immediately, as all standard physics formulas for power ($P = \tau\omega$) and work ($W = \tau\theta$) require radians.
