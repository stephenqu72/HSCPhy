To solve this problem, we must derive the expression for the orbital velocity of a satellite in a circular orbit.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Understand the Physics:** For a satellite to maintain a stable circular orbit, the gravitational force exerted by the central body (e.g., Earth) must provide the necessary centripetal force to keep the satellite moving in a circle.
2.  **Set up the Equations:**
    *   The gravitational force is given by Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation:
        $$F_g = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
        where $G$ is the gravitational constant, $M$ is the mass of the central body, $m$ is the mass of the satellite, and $r$ is the orbital radius.
    *   The centripetal force required for circular motion is:
        $$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
3.  **Equate the Forces:** Since the gravitational force provides the centripetal force ($F_g = F_c$), we equate them:
    $$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
4.  **Solve for $v$:**
    *   Divide both sides by $m$ (the mass of the satellite cancels out):
        $$\frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
    *   Multiply both sides by $r$:
        $$v^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$$
    *   Take the square root of both sides:
        $$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
5.  **Interpret the Result:**
    Since $G$ and $M$ are constants, the relationship is $v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}$. This means the orbital velocity $v$ is **inversely proportional to the square root of the orbital radius $r$**.
