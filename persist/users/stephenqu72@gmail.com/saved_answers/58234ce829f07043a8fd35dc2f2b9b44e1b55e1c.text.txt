### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must identify the actual physical forces acting on the car. In physics, when drawing a free-body diagram (FBD), we only include the **real forces** acting on the object.

1.  **Identify the real forces:**
    *   **Gravity ($mg$):** Acting vertically downwards from the centre of mass of the car.
    *   **Normal Force ($NRF$):** Acting perpendicular to the surface of the banked track.

2.  **Evaluate the options:**
    *   **Option A:** Correctly shows only the two real forces ($mg$ and $NRF$). The vector sum of these two forces provides the required **centripetal force** ($F_c$), which is the *net* force directed horizontally towards the centre of the circular path. 
    *   **Options B, C, and D:** These are incorrect because they include $F_c$ (centripetal force) as an individual force vector on the diagram. In HSC Physics, centripetal force is not a "new" or "additional" force; it is the *resultant* (or net) force of the existing forces. Including it in a force diagram is a common student misconception. 

3.  **Why the car stays at a constant height:**
    The vertical component of the Normal Force ($NRF \cos \theta$) balances the weight of the car ($mg$), which is why the car does not slide up or down the slope or sink into the track. The horizontal component of the Normal Force ($NRF \sin \theta$) provides the centripetal force required to keep the car moving in a circle. Since these are accounted for by $mg$ and $NRF$ alone, no other forces (like friction) are necessary for the car to navigate a banked track at the design speed.
