This question requires an understanding of energy transformation, nuclear physics, and electrical transmission systems.

### Part (a) Qualitative Analysis (9 marks)

To earn full marks, you must link the physical principles to each stage:

*   **Stage 1: Energy Production (Nuclear Fission):** The reactor uses neutron-induced fission of $^{235}U$. The mass defect between the reactants and products is converted into thermal energy ($E = \Delta mc^2$). This heat is used to boil water into high-pressure steam, which then exerts a torque on the turbine blades, converting thermal energy into mechanical kinetic energy.
*   **Stage 2: Energy Conversion (Electromagnetic Induction):** The mechanical energy from the turbine rotates a coil within a magnetic field (an AC generator). According to Faraday’s Law and Lenz’s Law, the changing magnetic flux through the coil induces an electromotive force (EMF), converting mechanical kinetic energy into electrical energy.
*   **Stage 3: Energy Transmission (Transformers and Power Lines):** Transformers are used to alter voltage levels. A step-up transformer ($1:20$) increases voltage to minimize current ($I = P/V$), which drastically reduces power loss due to heating ($P_{loss} = I^2R$) over long distances. Intermediate transformers adjust voltage for distribution, and a final step-down transformer ($30:1$) brings the voltage to a safe, usable level for the laboratory.

### Part (b) Calculation (5 marks)

**Step 1: Calculate energy per reaction**
Mass defect $\Delta m = [m(^{235}U) + m(n)] - [m(^{144}Ba) + m(^{89}Kr) + 2m(n)]$
*(Note: Usually 3 neutrons are released, but we use the masses provided. Assuming one neutron captured and net mass loss calculation):*
$\Delta m \approx (235.0439 + 1.0087) - (143.9229 + 88.9178 + 3 \times 1.0087) \approx 0.177 \, \text{u}$
Using $1 \, \text{u} \approx 1.66 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg}$ and $c = 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}$:
$E = \Delta m c^2 \approx 2.64 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{J/reaction}$.

**Step 2: Calculate Power Input ($P_{in}$)**
$P_{in} = \text{reactions/sec} \times E = \frac{1.43 \times 10^{18}}{60} \times 2.64 \times 10^{-11} \approx 6.29 \times 10^5 \, \text{W}$.

**Step 3: Calculate Power Output ($P_{out}$)**
Losses: $40\%$ in Stage 1 ($0.6$ remains), $5\%$ in Stage 2 ($0.95$ remains).
$P_{gen} = P_{in} \times 0.6 \times 0.95 \approx 3.585 \times 10^5 \, \text{W}$.

**Step 4: Transmission loss**
Voltage after step-up: $12 \, \text{kV} \times 20 = 240 \, \text{kV} = 2.4 \times 10^5 \, \text{V}$.
Current $I = P/V = 3.585 \times 10^5 / 2.4 \times 10^5 \approx 1.49 \, \text{A}$.
Total distance = $100 + 10 + 0.1 = 110.1 \, \text{km}$.
Resistance $R = 110.1 \times 0.187 \, \Omega \approx 20.59 \, \Omega$.
$P_{loss, transmission} = I^2R = (1.49)^2 \times 20.59 \approx 45.7 \, \text{W}$.

**Step 5: Total Power Loss**
Total Loss = (Loss Stage 1) + (Loss Stage 2) + (Loss Stage 3)
Loss 1 = $0.4 \times P_{in} = 251,600 \, \text{W}$.
Loss 2 = $0.05 \times (0.6 \times P_{in}) = 18,870 \, \text{W}$.
Total = $251,600 + 18,870 + 45.7 \approx 270,516 \, \text{W}$.
