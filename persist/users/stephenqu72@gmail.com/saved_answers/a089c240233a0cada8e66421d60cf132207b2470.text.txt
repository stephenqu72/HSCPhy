To find the work done on the electron, we use the relationship between work, charge, electric field, and distance.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) $1.92 \times 10^{-13}\text{ J}$**.

### Explanation

**1. Identify the given values:**
*   Electric field strength ($E$) = $6 \times 10^6\text{ V m}^{-1}$
*   Distance ($d$) = $20\text{ cm} = 0.2\text{ m}$
*   Charge of an electron ($q$) = $1.6 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$ (This is a standard physical constant).

**2. State the relevant formulas:**
The work done ($W$) to move a charge in an electric field is given by:
$$W = F \times d$$
Since the force on a charge in an electric field is $F = qE$, we substitute this to get:
$$W = qEd$$

**3. Perform the calculation:**
Substitute the values into the equation:
$$W = (1.6 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}) \times (6 \times 10^6\text{ V m}^{-1}) \times (0.2\text{ m})$$

Group the numbers and the powers of 10:
$$W = (1.6 \times 6 \times 0.2) \times 10^{-19 + 6}$$
$$W = 1.92 \times 10^{-13}\text{ J}$$

**4. Conclusion:**
Moving the electron against the electric field requires energy equal to the change in potential energy, resulting in $1.92 \times 10^{-13}\text{ J}$ of work done.
