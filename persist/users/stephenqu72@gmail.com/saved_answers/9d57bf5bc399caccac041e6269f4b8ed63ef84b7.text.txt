Hello! As an HSC Physics teacher, I’m happy to guide you through this classic question on the nature of light. This topic is fundamental to understanding the transition from classical physics to modern quantum theory.

### Answers and Explanations

**(a) Particle Nature Expectation (1 mark)**
*   **Result:** If light were composed of particles (corpuscles), you would expect to see two distinct bright bands on the viewing screen, corresponding exactly to the paths of the two slits.
*   **Diagram Description:** Draw two vertical lines on the "viewing screen" aligned perfectly with the position of the two slits.
*   **Explanation:** Newton's corpuscular theory predicted that light travels in straight lines. Therefore, particles passing through slits would simply hit the screen directly opposite each opening.

**(b) Wave Nature Expectation (1 mark)**
*   **Result:** You would observe a series of alternating bright and dark bands known as an interference pattern (or fringes).
*   **Diagram Description:** Draw a series of vertical, parallel lines on the "viewing screen," with a bright central fringe and decreasing intensity for fringes moving outward from the center.
*   **Explanation:** Waves passing through two slits act as secondary sources (Huygens' Principle). These waves overlap and undergo superposition.

**(c) Evidence for Wave Nature (3 marks)**
*   **Explanation:** To get 3 marks, you need to connect the observation to the physics of waves:
    1.  **Superposition:** The observation of an interference pattern demonstrates that light waves from the two slits undergo constructive interference (bright bands, where waves are in phase) and destructive interference (dark bands, where waves are $180^\circ$ out of phase).
    2.  **Diffraction:** For these waves to overlap in the first place, they must have spread out (diffracted) after passing through the slits.
    3.  **Conclusion:** Particle models cannot explain the existence of dark regions (where light would "cancel out" light). Only the wave model, through the mathematical behavior of constructive and destructive interference, explains why light intensity varies across the screen in this specific pattern.

---

### Tips for the Exam:
*   In part (c), always use the term **"interference"** and mention both **"constructive"** and **"destructive"** components to ensure full marks.
*   Remember that the **central maximum** is always a point of constructive interference.
