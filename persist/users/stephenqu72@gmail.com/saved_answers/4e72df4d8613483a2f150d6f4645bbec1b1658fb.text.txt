### Explanation
To solve this problem, you need to understand the fundamental principles of stellar evolution as represented on the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram:

1.  **The Main Sequence:** The H-R diagram plots stellar luminosity (or absolute magnitude, $M_V$) against temperature (or color index, $B-V$). Stars on the "Main Sequence" are all fusing hydrogen into helium in their cores.
2.  **The Role of Mass:** The position of a star on the Main Sequence is determined almost entirely by its **mass**. 
    *   **High-mass stars** have stronger gravitational pressure in their cores, leading to higher rates of nuclear fusion, higher temperatures (making them "bluer" with a lower $B-V$ index), and higher luminosities (lower absolute magnitude $M_V$). Vega is located near the top-left of the Main Sequence.
    *   **Low-mass stars** have lower core pressures, leading to lower fusion rates, lower surface temperatures (making them "redder" with a higher $B-V$ index), and lower luminosities (higher absolute magnitude $M_V$). Proxima Centauri is located at the bottom-right of the Main Sequence.
3.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **A is incorrect:** Both stars are on the Main Sequence, meaning they are both currently fusing hydrogen into helium.
    *   **B is incorrect:** Location in the galaxy does not determine a star's position on the Main Sequence.
    *   **C is incorrect:** Stars do not "move up" the main sequence; they remain in the same general region of the Main Sequence for the majority of their lives until they run out of hydrogen fuel.
    *   **D is correct:** As established by the Mass-Luminosity relationship, the original mass of a star is the primary factor that dictates where it lands on the Main Sequence and how it evolves. Vega's position reflects its significantly higher mass compared to the low-mass red dwarf, Proxima Centauri.
