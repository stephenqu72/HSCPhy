To solve this problem, we use **Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit ($r$):

$$\frac{T^2}{r^3} = k$$

When using units of Earth years for the period and Astronomical Units (AU) for distance, the constant $k$ becomes 1. Therefore, the relationship simplifies to:

$$\frac{T_1^2}{r_1^3} = \frac{T_2^2}{r_2^3}$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1. **Identify the variables:**
   - Earth ($1$): $T_1 = 1.00 \text{ year}$, $r_1 = 1.00 \text{ AU}$
   - Saturn ($2$): $T_2 = ?$ (to be calculated), $r_2 = 9.00 \text{ AU}$

2. **Set up the equation:**
   Using the ratio form:
   $$\frac{T_2^2}{r_2^3} = \frac{T_1^2}{r_1^3}$$
   Substitute the known values:
   $$\frac{T_2^2}{(9.00)^3} = \frac{(1.00)^2}{(1.00)^3}$$
   $$T_2^2 = \frac{1}{1} \times 9.00^3$$
   $$T_2^2 = 729$$

3. **Solve for $T_2$:**
   $$T_2 = \sqrt{729}$$
   $$T_2 = 27.0 \text{ Earth years}$$

**Answer:** The orbital period of Saturn is **27.0 Earth years**.
