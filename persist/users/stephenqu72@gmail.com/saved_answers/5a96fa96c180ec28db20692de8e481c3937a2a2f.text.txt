### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the historical context of the Geiger-Marsden experiment (often referred to as the Rutherford gold foil experiment) and the prevailing model of the atom at that time.

1.  **The Particles Used:** Geiger and Marsden used **alpha particles** (which are helium nuclei, $^4_2\text{He}^{2+}$) as "bullets" to probe the atom. This immediately allows us to eliminate options B and D, which refer to protons.
2.  **The Initial Expectation:** Before the experiment, the scientific community accepted the **"Plum Pudding Model"** proposed by J.J. Thomson. In this model, the atom was viewed as a diffuse sphere of positive charge with negatively charged electrons embedded throughout it, like plums in a pudding.
    *   Because the positive charge was thought to be spread out thinly over the entire volume of the atom, physicists believed that high-speed, heavy alpha particles would pass through the atom with minimal deflection.
    *   **Diagram C** correctly depicts the incident alpha particles passing straight through a uniform, "pudding-like" sphere of positive charge.
3.  **Contrast with Reality:** Diagram A represents the *actual* result of the experiment (large-angle scattering), which led Rutherford to discard the Plum Pudding model in favor of the nuclear model of the atom. However, the question specifically asks for what they **INITIALLY expected**, which corresponds to the outcome predicted by the Thomson model shown in Diagram C.
