### Explanation
To determine why (B) is the correct choice, let’s analyze the requirements for an orbit to be considered "geostationary":

1.  **Periodicity:** A geostationary satellite must have an orbital period exactly equal to Earth's rotational period (one sidereal day, approximately 23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4 seconds). This allows the satellite to appear stationary relative to a fixed point on the Earth's surface.
2.  **Orbital Plane:** For the satellite to remain stationary above a single point, it must orbit directly above the Earth's equator. If it were at any other latitude, the satellite would appear to oscillate north and south throughout the day. Therefore, all geostationary satellites must be located in an equatorial orbit.
3.  **Radius:** By applying Kepler's Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$), there is only one specific altitude where an object orbits with a period of one sidereal day, which is approximately $35,786$ km above sea level.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A)** The Moon is a natural satellite, but its orbital period is approximately $27.3$ days, not one day, and it does not follow an equatorial orbit.
*   **(C)** Satellites that photograph and map the Earth's surface (remote sensing) generally require low-Earth orbits (LEO) or polar orbits to scan the entire surface as the Earth rotates beneath them. A geostationary satellite remains fixed over one point and cannot map the entire globe.
*   **(D)** This describes a polar or near-polar orbiting satellite. Because a geostationary satellite is fixed over one point on the equator, it cannot "pass over" the entire surface.

### Summary
Geostationary satellites must orbit in the same direction as the Earth's rotation, have a circular orbit, and be positioned directly above the **equator** to maintain their fixed position relative to an observer on the ground.
