### Answer

In physics, the role of evidence is paramount: it serves as the final arbiter for the validity of theoretical models. Theories must be falsifiable and consistent with empirical observations. When new, high-quality evidence contradicts an existing paradigm, it forces scientists to modify or reject established theories in favor of new ones that better explain the observed data.

**1. Hubble and the expansion of the Universe:**
Before Edwin Hubble’s observations in the late 1920s, the prevailing scientific consensus (supported by Einstein's initial static cosmological model) was that the Universe was static and unchanging. By measuring the redshift of light from distant galaxies, Hubble observed that galaxies were moving away from Earth, with their velocity of recession proportional to their distance ($v = H_0d$). This empirical evidence provided the foundation for the Big Bang theory, forcing the scientific community to abandon the "static universe" concept in favor of a dynamic, expanding one.

**2. The Photoelectric Effect and Einstein:**
Classical wave theory failed to explain why the kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons depended on the frequency of the incident light rather than its intensity. Einstein proposed that light consists of discrete "quanta" or photons ($E = hf$). This theoretical shift was driven by the evidence from experiments conducted by Lenard and Millikan. The data could not be reconciled with classical physics, leading to the development of quantum mechanics and providing the necessary evidence to confirm the particle-like nature of electromagnetic radiation.

In both cases, evidence acted as a catalyst that either demolished an incorrect model or provided the necessary support to bridge the gap between theoretical prediction and physical reality.
