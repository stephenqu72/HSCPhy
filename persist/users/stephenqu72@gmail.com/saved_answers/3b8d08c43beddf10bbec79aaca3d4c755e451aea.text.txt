To solve this problem, we use the diffraction grating formula:

$$d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$$

Where:
*   $d$ is the grating spacing (constant for both lights).
*   $\theta$ is the angle of diffraction.
*   $n$ is the order of the maximum.
*   $\lambda$ is the wavelength.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Understand the condition:** The problem states that the two lights coincide, meaning they are diffracted at the exact same angle $\theta$. Therefore, the product $d \sin(\theta)$ must be the same for both.
2.  **Set up the equation:** 
    For the orange light ($n_1 = 4$, $\lambda_1 = 600\text{ nm}$):
    $$d \sin(\theta) = n_1 \lambda_1 = 4 \times 600$$
    For the blue light ($n_2 = 5$, $\lambda_2 = ?$):
    $$d \sin(\theta) = n_2 \lambda_2 = 5 \times \lambda_2$$
3.  **Solve for $\lambda_2$:**
    Since the diffraction angles are the same, we equate the two expressions:
    $$4 \times 600 = 5 \times \lambda_2$$
    $$2400 = 5 \times \lambda_2$$
    $$\lambda_2 = \frac{2400}{5}$$
    $$\lambda_2 = 480\text{ nm}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, the correct choice is **(B)**.
