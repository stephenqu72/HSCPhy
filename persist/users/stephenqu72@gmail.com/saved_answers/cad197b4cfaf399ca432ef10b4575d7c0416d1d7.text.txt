### Model Answer

The scientific method advances through a process of conjecture and refutation. In the 18th and 19th centuries, the nature of light was the subject of a intense debate between Newton’s **corpuscular model** (light as particles) and Huygens’ **wave model** (light as mechanical waves).

**1. The Corpuscular Model and its Initial Success**
Isaac Newton proposed that light consisted of streams of particles (corpuscles). This model successfully explained rectilinear propagation (shadows), reflection, and the laws of refraction, which were well-understood at the time. Because Newton was a pre-eminent authority, this model was widely accepted.

**2. The Challenge: Evidence for the Wave Model**
Validation of a model occurs when experimental evidence contradicts the predictions of the current model.
*   **Young’s Double-Slit Experiment (1801):** Thomas Young provided evidence of interference and diffraction. Newton's corpuscular model could not explain why light passing through two slits would create alternating bright and dark fringes. These phenomena are exclusive to wave motion (superposition), significantly weakening the corpuscular model.
*   **Foucault’s Speed of Light Experiment (1850):** A critical test involved measuring the speed of light in water. Newton’s model predicted that light should travel faster in denser media (due to an attractive force). Foucault’s experiment proved light traveled *slower* in water than in air, directly contradicting the corpuscular model and supporting the wave model.

**3. Synthesis and the Nature of Science**
The scientific community shifts models when the weight of evidence (the "new information" David Suzuki refers to) renders the old model incapable of explaining observed phenomena. The success of the wave model was cemented by Maxwell’s equations, which described light as an electromagnetic wave. This transition illustrates the provisional nature of scientific knowledge: models are not "Truth," but useful frameworks that persist only as long as they can withstand rigorous experimental testing.
