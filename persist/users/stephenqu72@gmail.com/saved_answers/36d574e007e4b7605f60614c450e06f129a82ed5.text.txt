### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) The atom is mostly empty space**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we must look at what Rutherford's gold foil experiment actually demonstrated. Before this experiment, the "Plum Pudding" model (proposed by J.J. Thomson) suggested that atoms were spheres of positive charge with electrons embedded throughout.

1.  **The Observations:** Rutherford fired alpha particles (positively charged) at a thin sheet of gold foil. Most particles passed straight through, some were slightly deflected, and a very small fraction bounced back at extreme angles.
2.  **The Deduction:** 
    *   Because most alpha particles passed through, Rutherford concluded that the atom must be mostly **empty space** rather than a solid mass of positive charge.
    *   Because some particles were deflected or bounced back, he concluded there must be a tiny, dense, positively charged center, which he called the **nucleus**.
3.  **Analyzing the options:**
    *   **(A)** Electrons were already discovered by J.J. Thomson (the Plum Pudding model) before Rutherford's experiment.
    *   **(B)** Bohr, not Rutherford, proposed that electrons exist in quantized energy shells surrounding the nucleus.
    *   **(C)** While Rutherford did identify that there is a positive nucleus, in the context of "significant changes" to the model, the most profound discovery that invalidated the "solid" Plum Pudding model was the realization that the vast majority of the atom is **empty space**. Option (D) captures the primary conclusion that fundamentally shifted our understanding of atomic structure.
