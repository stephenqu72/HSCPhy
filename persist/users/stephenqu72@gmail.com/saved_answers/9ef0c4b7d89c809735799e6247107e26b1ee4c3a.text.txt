To solve this problem, we use the standard constants for Earth:
- Mass of the Earth ($M$): $5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$
- Radius of the Earth ($R_E$): $6.37 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
- Gravitational constant ($G$): $6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ N m}^2\text{ kg}^{-2}$

Given:
- Mass of satellite ($m$): $11,000\text{ kg}$
- Altitude ($h$): $20,000\text{ km} = 2.0 \times 10^7\text{ m}$
- Total orbital radius ($r$): $R_E + h = 6.37 \times 10^6 + 2.0 \times 10^7 = 2.637 \times 10^7\text{ m}$

### a) Calculate the magnitude of its gravitational potential energy
The formula for gravitational potential energy ($U$) is:
$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$
Substituting the values:
$$U = -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (11,000)}{2.637 \times 10^7}$$
$$U \approx -1.66 \times 10^{11}\text{ J}$$
Since the question asks for the **magnitude**, we express it as a positive value:
$$\text{Magnitude} \approx 1.66 \times 10^{11}\text{ J}$$

### b) Calculate its orbital velocity
The formula for orbital velocity ($v$) is:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Substituting the values:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24})}{2.637 \times 10^7}}$$
$$v = \sqrt{1.51 \times 10^7}$$
$$v \approx 3886\text{ m/s} \text{ (or } 3.89 \times 10^3\text{ m/s)}$$

---
### Explanation of steps
1. **Define constants and variables:** Always convert altitude ($h$) to total orbital radius ($r$) by adding the Earth's radius ($R_E$). Ensure all units are in SI (meters, kilograms, seconds).
2. **Gravitational Potential Energy:** Remember that $U$ is defined as negative, meaning energy is required to move the object to infinity. The magnitude is simply the absolute value.
3. **Orbital Velocity:** This is derived by equating the gravitational force to the centripetal force ($F_g = F_c$). The final velocity formula is independent of the satellite's mass ($m$).
