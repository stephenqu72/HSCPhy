### Answer

To examine spectra from various sources, a spectroscope (or a diffraction grating/prism) is used to disperse light into its constituent wavelengths.

1. **Continuous Spectrum (Incandescent Source):**
   *   **Source:** A white-hot filament lamp (e.g., a standard incandescent light bulb).
   *   **Description:** When the light from the heated tungsten filament is passed through a spectroscope, it produces a continuous, unbroken band of colours ranging from violet to red. This indicates that all wavelengths of visible light are present, characteristic of a black-body radiator.

2. **Emission Spectrum (Gaseous Discharge Source):**
   *   **Source:** A low-pressure gas discharge tube (e.g., hydrogen or neon gas tube).
   *   **Description:** When a high voltage is applied, the excited gas emits light. The spectrum appears as a series of distinct, sharp, coloured lines on a black background. Each line corresponds to a specific wavelength (energy transition) unique to the element within the tube.

3. **Absorption Spectrum (Cool Gas Source):**
   *   **Source:** A continuous light source (like a white-hot lamp) viewed through a cooler gas (e.g., a sodium vapour cloud).
   *   **Description:** The light first passes through the cool gas, which absorbs specific wavelengths corresponding to the energy levels of its atoms. The resulting spectrum is a continuous band of colour interrupted by dark, sharp lines. These dark lines occur at the exact same positions as the emission lines for that specific element.
