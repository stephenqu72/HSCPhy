### Answer
The correct answer is **a) The gold leaf did not move.**

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the fundamental principles of the **photoelectric effect** as observed in the Hallwachs experiment:

1.  **The Photoelectric Effect:** When light (specifically UV light) hits a metal surface, it provides energy to the electrons within the metal. If the frequency of the light is above the threshold frequency of the metal, electrons are ejected from the surface. These are called photoelectrons.
2.  **Electrostatic Principles:** A gold leaf electroscope measures electric charge. When the metal cap is charged, the same charge distributes across the cap, the stem, and the gold leaf. Because the stem and the leaf have the same charge (like charges repel), the gold leaf is pushed away from the stem (extended outwards).
3.  **The Interaction:** 
    *   If the electroscope is **negatively charged**, the UV light causes the ejection of photoelectrons from the metal cap. This loss of negative charge causes the leaf to collapse as the repulsion between the stem and the leaf decreases.
    *   If the electroscope is **positively charged**, the metal cap has a deficiency of electrons. While the UV light still strikes the metal, the ejected photoelectrons are immediately attracted back to the positively charged surface due to strong electrostatic attraction. Additionally, it is much harder for photons to "knock off" electrons from a positively charged material because the work function effectively increases as the positive potential holds the remaining electrons more tightly.

Consequently, there is no net loss of charge on the electroscope. Since the charge remains constant, the electrostatic repulsion between the stem and the gold leaf remains constant, and the leaf does not move.
