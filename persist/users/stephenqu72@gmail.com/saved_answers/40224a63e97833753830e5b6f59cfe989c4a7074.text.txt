### Teacher's Solution

#### (a) (i) Diagram
To represent the forces, draw:
1. An arrow pointing **vertically downwards** from the ball, labeled $mg$ (weight).
2. An arrow pointing **along the string** towards the pivot point, labeled $T$ (tension).

#### (a) (ii) Equilibrium
The ball is **not in equilibrium**. While the net force in the vertical direction is zero, there is a **net resultant force** (the centripetal force) acting horizontally towards the center of the circular path to cause the acceleration required for circular motion. Equilibrium requires the net force to be zero.

#### (b) Tension in the string
The vertical component of the tension ($T \cos \theta$) must balance the weight ($mg$) for the ball to maintain a constant vertical height.
Given $m = 0.25\text{ kg}$, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$, and $\theta = 30^\circ$:
$$T \cos(30^\circ) = mg$$
$$T = \frac{0.25 \times 9.8}{\cos(30^\circ)}$$
$$T = \frac{2.45}{0.866} \approx 2.83\text{ N}$$

#### (c) Speed of rotation
The horizontal component of the tension ($T \sin \theta$) provides the centripetal force ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$):
$$T \sin \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
We know $T \sin \theta = (2.829) \sin(30^\circ) = 2.829 \times 0.5 = 1.4145\text{ N}$.
Now equate this to the centripetal force:
$$1.4145 = \frac{0.25 \times v^2}{0.33}$$
$$v^2 = \frac{1.4145 \times 0.33}{0.25}$$
$$v^2 = 1.867$$
$$v \approx 1.37\text{ m/s}$$

***

### Explanation of Concepts
1.  **Forces in Conical Pendulums:** Always decompose the tension into vertical and horizontal components. The vertical component counteracts gravity, and the horizontal component serves as the centripetal force.
2.  **Equilibrium vs. Uniform Circular Motion:** Students often confuse "constant speed" with "equilibrium." Because the direction of velocity is constantly changing, the object is accelerating, and thus the net force cannot be zero.
3.  **Algebraic steps:** Always ensure your units are consistent (kg, m, s) before calculating. Using $\tan \theta = \frac{F_c}{mg}$ is a useful shortcut derived from dividing the horizontal force equation by the vertical force equation, which eliminates $T$ and $m$ initially.
