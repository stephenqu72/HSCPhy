To solve this problem, we use the radioactive decay law:
$$N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$
Where $N_0$ is the initial mass, $N(t)$ is the mass at time $t$, and $\lambda$ is the decay constant.

### Part (a)
**Step 1: Determine the half-life ($t_{1/2}$) from the graph.**
The initial mass $N_0$ is $20,000 \text{ g}$. The half-life is the time taken for the mass to reach half its initial value, which is $10,000 \text{ g}$.
Looking at the graph, at $Mass = 10,000 \text{ g}$, the corresponding time is $15 \text{ hours}$.
Thus, $t_{1/2} = 15 \text{ hours}$.

**Step 2: Calculate the decay constant ($\lambda$).**
The relationship between half-life and the decay constant is:
$$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{0.693}{15} \approx 0.0462 \text{ h}^{-1}$$

---

### Part (b)
**Step 1: Set up the equation for one-tenth of the initial value.**
We want to find $t$ such that $N(t) = \frac{1}{10} N_0$.
$$\frac{1}{10} N_0 = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$
$$0.1 = e^{-\lambda t}$$

**Step 2: Solve for $t$.**
Take the natural logarithm of both sides:
$$\ln(0.1) = -\lambda t$$
$$t = \frac{-\ln(0.1)}{\lambda}$$
Using $\ln(0.1) \approx -2.303$ and our value for $\lambda$:
$$t = \frac{2.303}{0.0462} \approx 49.85 \text{ hours}$$

Rounding to significant figures consistent with the graph, it would take approximately **$50 \text{ hours}$**.
