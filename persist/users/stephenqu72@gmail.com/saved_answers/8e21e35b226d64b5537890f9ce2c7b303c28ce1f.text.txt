To solve this problem, we apply **Lenz's Law**, which states that an induced current will flow in a direction that opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it. This implies that the magnetic interaction between the magnet and the conductive ring will always act to oppose the motion of the magnet.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Approaching the Ring (Magnet moving to the left):**
    As the North pole of the magnet enters the ring from the right, the magnetic flux through the ring increases. To oppose this increase, the ring induces a current that creates a North pole on the right side (facing the incoming magnet). This creates a repulsive force between the magnet and the ring. Since the magnet is moving left, the repulsive force pushes the ring to the **left**.

2.  **Passing the Center:**
    When the center of the bar magnet is perfectly aligned with the plane of the ring, the net change in flux is momentarily zero (as the increasing flux from one side balances the decreasing flux from the other). At this exact instant, there is no induced current and **no force** between the magnet and the ring.

3.  **Exiting the Ring:**
    As the magnet continues to move to the left, the South pole of the magnet moves away from the ring on the left side. The magnetic flux through the ring is decreasing. To oppose this decrease, the ring induces a current that creates a North pole on the left side (to "pull" the receding South pole back). This creates an attractive force between the ring and the magnet. Since the ring is being pulled toward the magnet (which is on its left), the ring experiences a force to the **left**.

### Conclusion:
Throughout the entire motion, the magnetic interaction resists the movement of the magnet relative to the ring. In both the entry phase and the exit phase, the force exerted on the ring is in the same direction as the motion of the magnet. Therefore, the ring is pushed to the left as the magnet enters, has a moment of no force, and is then pulled to the left as the magnet exits.
