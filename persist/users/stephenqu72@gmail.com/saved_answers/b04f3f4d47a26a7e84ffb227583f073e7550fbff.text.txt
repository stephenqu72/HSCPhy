### Answer

The development of the atomic model is a quintessential example of the scientific method, where experimental anomalies necessitated the rejection of older models in favor of more sophisticated ones.

**1. J.J. Thomson’s Cathode Ray Tube (CRT) Experiment**
*   **Evidence:** By applying electric and magnetic fields to cathode rays, Thomson determined that the rays consisted of negatively charged particles (electrons) with a mass much smaller than that of an atom.
*   **Change to Model:** This disproved Dalton’s "indivisible atom" theory. Thomson proposed the "Plum Pudding" model, where negatively charged electrons were embedded in a sphere of uniform positive charge.

**2. Rutherford’s Gold Foil Experiment**
*   **Evidence:** When firing $\alpha$-particles at a thin gold foil, most passed through, but some were deflected at large angles, and a very small fraction bounced directly back.
*   **Change to Model:** The Plum Pudding model could not explain the backscattering. Rutherford concluded that the positive charge and most of the mass must be concentrated in a tiny, dense central **nucleus**. This replaced the Plum Pudding model with a planetary-style nuclear model.

**3. Chadwick’s Beryllium Bombardment Experiment (shown in the diagram)**
*   **Evidence:** James Chadwick bombarded beryllium with $\alpha$-particles, producing a highly penetrating, neutral radiation. When this radiation hit paraffin wax, it ejected protons. By applying conservation of momentum, Chadwick deduced the existence of a neutral particle with mass approximately equal to a proton.
*   **Change to Model:** This resolved the mass-charge discrepancy in the nucleus (where the mass of the nucleus was double what the proton count suggested). It led to the model of the nucleus as a dense collection of protons and neutrons, completing our foundational understanding of the atomic structure.
