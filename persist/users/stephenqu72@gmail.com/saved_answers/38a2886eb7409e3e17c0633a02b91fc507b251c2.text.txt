To solve this problem, we use the Doppler effect formula for a stationary observer and a moving source:

$$f_{obs} = f_s \left( \frac{v}{v \mp v_s} \right)$$

Where:
*   $f_{obs}$ is the observed frequency.
*   $f_s$ is the source frequency.
*   $v$ is the speed of sound (assume $340 \text{ m s}^{-1}$).
*   $v_s$ is the speed of the source ($221 \text{ m s}^{-1}$).

### Step 1: Find the source frequency ($f_s$)
When the plane moves **towards** the observer, the frequency increases:
$$1200 = f_s \left( \frac{340}{340 - 221} \right)$$
$$1200 = f_s \left( \frac{340}{119} \right)$$
$$f_s = 1200 \times \left( \frac{119}{340} \right) = 420 \text{ Hz}$$

### Step 2: Calculate the frequency when the plane moves away
When the plane moves **away**, the observed frequency decreases:
$$f_{obs} = 420 \left( \frac{340}{340 + 221} \right)$$
$$f_{obs} = 420 \left( \frac{340}{561} \right)$$
$$f_{obs} \approx 254.545... \text{ Hz}$$

**Answer:** The frequency heard by the observer as the aeroplane flies away is approximately **$255 \text{ Hz}$**.

***

### Explanation of the approach:
1.  **Identify the variables:** We know the speed of the source ($v_s = 221 \text{ m s}^{-1}$) and the observed frequency as it approaches ($1200 \text{ Hz}$). We use the standard speed of sound ($v = 340 \text{ m s}^{-1}$) for air.
2.  **Rearrange the formula:** We first isolate $f_s$ because the source frequency is the same regardless of the direction of motion. We use the "minus" sign in the denominator for an approaching source.
3.  **Apply to the second scenario:** We use the calculated $f_s$ in the Doppler formula, this time using a "plus" sign in the denominator because the source is moving away, which stretches the waves and lowers the frequency.
