To solve this problem, we need to find the point between the Earth and the Moon where the gravitational force exerted by the Earth on a spacecraft equals the gravitational force exerted by the Moon.

### Step 1: Identify the formula
The gravitational force between two objects is given by Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:
$$F = \frac{G M m}{r^2}$$
Where:
- $G$ is the gravitational constant.
- $M$ is the mass of the planet/moon.
- $m$ is the mass of the spacecraft.
- $r$ is the distance from the center of the mass.

### Step 2: Set up the equation
Let $d$ be the distance from the center of the Earth to the point where the net force is zero. The distance from the center of the Moon to this point will be $(R - d)$, where $R$ is the distance between the Earth and the Moon ($3.84 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$).

For the forces to be equal:
$$\frac{G M_E m}{d^2} = \frac{G M_M m}{(R - d)^2}$$

Canceling $G$ and $m$ from both sides:
$$\frac{M_E}{d^2} = \frac{M_M}{(R - d)^2}$$

### Step 3: Solve for $d$
Take the square root of both sides to simplify the equation:
$$\frac{\sqrt{M_E}}{d} = \frac{\sqrt{M_M}}{R - d}$$

Rearranging to solve for $d$:
$$\sqrt{M_E}(R - d) = \sqrt{M_M}d$$
$$R\sqrt{M_E} - d\sqrt{M_E} = d\sqrt{M_M}$$
$$R\sqrt{M_E} = d(\sqrt{M_E} + \sqrt{M_M})$$
$$d = \frac{R\sqrt{M_E}}{\sqrt{M_E} + \sqrt{M_M}}$$

### Step 4: Plug in the values
- $R = 3.84 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$
- $M_E = 5.98 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$
- $M_M = 7.34 \times 10^{22} \text{ kg}$

$$\sqrt{M_E} \approx 2.4454 \times 10^{12}$$
$$\sqrt{M_M} \approx 2.7092 \times 10^{11}$$

$$d = \frac{(3.84 \times 10^8) \times (2.4454 \times 10^{12})}{(2.4454 \times 10^{12} + 0.27092 \times 10^{12})}$$
$$d = \frac{9.3903 \times 10^{20}}{2.7163 \times 10^{12}}$$
$$d \approx 3.4569 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$$

Rounding to three significant figures, we get $3.46 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$.
