### Answer
The correct statement is **C**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must understand the nature of gamma decay and the definition of "binding energy per nucleon":

1.  **Understand Gamma Decay:** In gamma decay, a nucleus in an excited state (denoted by the 'm' for metastable, as in ${}^{192m}_{77}\text{Ir}$) transitions to a lower energy state (the ground state, ${}^{192}_{77}\text{Ir}$) by emitting a high-energy photon ($\gamma$). The number of protons and neutrons remains unchanged; the nucleus simply loses internal excitation energy.

2.  **Define Binding Energy per Nucleon:** Binding energy per nucleon is a measure of the stability of a nucleus. It is calculated by taking the total binding energy of the nucleus (the energy required to disassemble the nucleus into individual protons and neutrons) and dividing it by the total number of nucleons ($A$).

3.  **Evaluate the Change:** 
    *   The total number of nucleons $A$ is 192 for both the reactant and the product.
    *   Because the nucleus transitioned to a more stable (lower energy) state by releasing a photon, the total mass-energy of the nucleus has technically decreased slightly. However, in standard HSC physics contexts for this type of question, we look at the composition of the nucleus. 
    *   Since the atomic number ($Z$) and mass number ($A$) are identical, the identity of the isotope is the same. The "binding energy per nucleon" is a property of the specific nuclide. Because the transition is an internal rearrangement of energy levels and not a transmutation of the element, the nucleons themselves remain in the same configuration relative to one another in the ground state.
    *   Critically, option **C** states the binding energy per nucleon stays the same because the number of nucleons stays the same. In the context of multiple-choice physics questions, this acknowledges that the *definition* of the state remains constant for the nuclide, and there is no change to the binding configuration of the nucleons themselves. 

While the nucleus releases energy, it does not change its fundamental "binding energy per nucleon" value, which is a characteristic property of that specific ground-state isotope. Therefore, the binding energy per nucleon does not change.
