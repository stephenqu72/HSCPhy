### Explanation
To solve this, we need to consider the historical context of late 19th-century physics regarding Maxwell’s electromagnetic theory:

1.  **Maxwell’s Mathematical Framework:** James Clerk Maxwell published his equations in 1865, which proposed that light is an electromagnetic wave traveling at a speed $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$. At the time, this was a highly theoretical and complex mathematical model.
2.  **The Scientific Method:** In physics, a revolutionary theory (especially one that suggests light is an electromagnetic wave) requires empirical validation. In 1865, there was a significant lack of experimental equipment or techniques sensitive enough to detect high-frequency electromagnetic oscillations (radio waves).
3.  **The Turning Point:** Maxwell's theory did not gain widespread acceptance until Heinrich Hertz conducted his famous experiments in 1887. By generating and detecting electromagnetic waves in the laboratory, Hertz provided the necessary experimental evidence to prove Maxwell’s hypothesis. This fits the "20 years later" timeline mentioned in the question.

*   **(B):** The photoelectric effect, which demonstrated the particle-like nature of light, was not formally explained until Albert Einstein in 1905, well after Maxwell's theory was accepted.
*   **(C):** The speed of light ($c$) had been measured by figures like Ole Rømer (1676) and later refined by Fizeau and Foucault in the mid-19th century. By 1865, experimental measurements of the speed of light were already established.
