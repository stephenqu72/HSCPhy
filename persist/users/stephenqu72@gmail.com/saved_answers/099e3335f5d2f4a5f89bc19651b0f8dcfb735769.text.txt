### Answer
The experiment measured the speed of photons emitted by a rapidly moving neutral pion. The results showed that, regardless of the high speed of the source ($0.99975c$), the photons still travelled at the speed of light, $c$, relative to the laboratory frame. This provides experimental support for the second postulate of Einstein's theory of special relativity, which states that the speed of light in a vacuum is constant for all observers, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer.

### Explanation
To solve this question, you must apply the core principles of Einstein's Theory of Special Relativity:

1.  **Identify the key postulate:** Recall that the second postulate of special relativity asserts that the speed of light in a vacuum, denoted as $c$, is an invariant constant. It is independent of the state of motion of the light source.
2.  **Analyze the scenario:** In classical (Galilean) physics, one might expect the speed of the emitted photons to be the sum of the source velocity and the speed of light ($v_{total} = v_{source} + c$). However, special relativity forbids this.
3.  **Formulate the justification:** By measuring the photons from a pion moving at $0.99975c$, scientists demonstrated that the photons did not travel at $1.99975c$ (or any other speed), but exactly at $c$. This empirical evidence confirmed that light speed does not obey simple Galilean velocity addition, thus validating the second postulate of special relativity. 
4.  **Structure for marks:** In an HSC context, you need two clear points for the 2 marks:
    *   State that the experiment confirmed the speed of light is invariant (constant).
    *   Explain that this result is independent of the motion of the source (the pion), which directly supports Einstein’s second postulate.
