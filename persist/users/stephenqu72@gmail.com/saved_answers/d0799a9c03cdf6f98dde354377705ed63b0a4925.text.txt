### Answer
This scenario provides direct evidence for the phenomenon of **time dilation** as predicted by Einstein’s Special Theory of Relativity. 

According to classical physics, the muons should decay rapidly based on their proper lifetime ($\Delta t_0 = 2.2\ \mu s$), meaning almost none should reach the ground from $10\text{ km}$ away. However, because the muons are travelling at relativistic speeds ($0.98c$), time for the muons "slows down" relative to an observer on Earth. This effect is known as time dilation, where the observed lifetime of the muon is significantly extended:

$$\Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

Because their lifetime is extended, the muons are able to cover the $10\text{ km}$ distance before decaying. The fact that a much higher number of muons ($1$ in $8$) reach the ground compared to the classical prediction ($1$ in $45,000$) validates the relativistic prediction that time elapses more slowly for objects moving at speeds approaching the speed of light.
