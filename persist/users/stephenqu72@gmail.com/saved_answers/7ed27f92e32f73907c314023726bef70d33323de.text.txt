### Explanation

To understand why **(C)** is the correct answer, we need to evaluate the purpose and the results of Robert Millikan's famous 1909 oil drop experiment:

*   **Why (C) is correct:** Millikan’s goal was to measure the magnitude of the charge of a single electron. By observing tiny oil droplets suspended in an electric field where the gravitational force $F_g = mg$ was balanced by the electrostatic force $F_e = qE$, he found that the total charge on the droplets was always an integer multiple of a fundamental unit. This quantified the elementary charge ($e \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$), effectively proving that charge is quantized.

*   **Why (B) and (D) are incorrect:** It is a common HSC Physics trap to confuse Millikan's experiment with J.J. Thomson’s experiment. 
    *   **J.J. Thomson** used cathode ray tubes to determine the **charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$)** of the electron. 
    *   Once Millikan determined the charge ($q$) of the electron, physicists could take Thomson's known value for $q/m$ and use it to calculate the **mass of the electron** ($m = q / (q/m)$). 
    *   Option (D) incorrectly mentions the "mass of a proton," which is not the primary outcome of combining Millikan's data with Thomson's; their work was specifically focused on the properties of the electron.

*   **Why (A) is incorrect:** While the experiment did involve equating gravity and electrostatic force (the condition for suspension), this is a *description of the method*, not the *significance* (the "why it was important") of the experiment. The fundamental importance lies in the *result* (quantifying the charge), not the mechanism itself.
