### Answer and Explanation

#### (a) Reason for the late discovery of the neutron
**Answer:** The neutron is electrically neutral (has no charge).
**Justification:** Early experimental techniques for identifying subatomic particles, such as those used by J.J. Thomson (cathode ray tubes) or Millikan (oil drop experiment), relied on the interaction of the particle with electric or magnetic fields. Since neutrons lack a charge, they are not deflected by these fields, making them "invisible" to the detection methods available in the early 20th century.

#### (b) Inconsistency from the experiments
**Answer:** The Bothe-Becker experiment (1930) observed a highly penetrating "gamma radiation" when Beryllium was bombarded with alpha particles. 
**Explanation:** If the radiation were gamma rays, the energy required to eject protons from a paraffin wax target (as observed in later experiments by the Joliot-Curies) would be physically impossible according to the conservation of momentum and energy. The calculated energy for the "gamma rays" was inconsistent with the kinetic energy imparted to the protons, suggesting the particle involved must have had significant mass, unlike a massless photon.

#### (c) Method used by Chadwick
**Method:** Chadwick bombarded Beryllium-9 targets with alpha particles ($\alpha$) emitted from a Polonium source. This produced a stream of neutral radiation. He directed this radiation at various materials, including paraffin wax. By measuring the kinetic energy of the recoil protons emitted from the paraffin, he used the laws of conservation of momentum and energy to calculate that the incident neutral particle must have a mass approximately equal to that of a proton.

**Diagram Description:**
1. **Source:** Polonium-210 (emits $\alpha$ particles).
2. **Target:** Beryllium sheet.
3. **Emission:** Neutral radiation ($n$) hits a paraffin block.
4. **Detector:** Protons ($p^+$) recoil from the paraffin into an ionization chamber/detector.

#### (d) Evaluation of reliability
**Evaluation:** Chadwick’s method was highly reliable because it relied on fundamental conservation laws of physics—specifically conservation of mass-energy and momentum. By applying these laws to the collision between the unknown neutral particle and the protons in the paraffin, he was able to mathematically predict a mass consistent with that of a proton. Furthermore, the reproducibility of the experiment by other laboratories provided strong scientific evidence, confirming that his results were not an artifact of experimental error.
