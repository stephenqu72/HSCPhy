### Answer and Explanation

**Part (a)**
*   **Step 1:** Two protons fuse to form a deuterium nucleus (one proton and one neutron), releasing a positron ($e^+$) and a neutrino ($\nu$).
*   **Step 2:** The deuterium nucleus fuses with another proton to form a helium-3 nucleus, releasing a gamma-ray photon ($\gamma$).
*   **Step 3:** Two helium-3 nuclei fuse to form a stable helium-4 nucleus, releasing two excess protons back into the stellar environment.

**Part (b)**
The particle is a positron ($e^+$). It disappears via **annihilation** when it encounters an electron ($e^-$). The two particles collide and convert their entire mass into electromagnetic energy (gamma rays).

**Part (c)**
The energy is calculated using $E = mc^2$.
Since an electron and a positron each have a rest mass of $0.511 \text{ MeV}/c^2$, the total mass converted is $1.022 \text{ MeV}/c^2$.
$$E = (1.022 \text{ MeV}/c^2) \times c^2 = 1.022 \text{ MeV}$$

**Part (d)**
The statement is **invalid**. "Direct hydrogen-to-helium fusion" (the simultaneous collision of four protons) has an infinitesimally low probability of occurring in the Sun because it would require a four-body collision, which is statistically impossible under stellar conditions. The proton-proton chain is the dominant mechanism, and essentially $100\%$ of the energy produced by hydrogen fusion in the Sun is derived from these chain processes, not a "direct" fusion method.
