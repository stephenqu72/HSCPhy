To solve this, we must evaluate the scenario against Einstein's two postulates of Special Relativity:

1.  **The Principle of Relativity:** The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference. There is no experiment you can perform within a closed system moving at a constant velocity to determine your speed relative to an external frame.
2.  **The Constancy of the Speed of Light:** The speed of light in a vacuum, $c$, is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the light source or the observer.

**Evaluating the Conclusions:**
*   **Analysis of Conclusion II (The Incorrect View):** An observer in a stationary frame might incorrectly argue that if the train moves at $c$, light from the face can never reach the mirror (because the mirror is moving away at $c$), so no reflection would be seen. This violates the principle of relativity because it implies the passenger on the train could detect their absolute motion by the absence of a reflection.
*   **Analysis of Conclusion I (The Correct View):** According to the Principle of Relativity, the passenger is in their own inertial reference frame. Within that frame, the passenger is at rest and the mirror is at rest relative to them. Therefore, light travels from their face to the mirror and reflects back at the standard speed $c$ just as it would if they were standing still on the ground.
*   **Addressing the "Speed of Light" constraint:** Note that in physics, a massive object (like a train) cannot actually reach the speed of light $c$. However, the thought experiment is designed to test the application of relativity. If the passenger is in an inertial frame, the laws of electromagnetism (Maxwell's equations) must hold, ensuring that light behaves normally relative to the passenger.
