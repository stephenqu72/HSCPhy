### Analysis of the Evidence

**1. Addressing Classical Prediction 1: "Light of any frequency will cause emission"**
*   **Evidence from Graph A:** Graph A shows that the maximum kinetic energy of electrons is zero until a specific threshold frequency ($f_0$) is reached. Below this frequency, no electrons are emitted regardless of the duration of exposure. This contradicts the classical prediction that if you wait long enough or use high enough intensity, any light frequency should eventually cause emission.
*   **Evidence from Graph C:** Graph C shows that the photoelectric current (which indicates that emission is occurring) is zero below the threshold frequency and remains constant above it regardless of frequency changes. This further proves that emission is dependent on the *frequency* of the incident light, not just its presence.

**2. Addressing Classical Prediction 2: "The more intense the light, the more kinetic energy the electrons will have"**
*   **Evidence from Graph B:** Graph B shows that as light intensity increases, the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted electrons remains constant. This directly refutes the classical prediction, which suggested that higher intensity waves would deliver more energy to the electrons.
*   **Evidence from Graph D:** Graph D shows that the current is proportional to light intensity. This indicates that while increasing intensity increases the *number* of electrons emitted per second (the rate of emission), it does not change the individual kinetic energy of those electrons.

### Summary Explanation
Classical wave theory failed because it viewed light energy as continuous and dependent on amplitude (intensity). The experimental data proves that:
*   Photoelectric emission is determined by the **photon energy** ($E = hf$), which is dependent solely on frequency.
*   The threshold frequency ($f_0$) exists because a minimum amount of work (the work function, $\Phi$) must be done to release an electron; if $hf < \Phi$, no emission occurs.
*   Increasing intensity increases the number of photons hitting the metal, thereby increasing the number of electrons ejected (current), but does not change the energy of individual photons.
