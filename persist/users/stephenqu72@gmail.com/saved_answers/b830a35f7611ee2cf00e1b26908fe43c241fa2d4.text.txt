### Answer

**(a)** To identify where a White Dwarf began its evolution, place an 'X' on the upper-left (hot, high-luminosity) portion of the Main Sequence band.

**(b)** The evolution of a star is determined by its initial mass. 
*   **White Dwarf precursors:** Low-to-medium mass stars (like our Sun) spend most of their lives on the Main Sequence fusing hydrogen into helium. Once hydrogen is exhausted, they expand into Red Giants, fusing helium into carbon and oxygen, before shedding their outer layers to form a planetary nebula, leaving behind a dense carbon-oxygen core known as a White Dwarf.
*   **Supergiants:** High-mass stars evolve much faster. After the Main Sequence, they expand into Supergiants. Because of their immense gravitational pressure, they are capable of further nucleosynthesis beyond helium, fusing increasingly heavier elements (carbon, neon, oxygen, silicon) until they reach iron. Unlike the progenitor of a White Dwarf, a Supergiant will eventually undergo a core-collapse supernova, potentially leaving behind a neutron star or black hole rather than a White Dwarf.
