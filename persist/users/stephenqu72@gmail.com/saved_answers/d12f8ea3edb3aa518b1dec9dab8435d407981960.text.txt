### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the historical context of the debate between Isaac Newton’s **corpuscular (particle) theory of light** and the **wave theory of light** (proposed by Christiaan Huygens and others).

1.  **Newton’s Argument:** Newton used the fact that we cannot see around corners (i.e., light does not appear to bend around obstacles significantly in everyday experience) to argue that light must be composed of particles moving in straight lines. In contrast, sound waves have long wavelengths and undergo noticeable **diffraction** (bending around obstacles).
2.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **A is correct:** Newton believed that light consisted of "corpuscles" (particles). Because particles travel in straight lines and do not exhibit obvious diffraction like waves, he used this to argue against the wave model. The statement correctly identifies that Newton's model denied the wave property of diffraction.
    *   **B is incorrect:** While Newton's corpuscular model could explain reflection, it struggled significantly to explain diffraction.
    *   **C is incorrect:** While this statement is scientifically true (light is a wave and *does* produce interference and diffraction), this supports the **wave theory**, not Newton’s corpuscular theory.
    *   **D is incorrect:** Interference patterns are a hallmark of wave behavior and directly contradicted Newton’s corpuscular theory; therefore, they do not support his model.

In the context of the HSC physics curriculum, recognizing that Newton’s model was limited because it could not account for wave phenomena like diffraction is a key learning outcome.
