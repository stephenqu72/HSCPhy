To determine which spectrum is most likely emitted by a star, we must understand the physics of stellar atmospheres.

### Explanation

1.  **Stellar Spectra Basics**: A star consists of a hot, dense core (which emits a continuous blackbody spectrum) surrounded by a cooler, thinner outer atmosphere (the photosphere/chromosphere). As the light from the core passes through the cooler gases of the star's atmosphere, specific elements absorb photons at characteristic wavelengths. This creates an **absorption spectrum**—a continuous rainbow of colors interrupted by dark lines.
2.  **Analyzing the Options**:
    *   **(A)** represents an **absorption spectrum**. It shows a continuous background (the rainbow) with distinct black lines indicating where specific wavelengths of light have been absorbed by the gases in the star's atmosphere. This perfectly matches the physical model of a star.
    *   **(B)** and **(C)** represent **emission spectra**. These consist of bright lines on a dark background, which occur in hot, low-density gases (like a nebula or a discharge lamp), not typical stellar light.
    *   **(D)** represents a pure **continuous spectrum** with no absorption lines. While a star emits a continuous spectrum, we almost always observe absorption lines due to the star's atmosphere, making (A) a much more realistic representation of a stellar spectrum.

### Conclusion
The correct answer is **(A)**. This is the characteristic signature of a star, where a continuous spectrum is modified by the absorption of light by elements in the star's atmosphere.
