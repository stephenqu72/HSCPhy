To solve this problem, we apply the principles of quantum physics, specifically the relationship between energy, wavelength, and de Broglie matter waves.

### Part a: Photon Energy Calculation
To calculate the energy of a photon given its wavelength ($\lambda$), we use the formula:
$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$
Where:
- $h$ (Planck's constant) $\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$
- $c$ (speed of light) $\approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
- $\lambda = 8.7 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m}$

$$E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{8.7 \times 10^{-10}} \approx 2.2848 \times 10^{-16} \text{ J}$$
Rounding to two significant figures, we get **$2.3 \times 10^{-16} \text{ J}$**.

### Part b: Accelerating Voltage for Electrons
To produce the same diffraction pattern, the electrons must have the same de Broglie wavelength as the original X-rays ($\lambda = 8.7 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m}$). 

**Step 1: Calculate the required momentum ($p$) of the electrons**
Using the de Broglie relation $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$:
$$p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{8.7 \times 10^{-10}} \approx 7.616 \times 10^{-25} \text{ kg m/s}$$

**Step 2: Relate momentum to kinetic energy ($K$)**
The kinetic energy is related to momentum by $K = \frac{p^2}{2m}$, where $m$ is the mass of an electron ($9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$):
$$K = \frac{(7.616 \times 10^{-25})^2}{2 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31}} \approx 3.183 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

**Step 3: Relate kinetic energy to accelerating voltage ($V$)**
The energy gained by an electron is $K = qV$, where $q$ is the elementary charge ($1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$):
$$V = \frac{K}{q} = \frac{3.183 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}} \approx 1.987 \text{ V}$$

Rounding to two significant figures (consistent with the data provided), the voltage is approximately **$2.0 \text{ V}$**.
