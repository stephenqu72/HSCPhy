### Answer
Kepler’s third law of planetary motion, also known as the Law of Periods, states that the square of a planet's orbital period ($T$) is directly proportional to the cube of its average distance (semi-major axis, $r$) from the Sun. 

Mathematically, this is expressed as:
$$\frac{T^2}{r^3} = k$$
Where $k$ is a constant that is the same for all planets orbiting the same central body (the Sun). This means that as a planet's distance from the Sun increases, its orbital period increases at a specific rate; specifically, planets further from the Sun take significantly longer to complete one orbit than those closer to the Sun.

### How to solve this
1.  **State the relationship clearly:** Start by explicitly mentioning the relationship between the two variables: the period ($T$) and the distance ($r$). You must note that it is the *square* of the period and the *cube* of the distance.
2.  **Provide the formula:** In physics, a 3-mark question involving a law almost always requires the equation. Writing $\frac{T^2}{r^3} = \text{constant}$ or $\frac{T_1^2}{r_1^3} = \frac{T_2^2}{r_2^3}$ demonstrates a deeper conceptual understanding.
3.  **Define the variables:** Always define what your symbols mean. State that $T$ is the orbital period and $r$ is the average orbital radius (or semi-major axis).
4.  **Add context:** Briefly explain what the law implies physically. Mention that this constant is the same for all bodies orbiting the same central mass, which shows you understand the broader application of the law.
