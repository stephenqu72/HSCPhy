To determine the age of the star clusters, we must look at the **main sequence turn-off point**. As a star cluster ages, the most massive (and hottest) stars on the main sequence exhaust their hydrogen fuel first and evolve off the main sequence into the giant branch. Therefore, the lower the temperature at which the stars begin to deviate from the main sequence, the older the cluster.

### Step-by-Step Analysis:

1.  **Cluster $Y$:** The main sequence extends all the way to the highest temperatures shown ($> 20,000\text{ K}$). This indicates that even the most massive, short-lived stars are still on the main sequence. Therefore, **Cluster $Y$ is the youngest.**
2.  **Cluster $Z$:** The main sequence turn-off point is at approximately $10,000\text{ K}$. Some of the hotter stars have already moved off the main sequence toward the giant branch. This cluster is older than $Y$ but still relatively young.
3.  **Cluster $X$:** The main sequence turn-off point is at a much lower temperature, around $5,000\text{ K}$ to $6,000\text{ K}$. Many of the stars that were once on the main sequence have already evolved into giants. Since the turn-off point has progressed furthest down the main sequence, **Cluster $X$ is the oldest.**

**Conclusion:**
Ordering them from youngest to oldest, we get: **$Y$, $Z$, $X$**.
