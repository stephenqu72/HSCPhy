To solve this problem, we must analyze the forces acting on the cyclist as they travel along the circular path of the ramp.

### Analysis
The cyclist is moving in a circular path. For any object moving in a circle at a constant speed, there must be a **net centripetal force** directed toward the center of the circle. This net force is responsible for changing the direction of the velocity vector.

1.  **Evaluate (A):** The normal force ($F_N$) acts perpendicular to the ramp's surface. As the cyclist moves up the curved ramp, the angle of the ramp changes continuously. Because the normal force must support the component of gravity perpendicular to the surface while also contributing to the centripetal acceleration ($F_N - mg\cos(\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{r}$), it will change as $\theta$ changes. Thus, it is not constant.
2.  **Evaluate (B):** The normal force is not equal to the centripetal force. The centripetal force is the *resultant* force pointing to the center of the circle. The normal force is just one of the forces contributing to this resultant (along with the component of gravity).
3.  **Evaluate (C):** The normal force is rarely equal to the weight ($mg$) on a slope. It is generally $mg\cos(\theta)$ on a static slope, and since the bike is undergoing circular motion, the normal force must also account for the centripetal acceleration.
4.  **Evaluate (D):** By definition, an object moving in a circular path must experience a net force directed toward the center of that circle to maintain its circular trajectory. This is the definition of centripetal force. Since the cyclist is moving along a circular ramp at a constant speed (meaning there is no tangential acceleration), the net force acting on the cyclist is solely the centripetal force, which points toward the center of the circle.

### Conclusion
The correct statement is **(D)**. The net force on an object undergoing uniform circular motion must be the centripetal force, which is directed toward the center of the circular path.
