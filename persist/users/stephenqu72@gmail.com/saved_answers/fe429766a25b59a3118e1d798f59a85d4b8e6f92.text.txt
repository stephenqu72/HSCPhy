### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the historical physics context of the cathode ray tube (CRT) experiments conducted by scientists like J.J. Thomson.

1.  **The Paddle Wheel Experiment:** In this specific variation of the cathode ray tube experiment, a small, lightweight paddle wheel is placed in the path of the cathode rays. When the tube is energized, the cathode rays strike the paddles, causing the wheel to rotate and move along the tracks.
2.  **Interpreting the Motion:** The rotation of the wheel demonstrates that the cathode rays exert a mechanical force on the object. In classical physics, a force is required to change the motion of an object. The ability of the "rays" to move a solid object indicates that they possess **momentum** ($p = mv$).
3.  **Particle Nature:** Momentum is a property associated with physical particles having mass. Since the rays were able to transfer this momentum to the paddle wheel, it provides direct evidence that cathode rays are streams of particles (electrons) rather than simple electromagnetic waves.
4.  **Evaluating Options:**
    *   (A) is incorrect; the experiment does not involve testing gravitational interaction.
    *   (B) is **correct** because it accurately describes the physical evidence: the transfer of momentum by particles causes the mechanical rotation.
    *   (C) is incorrect; while cathode rays do interact with electric and magnetic fields (showing they are charged), that is a different experiment (deflection plates).
    *   (D) is incorrect; while energy transfer occurs, the movement is evidence of momentum transfer (a particle property), not just wave-based energy transfer.
