To solve this problem, we need to relate the work done by the electric field on the electron to the change in the electron's kinetic energy.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

**1. Identify given values:**
*   Initial velocity, $u = 0 \text{ ms}^{-1}$
*   Final velocity, $v = 6 \times 10^6 \text{ ms}^{-1}$
*   Distance, $d = 5 \text{ cm} = 0.05 \text{ m}$
*   Mass of an electron, $m \approx 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
*   Charge of an electron, $q \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$

**2. Relate work done to kinetic energy:**
The work done by the electric field ($W = Fd = qEd$) equals the change in kinetic energy ($\Delta EK = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$):
$$qEd = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

**3. Rearrange to solve for the electric field ($E$):**
$$E = \frac{mv^2}{2qd}$$

**4. Substitute the values:**
$$E = \frac{(9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}) \times (6 \times 10^6 \text{ ms}^{-1})^2}{2 \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (0.05 \text{ m})}$$

*   Calculate the numerator: $9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 36 \times 10^{12} \approx 3.28 \times 10^{-17}$
*   Calculate the denominator: $2 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 0.05 = 0.16 \times 10^{-19} = 1.6 \times 10^{-20}$
*   Divide: $E \approx \frac{3.28 \times 10^{-17}}{1.6 \times 10^{-20}} \approx 2.05 \times 10^3 \text{ NC}^{-1}$

Rounding this to one significant figure (as per the options), we get $2 \times 10^3 \text{ NC}^{-1}$.

**Conclusion:**
The correct option is **C**.
