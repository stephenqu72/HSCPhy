This question asks you to connect two major milestones in the history of quantum physics: Max Planck’s resolution of the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe" and Niels Bohr’s development of the quantum model of the atom.

### Answer

**1. Planck’s Solution to the Black-Body Radiation Problem:**
Classical physics, based on Maxwell’s electromagnetism, predicted that an ideal black body would emit infinite energy at short wavelengths (the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe"). To resolve this, Max Planck proposed that electromagnetic energy is not emitted continuously but in discrete "packets" or "quanta." He introduced the relationship $E = nhf$, where $E$ is the energy, $h$ is Planck's constant, $n$ is an integer, and $f$ is the frequency. By quantizing the energy states of the oscillators in the black-body walls, he successfully derived a formula that matched the experimental curves shown in the image.

**2. Influence on Bohr’s Model (13 years later):**
In 1913, Niels Bohr applied Planck’s concept of energy quantization to the structure of the atom. Bohr postulated that electrons in a hydrogen atom exist in discrete, stationary energy levels or "orbits" and that they cannot exist in between these levels. He used Planck’s constant $h$ to describe the energy transitions: when an electron moves from a higher energy level ($E_2$) to a lower one ($E_1$), it emits a photon with energy $hf = E_2 - E_1$. This successfully explained the stability of the atom and the observed discrete line spectra of hydrogen.
