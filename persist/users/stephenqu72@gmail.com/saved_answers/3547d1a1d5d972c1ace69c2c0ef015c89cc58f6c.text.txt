To determine the mass of Jupiter, we use Kepler's Third Law in the form derived from Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:

$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

Rearranging for the mass of the central body ($M$):
$$M = \frac{4\pi^2}{G} \times \text{gradient of the graph}$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Calculate the gradient ($m$) of the graph:**
Pick two points from the line of best fit. Looking at the graph:
*   Point 1: $(x_1, y_1) = (2 \times 10^9, 0.5 \times 10^{24})$
*   Point 2: $(x_2, y_2) = (10 \times 10^9, 3.0 \times 10^{24})$

$$m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{(3.0 - 0.5) \times 10^{24}}{(10 - 2) \times 10^9} = \frac{2.5 \times 10^{24}}{8 \times 10^9} = 3.125 \times 10^{14} \, \text{m}^3\text{s}^{-2}$$

**2. Substitute into the formula:**
Use $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$:
$$M = \frac{4\pi^2}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}} \times (3.125 \times 10^{14})$$
$$M \approx (5.92 \times 10^{11}) \times (3.125 \times 10^{14})$$
$$M \approx 1.85 \times 10^{27} \, \text{kg}$$

*(Note: Depending on the exact line of best fit chosen, an answer in the range of $1.8 \times 10^{27} \, \text{kg}$ to $1.9 \times 10^{27} \, \text{kg}$ is acceptable for this HSC-style question.)*

### Explanation:
1.  **Identify the physics:** Kepler’s Third Law relates the cube of the orbital radius ($r^3$) to the square of the orbital period ($T^2$). The graph plots these directly.
2.  **Relate to constants:** The slope (gradient) of the $r^3$ vs $T^2$ graph is equivalent to $\frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$.
3.  **Calculation:** We find the slope from the graph, then rearrange the Kepler's Third Law equation to solve for the mass of the primary body ($M$), ensuring all units are in SI units (meters and seconds) before final calculation.
