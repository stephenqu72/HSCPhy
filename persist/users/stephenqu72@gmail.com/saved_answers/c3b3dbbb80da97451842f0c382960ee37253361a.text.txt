### Answer
Initially, as the DC motor is connected to a power supply, the current is high because the armature is stationary (no back EMF is produced). As the motor begins to rotate, it generates a back electromotive force (back EMF), denoted as $\varepsilon$. According to Lenz’s Law, this back EMF opposes the supply voltage $V$. 

The armature current $I$ is determined by the equation:
$$I = \frac{V - \varepsilon}{R}$$
where $R$ is the resistance of the armature. As the speed of the motor increases, the back EMF increases. This causes the numerator $(V - \varepsilon)$ to decrease, leading to a corresponding decrease in the armature current until it reaches a low, steady value when the motor reaches its terminal speed.
