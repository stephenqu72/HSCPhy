To solve this problem, we apply the principles of Special Relativity, specifically **Time Dilation**.

### (a) Find the speed of the particle
The proper time ($\Delta t_0$) is $6.5\ \mu s$. The dilated time ($\Delta t$) is the proper time plus $0.40\ \mu s$:
$$\Delta t = 6.5 + 0.40 = 6.9\ \mu s$$
Using the time dilation formula $\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$, we solve for the Lorentz factor $\gamma$:
$$\gamma = \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta t_0} = \frac{6.9}{6.5} \approx 1.0615$$
Since $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$, then $1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2} = \frac{1}{\gamma^2}$:
$$\frac{v^2}{c^2} = 1 - \frac{1}{(1.0615)^2} = 1 - 0.8876 \approx 0.1124$$
$$v = \sqrt{0.1124}c \approx 0.335c$$
The particle is travelling at **$0.335c$**.

### (b) Lifetime if the laboratory is moved
If the scientist moves with the Zugon (the laboratory is propelled), they are in the same inertial reference frame as the particle. Therefore, the scientist measures the **proper time**.
The lifetime recorded would be **$6.5\ \mu s$**.

### (c) Find the momentum of the Zugon
Momentum in special relativity is given by $p = \gamma mv$. We are given $m = 190\ \text{MeV}/c^2$ and we found $\gamma \approx 1.0615$ and $v \approx 0.335c$.
$$p = \gamma \cdot m \cdot v$$
$$p = 1.0615 \cdot \left(190\ \text{MeV}/c^2\right) \cdot (0.335c)$$
$$p \approx 67.55\ \text{MeV}/c$$

---

### Teacher's Explanation
1.  **Time Dilation:** Always identify $\Delta t_0$ as the time measured in the frame where the particle is stationary (proper time). The "dilated" time is always longer.
2.  **Frames of Reference:** In part (b), remember that relativity is about *relative* motion. If you move *with* the clock, you are measuring the proper time, regardless of how fast your lab is going relative to the Earth.
3.  **Units:** For part (c), keeping the mass in $\text{MeV}/c^2$ makes the calculation simple, as the units for momentum will naturally result in $\text{MeV}/c$.
