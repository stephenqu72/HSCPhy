To solve this problem, we use **Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis ($r$) of its orbit. This is expressed as:

$$\frac{T_1^2}{r_1^3} = \frac{T_2^2}{r_2^3}$$

### Step-by-step Solution:

1.  **Identify the given information:**
    *   Let the Earth's orbital period be $T_E = 1$ Earth year.
    *   Let the Earth's orbital radius be $r_E = 1$ AU.
    *   The ratio of the radius of Mars' orbit ($r_M$) to Earth's orbit ($r_E$) is $1.5:1$, so $r_M = 1.5 r_E$.
    *   We need to find the period of Mars ($T_M$).

2.  **Set up the equation:**
    Using the ratio version of Kepler's Third Law:
    $$\left(\frac{T_M}{T_E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{r_M}{r_E}\right)^3$$

3.  **Substitute the values:**
    $$\left(\frac{T_M}{1}\right)^2 = (1.5)^3$$
    $$T_M^2 = 1.5 \times 1.5 \times 1.5$$
    $$T_M^2 = 3.375$$

4.  **Solve for $T_M$:**
    $$T_M = \sqrt{3.375}$$
    $$T_M \approx 1.837 \text{ Earth years}$$
