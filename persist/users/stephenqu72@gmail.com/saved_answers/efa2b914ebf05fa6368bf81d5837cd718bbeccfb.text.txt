### Part (a) Identifying the Polarised Items
**Answer:**
1. **Identify the sunglasses:** Take one pair of sunglasses and rotate it while looking through it at the reflection of light off a non-metallic surface (or by rotating one pair of glasses in front of the other). If the intensity of light changes as you rotate the glasses, they are polarised. If the intensity remains constant regardless of the orientation, they are non-polarised.
2. **Identify the light source:** Once the polarised sunglasses are identified, hold them in front of each light source and rotate the glasses. If the light intensity varies as you rotate the glasses, that light source is polarised. If the light intensity remains constant, the light source is unpolarised.

**Teacher's Tip:** For the 3 marks, you must clearly show the two-step logic. The key principle is that polarisers act as a filter, and rotating them against a polarised source will lead to a change in intensity according to Malus's Law ($I = I_0 \cos^2 \theta$).

### Part (b) Particle Model vs. Wave Model
**Answer:**
*   **The Wave Model:** Light is an electromagnetic transverse wave, consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Because these oscillations can occur in any plane perpendicular to the direction of travel, unpolarised light has oscillations in all directions. A polariser restricts these oscillations to a single plane. This model **successfully explains polarisation**, as it accounts for the transverse nature of waves.
*   **The Particle Model:** In the classical particle model (Newton’s corpuscular theory), light consists of streams of particles (corpuscles). Because particles are independent entities, there is no inherent "orientation" or "plane" associated with their motion that could be restricted by a filter in a way that matches experimental evidence of polarisation. Therefore, the **particle model fails to explain polarisation**, providing critical historical evidence that supported the wave model of light.

**Teacher's Tip:** To get all 5 marks, ensure you use the word "transverse" when discussing the wave model and clearly state that the failure of the particle model was a turning point in the scientific consensus that light must be a wave.
