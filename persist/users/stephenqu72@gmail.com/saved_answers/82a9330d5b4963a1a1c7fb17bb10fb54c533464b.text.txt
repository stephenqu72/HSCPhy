### Answer
The correct answer is **A. polarisation**.

### Explanation
To determine which phenomenon best demonstrates that light waves are transverse, we must understand the fundamental difference between transverse and longitudinal waves:

1.  **Transverse Waves:** These waves oscillate perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation. Because they have a specific orientation (e.g., vertical or horizontal oscillations), they can be filtered.
2.  **Longitudinal Waves:** These waves (such as sound) oscillate parallel to the direction of propagation. Because they oscillate along the line of travel, there is no "orientation" to filter out; therefore, longitudinal waves cannot be polarised.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **Interference (B):** This occurs when two waves superpose to form a resultant wave of greater, lower, or the same amplitude. This property is exhibited by both transverse waves (light) and longitudinal waves (sound). Therefore, it does not distinguish between the two.
*   **Dispersion (C):** This is the separation of light into its constituent colours due to the frequency-dependent refractive index of a material. It is a property of wave motion in general and does not confirm the transverse nature of the wave.
*   **Diffraction (D):** This is the bending of waves around obstacles or through apertures. Like interference, diffraction is a characteristic property of all wave motion, including longitudinal sound waves.

**Conclusion:**
Polarisation is the unique signature of transverse waves. Since light can be polarised (e.g., by using a polarizing filter), it proves that light must have an oscillation component that is perpendicular to its direction of travel.
