To solve these problems, we use Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion and the formula for gravitational acceleration.

---

### Part (a): Calculating the Orbital Period

**Concept:** Kepler's Third Law states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius ($r$), specifically:
$$\frac{T^2}{r^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$$
When comparing two systems, it is easiest to use ratios where the period is in years ($T$), the radius is in astronomical units ($AU$), and the mass is in solar masses ($M_{\odot}$):
$$\frac{T_1^2}{T_2^2} = \frac{r_1^3}{r_2^3} \times \frac{M_2}{M_1}$$

**Calculation:**
Given: 
- Earth: $T_E = 1 \text{ year}$, $r_E = 1 \text{ AU}$, $M_{Sun} = 1 M_{\odot}$
- Kepler 186f: $T_K = ?$, $r_K = 0.41 \text{ AU}$, $M_{star} = 0.54 M_{\odot}$

$$T_K^2 = \frac{r_K^3}{M_{star}} \times T_E^2$$
$$T_K^2 = \frac{0.41^3}{0.54} \times 1^2$$
$$T_K^2 = \frac{0.068921}{0.54} \approx 0.12763$$
$$T_K = \sqrt{0.12763} \approx 0.35725 \text{ years}$$

Convert to days: $0.35725 \times 365 \approx 130.4$ days.
**Answer:** The orbital period is **130 days**.

### Part (b): Calculating Surface Gravity

**Concept:** The surface gravity ($g$) is given by $g = \frac{GM}{R^2}$. We compare this to Earth's gravity ($g_E$) using ratios:
$$\frac{g_K}{g_E} = \frac{M_K}{M_E} \times \left(\frac{R_E}{R_K}\right)^2$$

**Calculation:**
Given: $M_K = 1.4 M_E$ and $R_K = 1.2 R_E$.
$$\frac{g_K}{g_E} = 1.4 \times \left(\frac{1}{1.2}\right)^2$$
$$\frac{g_K}{g_E} = 1.4 \times \frac{1}{1.44} \approx 0.972$$

**Answer:** The gravity is approximately $0.97g$ (or $9.5 \text{ m/s}^2$). Since this is very close to Earth's gravity, it would be **suitable for human life**.

---

### How to solve it:
1. **For (a):** Recognize that the mass of the host star is different from the Sun. Use the form of Kepler's Third Law that accounts for central mass ($T^2 \propto r^3/M$). Plugging in the values in units of AU and solar masses simplifies the algebra significantly.
2. **For (b):** Use the gravitational field strength formula. By expressing the planet's mass and radius as multiples of Earth's values, the constants $G$ and Earth's mass/radius cancel out, allowing you to find the ratio directly.
