To determine the composition of the star, we use the principle of **spectroscopic analysis**. Each element has a unique "fingerprint" of absorption lines (the black vertical bars on the spectrum). To identify the composition of the star, we must check if the lines in the "Star's Spectrum" align perfectly with the lines of the reference elements provided.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Analyze the "Star's Spectrum":** Observe the positions of the black absorption lines in the bottom spectrum. There are lines in the violet, blue, green, yellow, and orange/red regions.
2.  **Compare with Helium:** Look at the "Helium" reference spectrum. You will notice that every black line in the Helium spectrum aligns perfectly with a corresponding line in the "Star's Spectrum."
3.  **Compare with Magnesium:** Look at the "Magnesium" reference spectrum. There is a single black line located in the green region. By looking down to the "Star's Spectrum," you can see that it also contains a line in that exact position.
4.  **Evaluate the options:**
    *   Hydrogen has lines in the far violet that are not present or do not align with all the star's lines.
    *   Sodium has a double line in the yellow/orange region that does not appear in the star's spectrum.
    *   Since Helium and Magnesium lines account for the positions of the lines observed in the star, we conclude the star contains Helium and Magnesium.

**Correct Option: d) Helium and Magnesium**

### How to solve it:
When presented with spectral analysis questions:
*   **Vertical Alignment:** Imagine a vertical line drawn down from each absorption mark on the reference element. If that line passes through a corresponding mark on the unknown spectrum, that element is likely present.
*   **Exclusion:** If an element has a signature line that is clearly *missing* from the star's spectrum, you can eliminate that element immediately (e.g., Sodium's signature double lines are missing from the star, so any option containing Sodium is incorrect).
