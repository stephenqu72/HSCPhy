To solve these problems, we use the principles of Special Relativity, specifically time dilation and length contraction.

### Part a: Calculate the ratio $\frac{v}{c}$

The astronaut is in the same reference frame as the radioisotope, so the astronaut measures the **proper time** ($t_0 = 4.0\text{ s}$). The scientist on Earth observes the spacecraft moving, so they measure the **dilated time** ($t = 5.0\text{ s}$).

The time dilation formula is:
$$t = \gamma t_0 = \frac{t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

Substitute the values:
$$5.0 = \frac{4.0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$
$$\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} = \frac{4.0}{5.0} = 0.8$$
$$1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2} = 0.64$$
$$\frac{v^2}{c^2} = 0.36$$
$$\frac{v}{c} = 0.6$$

**Answer: 0.6**

### Part b: Calculate the distance measured by the astronaut

The distance $L_0 = 3.80 \times 10^8\text{ m}$ is the **proper length** because it is measured by the observer (the scientist on Earth) relative to whom the Moon and Earth are at rest. The astronaut is moving relative to these objects, so the astronaut measures a **contracted length** ($L$).

The length contraction formula is:
$$L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$

Using $\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} = 0.6$ from part (a):
$$L = (3.80 \times 10^8) \times 0.6 = 2.28 \times 10^8\text{ m}$$

**Answer: $2.28 \times 10^8\text{ m}$**

### Part c: Find the time measured by the astronaut

The astronaut observes the Earth and Moon moving towards them at a speed $v$. The distance they measure between these objects is $L = 2.28 \times 10^8\text{ m}$.

First, find $v$:
$$v = 0.6c = 0.6 \times (3.0 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}) = 1.8 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$$

The time measured by the astronaut ($t_{astro}$) is:
$$t_{astro} = \frac{L}{v} = \frac{2.28 \times 10^8}{1.8 \times 10^8} \approx 1.2666...\text{ s}$$

Rounding to two significant figures (consistent with the input data):
**Answer: $1.3\text{ s}$**
