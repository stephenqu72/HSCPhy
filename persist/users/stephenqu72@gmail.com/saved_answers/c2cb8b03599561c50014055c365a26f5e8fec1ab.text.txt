### Answer
The correct option is **(A) Two inertial systems.**

### Explanation
Special Relativity describes the physical laws that govern the behavior of objects moving at constant velocities relative to one another. To observe or calculate effects like time dilation, length contraction, or the relativity of simultaneity, we must compare the measurements made in one frame of reference to those made in another.

1.  **Inertial Frames:** An inertial frame is a reference frame in which an object that is not subject to any net external force moves at a constant velocity (or remains at rest). Special Relativity is restricted to these frames.
2.  **Why "Two"?** Relativity is inherently about comparison. We define one frame (often called the "stationary" frame, $S$) and another frame (the "moving" frame, $S'$) that is moving at a constant velocity $v$ relative to the first. Without comparing at least two inertial systems, you cannot establish the relative motion required to demonstrate the relativistic effects mentioned above.
3.  **Excluding Non-Inertial Systems:** Systems that are accelerating or rotating are considered "non-inertial." These fall under the domain of **General Relativity**, not Special Relativity. Therefore, options (C) and (D) are incorrect.

By comparing measurements (such as time intervals $\Delta t$ and $\Delta t'$) between two inertial observers, we arrive at the Lorentz transformations, which are the cornerstone of the theory.
