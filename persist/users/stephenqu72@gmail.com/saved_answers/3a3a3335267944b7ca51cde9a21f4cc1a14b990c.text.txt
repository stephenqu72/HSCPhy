### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) Deep Inelastic Scattering**.

### Explanation
To determine whether a particle is "fundamental" (meaning it cannot be broken down into smaller components), scientists use high-energy particle accelerators to perform scattering experiments.

1.  **Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS):** In these experiments, high-energy leptons (such as electrons) are fired at nucleons (protons and neutrons). Because the electrons have a very short de Broglie wavelength (due to their high momentum, $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$), they are able to "probe" the interior of the nucleon. The results showed that these electrons were scattering off point-like charged particles inside the nucleons, which were later identified as quarks. This provided experimental evidence that neutrons and protons are composite particles made of quarks.
2.  **Why other options are incorrect:**
    *   **(A) Neutron diffraction:** This is a technique used to study the structure of materials (like crystals) by observing how neutrons reflect off atoms. It treats the neutron as a wave, not as a composite particle.
    *   **(C) Alpha particles incident on Be foil:** This refers to the experiment performed by James Chadwick in 1932, which led to the *discovery* of the neutron, not the discovery of its internal structure.
    *   **(D) Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) and ATLAS (A Toroidal LHC ApparatuS):** These are large-scale detectors at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). While they investigate subatomic physics and the Higgs boson, the specific foundational evidence that nucleons are not fundamental was established earlier via Deep Inelastic Scattering.
