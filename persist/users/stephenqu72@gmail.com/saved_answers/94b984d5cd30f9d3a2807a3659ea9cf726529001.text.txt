### Answer

**(a) Outline how this process is able to emit large amounts of energy.**
The energy emitted during the fusion of hydrogen into helium is due to the **mass defect**. The total mass of the four hydrogen nuclei is slightly greater than the mass of the resulting helium nucleus. This "missing" mass ($\Delta m$) is converted into energy ($E$) according to Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, defined by the equation:
$$E = \Delta m c^2$$
Because the speed of light ($c$) is a very large constant, even a tiny amount of mass loss results in a massive release of energy.

**(b) Outline the pathways believed to be followed in the nucleosynthesis of helium in main sequence stars.**
Since the probability of more than two particles colliding is negligible, nucleosynthesis occurs via two specific chain reaction pathways that involve two-body collisions:

1.  **The Proton-Proton ($p-p$) Chain:** This is dominant in stars like our Sun (lower mass stars). It involves sequential two-particle interactions where protons fuse to form deuterium, then helium-3, and eventually helium-4.
2.  **The CNO Cycle (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle):** This is dominant in more massive, hotter main sequence stars. Carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen nuclei act as catalysts to fuse four hydrogen nuclei into one helium nucleus. Like the $p-p$ chain, each step in this cycle only involves the collision of two nuclei at a time.

*   **For part (b):** The prompt gives you a crucial clue: "collisions involving more than two particles are very small." The solution is the existence of multi-step "chains" or "cycles" where only two particles interact at every individual step. If you mention the $p-p$ chain and the CNO cycle, you cover the two standard mechanisms expected in the HSC syllabus.
