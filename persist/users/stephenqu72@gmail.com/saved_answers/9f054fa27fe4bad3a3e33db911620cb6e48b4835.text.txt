### Answer
When the switch is closed, current flows through the coil $EFGH$. According to the motor effect, a current-carrying conductor placed within an external magnetic field experiences a force. The magnitude of this force is given by $F = nBIL\sin(\theta)$.

Because the current flows in opposite directions in segments $EF$ and $HG$ (e.g., if $EF$ experiences an upward force, $HG$ experiences a downward force), a **torque** is created about the central axis. This torque causes the coil to rotate. As the coil reaches the vertical position, the split-ring commutator reverses the direction of the current in the coil. This reversal ensures that the forces on each side of the coil always act in the same rotational direction, allowing for continuous rotation in one direction.
