### Explanation
To solve this question, we must identify the process depicted in the diagram and understand its astrophysical context:

1.  **Identify the process:** The diagram shows the **CNO cycle** (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle). In this cycle, carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen nuclei act as catalysts to fuse four hydrogen nuclei (protons) into one helium nucleus ($^4\text{He}$). The presence of neutrinos ($\nu$), gamma rays ($\gamma$), and the specific sequence of reactions involving isotopes like $^{12}\text{C}$, $^{13}\text{N}$, $^{13}\text{C}$, $^{14}\text{N}$, $^{15}\text{O}$, and $^{15}\text{N}$ are the characteristic markers of the CNO cycle.

2.  **Astrophysical context:** In physics and astrophysics, main sequence stars derive their energy from the fusion of hydrogen into helium. There are two primary mechanisms for this:
    *   **The Proton-Proton ($p$-$p$) Chain:** This is the dominant process in stars like our Sun (relatively lower mass).
    *   **The CNO Cycle:** This process becomes the dominant method of hydrogen fusion in stars that are significantly more massive than the Sun. Because these stars have higher core temperatures, the increased kinetic energy allows protons to overcome the higher electrostatic repulsion of the carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen nuclei.

3.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   Since the CNO cycle is a mechanism for hydrogen burning, it occurs while a star is on the **main sequence**.
    *   While high-mass main sequence stars use the CNO cycle, it is not a defining characteristic of white dwarfs (which are remnants) or post-main sequence stars (which are typically burning helium or heavier elements in shells).

Therefore, the process shown is indicative of hydrogen fusion in massive **main sequence stars**.
