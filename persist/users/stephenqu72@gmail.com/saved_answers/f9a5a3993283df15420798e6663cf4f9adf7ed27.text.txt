To solve this problem, we use Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation, which is defined by the formula:

$$F = \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

Where:
- $F$ is the gravitational force.
- $G$ is the gravitational constant.
- $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the masses of the two objects.
- $r$ is the separation distance between the centers of the two objects.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1. **Identify the original force:**
   The initial force is $F = \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2}$.

2. **Apply the changes:**
   - Both masses are halved: The new masses are $m_1' = \frac{1}{2}m_1$ and $m_2' = \frac{1}{2}m_2$.
   - The separation is halved: The new distance is $r' = \frac{1}{2}r$.

3. **Calculate the new force ($F'$):**
   Substitute the new values into the gravitational formula:
   $$F' = \frac{G (\frac{1}{2}m_1)(\frac{1}{2}m_2)}{(\frac{1}{2}r)^2}$$

4. **Simplify the expression:**
   - Numerator: $G \cdot \frac{1}{4} m_1 m_2$
   - Denominator: $(\frac{1}{2}r)^2 = \frac{1}{4} r^2$

   Now combine them:
   $$F' = \frac{\frac{1}{4} G m_1 m_2}{\frac{1}{4} r^2}$$
   
   The $\frac{1}{4}$ in the numerator and the $\frac{1}{4}$ in the denominator cancel each other out:
   $$F' = \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

5. **Conclusion:**
   Since $F' = F$, the new gravitational force is exactly the same as the original force.

**Correct Option: (B) $F$**
