### Teacher's Answer

To ensure a first-hand investigation is both **valid** and **reliable**, the following steps are required:

1.  **Ensuring Validity:** You must design the experiment to ensure it actually tests the hypothesis. This requires identifying and controlling all independent, dependent, and controlled variables. A fair test must be established where only one independent variable is changed while all other variables are kept constant to ensure that the results are due to the independent variable alone.
2.  **Ensuring Reliability:** You must ensure the consistency of the results. This is achieved by performing multiple trials of the experiment and calculating an average. Consistency across repeated trials indicates that random errors have been minimized.

**Can an experiment be reliable and not valid?**
Yes. An experiment can be highly reliable (producing consistent results every time) but not valid if the experimental design is flawed in a way that does not measure what it claims to measure. For example, if you are trying to measure the effect of temperature on reaction rate but your thermometer is calibrated incorrectly, your results will be consistent (reliable) every time you repeat the test, but they will be incorrect regarding the actual variable being tested (invalid).
