### Answer
Different gases produce different spectra because each element has a unique set of discrete energy levels for its electrons. When a high voltage is applied to a discharge tube, electrons collide with gas atoms, exciting them to higher energy levels. As these electrons transition back to lower energy states, they emit photons of light with energy equal to the difference between the levels, given by $\Delta E = E_{initial} - E_{final}$. Since the energy differences are specific to the electronic structure of each gas, each element emits a unique set of wavelengths, resulting in a characteristic line emission spectrum.

### How to Solve
To score full marks on this 3-mark HSC question, you must demonstrate a clear understanding of the quantum mechanical model of the atom as it relates to spectroscopy. Follow this structure:

1.  **Energy Levels (1 mark):** State that atoms have unique, discrete electronic energy levels determined by their atomic structure. Mention that no two elements have the identical set of energy levels.
2.  **Excitation and De-excitation (1 mark):** Explain the process. High voltage in the discharge tube provides the energy for electrons to move from ground states to excited states. Crucially, mention that when electrons return to lower states, they release energy as photons.
3.  **Wavelength/Photon Relationship (1 mark):** Connect the energy of the emitted photon to its wavelength using the relationship $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$ (or simply $\Delta E = hf$). Because the transition energies ($\Delta E$) are unique to the specific element, the resulting emitted wavelengths are also unique, creating a distinct "fingerprint" spectrum.

**Teacher's Tip:** In HSC Physics, always emphasize the word "discrete" when discussing energy levels. Using terms like "characteristic emission spectrum" or "unique electronic configuration" will help you secure those high-band marks.
