### Answer

The current model of light is the **quantum mechanical model**, which proposes that light exhibits **wave-particle duality**. This means light is not exclusively a wave or a particle; rather, it behaves as both depending on the context of the interaction.

**1. Evidence for the Wave Model:**
*   **Interference and Diffraction:** Phenomena like Young's Double Slit experiment demonstrate that light undergoes constructive and destructive interference, forming fringe patterns that can only be explained by overlapping wavefronts. Diffraction (the bending of light around edges) is also a classic wave property.
*   **Polarization:** The ability of light to be polarized—where its oscillations are restricted to a single plane—confirms that light is a transverse wave.
*   **Maxwell’s Equations:** Classical electromagnetism describes light as oscillating electric and magnetic fields propagating through space at speed $c$.

**2. Evidence for the Particle Model:**
*   **Photoelectric Effect:** When light hits a metal surface, it ejects electrons only if the frequency (energy) is above a threshold value, regardless of intensity. This cannot be explained by classical wave theory. Einstein proposed that light consists of discrete packets of energy called **photons**, where $E = hf$.
*   **Compton Scattering:** The scattering of X-rays by electrons shows a change in wavelength, which can only be explained by the conservation of momentum and energy during a particle-like collision between a photon and an electron.

**3. Synthesis (The Modern View):**
Modern physics, through de Broglie's hypothesis ($\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$), reconciles these models. Light is viewed as a quantum field where the wave function describes the probability of finding a photon at a specific point. We use the wave model to explain the *propagation* of light and the particle model to explain the *interaction* of light with matter.
