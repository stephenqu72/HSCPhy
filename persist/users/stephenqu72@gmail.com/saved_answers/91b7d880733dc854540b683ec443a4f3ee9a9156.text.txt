### Explanation
To understand resonance in the context of sound waves, we must look at how standing waves are created within a medium (like a pipe or a string):

1.  **The Physics of Resonance:** Resonance occurs when an object or a system is driven to oscillate at its natural frequency. In acoustics, this typically happens when a traveling sound wave meets its own reflection.
2.  **Superposition:** When the incoming (incident) wave and the reflected wave overlap, they undergo **superposition**. If the conditions are right (i.e., the waves have the same frequency and amplitude but are traveling in opposite directions), they form a **standing wave**. 
3.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **A is incorrect:** While interference occurs, simply saying "interference of two travelling sound waves" is too broad; it does not specifically describe the mechanism of resonance, which requires the formation of a stable, stationary wave pattern.
    *   **B is incorrect:** Resonance does not occur *whenever* a wave enters a pipe. The wave must match the specific "natural" or "resonant" frequencies of that pipe (governed by its length and boundary conditions) to cause resonance.
    *   **C is correct:** This accurately describes the formation of standing waves through the superposition of incident and reflected waves, which is the physical basis for resonance in acoustic systems.
    *   **D is incorrect:** Resonance frequently occurs in open pipes (e.g., flutes or organ pipes) whenever the frequency of the source matches one of the harmonics of the pipe.
