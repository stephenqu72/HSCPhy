### Answer
Hubble's Law states that the recessional velocity of a galaxy is directly proportional to its distance from the observer. This can be expressed by the equation:
$$v = H_0 d$$
where $v$ is the recessional velocity, $d$ is the distance to the galaxy, and $H_0$ is the Hubble constant.

Evidence supporting this law includes the observed **redshift** of light from distant galaxies. By measuring the displacement of spectral lines (such as hydrogen absorption lines) toward the red end of the electromagnetic spectrum, astronomers can calculate the recessional velocity of galaxies using the Doppler effect. Observations show that galaxies further away exhibit greater redshifts, consistently confirming the linear relationship predicted by Hubble's Law.

### How to solve
This is a standard 2-mark recall and explanation question often found in HSC Physics exams (Module 8: The Universe). 

1.  **Break down the question:** The prompt asks for two distinct parts. One mark is allocated for stating the law, and the second is for outlining the evidence.
2.  **Stating the Law:** You must identify that the relationship is linear. It is best practice to provide both the verbal statement and the mathematical formula ($v = H_0 d$). Ensure you define the variables ($v$ for velocity, $d$ for distance).
3.  **Providing Evidence:** Link the concept of "recessional velocity" to its observable manifestation: **redshift**. When light from distant galaxies is analyzed using spectroscopy, the lines are shifted towards the red end of the spectrum. Explain that the degree of this shift (redshift) correlates with distance, which is the primary observational evidence for the expansion of the universe.
