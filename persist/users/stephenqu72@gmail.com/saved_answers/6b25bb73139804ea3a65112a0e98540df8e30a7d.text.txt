### Part (a): Calculation
To calculate the relativistic momentum ($p$) of an electron, we use the formula:
$$p = \gamma mv = \frac{mv}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

**1. Identify constants:**
* Mass of electron ($m$) $\approx 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
* Velocity ($v$) $= 0.4c$
* Speed of light ($c$) $\approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$

**2. Calculate the Lorentz factor ($\gamma$):**
$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (0.4)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.16}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.84}} \approx 1.091$$

**3. Calculate momentum:**
$$p = 1.091 \times (9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}) \times (0.4 \times 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})$$
$$p \approx 1.091 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 1.2 \times 10^8$$
$$p \approx 1.19 \times 10^{-22} \text{ kg m/s}$$

---

### Part (b): Explanation
In classical mechanics, momentum ($p=mv$) is a linear function of velocity, meaning doubling the velocity would exactly double the momentum. However, in special relativity, momentum is defined by $p = \gamma mv$. 

As the velocity $v$ approaches the speed of light $c$, the Lorentz factor $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$ increases exponentially rather than linearly. 

*   At $0.4c$, $\gamma \approx 1.09$.
*   At $0.8c$, $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (0.8)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.36}} \approx 1.67$.

Because the Lorentz factor increases significantly as $v$ increases, the momentum $p$ increases at a rate greater than the increase in velocity. Therefore, when the velocity doubles from $0.4c$ to $0.8c$, the combined effect of the doubling velocity and the increasing $\gamma$ factor results in a total momentum that is more than double the initial value.
