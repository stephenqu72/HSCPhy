To answer this 7-mark HSC Physics question, you need to select two distinct types of orbits (typically Geostationary/Geosynchronous and Low Earth Orbit), compare their periods and altitudes, and justify their applications using the physics of orbital motion.

### Answer
Two common types of satellites are **Geostationary Earth Orbit (GEO)** satellites and **Low Earth Orbit (LEO)** satellites.

**1. Comparison of Properties:**
*   **GEO:** These satellites orbit above the equator with a period of $T = 24 \text{ hours}$ ($86,400 \text{ s}$). Because their period matches Earth’s rotation, they remain fixed relative to a point on the surface.
*   **LEO:** These satellites orbit at much lower altitudes, typically between $200\text{--}2,000 \text{ km}$. They have much shorter orbital periods, often around $90\text{--}120 \text{ minutes}$.

**2. Calculations:**
We use the formula for orbital period derived from $F_g = F_c$:
$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{4\pi^2 mr}{T^2} \implies T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}}$$

*   **For GEO:** To find the radius $r$, we rearrange to $r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}}$.
    Using $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ Nm}^2/\text{kg}^2$, $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$, and $T = 86,400 \text{ s}$:
    $$r \approx 42,200 \text{ km from the Earth's centre.}$$
    Since the Earth's radius is $\approx 6,370 \text{ km}$, the altitude is $\approx 35,800 \text{ km}$.

*   **For LEO:** At an altitude of $500 \text{ km}$, $r = 6,870 \text{ km} = 6.87 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$.
    $$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 (6.87 \times 10^6)^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}} \approx 5,700 \text{ seconds} \approx 95 \text{ minutes.}$$

**3. Application:**
*   **GEO:** Used for **communications and weather monitoring** (e.g., NBN Sky Muster or TV broadcasts) because a satellite dish on the ground can be permanently pointed at a fixed spot in the sky without tracking the satellite.
*   **LEO:** Used for **Earth observation, high-resolution imaging, and global internet constellations (e.g., Starlink)**. The low altitude allows for high-resolution photography and lower signal latency, but because they move across the sky quickly, a network (constellation) of satellites is required for continuous coverage.
