The correct answer is **D. Chemical composition**.

### Explanation

To solve this question, you need to understand the physics of stellar spectra:

1.  **Origin of Absorption Lines:** As light produced in the hot core of a star passes through the cooler gases in the star's outer atmosphere (the photosphere/chromosphere), specific atoms and ions absorb particular wavelengths of light.
2.  **Quantized Energy Levels:** Electrons within these atoms can only exist in discrete energy levels. An electron will absorb a photon only if the photon's energy exactly matches the difference between two energy levels ($E = \Delta E$).
3.  **The "Fingerprint":** Because each element (e.g., Hydrogen, Helium, Calcium) has a unique electronic structure, it has a unique set of energy level differences. Consequently, each element produces a unique pattern of absorption lines—often called a "spectral fingerprint."
4.  **Why other options are incorrect:**
    *   **Size:** The size (radius) of a star influences its luminosity and can affect the *broadening* of spectral lines, but it is not responsible for the specific *position* of the lines.
    *   **Colour:** Colour is a result of the star's surface temperature (Wien's Law), which determines the overall shape of the continuous blackbody spectrum, not the specific absorption lines.
    *   **Distance from Earth:** Distance affects the apparent brightness ($b \propto 1/d^2$) and causes a redshift if the star is moving away, but it does not dictate the presence of the absorption lines themselves.

By identifying the specific wavelengths where light is missing from the continuous spectrum, astronomers can determine exactly which elements are present in the star's atmosphere.
