### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) stellar spectra.**

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the fundamental physics behind Hubble’s Law and the discovery of the expanding universe.

1.  **The Physics:** Edwin Hubble analyzed the light coming from distant galaxies. By examining their **spectra** (the distribution of light across different wavelengths), he observed characteristic absorption lines, such as the Calcium H and K lines.
2.  **The Observation:** He noticed that these absorption lines were shifted toward the red end of the spectrum compared to their laboratory wavelengths. This phenomenon is known as **redshift** ($z$).
3.  **The Conclusion:** Redshift indicates that an object is moving away from the observer. By comparing the redshift of various galaxies to their distances (which he determined using Cepheid variable stars), Hubble discovered a direct proportionality between distance and velocity, now expressed by Hubble's Law:
    $$v = H_0 d$$
    where $v$ is the recessional velocity, $H_0$ is the Hubble constant, and $d$ is the distance to the galaxy.
4.  **Elimination:**
    *   While stellar luminosities (B) and Cepheid variables were essential for calculating the **distances** to these galaxies, the evidence for the **motion** (recession) required the analysis of the light's wavelength shift, which is only visible in the **spectra** of the objects.

Therefore, the observations of the light's spectral features (stellar spectra) were the critical factor in identifying the Doppler-like shift that proved the expansion of the universe.
