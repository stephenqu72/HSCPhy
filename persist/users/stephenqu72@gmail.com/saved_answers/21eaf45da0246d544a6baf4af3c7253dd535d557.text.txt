To excel in this HSC-style physics question, you must focus on the relationship between atomic energy levels and electromagnetic radiation. Here is the breakdown of the answers and the logic behind them.

### (a) Explain how discharge tubes with different gases can produce different spectra. (3 marks)

**Answer:**
1.  **Excitation:** Electrons in the gas atoms are excited to higher energy levels when they collide with high-energy electrons accelerated by the electric field in the discharge tube.
2.  **De-excitation:** When these electrons drop back to lower energy levels, they emit a photon with energy equal to the difference between the levels: $E_{photon} = E_2 - E_1 = hf$.
3.  **Unique Energy Levels:** Each element has a unique set of electron energy levels due to its specific atomic structure. Consequently, the transition energies differ, resulting in a unique set of emitted frequencies (a unique emission spectrum) for each gas.

---

### (b) What is the relevance of spectra produced by discharge tubes to the spectral analysis of stars? (2 marks)

**Answer:**
Discharge tubes produce "fingerprint" emission spectra for known elements. By comparing these known laboratory emission lines with the **absorption spectra** observed from stars (where the cooler outer layers of a star absorb specific wavelengths of light from the hotter core), astronomers can identify the chemical composition of a star’s atmosphere.

---

### (c) Explain how spectra from stars can reveal its rotational velocity. (4 marks)

**Answer:**
1.  **Doppler Effect:** As a star rotates, one side of the star moves toward the observer (blueshift) and the opposite side moves away (redshift).
2.  **Line Broadening:** This simultaneous approach and recession causes the spectral absorption lines to spread out or "widen" compared to a non-rotating star (a phenomenon known as rotational Doppler broadening).
3.  **Measurement:** The extent of this broadening is directly proportional to the star's rotational velocity. By measuring the width of these lines, astronomers can calculate the speed at which the star is rotating.

---

### How to solve these types of questions
*   **Part (a):** Always link the emission of light to the **Bohr Model**. Mention that energy levels are discrete and unique to each element.
*   **Part (b):** Connect the laboratory results (emission) to the astrophysical reality (absorption). The key word is "comparison" or "reference."
*   **Part (c):** Whenever you see "velocity" and "spectra," immediately think of the **Doppler Effect**. Relate the Doppler shift to the geometry of a rotating object.
