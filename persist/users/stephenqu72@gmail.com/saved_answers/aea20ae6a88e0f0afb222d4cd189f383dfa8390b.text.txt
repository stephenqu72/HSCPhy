### Answer
Muons travel at speeds very close to the speed of light ($c$). According to Einstein's theory of special relativity, this causes **time dilation**. From the perspective of an observer on Earth, the muon's internal "clock" runs significantly slower, extending its mean lifetime. Consequently, the muon exists for long enough to travel the distance from the upper atmosphere to the Earth's surface before decaying.

### How to solve it
To secure the 2 marks for this HSC Physics question, you must demonstrate an understanding of special relativity, specifically focusing on the phenomenon of time dilation. Here is the step-by-step breakdown:

1.  **Identify the cause:** State that muons travel at relativistic speeds (close to the speed of light). This is the necessary condition for relativistic effects to occur.
2.  **Explain the consequence:** Explicitly use the term **"time dilation."** Explain that because the muon is moving at such high speeds, time passes more slowly for the muon relative to an observer on Earth (given by the formula $\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$, where $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor).
3.  **Link to the outcome:** Conclude by explaining that because the muon's lifetime is dilated (extended) in the Earth's frame of reference, it survives for a longer duration, allowing it to cover the required distance to reach the ground despite its short proper half-life.

*Teacher's Tip:* You can also answer this using **length contraction**. You could state that from the muon's frame of reference, the distance to the Earth's surface is contracted to a much smaller value, meaning the muon can reach the surface within its own short proper lifetime. Either explanation is correct, but time dilation is the most standard approach for this specific HSC syllabus dot point.
