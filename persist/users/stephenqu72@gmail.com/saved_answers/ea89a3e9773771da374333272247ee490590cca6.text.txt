To determine the factors that influence a planet's orbital period, we look to Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion, which is derived from Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation and the physics of circular motion.

### The Physics Explanation

For a planet of mass $m$ orbiting a star of mass $M$ at a radius $r$, the gravitational force provides the centripetal force required for the orbit:

$$F_g = F_c$$

$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Since the orbital speed $v$ is the circumference of the orbit divided by the period ($v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$), we can substitute this into the equation:

$$\frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{(\frac{2\pi r}{T})^2}{r}$$

$$\frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}$$

Rearranging for the orbital period $T$:

$$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}$$

$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}}$$

### Analysis
Looking at the final equation:
1.  **Mass of the star ($M$):** This is in the denominator, so it affects the period.
2.  **Radius of the orbit ($r$):** The period depends on the radius ($T \propto \sqrt{r^3}$).
3.  **Mass of the planet ($m$):** The mass of the planet cancels out on both sides of the equation, meaning it does **not** affect the orbital period (assuming $m \ll M$).

Therefore, the orbital period is determined solely by the **mass of the star** and the **radius of the orbit**.

**Correct Option: (B)**
