### Answer

**(a)** If the separation between the slits ($d$) is increased, the spacing between the interference fringes ($\Delta y$) will **decrease**.

**(b)** The interference pattern will disappear, and instead, a single-slit diffraction pattern will be observed on the screen (resulting in a single, broader bright central maximum).

---

### Explanation

**(a)** To solve this, you must recall the formula for fringe separation in Young’s double-slit experiment:
$$\Delta y = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$$
Where:
- $\Delta y$ is the fringe separation (distance between adjacent bright fringes).
- $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light used.
- $L$ is the distance from the slits to the screen.
- $d$ is the separation between the two slits.

From this relationship, we can see that fringe separation $\Delta y$ is **inversely proportional** to the slit separation $d$. Therefore, if $d$ increases, the denominator of the fraction increases, causing the overall value of $\Delta y$ to decrease.

**(b)** The formation of an interference pattern requires the superposition of coherent light waves originating from two different sources (the two slits). If one slit is covered, there is no longer a second source to interfere with the light from the first. Consequently, the interference effect is lost, and the light from the remaining open slit undergoes single-slit diffraction, creating a wider central maximum with much lower intensity than the original central fringe.
