**Answer:**
The correct option is **(D) Some alpha particles were deflected at angles greater than $90^\circ$.**

**Explanation:**
To solve this, we must consider the scientific context of the Rutherford gold foil experiment:

1.  **The Context:** At the time, the prevailing model was the "Plum Pudding" model (J.J. Thomson), which proposed that positive charge was spread out thinly throughout the atom. If this model were correct, alpha particles (which are heavy and positively charged) should have passed through the thin gold foil with minimal deflection.
2.  **The Observations:**
    *   **The majority** of alpha particles did pass through unhindered (consistent with the idea that the atom is mostly empty space).
    *   **The key discovery:** A very small fraction of alpha particles experienced significant deflections, including some that were reflected backwards at angles greater than $90^\circ$.
3.  **The Conclusion:** Classical physics could not explain how a heavy, fast-moving alpha particle could be bounced back by a "diffuse" positive charge. This evidence proved that the atom's positive charge (and most of its mass) must be concentrated in an extremely small, dense region—the **nucleus**. 

While option (A) is an observation, it was the unexpected, "startling" observation of large-angle deflections (the "some" particles mentioned in option D) that provided the specific evidence required to reject the Plum Pudding model and propose the nuclear model of the atom.
