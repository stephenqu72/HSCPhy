As an HSC Physics teacher, I will guide you through this classic question on the nature of cathode rays.

### (a) Explain how this shadow is formed. (2 Marks)

**Answer:**
The high voltage causes the emission of cathode rays (electrons) from the cathode. These rays travel in straight lines towards the anode. Because the metal cross acts as an obstacle, it blocks the path of these particles, preventing them from striking the glass behind it. The surrounding glass fluoresces due to the impact of the cathode rays, creating a dark silhouette or "shadow" where the rays were obstructed.

**Teacher's Tip:** To score full marks, you must link the **rectilinear propagation** (straight-line travel) of cathode rays to their physical obstruction by the cross.

### (b) When a magnet is moved towards this gas discharge tube, the shadow of the cross is observed to move across the glass. Explain this observation. (2 Marks)

**Answer:**
The cathode rays are composed of negatively charged particles (electrons). According to the **Lorentz force law** (or motor effect), a charged particle moving through a magnetic field experiences a force defined by $F = qvB\sin\theta$. This force causes the path of the electrons to deflect, shifting the beam’s trajectory and causing the shadow to move on the glass.

**Teacher's Tip:** You must explicitly state that cathode rays consist of **charged particles** and that they experience a **force** in a magnetic field, confirming they are not just electromagnetic radiation.

### (c) State ONE way in which work involving gas discharge tubes contributed to the understanding of atomic structure. (2 Marks)

**Answer:**
The study of gas discharge tubes led to the discovery of the **electron** (by J.J. Thomson). By measuring the deflection of these rays in electric and magnetic fields, scientists were able to determine the charge-to-mass ratio ($e/m$) of the electron. This provided the first evidence that the atom was not indivisible, but composed of subatomic particles, ultimately leading to models like the "plum pudding" model and later confirming the existence of a core of negative charge.

**Teacher's Tip:** Always connect the experiment to a specific outcome (e.g., discovery of the electron or the subatomic nature of matter) rather than just saying "it showed atoms have parts."
