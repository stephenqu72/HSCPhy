To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of transformer physics, specifically the relationship between voltage, current, and power efficiency.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Understand the Ideal Scenario:** For an **ideal** transformer, the power input ($P_{in}$) equals the power output ($P_{out}$), meaning $V_p I_p = V_s I_s$.
    *   Input Power: $P_{in} = 240\text{ V} \times 3.6\text{ A} = 864\text{ W}$.
    *   If the transformer were 100% efficient, the output current $I_s$ would be:
        $$I_s = \frac{P_{in}}{V_s} = \frac{864\text{ W}}{30\text{ V}} = 28.8\text{ A}$$

2.  **Analyze the "Non-Ideal" Factor:** The problem explicitly states that the transformer is **non-ideal**. In the real world, transformers lose energy due to factors like resistance in the coils (Joule heating), eddy currents, and magnetic hysteresis. This means that the output power is always **less** than the input power ($P_{out} < P_{in}$).

3.  **Evaluate the Options:**
    *   We calculated that the ideal output current would be $28.8\text{ A}$.
    *   Because the transformer is non-ideal, some power is lost as heat. Therefore, the actual power delivered to the device will be lower than the input power.
    *   Since $P_{out} = V_s \times I_s$, and $V_s$ is fixed at $30\text{ V}$, the output current $I_s$ must be **lower** than the current in an ideal scenario ($28.8\text{ A}$).
    *   Looking at the options:
        *   a) $29\text{ A}$ (Higher than ideal)
        *   b) $28\text{ A}$ (Lower than ideal - **This fits**)
        *   c) $30\text{ A}$ (Higher than ideal)
        *   d) $31\text{ A}$ (Higher than ideal)

By choosing the value slightly lower than the ideal calculation, we account for the energy dissipation inherent in a non-ideal transformer.

**Answer:** **b) 28A**
