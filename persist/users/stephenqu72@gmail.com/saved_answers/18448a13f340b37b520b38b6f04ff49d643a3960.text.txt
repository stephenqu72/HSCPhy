To solve this question, we must look at the physical implications of the experimental setup historically used by J.J. Thomson and others to investigate the nature of cathode rays.

1.  **Analyze the Physics:** When cathode rays strike the vanes of the paddlewheel, the wheel rotates. In physics, for an object to be set into motion from rest (or to have work done on it to overcome friction), a force must be applied. According to Newton's laws and the principle of conservation of momentum, this force is provided by the transfer of momentum from the cathode rays to the paddlewheel vanes.
2.  **Evaluate the Options:**

Because the cathode rays are capable of exerting a mechanical force (doing work) to rotate the wheel, they must possess momentum. This observation was crucial in proving that cathode rays are composed of material particles (electrons) rather than being purely electromagnetic waves (like light).
    *   **Option C:** This is incorrect because carrying energy is a property of both waves *and* particles. Furthermore, the rotation specifically demonstrates *momentum transfer*, which is the defining characteristic of a particle model in this context.

By identifying that mechanical rotation requires the transfer of momentum, we can conclude that cathode rays behave like a stream of particles with mass.
