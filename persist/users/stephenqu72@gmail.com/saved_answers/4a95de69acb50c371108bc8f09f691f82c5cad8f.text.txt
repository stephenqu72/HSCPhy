### Suggested Response

The scientific method functions as a feedback loop where theoretical models generate predictions, and experimental observations test these predictions. If experiments contradict the model, the theory must be modified or replaced, driving the evolution of scientific understanding.

**The Wave Model of Light:**
In the 19th century, Maxwell’s theoretical electromagnetic equations predicted light as a self-propagating wave. This was strongly supported by Thomas Young’s Double Slit experiment, which demonstrated interference and diffraction patterns—phenomena exclusive to waves. Consequently, the wave model became the consensus, as it provided a robust framework that matched experimental observations.

**The Failure of Classical Theory:**
Scientific progress often occurs when anomalies arise. At the turn of the 20th century, the wave model failed to explain the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe" in blackbody radiation. Classical physics predicted an infinite energy output at high frequencies, which contradicted experimental data. Similarly, the Photoelectric Effect demonstrated that the maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons depended on the frequency of light, not the intensity, which the wave model could not explain.

**The Quantum Revolution:**
To reconcile these discrepancies, Max Planck proposed that energy is quantized, $E = hf$. Albert Einstein extended this to suggest that light itself consists of discrete packets of energy, or photons. This new theoretical model allowed for the mathematical prediction of the Photoelectric Effect:
$$K_{max} = hf - \phi$$
Where $h$ is Planck's constant, $f$ is frequency, and $\phi$ is the work function. This new theoretical model was experimentally verified by Millikan, who confirmed the linear relationship between frequency and kinetic energy.

**Conclusion:**
The shift from the wave model to the dual wave-particle model demonstrates that scientific understanding is not static. Scientists utilize theoretical models to interpret observations, but when experiments provide anomalous data, the theoretical framework is refined. This ongoing tension between theory and experimentation is the primary driver of scientific advancement, leading us to our modern understanding of wave-particle duality.
