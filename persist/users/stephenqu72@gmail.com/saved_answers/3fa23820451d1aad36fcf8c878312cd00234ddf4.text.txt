### Model Answer

To achieve full marks in this 8-mark HSC Physics response, you must demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of the limitations of the classical wave model and the specific phenomena that necessitated the quantum model.

**1. The Classical Wave Model**
Classical physics describes light as an electromagnetic wave, consistent with Maxwell’s equations. This model is highly successful in explaining macroscopic wave phenomena, specifically:
*   **Interference and Diffraction:** The wave model accurately predicts patterns observed in Young's Double Slit experiment and diffraction gratings.
*   **Refraction and Reflection:** It explains how light propagates through media and interacts with boundaries.
The classical model views light as a continuous stream of energy where intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude.

**2. The Failure of the Classical Model**
The classical model fails when applied to phenomena involving the interaction of light with matter at the atomic level, specifically:
*   **Blackbody Radiation:** Classical theory (the Rayleigh-Jeans Law) predicted that an object would emit infinite ultraviolet radiation, known as the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe." It could not explain the observed intensity distribution of blackbody radiation.
*   **The Photoelectric Effect:** Classical physics predicted that higher intensity light would increase the kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons. Instead, experiments showed that kinetic energy depends on the **frequency** of the light, not the intensity, and that a "threshold frequency" exists below which no electrons are emitted, regardless of intensity.

**3. The Quantum Model**
The quantum model, proposed by Planck and Einstein, treats light as discrete "packets" or quanta of energy, called photons. The energy of a photon is given by:
$$E = hf$$
where $h$ is Planck’s constant and $f$ is the frequency. This model explains:
*   **Blackbody Radiation:** By quantizing energy, Planck demonstrated that the probability of emitting high-energy photons is low, preventing the ultraviolet catastrophe.
*   **Photoelectric Effect:** Einstein showed that a single photon interacts with a single electron. If $hf$ is greater than the work function $\Phi$, the electron is ejected. Increasing intensity only increases the *number* of photons, not the energy of individual photoelectrons.

**Conclusion**
The two models are required because neither is universally sufficient. The classical wave model remains the best tool for describing light propagation and interference, while the quantum model is essential for understanding how light interacts with matter and energy at the atomic scale (Wave-Particle Duality).
