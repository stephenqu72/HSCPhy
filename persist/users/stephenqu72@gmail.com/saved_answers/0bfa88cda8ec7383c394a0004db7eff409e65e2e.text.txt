To answer this question effectively for an HSC Physics exam, you need to contrast the classical wave theory's predictions with the empirical evidence observed during photoelectric experiments.

### Answer
One key observation inconsistent with the wave model is the **existence of a threshold frequency**. 

According to the classical wave model, the energy of a light wave is determined by its intensity (amplitude), not its frequency. Therefore, the wave model predicted that any frequency of light should be able to eject electrons from a metal surface, provided the light was intense enough or shone on the surface for a long enough duration to allow the electrons to accumulate sufficient energy. 

However, experimental results showed that if the frequency of incident light is below a specific value—the threshold frequency ($f_0$)—no electrons are ejected, regardless of the light's intensity or the duration of exposure. This observation provided evidence that light interacts with matter as discrete packets of energy (photons), where the energy of an individual photon ($E = hf$) must exceed the work function ($\Phi$) of the metal to eject an electron.

### How to solve this type of question
1.  **State the observation:** Clearly identify a specific phenomenon from the photoelectric effect (e.g., threshold frequency, lack of time delay, or the relationship between maximum kinetic energy and frequency).
2.  **Explain the wave prediction:** Describe what classical physics expected to happen based on the wave nature of light (typically involving intensity/amplitude being the driver of energy).
3.  **Explain the contradiction:** Conclude by explaining why the observation invalidates the wave model and supports the particle (photon) model of light.

**Alternative points you could have used for this question:**
*   **Instantaneous emission:** The wave model predicted a time lag for energy accumulation, whereas experiments showed electrons are ejected instantaneously.
*   **Kinetic energy dependence:** The wave model predicted that maximum kinetic energy should depend on light intensity, while experiments showed it only depends on the frequency of the incident light.
