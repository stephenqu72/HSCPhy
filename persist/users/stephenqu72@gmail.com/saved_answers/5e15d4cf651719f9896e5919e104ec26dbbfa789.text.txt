To solve these problems, we use the principles of orbital mechanics, specifically Kepler's Third Law and the circular orbit velocity formula. We use the Earth's gravitational constant $GM = 3.986 \times 10^{14} \, \text{m}^3\text{s}^{-2}$.

### (a) Calculate the period of the satellite
We use Kepler’s Third Law: 
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$
Rearranging for $T$: 
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}}$$
Given $r = 6.671 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$:
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (6.671 \times 10^6)^3}{3.986 \times 10^{14}}}$$
$$T \approx \sqrt{4.675 \times 10^7} \approx 5543 \, \text{seconds} \approx 92.4 \, \text{minutes}$$

### (b) Calculate the speed of the satellite
The orbital speed $v$ for a circular orbit is given by:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{3.986 \times 10^{14}}{6.671 \times 10^6}}$$
$$v \approx \sqrt{5.975 \times 10^7} \approx 7730 \, \text{m/s} \, (7.73 \, \text{km/s})$$

### (c) Explain why a satellite of $1000 \, \text{kg}$ at the same radius would have the same period
The period of an object in a circular orbit depends only on the mass of the central body ($M$) and the orbital radius ($r$). In the equation $T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM}}$, the mass of the satellite ($m$) does not appear. Physically, while the gravitational force acting on the $1000 \, \text{kg}$ satellite is ten times greater than on the $100 \, \text{kg}$ satellite, its inertia (resistance to acceleration) is also ten times greater. According to Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$), the increased force exactly compensates for the increased mass, resulting in the same centripetal acceleration required to maintain the same orbit.
