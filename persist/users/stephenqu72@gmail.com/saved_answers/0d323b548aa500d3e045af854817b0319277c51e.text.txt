### Answer

**(a)** The interference pattern consists of alternating bright and dark fringes. Bright fringes are created by constructive interference (where light waves arrive in phase, crest-to-crest or trough-to-trough), and dark fringes are created by destructive interference (where light waves arrive out of phase, crest-to-trough). This behaviour is a unique characteristic of waves, which particles cannot replicate, thus providing evidence that light propagates as a wave.

**(b)** Experiments with single photons fired one at a time show that an interference pattern still builds up over time on the detector. This challenges the classical wave model because a single "particle" of light (a photon) cannot interfere with itself if it were purely a localized particle, nor is there a continuous wave present to interfere. Quantum theory resolves this by introducing the concept of **wave-particle duality**. It describes the photon using a probability wave (wave function); the photon travels through both slits simultaneously as a probability distribution. It only "collapses" into a discrete point upon interaction with the detector. The interference pattern is essentially a map of the probability of where photons are likely to strike.

    *   **The Resolution:** Introduce **wave-particle duality**. Explain that quantum mechanics views the photon as having both characteristics. Specifically, refer to the probability wave (or wave function) and explain that the pattern is a manifestation of where it is *likely* for a photon to land, rather than the path of a classical object.
