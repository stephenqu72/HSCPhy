To solve this problem, we apply the Work-Energy Theorem. The kinetic energy of the electron is dissipated by the work done by the electric field over the distance $d$.

### Part (c)
The work done by the electric force $F = qE$ over distance $d$ equals the initial kinetic energy:
$$W = \Delta KE$$
$$Fd = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
$$(qE)d = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
Rearranging for the relationship:
$$d = \left( \frac{m}{2qE} \right) v^2$$

### Part (d)
From the derivation in part (c), the graph of $d$ vs $v^2$ should be a straight line with slope $k = \frac{m}{2qE}$.
1. Determine the slope of your line of best fit from the graph: $k = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta v^2}$. Ensure units are consistent (convert $\mu m$ to $m$).
2. Use the known values for the electron: $m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31} kg$ and $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} C$.
3. Rearrange the slope formula to solve for $E$:
$$E = \frac{m}{2qk}$$

$$E = \frac{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}{2(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(1.8 \times 10^{-12})} \approx 1581 \, V/m$$
