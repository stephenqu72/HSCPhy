### Explanation
To solve this question, we need to consider how the historical development of the wave model of light addressed the limitations of Huygens' original theory.

1.  **Type of wave (Polarisation):** Huygens originally modeled light as a longitudinal wave (similar to sound). However, longitudinal waves consist of oscillations parallel to the direction of energy transfer, which cannot exhibit polarisation because there is no "side-to-side" orientation to filter. For light to exhibit polarisation—as seen in the double refraction of calcite—the oscillations must be perpendicular to the direction of propagation. This necessitates that light be a **transverse** wave. Young and Fresnel later refined the wave theory to incorporate this transverse nature.

2.  **Origin of colour:** In the wave model of light, the physical property of a wave that determines its colour is its **wavelength** (or frequency). Newton proposed that different colours were due to different sized "corpuscles," but the wave model corrected this by identifying that different colours correspond to different wavelengths of the light wave. Amplitude, by contrast, is related to the intensity or brightness of the light, not its colour.

Combining these two points:
*   The wave must be **transverse** to allow for polarisation.
*   Colour is determined by **wavelength**.

Therefore, row B is the only correct combination.
