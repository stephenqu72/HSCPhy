### Sample Answer

The particle model of light was revived to explain phenomena that the classical wave theory could not account for, most notably the **photoelectric effect**.

**Evidence supporting the particle model:**
1.  **The Photoelectric Effect:** In this phenomenon, light incident on a metal surface causes the emission of photoelectrons. Classical wave theory predicted that increasing light intensity would increase the kinetic energy of electrons; however, experiment showed that kinetic energy depends on the **frequency** of the light, not the intensity.
2.  **Threshold Frequency:** There exists a specific minimum frequency, $f_0$, below which no emission occurs, regardless of intensity. This implies light arrives in discrete packets (quanta) with energy $E = hf$.
3.  **Instantaneous Emission:** Wave theory predicted a "lag" time for energy absorption, but electrons are emitted near-instantaneously, supporting a "one-photon-to-one-electron" collision process.

**Implications for the quantum model:**
Einstein utilized Planck’s hypothesis (originally used for blackbody radiation) to propose that light consists of particles called **photons**. This led to the equation $E_k = hf - \Phi$, where $\Phi$ is the work function. This evidence effectively ended the dominance of classical wave theory, establishing the **wave-particle duality** of light. It fundamentally shifted physics from a deterministic classical framework to a probabilistic quantum one, proving that electromagnetic radiation is quantized, which served as the cornerstone for modern quantum mechanics.
