To solve this problem, we must analyze the motion of a charged particle (a positron) moving in a uniform magnetic field.

### 1. Identify the components
*   **Positron charge ($q$):** $+1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}$
*   **Mass ($m$):** $\approx 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \, \text{kg}$
*   **Magnetic field ($\vec{B}$):** $3.1 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{T}$ in the $+x$ direction.
*   **Velocity ($\vec{v}$):** The velocity is at $45^\circ$ to the $x$ and $z$ axes. We can decompose this into:
    *   $v_x = v \cos(45^\circ) = (2.2 \times 10^4) \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 1.556 \times 10^4 \, \text{m/s}$
    *   $v_z = v \sin(45^\circ) = (2.2 \times 10^4) \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 1.556 \times 10^4 \, \text{m/s}$
    *   $v_y = 0 \, \text{m/s}$

### 2. Determine the motion
The magnetic force is $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$.
*   The $x$-component of velocity ($v_x$) is parallel to the magnetic field. Since $\sin(0^\circ) = 0$, the magnetic force on this component is zero. The positron will travel at a constant velocity $v_x$ along the $x$-axis.
*   The $z$-component of velocity ($v_z$) is perpendicular to the magnetic field. This creates circular motion in the $yz$-plane.

### 3. Quantitative Analysis
**Time taken to travel $1.0 \, \text{m}$ in the $x$-direction:**
$$t = \frac{\Delta x}{v_x} = \frac{1.0}{1.556 \times 10^4} \approx 6.43 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{s}$$

**Circular motion parameters:**
The radius of the circular path is given by $r = \frac{mv_\perp}{qB}$:
$$r = \frac{(9.109 \times 10^{-31})(1.556 \times 10^4)}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(3.1 \times 10^{-6})} \approx 0.0285 \, \text{m} \, (2.85 \, \text{cm})$$

The angular velocity is $\omega = \frac{qB}{m}$:
$$\omega = \frac{(1.602 \times 10^{-19})(3.1 \times 10^{-6})}{9.109 \times 10^{-31}} \approx 5.45 \times 10^5 \, \text{rad/s}$$

**Angle rotated in the $yz$-plane:**
$$\theta = \omega t = (5.45 \times 10^5) \times (6.43 \times 10^{-5}) \approx 35.0 \, \text{radians}$$
Since $35.0 / 2\pi \approx 5.57$ rotations, the positron completes approximately 5.5 full circles while traveling 1 meter in the $x$-direction.

### Conclusion
The positron undergoes **helical motion**. It moves with a constant velocity of $1.56 \times 10^4 \, \text{m/s}$ in the $+x$ direction, while simultaneously performing uniform circular motion in the $yz$-plane with a radius of $2.85 \, \text{cm}$. Over a distance of $1.0 \, \text{m}$ in the $x$-direction (taking $6.43 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{s}$), it will complete approximately 5.5 full helical rotations.
