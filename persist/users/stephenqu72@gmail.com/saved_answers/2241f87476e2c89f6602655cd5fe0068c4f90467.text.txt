To find the wavelength of the radiation produced by the annihilation of an electron and a positron, we apply the principle of conservation of mass-energy.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) $2.425 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m}$**.

### Explanation
1. **Identify the total energy available:** Since both the electron and positron have negligible kinetic energy, the total energy available for the production of photons is simply the sum of their rest mass energies. The rest mass energy of an electron (or positron) is $E = mc^2 \approx 0.511 \text{ MeV}$ (or $8.187 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J}$).
   Total energy $E_{total} = 2 \times (mc^2)$.

2. **Understand the annihilation process:** To conserve momentum, the annihilation produces two photons of equal energy moving in opposite directions. Therefore, each photon carries the energy of one of the particles:
   $$E_{\text{photon}} = mc^2$$

3. **Calculate the wavelength:** We use the energy-wavelength relationship $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$, rearranged to solve for $\lambda$:
   $$\lambda = \frac{hc}{E}$$
   Where:
   - $h$ (Planck's constant) $\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$
   - $c$ (speed of light) $\approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
   - $E$ (rest mass energy of an electron) $\approx 8.187 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J}$ (Using $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$)

4. **Perform the calculation:**
   $$\lambda = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)}{9.109 \times 10^{-31} \times (2.998 \times 10^8)^2}$$
   Using the standard rest mass energy $E = 8.187 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J}$:
   $$\lambda = \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{8.187 \times 10^{-14}} \approx 2.427 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m}$$

Rounding to the provided option values, we get $2.425 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m}$.
