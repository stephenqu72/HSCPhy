To solve this problem, we use the principle of conservation of energy. An electric charge $q$ accelerated through a potential difference $V$ gains kinetic energy equal to the electrical potential energy lost:

$$W = qV = \Delta K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

### Step-by-step Solution:

1.  **Establish the relationship:** From the equation $qV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, we can see that since $q$ and $m$ (mass of the charge) are constants, the square of the speed $v^2$ is directly proportional to the potential difference $V$:
    $$v^2 \propto V \implies v \propto \sqrt{V}$$

2.  **Set up a ratio:** Since $v$ is proportional to $\sqrt{V}$, we can write:
    $$\frac{v_2}{v_1} = \sqrt{\frac{V_2}{V_1}}$$

3.  **Plug in the known values:**
    *   $V_1 = 300\text{ V}$
    *   $v_1 = 9.0 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$
    *   $V_2 = 150\text{ V}$
    *   $v_2 = ?$

    $$\frac{v_2}{9.0 \times 10^6} = \sqrt{\frac{150}{300}}$$
    $$\frac{v_2}{9.0 \times 10^6} = \sqrt{0.5}$$

4.  **Calculate:**
    $$\sqrt{0.5} \approx 0.7071$$
    $$v_2 = 0.7071 \times 9.0 \times 10^6$$
    $$v_2 \approx 6.36 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$$

Rounding to two significant figures, we get $6.4 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$.
