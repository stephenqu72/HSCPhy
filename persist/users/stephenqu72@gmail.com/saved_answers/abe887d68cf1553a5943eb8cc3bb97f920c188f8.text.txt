To solve this problem, we are applying **Hubble’s Law**, which states that the recessional velocity $v$ of a galaxy is proportional to its distance $d$ from the observer, expressed by the equation:
$$v = H_0 d$$
where $H_0$ is the Hubble constant.

### Part (a): Graphing the data
To graph this, you need to set up your axes:
1.  **Horizontal Axis (x-axis):** Distance from Earth (Mpc). Scale this to accommodate values from $0$ to $100$. Each grid square could represent $10\text{ Mpc}$.
2.  **Vertical Axis (y-axis):** Recessional velocity ($\text{km s}^{-1}$). Scale this to accommodate values from $0$ to $4500$. Each grid square could represent $500\text{ km s}^{-1}$.
3.  **Plot the points:** Plot the coordinate pairs $(11, 850)$, $(14, 1050)$, $(32, 1900)$, $(51, 3150)$, and $(62, 4000)$.
4.  **Line of Best Fit:** Use a ruler to draw a straight line that passes as close as possible to all points, ideally passing through the origin $(0,0)$, as Hubble's Law is a direct proportionality.

### Part (b): Predicting the recessional velocity for Galaxy Zeta
1.  Locate $98\text{ Mpc}$ on your horizontal axis.
2.  Follow the vertical line up from $98$ until it meets your line of best fit.
3.  From that point, follow the horizontal line to the left to read the value on the velocity axis.

**Calculated prediction (theoretical):**
Using the average slope (Hubble constant) from the data:
- Average $H_0 \approx \frac{4000}{62} \approx 64.5\text{ km s}^{-1}\text{ Mpc}^{-1}$.
- For Zeta ($98\text{ Mpc}$): $v = 64.5 \times 98 \approx 6321\text{ km s}^{-1}$.
