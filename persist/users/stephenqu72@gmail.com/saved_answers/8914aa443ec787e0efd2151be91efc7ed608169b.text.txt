Hello! As an HSC Physics teacher, I’m happy to guide you through this Standard Model question. This is a classic "Topic 9: Quanta to Quarks" style question. Let's break it down.

### Answer and Explanation

**(a) Describe and explain TWO pieces of evidence that baryons are not fundamental.**

*   **Evidence 1: The "Particle Zoo" and patterns in Scattering Experiments.**
    *   **Description/Explanation:** During the mid-20th century, physicists discovered a massive number of different hadrons (mesons and baryons). If they were fundamental, there would not be so many with varying masses and properties. Furthermore, Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS) experiments—where high-energy electrons were fired at protons—showed that the electrons bounced off internal point-like structures called "partons" (later identified as quarks), proving the proton had an internal substructure.
*   **Evidence 2: The Charge and Conservation laws (The Quark Model).**
    *   **Description/Explanation:** The existence of particles like the $\Omega^-$ (omega-minus) baryon required the theoretical introduction of the "strangeness" quantum number and the quark model (by Gell-Mann) to organize them into patterns like the "Eightfold Way." The fractional charges of quarks (e.g., $+2/3e$ and $-1/3e$) allow for the observed integer charges of baryons (e.g., a proton with $+1e$) when combined, which would be impossible if baryons were indivisible fundamental entities.

**(b) Identify the quark composition of a proton and a neutron.**

*   **Proton:** The proton consists of two up quarks and one down quark: $uud$.
    *   *Check:* $(+2/3) + (+2/3) + (-1/3) = +1$ (The charge of a proton).
*   **Neutron:** The neutron consists of one up quark and two down quarks: $udd$.
    *   *Check:* $(+2/3) + (-1/3) + (-1/3) = 0$ (The charge of a neutron).

**(c) Outline a major limitation of the Standard Model of Matter at the moment.**

*   **Answer:** The Standard Model does not incorporate gravity. It cannot account for the force of gravity within the framework of quantum mechanics (i.e., it lacks a theoretical "graviton" that fits the model) and it fails to explain the existence of dark matter and dark energy, which make up the majority of the mass-energy of the universe.

---

### How to solve it:
1.  **For (a):** Focus on the *history of particle physics*. Whenever you see "evidence for non-fundamental particles," think about **scattering experiments** (like Rutherford’s gold foil, but for protons) and **classification schemes** (how the Eightfold Way predicted the quarks).
2.  **For (b):** Remember the charge of up ($+2/3$) and down ($-1/3$) quarks. Always sum them up to verify your answer—this is a great way to avoid silly mistakes in the exam.
3.  **For (c):** Keep a list of "Limitations of the Standard Model" in your revision notes. Gravity and Dark Matter/Energy are the two most common answers expected by HSC markers.
