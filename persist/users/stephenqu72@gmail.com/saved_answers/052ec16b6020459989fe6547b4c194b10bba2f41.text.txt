To determine the change in speed required for the satellite, we use the formula for the orbital velocity of a satellite in circular motion:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

where:
- $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$
- $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \, \text{kg}$ (Mass of Earth)
- $R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$ (Radius of Earth)
- $r$ is the distance from the center of the Earth ($r = R_E + h$)

### Step 1: Calculate initial orbital velocity ($v_1$) at $h_1 = 850 \, \text{km}$
The orbital radius is $r_1 = 6,370,000 + 850,000 = 7,220,000 \, \text{m}$.
$$v_1 = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}{7,220,000}} \approx 7,434 \, \text{m s}^{-1}$$

### Step 2: Calculate final orbital velocity ($v_2$) at $h_2 = 650 \, \text{km}$
The orbital radius is $r_2 = 6,370,000 + 650,000 = 7,020,000 \, \text{m}$.
$$v_2 = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}{7,020,000}} \approx 7,539 \, \text{m s}^{-1}$$

### Step 3: Determine the change in speed
The new velocity ($v_2$) is greater than the initial velocity ($v_1$). 
$$\Delta v = v_2 - v_1 = 7,539 - 7,434 = 105 \, \text{m s}^{-1}$$

Because the satellite moves into a lower orbit (where the gravitational potential is stronger), it must increase its orbital speed to maintain a stable circular path at that lower altitude.

**Answer:** The speed must **increase by $105 \, \text{m s}^{-1}$**. Therefore, the correct option is **A**.
