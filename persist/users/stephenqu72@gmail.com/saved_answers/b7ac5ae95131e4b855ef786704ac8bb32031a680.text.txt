To determine the most likely dominant nucleosynthesis reaction occurring in the core of star X, we must analyze its position on the Hertzsprung-Russell (HR) diagram.

### Answer
The correct option is **(B)**.

### Explanation
1.  **Locate Star X:** Looking at the provided HR diagram, Star X is located on the Main Sequence, specifically in the upper-left region. Its temperature is between $10,000\text{ K}$ and $20,000\text{ K}$, and its luminosity is significantly higher than that of the Sun (well above $10^2\text{ L}_{\text{sun}}$).
2.  **Understand Stellar Nucleosynthesis:** Main Sequence stars fuse hydrogen into helium in their cores. There are two primary mechanisms for this:
    *   **Proton-proton (p-p) chain:** This is the dominant process in stars with lower core temperatures (like the Sun, which is $\approx 5,800\text{ K}$).
    *   **CNO Cycle (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle):** This process is much more temperature-sensitive. It becomes the dominant energy production mechanism in stars with higher core temperatures, typically those more massive than about $1.3$ solar masses ($M_{\odot}$), which correspond to the hotter, more luminous upper-left portion of the Main Sequence.
3.  **Evaluate the Position:** Since Star X is a massive, hot, luminous Main Sequence star, its core temperature is sufficiently high for the CNO cycle to be the primary energy source. While the CNO cycle is dominant, the proton-proton chain may still occur at a much lower rate in the outer regions of the core or if conditions vary, but the CNO cycle is the characteristic reaction for a star of this spectral type.

Therefore, the star's high temperature and luminosity indicate that the CNO cycle is the dominant process, supplemented by a small amount of proton-proton chain activity.
