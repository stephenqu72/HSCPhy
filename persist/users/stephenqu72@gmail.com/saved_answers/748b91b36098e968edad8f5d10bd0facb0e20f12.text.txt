### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the historical context of classical electromagnetic theory versus quantum physics:

1.  **Maxwell’s Equations (Classical Electromagnetism):** James Clerk Maxwell developed a set of equations that successfully described light as an electromagnetic wave. These equations predicted:
    *   **The speed of light ($c$):** Maxwell’s equations derived the speed of light in a vacuum as $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0 \mu_0}}$, confirming that light is an electromagnetic wave (Option A).
    *   **The wave nature of light:** Maxwell’s theory explicitly treats light as oscillating electric and magnetic fields, providing the foundation for wave optics (Option C).
    *   **The electromagnetic spectrum:** Because the equations define waves based on frequency and wavelength, they theoretically predict the existence of other electromagnetic wavebands beyond visible light, such as radio waves or X-rays (Option D).

2.  **The Photoelectric Effect (Quantum Physics):** The photoelectric effect involves the emission of electrons when light shines on a material. Classical wave theory (Maxwell’s equations) predicts that the intensity of light should determine the energy of the emitted electrons. However, experiments showed that the energy of electrons depends on the *frequency* of the light, not the intensity. This discovery, explained by Albert Einstein using the concept of light quanta (photons), demonstrated that light has a particle-like nature. Since Maxwell’s equations are purely classical wave-based equations, they fail to explain the discrete, quantum nature of the photoelectric effect.

Therefore, the aspect of light that Maxwell's equations cannot describe is the set of observations associated with the photoelectric effect.
