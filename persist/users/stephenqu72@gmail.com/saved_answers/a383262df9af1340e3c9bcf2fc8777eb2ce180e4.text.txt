### Part (a) Identification of Regions
To answer this, you need to link the mass of the star to the efficiency of fusion cycles.

*   **i. p-p (proton-proton) hydrogen fusion:** This process is the primary energy source for lower-mass stars (like our Sun). On the diagram, these stars occupy the lower part of the Main Sequence, indicated by the **$A_2$** region (stars $< 8M_\odot$).
*   **ii. CNO hydrogen fusion:** This cycle requires much higher core temperatures to overcome the higher electrostatic repulsion of the nuclei involved. It is the dominant process in high-mass stars, which are located in the upper part of the Main Sequence, indicated by the **$A_1$** region (stars $> 8M_\odot$).

### Part (b) Evolution of a Star from Region $A_2$
To earn full marks here, you must demonstrate your understanding of the link between the HR diagram movement and internal structural changes.

**Answer:**
A star starting in region $A_2$ (a low-mass star on the Main Sequence) will evolve as follows:

1.  **Movement:** The star moves horizontally to the right towards region **B** (the Red Giant Branch) and then moves upwards into region **C**.
2.  **Structural Changes:**
    *   **Hydrogen Exhaustion:** Once hydrogen in the core is depleted, the core contracts due to gravity, causing the outer envelope of the star to expand and cool significantly. This expansion leads to the move towards the right (cooler temperatures).
    *   **Hydrogen Shell Burning:** As the core contracts, it heats up, igniting hydrogen fusion in a shell surrounding the core. This increases the star's luminosity, moving it upwards.
    *   **Helium Ignition:** Eventually, the core becomes hot enough to begin helium fusion (the "helium flash" for low-mass stars). The star then moves toward the horizontal branch and eventually reaches the Red Giant/Supergiant regions (like **C**), characterized by high luminosity and lower surface temperatures.
3.  **Final Stage:** After exhausting its nuclear fuel, the star will shed its outer layers as a planetary nebula and leave behind the hot, dense core, which will move down and to the left to become a **White Dwarf** (region **D**).

### How to solve this:
1.  **Understand Stellar Physics:** Remember that mass is the primary determinant of a star's lifetime and its fusion path.
2.  **Relate Temperature to Fusion:** High-mass stars ($> 8M_\odot$) have hotter cores, enabling the CNO cycle. Low-mass stars have cooler cores, relying on the p-p chain.
3.  **Use the HR Diagram as a Map:** Think of the HR diagram not just as a static plot, but as a path. The Main Sequence is the "life" of the star; leaving it signifies the end of hydrogen core fusion.
4.  **Structure your essay/response:** In part (b), always link the **observable property** (e.g., luminosity, temperature) to the **internal physical process** (e.g., core contraction, shell burning).
