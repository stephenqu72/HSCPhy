To determine if the bead can remain fixed at point $C$, we must analyze the forces acting on the bead in the rotating frame of the hoop.

### Answer
No, it is not possible for the bead to remain fixed at point $C$.

### Explanation
To remain at a fixed position on the rotating hoop, the net force on the bead must provide exactly the required centripetal acceleration directed toward the vertical axis of rotation.

**1. Qualitative Analysis:**
At point $C$, the hoop is vertical (tangent to the circle at the horizontal radius). The forces acting on the bead are:
*   **Gravity ($mg$):** Acting vertically downwards.
*   **Normal force ($N$):** Acting horizontally towards the center of the hoop (due to the bead being pushed against the side of the hoop).

For the bead to remain at $C$, the vertical component of the net force must be zero. However, the only vertical force acting on the bead is gravity ($mg$), which acts downwards. There is no upward force to counteract gravity at point $C$. Because there is a net unbalanced force in the vertical direction, the bead cannot remain in equilibrium and will slide downwards.

**2. Quantitative Analysis:**
Let $R$ be the radius of the hoop. At point $C$, the horizontal distance from the axis of rotation is $r = R$.
*   The centripetal force required is $F_c = m \omega^2 R$.
*   The normal force $N$ provides this centripetal force, so $N = m \omega^2 R$.
*   Summing forces in the vertical direction: $\sum F_y = N_y - mg$. 
*   Since the hoop is vertical at $C$, the normal force $N$ is purely horizontal. Therefore, $N_y = 0$.
*   The vertical equation becomes: $\sum F_y = 0 - mg = -mg$.

Since the vertical acceleration $a_y = -g$, the bead must accelerate downwards due to gravity. Because there is no upward component of the normal force to balance the weight of the bead, the bead will always slide down from point $C$ regardless of the angular velocity $\omega$.
