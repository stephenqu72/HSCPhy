### Explanation

To solve this problem, we need to understand how the frequency ($f$) of rotation affects the induced electromotive force (EMF) in an AC generator. The induced voltage $\varepsilon$ in a rotating coil is given by the formula derived from Faraday’s Law:

$$\varepsilon = NBA\omega \sin(\omega t)$$

Where:
- $N$ is the number of turns.
- $B$ is the magnetic field strength.
- $A$ is the area of the coil.
- $\omega$ is the angular velocity, where $\omega = 2\pi f$.

**1. Effect on Amplitude (Peak Voltage):**
The peak voltage ($\varepsilon_{max}$) is equal to $NBA\omega$. Since $\omega = 2\pi f$, we can write:
$$\varepsilon_{max} = NBA(2\pi f)$$
This shows that the peak voltage is directly proportional to the frequency of rotation. 
- Original frequency ($f_1$) = $50\text{ rev/s}$
- New frequency ($f_2$) = $25\text{ rev/s}$
Since the frequency is halved ($f_2 = 0.5f_1$), the new peak voltage will also be halved. In the original graph, the peak voltage is $1$. In the new graph, the peak voltage must be $0.5$.

**2. Effect on Period (Horizontal scaling):**
The period ($T$) of the wave is the inverse of the frequency ($T = 1/f$).
- Original frequency ($f_1$) = $50\text{ Hz}$
- New frequency ($f_2$) = $25\text{ Hz}$
Since the frequency is halved, the period must double ($T_2 = 2T_1$). This means the wave will oscillate half as fast, stretching the graph horizontally to be twice as wide for each cycle.

**Conclusion:**
By comparing the options, we look for a graph where:
- The peak amplitude is $0.5$ (half of the original $1$).
- The wavelength (period) is doubled (one full cycle takes twice as long).

Graph **B** shows an amplitude of $0.5$ and a wave that is stretched out to half the frequency, matching our calculated requirements.
