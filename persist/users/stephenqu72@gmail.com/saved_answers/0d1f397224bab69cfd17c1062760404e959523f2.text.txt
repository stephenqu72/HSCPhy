### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the historical and physical significance of Thomas Young’s double-slit experiment:

1.  **Context:** In the early 19th century, the nature of light was debated between the "particle model" (championed by Newton) and the "wave model." Young's experiment provided the evidence needed to support the wave theory of light.
2.  **The Mechanism:**
    *   **Diffraction:** As light passes through the two narrow slits, the slits act as point sources, causing the light waves to spread out (diffract).
    *   **Interference:** These two sets of circular waves overlap on the screen. Where the peaks of the waves meet (path difference is an integer multiple of the wavelength, $n\lambda$), **constructive interference** occurs, creating a bright fringe. Where the peak of one wave meets the trough of another (path difference is an odd multiple of half-wavelength, $(n + 1/2)\lambda$), **destructive interference** occurs, creating a dark fringe.
3.  **Evaluating the distractors:**
    *   **(A)** describes a particle collision model, which was the rejected Newtonian view that could not explain the interference fringes.
    *   **(B)** refers to polarisation, which is a property of transverse waves but does not explain the spatial intensity patterns observed in the double-slit experiment.
    *   **(C)** refers to refraction (the bending of light due to a change in medium), which is not the mechanism responsible for creating interference patterns.

Therefore, the observation of these light and dark fringes is the definitive proof of the wave nature of light, explained precisely by diffraction and interference.
