To answer this question effectively, you need to connect the early universe's cosmological evolution to stellar nucleosynthesis and the characteristics of main-sequence stars.

### Part a) Big Bang and Star Formation
**Explanation:**
1.  **Early Universe:** Following the Big Bang, the universe was an extremely hot, dense plasma of energy. As it expanded and cooled (during the era of nucleosynthesis), quarks formed protons and neutrons. Further cooling allowed these to fuse into light nuclei (primarily Hydrogen and Helium).
2.  **Formation of Matter:** Eventually, the universe cooled enough for electrons to bind to nuclei, forming neutral atoms (recombination). This allowed radiation to travel freely.
3.  **Star Formation:** Gravity caused denser regions of gas (H and He) to collapse. As these clouds contracted, gravitational potential energy was converted into thermal energy. Once the core temperature was sufficient (roughly 10 million K), nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium began, "turning on" the star and establishing it on the main sequence. Star X and Y formed in this manner from these primordial gas clouds.

### Part b) Calculation of Energy
**Explanation:**
The CNO cycle effectively converts 4 Hydrogen nuclei into 1 Helium nucleus. The mass of the carbon nucleus acts as a catalyst and remains unchanged. We use the masses: $m_H = 1.007825\,u$ (standard value for Hydrogen-1) and $m_{He} = 4.002602\,u$. 
*Note: The equation shows $4 \times {}^1_1H$ being converted to $1 \times {}^4_2He$.*

1.  **Mass defect ($\Delta m$):**
    $$\Delta m = (4 \times 1.007825\,u) - 4.002602\,u$$
    $$\Delta m = 4.031300\,u - 4.002602\,u = 0.028698\,u$$
2.  **Convert to energy:** Using $1\,u = 931.5\,\text{MeV}/c^2$:
    $$E = 0.028698 \times 931.5\,\text{MeV} \approx 26.73\,\text{MeV}$$

### Part c) Which star (X or Y)?
**Answer:** The CNO cycle is most likely to occur in **Star X**.

**Explanation:**
The CNO cycle requires higher temperatures (typically $> 1.7 \times 10^7\,\text{K}$) than the proton-proton chain because the nuclei involved (Carbon, Nitrogen, Oxygen) have higher atomic numbers and therefore higher Coulomb repulsion (electromagnetic force) to overcome. Looking at the H-R diagram, **Star X** is a high-luminosity, high-temperature (spectral class O or B) star, whereas **Star Y** is a lower-luminosity, cooler (spectral class K or M) star. Therefore, Star X provides the necessary high core temperature for the CNO cycle to dominate.
