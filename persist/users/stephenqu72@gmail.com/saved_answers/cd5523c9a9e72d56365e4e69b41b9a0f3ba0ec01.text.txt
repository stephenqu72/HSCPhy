### Solution and Explanation

#### (a) Calculate the initial velocity
To calculate the initial velocity, we equate the maximum kinetic energy of the ejected electrons ($E_k$) to the energy required to stop them, which is the stopping voltage ($V_s$) multiplied by the charge of an electron ($e$).

$$E_k = qV_s \implies \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = eV_s$$

Given:
- $V_s = 4.9\text{ V}$
- $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$
- $m = 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2eV_s}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \times 4.9}{9.109 \times 10^{-31}}}$$
$$v \approx 1.31 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$$

#### (d) Calculate the work function
The Einstein photoelectric equation is $hf = \Phi + E_k$, which can be rearranged to $V_s = \frac{h}{e}f - \frac{\Phi}{e}$.
1. Find the x-intercept of your graph ($f_0$, the threshold frequency).
2. The work function $\Phi$ is calculated as:
$$\Phi = hf_0$$
Where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$.

#### (e) Explain the quantum model
The quantum model (Einstein’s photon theory) accounts for the results as follows:
1. **Quantization:** Light exists as discrete packets of energy called photons, where $E = hf$.
2. **One-to-one interaction:** Each photon interacts with a single electron. If the photon's energy is less than the work function ($\Phi$), no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity.
3. **Threshold Frequency:** There is a minimum frequency ($f_0$) required to overcome the binding energy (work function) of the metal.
4. **Conservation of Energy:** Any energy from the photon in excess of the work function is converted into the kinetic energy of the ejected electron. Increasing the frequency of light increases the kinetic energy (and thus stopping voltage), while increasing intensity only increases the *number* of photoelectrons emitted per second.
