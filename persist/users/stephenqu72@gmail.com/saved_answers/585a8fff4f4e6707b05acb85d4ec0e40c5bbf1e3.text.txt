To determine the most reliable experimental setup, we must look at the principles of a fair test in physics.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
To investigate the relationship between the number of turns in a solenoid ($N$) and the strength of the magnetic field ($B$), you must apply the scientific method correctly:

1.  **Independent Variable:** This is the variable you intentionally change. In this experiment, it is the **number of turns**.
2.  **Dependent Variable:** This is the variable you measure. It is the **strength of the magnetic field** (which would be measured by a sensor/meter for each row).
3.  **Control Variables:** These are the variables that must remain constant to ensure a fair test. The most significant control variable here is the **current ($I$)** flowing through the solenoid, as the magnetic field of a solenoid is defined by the formula:
    $$B = \mu_0 \frac{N}{L} I$$
    Where $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space, $N$ is the number of turns, $L$ is the length, and $I$ is the current. If you change both the current and the number of turns simultaneously (as seen in option A), you cannot determine which factor caused any observed change in the magnetic field.

**Evaluating the Options:**
*   **A:** Both current and number of turns are changing. This makes it impossible to isolate the effect of the number of turns.
*   **B:** The **current is held constant** ($10\text{ A}$) while the **number of turns is varied** ($50, 100, 150, 200, 250$). This is the correct experimental design to observe how $N$ affects $B$.
*   **C:** The number of turns is held constant. You cannot investigate the effect of turns if you never change them.
*   **D:** The current is held constant, but the increments in the number of turns are very small and irregular compared to the range, and it is less comprehensive than the wider, controlled range provided in option B.

Therefore, **B** is the only experiment that correctly isolates the independent variable while maintaining strict control over the secondary factor (current).
