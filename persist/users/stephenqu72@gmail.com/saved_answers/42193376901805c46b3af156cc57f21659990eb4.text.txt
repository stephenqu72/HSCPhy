### Answer
The proton-proton (p-p) chain is the dominant nuclear fusion process in stars like our Sun. It involves the fusion of four hydrogen nuclei (protons) into one helium-4 nucleus ($^{4}He$). 

The process proceeds through a series of steps where protons combine to form deuterium, then helium-3, and finally helium-4, releasing positrons, neutrinos, and gamma-ray photons at various stages. The total mass of the final helium-4 nucleus is less than the combined mass of the four original protons. This "missing mass" is known as the **mass defect** ($\Delta m$).

According to Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = \Delta mc^2$, this lost mass is converted directly into energy. This energy is released primarily as kinetic energy of the products and high-energy gamma-ray photons, which eventually provide the thermal pressure required to support the star against gravitational collapse.

This does not violate the law of conservation of energy because mass and energy are essentially two forms of the same physical quantity. The "lost" mass is simply transformed into an equivalent amount of energy; therefore, the total mass-energy of the system remains conserved throughout the reaction.
