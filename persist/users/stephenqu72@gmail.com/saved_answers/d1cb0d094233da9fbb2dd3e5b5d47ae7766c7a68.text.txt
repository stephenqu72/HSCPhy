### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:**
The key evidence is the existence of a **threshold frequency** (or cutoff wavelength) below which no photoelectrons are emitted, regardless of the intensity of the light. Additionally, the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons depends on the **frequency** of the light, not its intensity.

**Explanation:**
To score full marks, you must contrast this with the classical wave model. According to the wave model, energy is proportional to intensity (amplitude). Classical physics predicted that any frequency of light should eventually eject an electron if the intensity was high enough, or if given enough time to "soak up" energy. The observation that there is a strict threshold frequency below which no emission occurs—even at high intensity—directly contradicts the wave model and supports Einstein’s photon model, where light arrives in discrete packets (quanta) with energy $E = hf$.

---

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
The energy is approximately $6.63 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$ (or $4.14\text{ eV}$).

**Calculation:**
We use the relationship between energy, Planck's constant, the speed of light, and wavelength:
$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

Where:
- $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J s}$
- $c = 3.00 \times 10^{8}\text{ m/s}$
- $\lambda = 300\text{ nm} = 300 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}$

Substitute the values:
$$E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)}{300 \times 10^{-9}}$$
$$E = \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{3 \times 10^{-7}}$$
$$E \approx 6.63 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$$

To convert to electron-volts (eV), divide by $1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J/eV}$:
$$E \approx \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 4.14\text{ eV}$$

**Explanation:**
The "threshold wavelength" is the longest wavelength capable of providing exactly the work function energy required to liberate an electron from the metal surface. Any wavelength longer than this would have a lower frequency, meaning the photon energy would be less than the work function, resulting in no emission.
