### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental physical principle behind Kepler’s Second Law.

1.  **Kepler’s Second Law:** This law states that a line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. This implies that a planet moves faster when it is closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when it is further away (aphelion).
2.  **Angular Momentum:** Kepler's Second Law is a direct consequence of the **conservation of angular momentum**. Since gravity acts as a central force (directed toward the Sun), there is no torque acting on the planet. Therefore, angular momentum $L = mvr \sin(\theta)$ remains constant.
3.  **Energy Considerations:** As a planet moves closer to the central body (the Sun), it moves into a region of lower gravitational potential energy. 
    *   The gravitational potential energy is given by $U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$. As $r$ decreases, the magnitude of the negative potential energy increases, meaning the system loses gravitational potential energy (it becomes more negative).
    *   By the **Law of Conservation of Energy**, this loss in gravitational potential energy must be converted into an increase in kinetic energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$). This is why the planet speeds up as it approaches the central body, which is consistent with the conservation of angular momentum requirement for sweeping out equal areas.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A)** is incorrect because the planet gains, not loses, kinetic energy as it moves closer to the central body.
*   **(B)** refers to Kepler's First Law (that orbits are ellipses, not circles).
*   **(C)** refers to Kepler's Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$).
