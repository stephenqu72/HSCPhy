### Explanation
To solve this, we need to break the graph down into two key components: the overall shape and the specific features labeled $Y$.

1.  **Analyze the Features ($Y$):**
    Looking at the graph, the "dips" or "gaps" in the continuous intensity curve are called **absorption lines**. These occur when cooler gas in the outer layers of a star absorbs specific wavelengths of light from the hotter core. If they were emission lines, you would see sharp spikes (increases) in intensity above the continuous background rather than gaps. Therefore, $Y$ must be **absorption lines**.

2.  **Analyze the Source:**
    *   A **discharge tube** typically produces a discrete "emission spectrum" consisting of only a few sharp lines against a dark background (no continuous spectrum).
    *   A **star** behaves similarly to a blackbody, producing a continuous range of wavelengths (the smooth, curved shape of the graph), but with dark absorption lines superimposed on top caused by the stellar atmosphere. This graph matches the characteristic profile of a stellar spectrum, specifically one showing hydrogen Balmer lines (e.g., the line at $656 \text{ nm}$ is the $H\alpha$ line).

Since the spectrum is continuous with dark absorption lines, it is definitively a **star**.

Comparing this to your options:
*   **A:** Star / Absorption lines — **Correct.**
*   **B:** Discharge tube / Absorption lines — Incorrect (discharge tubes don't produce the continuous background).
*   **C:** Star / Emission lines — Incorrect (the features are gaps, not peaks).
*   **D:** Discharge tube / Emission lines — Incorrect (the features are gaps, and the background is continuous).
