### Answer
Young’s double-slit experiment provides evidence for the wave-like nature of light because it produces an **interference pattern** on the screen. When coherent light passes through two closely spaced slits, the waves diffract and overlap, creating regions of **constructive interference** (bright fringes) where crests meet crests, and **destructive interference** (dark fringes) where crests meet troughs. This behavior is exclusive to waves. Conversely, if light were composed of particles, the particles would simply pass through the slits and create two distinct bright bands on the screen, failing to explain the observed superposition of waves.

### How to solve this
To secure full marks (3 marks) in an HSC Physics response, you must follow a logical structure that compares the wave model to the particle model:

1.  **Identify the phenomenon:** Start by stating that the experiment produces an interference pattern, which is a characteristic behavior of waves.
2.  **Explain the wave mechanism:** Briefly mention how the two slits act as coherent sources, causing the light waves to undergo superposition (diffraction and subsequent interference). Explicitly mention that constructive interference creates bright fringes and destructive interference creates dark fringes.
3.  **Refute the particle model:** Contrast this with what would happen if light were a particle. According to Newton’s corpuscular theory, particles moving in straight lines would only pass through the two slits to produce two distinct bright vertical lines on the screen, not an interference pattern. 

**Teacher's Tip:** In your exams, always use the terminology "superposition" and "interference" to demonstrate your understanding of the wave model. Avoid just saying "it shows waves"—be specific about the *interference pattern* as the evidence.
