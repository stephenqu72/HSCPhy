### Answer

**How Electromagnetic Braking Occurs:**
Electromagnetic braking relies on **Lenz's Law** and the principle of **Eddy Currents**. As the aluminium disc rotates through the magnetic field generated by the electromagnets, the changing magnetic flux experienced by the disc induces electromotive force (EMF) within the metal. This results in the flow of eddy currents within the disc. According to Lenz's Law, these currents create their own magnetic fields that oppose the change in the external magnetic flux. The interaction between the external magnetic field and the magnetic field created by the eddy currents produces a resistive torque that acts against the rotation of the disc, causing the centrifuge to decelerate.

**Why it is Necessary to Vary the Current:**
The magnitude of the braking force depends on the strength of the magnetic field, which is determined by the current supplied to the electromagnets ($F \propto B^2$, where $B$ is the magnetic field strength).
1.  **Safety:** If the deceleration is too rapid, the large resistive torque may cause physical disturbance or "resuspension" of the separated delicate sample components. By controlling the current, we can modulate the braking torque to ensure the rate of change of angular velocity is within safe limits for the sample.
2.  **Efficiency:** The strength of the eddy currents (and thus the braking torque) is proportional to the velocity of the disc. As the centrifuge slows down, the induced EMF decreases, which would naturally cause the braking force to drop. By increasing the current as the centrifuge slows, the magnetic field $B$ is increased to compensate for the decrease in rotational speed, maintaining a consistent and efficient deceleration profile that avoids wasting time.
