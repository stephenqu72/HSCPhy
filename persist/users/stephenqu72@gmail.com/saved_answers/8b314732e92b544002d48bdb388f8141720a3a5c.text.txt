### Teacher's Solution and Explanation

To solve this problem, we use Kepler's Third Law, which states that for orbiting bodies, the square of the orbital period ($T^2$) is directly proportional to the cube of the orbital radius ($r^3$). Mathematically, this is expressed as:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

#### (a) Graphing the data
To verify the law, you must plot **$(\text{Orbital radius})^3$ on the x-axis** vs **$(\text{Orbital period})^2$ on the y-axis** (or vice versa).
*   **Data points:** $(1.2, 2.34)$, $(4.1, 9.61)$, and $(14.9, 38.4)$.
*   **Action:** Plot these points and draw a **straight line of best fit** passing through the origin $(0,0)$.

#### (b) Explanation
The graph provides evidence because the plotted data points form a **straight line passing through the origin**. A linear relationship between $r^3$ and $T^2$ confirms that $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \text{constant}$, which is exactly what Kepler’s Third Law predicts.

#### (c) Determining the mass of Jupiter
From the formula $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$, we can rearrange it to find the mass of Jupiter ($M$):
$$M = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{G T^2}$$
1.  **Calculate the gradient ($m$):** Take two points on your line of best fit. $m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{T^2}{r^3}$.
    *   *Note: Ensure you account for the power of 10 in the table units ($10^{10} / 10^{26} = 10^{-16}$).*
2.  **Substitute into the rearranged formula:** Since your graph is $T^2$ vs $r^3$, the gradient is equal to $\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$.
3.  Therefore: $M = \frac{4\pi^2}{G \times \text{gradient}}$.
4.  Using $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$ and the average gradient from the table:
    *   Gradient $\approx \frac{38.4 \times 10^{10}}{14.9 \times 10^{26}} \approx 2.58 \times 10^{-16} \, \text{s}^2/\text{m}^3$.
    *   $M = \frac{4\pi^2}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (2.58 \times 10^{-16})} \approx 2.3 \times 10^{27} \, \text{kg}$.

*(Note: The actual mass of Jupiter is approximately $1.9 \times 10^{27} \, \text{kg}$; variations in your calculation may occur based on your line of best fit).*
