### Answer and Explanation

#### Part a)
To represent the forces acting on the system at point $M$, you must draw three distinct arrows:
1.  **Weight ($W = mg$):** An arrow pointing vertically downwards from point $M$.
2.  **Normal Force ($N$):** An arrow pointing perpendicular to the slope, upwards and away from the surface.
3.  **Friction ($F_f$):** An arrow pointing up the slope, parallel to the surface, opposing the motion.

*Teacher's Tip:* Ensure the arrows originate from point $M$ and are labeled correctly. The normal force must be at a $90^\circ$ angle to the slope.

#### Part b)
Since Maia is moving at a **constant speed**, the net force acting on her is zero ($a = 0$). This means the component of gravity pulling her down the slope must be exactly balanced by the frictional forces (including air resistance) acting up the slope.

The component of weight acting down the slope is:
$$F_g(\text{parallel}) = mg \sin(\theta)$$

Given: $m = 65\text{ kg}$, $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$, and $\theta = 5^\circ$.
$$F_f = 65 \times 9.8 \times \sin(5^\circ)$$
$$F_f \approx 65 \times 9.8 \times 0.08715$$
$$F_f \approx 55.54\text{ N}$$

Rounding to two significant figures (consistent with the data provided), the frictional force is **$56\text{ N}$**.

#### Part c)
*   **Momentum:** Momentum is a vector quantity ($p = mv$). When Maia grabs the pole, the pole exerts an external impulse on the system. This impulse changes Maia's momentum to zero. By Newton's Third Law, Maia exerts an equal and opposite force on the pole, transferring her momentum to the pole (and subsequently to the Earth, to which the pole is anchored).
*   **Kinetic Energy:** Kinetic energy ($E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) is a scalar quantity. As she stops, her kinetic energy is not "transferred" like momentum; instead, it is **transformed** (dissipated) into other forms of energy, primarily thermal energy (heat) in her hands, the pole, and the bearings of the skateboard due to the work done by the braking forces.
