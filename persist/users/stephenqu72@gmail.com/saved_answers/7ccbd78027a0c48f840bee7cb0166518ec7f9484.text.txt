To solve this problem, we need to consider the physical properties of waves as they encounter obstacles, specifically focusing on the phenomenon of **diffraction**.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
1.  **Understanding Diffraction:** Diffraction is the bending of waves around the corners of an obstacle or through an aperture. The extent to which a wave diffracts depends on the ratio of the wavelength ($\lambda$) to the size of the obstacle ($w$). Significant diffraction occurs when the wavelength is comparable to the size of the obstacle.
2.  **Comparing Sound and Light:**
    *   **Sound waves** have relatively long wavelengths (typically ranging from a few centimeters to several meters for audible sound). These wavelengths are often comparable in size to building corners, doors, or windows, allowing sound to "bend" effectively around corners.
    *   **Light waves** have extremely short wavelengths (on the order of $400$–$700$ nm). Because their wavelengths are so much smaller than the physical dimensions of buildings, they undergo negligible diffraction at a building corner. They travel primarily in straight lines (rectilinear propagation).
3.  **Why B is correct:** Because light waves have a much shorter wavelength than sound waves, they do not diffract significantly around the corner of the building, meaning they cannot reach the person's eyes. Sound waves, having longer wavelengths, diffract significantly around the corner, reaching the person's ears. Therefore, the difference in their ability to diffract is why the person can hear the car but cannot see it.

*   **Why others are incorrect:** 
    *   **A:** While true that light is faster, speed is not the primary mechanism that allows waves to "turn corners." 
    *   **C:** Refraction involves the bending of waves due to a change in medium, which is not the primary factor here. 
    *   **D:** While light is a transverse wave, its transverse nature does not explain its inability to diffract around large obstacles compared to sound.
