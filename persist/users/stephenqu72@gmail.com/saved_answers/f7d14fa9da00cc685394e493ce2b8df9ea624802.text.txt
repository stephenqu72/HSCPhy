To calculate the displacement of the projectile 5 seconds after launch, we need to determine its horizontal ($x$) and vertical ($y$) positions relative to the launch point at $t = 5\text{ s}$.

### 1. Identify the Components of Initial Velocity
The initial velocity $u = 40\text{ ms}^{-1}$ at an angle $\theta = 60^\circ$ is split into horizontal and vertical components:
- $u_x = u \cos(60^\circ) = 40 \times 0.5 = 20\text{ ms}^{-1}$
- $u_y = u \sin(60^\circ) = 40 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 34.64\text{ ms}^{-1}$

### 2. Calculate Horizontal Displacement ($\Delta x$)
Since there is no air resistance, horizontal velocity is constant:
$$\Delta x = u_x \times t = 20 \times 5 = 100\text{ m}$$

### 3. Calculate Vertical Displacement ($\Delta y$)
We use the kinematic equation $\Delta y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2} a_y t^2$, where $a_y = -9.8\text{ ms}^{-2}$ (taking upward as positive):
$$\Delta y = (34.64 \times 5) + (0.5 \times -9.8 \times 5^2)$$
$$\Delta y = 173.2 - 122.5 = 50.7\text{ m}$$
*(Note: Since we are asked for displacement from the **launch point**, we use the displacement formula directly, which accounts for the path taken relative to the start; the 80m cliff height is irrelevant to the displacement relative to the launch point.)*

### 4. Calculate Resultant Displacement ($s$)
The total displacement is the vector sum of $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$:
$$s = \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2}$$
$$s = \sqrt{100^2 + 50.7^2} = \sqrt{10000 + 2570.49}$$
$$s \approx \sqrt{12570.49} \approx 112.12\text{ m}$$

To find the angle $\phi$ below/above the horizontal:
$$\tan(\phi) = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{50.7}{100} = 0.507$$
$$\phi = \arctan(0.507) \approx 26.89^\circ$$

**Answer:** The displacement is $112.12\text{ m}$ at an angle of $26.89^\circ$ above the horizontal.

### How to solve:
1. **Resolve Velocity:** Always start by splitting the initial velocity into horizontal and vertical components using trigonometry.
2. **Horizontal Motion:** Recognize that in standard projectile motion, $v_x$ is constant because there is no horizontal acceleration.
3. **Vertical Motion:** Use the vertical displacement equation. Remember that "displacement from launch point" means the net change in position, so do not add the cliff height to the displacement calculation unless the question asks for the height relative to the ground.
4. **Pythagorean Theorem:** Combine the horizontal and vertical displacements to find the magnitude of the straight-line displacement.
