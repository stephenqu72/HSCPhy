### Explanation
To understand why increasing the distance between the mirrors is the most effective adjustment, let's look at the physics behind Foucault’s experiment.

1.  **The Relationship:** The light travels from the rotating mirror to the fixed mirror and back. Let $d$ be the distance between the mirrors. The total distance traveled by the light is $2d$.
2.  **Time Elapsed:** The time $t$ taken for the light to travel this distance is:
    $$t = \frac{2d}{c}$$
    where $c$ is the speed of light.
3.  **Angle of Rotation:** During this time $t$, the mirror rotates at an angular velocity $\omega$. The angle $\theta$ through which the mirror rotates is:
    $$\theta = \omega t$$
    Substituting the expression for $t$:
    $$\theta = \omega \left( \frac{2d}{c} \right)$$
4.  **Reducing Uncertainty:** The question states that a smaller measured angle leads to more uncertainty. To reduce uncertainty, we need to **increase the value of $\theta$**.
    *   Looking at the formula $\theta = \frac{2\omega d}{c}$, we see that $\theta$ is directly proportional to both the angular velocity $\omega$ and the distance $d$.
    *   While increasing $\omega$ (Option C) would theoretically increase the angle, it is practically very difficult to increase the rotational speed of a physical mirror without causing it to shatter or become unstable.
    *   **Increasing the distance $d$** (Option A) provides a direct, stable, and linear way to increase the time the light spends in transit, which directly results in a larger, more measurable angle $\theta$ without requiring extreme mechanical engineering to push the limits of the mirror's rotational frequency.

Therefore, increasing the distance between the mirrors is the most practical and effective adjustment to increase the angle and reduce measurement uncertainty.
