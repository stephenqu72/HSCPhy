### Answer

The atomic model evolved through a process of scientific refinement, where each scientist addressed the failures of their predecessor’s model.

**1. Rutherford’s Nuclear Model:**
Rutherford’s 1909 gold foil experiment disproved the "plum pudding" model. He discovered the tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus, proposing that electrons orbit the nucleus like planets. 
*   **Contribution:** Established the existence of the nucleus and empty space within the atom.
*   **Limitation:** Classically, an orbiting electron is an accelerating charge and should radiate energy, causing it to spiral into the nucleus. Rutherford’s model could not explain the stability of the atom or the discrete emission spectra of elements.

**2. Bohr’s Quantized Model:**
Bohr applied Planck’s quantum theory to Rutherford’s model to resolve the stability issue. He postulated that electrons exist in stationary, quantized energy levels ($n=1, 2, 3...$) where they do not radiate energy.
*   **Contribution:** Successfully explained the stability of the atom and predicted the spectral lines of hydrogen using the formula $E = hf$.
*   **Limitation:** Bohr’s model failed for multi-electron atoms and could not explain the fine structure of spectral lines or the intensity of transitions. It incorrectly treated electrons as particles in defined circular paths.

**3. Schrodinger’s Quantum Mechanical Model:**
Schrodinger moved away from the "orbit" concept, proposing that electrons behave as waves. He developed the wave equation, $\hat{H}\psi = E\psi$, which defines an electron’s probability of existence in a region of space known as an **orbital**.
*   **Contribution:** This model replaced deterministic orbits with probabilistic distributions (electron clouds). It accounts for the wave-particle duality of matter and explains the complex spectral data of all atoms by using quantum numbers ($n, l, m_l, m_s$) to describe electron density. 
*   **Assessment:** Schrodinger’s contribution is the most significant for modern chemistry and physics, as it provides the most accurate mathematical description of atomic behavior, successfully bridging the gap between classical physics and quantum mechanics.
