To answer this question effectively, we must apply our knowledge of the **Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram** and stellar evolution. In HSC Physics, questions about "star P" usually refer to a star moving off the Main Sequence.

### Answer
As star $P$ evolves off the Main Sequence (e.g., towards the Red Giant branch), the star's surface temperature will **decrease**. Consequently, the absorption spectrum will change in the following ways:
1.  **Shift in Peak Intensity:** According to Wien’s Law ($\lambda_{peak} = \frac{b}{T}$), as the temperature ($T$) decreases, the peak wavelength ($\lambda_{peak}$) of the continuous spectrum will shift toward the **red/longer wavelength end** of the spectrum.
2.  **Changes in Absorption Lines:** The intensities of specific absorption lines will change. As the star cools, the ionization states of the elements in its atmosphere change. For example, hydrogen Balmer lines may weaken, while lines corresponding to heavier elements, molecules (like $TiO$), or neutral metals may become more prominent as the lower temperature allows these species to exist in the stellar atmosphere.

### How to solve this
1.  **Identify the Stage:** Recognize that when a star leaves the Main Sequence, it typically expands and cools, becoming a Giant or Supergiant.
2.  **Apply Wien’s Displacement Law:** Use the relationship $\lambda_{peak} \propto \frac{1}{T}$. Explain that a cooling surface means the blackbody radiation curve shifts right (towards red).
3.  **Link to Spectroscopy:** Remember that absorption lines are temperature-dependent (Saha-Boltzmann ionization). A cooler star has a different "spectral type" (e.g., shifting from type B or A toward G, K, or M). Therefore, you must mention that the *composition* of the visible absorption lines changes because different energy levels are being populated at the new surface temperature.
