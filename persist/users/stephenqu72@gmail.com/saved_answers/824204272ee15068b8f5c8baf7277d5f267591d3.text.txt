### Part (a): Finding the slit spacing ($d$)
**Answer:**
The slit spacing $d$ is the reciprocal of the number of lines per unit length.
$$d = \frac{1}{1250 \text{ lines/mm}} = 0.0008 \text{ mm} = 8.0 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} = 800 \text{ nm}$$

**Explanation:**
The grating has 1250 lines in every millimeter. The distance $d$ between the center of one slit and the next is simply the total length (1 mm) divided by the number of lines ($N$). Always convert your result to standard SI units (meters) to ensure your calculations in later parts remain consistent.

### Part (b): Finding the wavelength ($\lambda$)
**Answer:**
Using the diffraction grating formula $d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$, where $n=1$, $\theta = 16^\circ$, and $d = 8 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$:
$$\lambda = \frac{d \sin(\theta)}{n}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{8 \times 10^{-7} \times \sin(16^\circ)}{1}$$
$$\lambda \approx 2.205 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} \approx 220.5 \text{ nm}$$

**Explanation:**
The formula $d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$ represents the condition for constructive interference. Here, $n$ is the order of the maximum. Since we are looking at the "first-order" maximum, we set $n=1$. Plug in your $d$ value from part (a) and the provided angle. Make sure your calculator is in **Degree mode**.

### Part (c): Finding the angle ($\theta$) for the second-order maximum
**Answer:**
We use the same formula $d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$, but solve for $\theta$. Here $n=2$, $\lambda = 375 \text{ nm} = 3.75 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$:
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{n\lambda}{d}$$
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{2 \times 3.75 \times 10^{-7}}{8 \times 10^{-7}}$$
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{7.5 \times 10^{-7}}{8 \times 10^{-7}} = 0.9375$$
$$\theta = \arcsin(0.9375) \approx 69.64^\circ$$

**Explanation:**
Rearrange the formula to solve for the unknown angle by using the inverse sine function ($\sin^{-1}$). By inputting the values for $n=2$ and the new wavelength, you find the angular position where the second-order interference pattern appears.
