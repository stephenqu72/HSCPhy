To answer this question effectively for an HSC-level Physics exam, you need to connect the concepts of thermal energy, particle motion, and the emission of electromagnetic radiation (blackbody radiation).

### Answer
When a metal object is heated, the internal thermal energy of the object increases. This causes the atoms within the metal lattice to oscillate or vibrate with greater amplitude. According to the principles of classical and quantum physics, these accelerating charged particles (electrons within the atoms) emit electromagnetic radiation. 

As the temperature of the object increases, the distribution of emitted wavelengths shifts according to Wien’s Displacement Law ($ \lambda_{max} \propto 1/T $). At lower temperatures, the radiation is primarily in the infrared spectrum, which is invisible to the human eye. As the temperature rises, the peak of the emission spectrum shifts towards shorter wavelengths. When the object reaches a sufficient temperature (approximately $800-1000 K$), a significant portion of the emitted radiation enters the visible spectrum, specifically at the long-wavelength end (the red portion of the visible spectrum), causing the metal to appear to "glow red."

### How to solve this (HSC Exam Strategy)
To secure all 4 marks, ensure your response follows this logical structure:
1.  **Link temperature to atomic motion:** Mention that heating increases the kinetic energy of particles (vibrations).
2.  **Explain the emission of radiation:** Explain that the acceleration of charged particles produces electromagnetic radiation.
3.  **Reference the shift in wavelength:** Mention the change in the intensity-wavelength distribution (Blackbody radiation).
4.  **Connect to the visible spectrum:** Explicitly state that as temperature increases, the peak intensity shifts from the infrared into the visible red range.

**Key concepts to include for full marks:**
*   Oscillating charges produce electromagnetic waves.
*   The relationship between temperature and the peak wavelength of emitted light.
*   The transition from invisible infrared to visible red light.
