### Explanation

To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental difference between the physical processes that produce incandescent light and laser light:

1.  **Incandescent Light:** This is produced by thermal radiation from a hot solid (such as a tungsten filament). Because the atoms in the solid are constantly colliding and vibrating with a wide range of thermal energies, they emit photons across a broad, continuous range of wavelengths. This is described by the blackbody radiation curve, which is a **continuous spectrum**.

2.  **Laser Light:** The word LASER stands for "Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation." This process involves stimulated emission, where electrons drop between two specific energy levels in an atom (or quantum system). Because these energy levels are discrete and well-defined, the energy of the emitted photons, $E = hf$, is restricted to a very specific frequency. Consequently, laser light is monochromatic, meaning it has a **very narrow frequency range** (a sharp spectral line).

**Analysis of the options:**
*   **A is correct:** It accurately identifies that incandescent light is continuous and laser light is restricted to a narrow frequency range (monochromatic).
*   **B is incorrect:** Laser light does not produce a continuous spectrum, and incandescent light is not inherently concentrated in the yellow region.
*   **C is incorrect:** It incorrectly claims laser light has a wide spectrum and incandescent light has a narrow one, which is the exact opposite of the physical reality.
*   **D is incorrect:** It ignores the continuous nature of the broad range of wavelengths emitted by incandescent sources.
