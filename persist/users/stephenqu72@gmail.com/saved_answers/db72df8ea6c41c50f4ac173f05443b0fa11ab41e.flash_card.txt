### Front
In projectile motion (neglecting air resistance), how does the total velocity of an object change throughout its trajectory, and why is it at its maximum just before hitting the ground?

### Back
- **Key idea:** Total velocity is the vector sum of constant horizontal velocity ($v_x$) and changing vertical velocity ($v_y$). As the object falls, gravity increases $|v_y|$, thereby increasing the resultant magnitude ($v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$).
- **Formula / law / constant / figure:** $v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$; $a_y = g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$ (downward).
- **When to use it:** To determine velocity changes or compare speeds at different points along a parabolic path.
- **Common trap:** Assuming velocity is zero at the start (it must have initial horizontal velocity) or assuming acceleration is zero at the peak of the trajectory (it remains $g$ throughout).
