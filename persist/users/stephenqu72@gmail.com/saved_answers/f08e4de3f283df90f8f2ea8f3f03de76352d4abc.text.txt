To solve this problem, we use the physics of circular motion to simulate artificial gravity.

### Part (a)
To simulate gravity on the surface of the Earth, the centripetal acceleration $a_c$ provided by the rotation must equal $g \approx 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$. The formula for centripetal acceleration is:
$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
Given:
- Radius $r = 100\ \text{m}$
- Acceleration $a_c = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$

Rearranging for speed $v$:
$$v = \sqrt{a_c \cdot r}$$
$$v = \sqrt{9.8 \cdot 100}$$
$$v = \sqrt{980}$$
$$v \approx 31.3\ \text{m/s}$$

**Answer:** The space station must rotate at approximately $31.3\ \text{m/s}$.

---

### Part (b)
To determine if there is a weight gain or loss, we compare the new centripetal acceleration to $g$. The acceleration can be expressed in terms of angular velocity $\omega$ as $a_c = r\omega^2$.
- New angular velocity $\omega = 0.3\ \text{rad/s}$
- $r = 100\ \text{m}$

New acceleration:
$$a_c = 100 \cdot (0.3)^2$$
$$a_c = 100 \cdot 0.09 = 9\ \text{m/s}^2$$

Since the required gravity was $9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$, and the new acceleration is only $9\ \text{m/s}^2$, the inhabitants experience less acceleration than standard Earth gravity.

**Answer:** The inhabitants will have an **apparent weight loss**. This is because the new centripetal acceleration ($9\ \text{m/s}^2$) is less than the acceleration due to gravity on Earth ($9.8\ \text{m/s}^2$), resulting in a lower normal force exerted by the floor.
