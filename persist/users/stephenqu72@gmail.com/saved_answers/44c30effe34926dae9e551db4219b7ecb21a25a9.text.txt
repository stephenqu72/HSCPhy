To solve this problem, we must identify the **net force** (or centripetal force) acting on objects moving in circular paths.

### 1. Analysis of the car at the top of the bridge
The car is undergoing circular motion. The net force required to keep it moving in that circle is the centripetal force, defined as:
$$F_{net} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
In terms of the vertical forces acting on the car, there is the downward gravitational force ($mg$) and the upward normal force ($N$) from the bridge. Since the net force must be directed toward the center (downwards), we have:
$$mg - N = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Thus, the net force itself is simply $\frac{mv^2}{r}$.

### 2. Analysis of the satellite in circular orbit
For a satellite, the only force providing the centripetal force is gravity. According to Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation, the force of gravity is:
$$F_g = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
Since this gravitational force acts as the centripetal force for the circular orbit, the net force is:
$$F_{net} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

### Conclusion
*   For the **car**, the net force providing the circular motion is $\frac{mv^2}{r}$.
*   For the **satellite**, the net force providing the circular motion is $\frac{mv^2}{r}$, which is equivalent to $\frac{GMm}{r^2}$.

In circular motion, the net force is by definition the centripetal force, which is $\frac{mv^2}{r}$ in both cases.
