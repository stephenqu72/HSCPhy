### Answer

| Car's motion | Description of back EMF |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Car is switched off** | No back EMF is produced because there is no current flowing and no rotation of the motor armature. |
| **Car is switched on and held in the air** | The motor reaches a high constant speed. Back EMF is produced at its maximum value (approaching the supply voltage), resulting in a low current. |
| **Car is moving on grass** | The load increases as the motor struggles against friction/resistance. The motor slows down, causing the back EMF to decrease. |
| **Car is stationary on grass** | The armature stops rotating entirely. Since back EMF is proportional to speed ($E \propto \omega$), the back EMF becomes zero. |
| **Car is switched off** | No back EMF is produced as the circuit is open and the motor is stationary. |

***

### Explanation
To solve this question, you must understand the relationship between the rotation of a DC motor and the production of back electromotive force (back EMF).

1.  **The Physics Principle:** Back EMF is an induced voltage that opposes the supply voltage. It is generated by the motor acting as a generator while it rotates. Mathematically, the magnitude of back EMF is proportional to the angular velocity of the motor ($\omega$):
    $$E = k\phi\omega$$
    where $k$ is a constant related to the construction of the motor and $\phi$ is the magnetic flux.

2.  **Applying the Physics:**
    *   **No rotation:** If the motor isn't spinning, $\omega = 0$, so $E = 0$.
    *   **High speed (free spinning):** When there is no load, the motor spins very fast. Consequently, the back EMF is at its highest, which opposes the $9\text{ V}$ battery, keeping the current drawn by the motor low.
    *   **Load applied (grass):** When the motor works harder (or eventually stalls), the rotational speed $\omega$ drops. As speed drops, back EMF drops.
    *   **Stalling:** When the axle is caught and the motor stops, rotation ceases. Even though the circuit is connected, there is no induced back EMF. This is why motors often overheat when stalled—without the back EMF to "push back" against the supply, a large current flows through the armature.
