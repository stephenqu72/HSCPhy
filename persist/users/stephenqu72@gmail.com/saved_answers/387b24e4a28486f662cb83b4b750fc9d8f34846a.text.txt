The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we must look at the historical context of blackbody radiation and the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe."

1.  **The Problem:** Classical physics (using the Rayleigh-Jeans law) predicted that as the frequency of light emitted by a blackbody increased, the intensity would increase towards infinity. This meant that an object should emit infinite amounts of ultraviolet and X-ray radiation, which contradicts physical reality—this failure was termed the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe."
2.  **The Solution:** Max Planck solved this by proposing that electromagnetic energy is not continuous. He hypothesized that energy is emitted or absorbed only in discrete packets, or "quanta." The energy of a quantum is given by:
    $$E = nhf$$
    where $n$ is an integer, $h$ is Planck's constant, and $f$ is the frequency.
3.  **Why (C) is correct:** By quantizing energy, Planck showed that at very high frequencies, the energy required to emit a single "quantum" is so large that the probability of emitting radiation at those high frequencies drops to near zero. This effectively "cuts off" the infinite energy output predicted by classical theory.
4.  **Why others are incorrect:**
    *   **(A)** The Aether was a discredited medium for light waves, not related to blackbody radiation.
    *   **(B)** The "work function" is a concept related to the photoelectric effect (Einstein), not the Ultraviolet Catastrophe.
    *   **(D)** Wien's displacement law was an empirical observation that worked for high frequencies but failed for low ones; the catastrophe was not caused by a constant error, but by a fundamental misunderstanding of the nature of energy.
