This question relates to the **Geiger-Marsden experiment** (often referred to as the Rutherford Gold Foil experiment). To secure full marks, you must structure your response to cover the historical context, the empirical findings, and the subsequent theoretical shift.

### Answer
**Background:** 
In 1907, Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden, working under Ernest Rutherford, performed this experiment to test the prevailing "Plum Pudding" model of the atom proposed by J.J. Thomson. The Plum Pudding model suggested that the atom consisted of a diffuse, positively charged "pudding" with negatively charged electrons embedded within it. It was expected that the relatively heavy and fast-moving alpha particles would pass straight through the thin gold foil with minimal deflection.

**Experimental Results:**
The experiment observed that:
1. The vast majority of alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil with little to no deflection.
2. A very small fraction of alpha particles were deflected at large angles.
3. Approximately 1 in 8,000 alpha particles were deflected backward (angles greater than $90^\circ$).

**Proposed Model:**
These results contradicted the Plum Pudding model, leading Rutherford to propose a new model of the atom:
* **The Nuclear Model:** The atom contains a tiny, dense, and positively charged center called the nucleus, which contains almost all of the atom's mass.
* **Empty Space:** The rest of the atom is mostly empty space, through which the electrons orbit or exist at a distance from the nucleus.
* **Electrostatic Repulsion:** Large deflections occur when an alpha particle (positively charged) happens to pass very close to the positively charged nucleus, resulting in a strong electrostatic force of repulsion.
