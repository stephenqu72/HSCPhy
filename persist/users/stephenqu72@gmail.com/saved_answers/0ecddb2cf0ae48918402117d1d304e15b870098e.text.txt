As an HSC teacher, I look for a structured, logical progression in an essay-style response. This question asks for a critical evaluation ("To what extent"), meaning you must show how experimental evidence has driven the evolution of scientific models, rather than just listing facts.

### How to approach this answer
To score full marks (9 marks), you should structure your response chronologically to demonstrate the shift in paradigms:

1.  **Introduction:** Define the historical tension between the particle model (Newton) and the wave model (Huygens). State that experimental evidence has been the primary driver in shifting our understanding towards the current quantum model.
2.  **The Wave Model Triumph:** Discuss how Thomas Young’s Double Slit experiment (1801) provided definitive evidence of interference and diffraction, effectively dismissing Newton’s corpuscular theory for over a century.
3.  **The Crisis (The Particle Model Returns):** Explain how the wave model failed to explain the Photoelectric Effect. Describe how Einstein used Planck’s quanta idea to show light interacts as discrete "packets" (photons). The equation $E = hf$ is essential here.
4.  **Synthesis:** Conclude that neither model is complete on its own. Experimental evidence forced the acceptance of "wave-particle duality," as light exhibits wave properties during propagation (diffraction/interference) and particle properties during interaction (photoelectric effect/Compton scattering).

---

### Sample Answer Outline
The history of physics reveals that our understanding of light is entirely dependent on experimental evidence, which has repeatedly challenged and refined established theoretical models.

**1. The Wave Theory:** Initially, the debate focused on whether light was a stream of particles or waves. Newton championed the particle model, but it could not easily explain diffraction. Young’s Double Slit experiment provided empirical evidence of interference patterns ($d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$), which could only be explained if light behaved as a wave. This led to the dominance of the wave theory, further supported by Maxwell’s equations, which identified light as an electromagnetic wave.

**2. The Failure of Classical Physics:** By the late 19th century, the wave model could not explain the Photoelectric Effect. According to wave theory, increasing intensity should increase electron kinetic energy, yet experiments showed that energy was dependent on frequency, not intensity. Einstein’s proposal—that light energy is quantized into photons where $E = hf$—perfectly matched the experimental results, reviving the particle concept.

**3. The Modern Synthesis:** The experimental evidence for both phenomena necessitated a paradigm shift. We now understand light through the principle of wave-particle duality. Light propagates as an electromagnetic wave, but interacts with matter as a localized particle. 

**Conclusion:** Our understanding has been shaped entirely by experimental evidence. Each time a model reached its limits, experimental data forced physicists to abandon simplistic views in favour of the more complex, nuanced framework of quantum mechanics. The "extent" to which our understanding has been influenced is total; without the experimental anomalies of the 19th and 20th centuries, we would still be restricted to classical, incomplete theories.
