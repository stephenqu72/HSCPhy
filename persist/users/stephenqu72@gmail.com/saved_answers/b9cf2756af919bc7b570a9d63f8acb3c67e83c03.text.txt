To determine the energy produced by a star from its mass loss, we use Albert Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, defined by the formula:

$$E = mc^2$$

Where:
- $E$ is the energy in Joules (J)
- $m$ is the mass in kilograms (kg)
- $c$ is the speed of light, approximately $3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ ms}^{-1}$

### Step-by-step Solution:

1.  **Identify the given information:**
    The rate of mass loss ($\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t}$) is $4.0 \times 10^9 \text{ kgs}^{-1}$. We want to find the energy produced per second, which is the power ($P = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}$).

2.  **Set up the equation:**
    Since $E = mc^2$, the rate of energy production per second is:
    $$\frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t} \times c^2$$

3.  **Substitute the values:**
    $$\text{Energy per second} = (4.0 \times 10^9 \text{ kg}) \times (3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})^2$$
    $$\text{Energy per second} = (4.0 \times 10^9) \times (9.0 \times 10^{16})$$
    $$\text{Energy per second} = 36 \times 10^{25} \text{ J/s}$$

4.  **Convert to standard scientific notation:**
    $$36 \times 10^{25} = 3.6 \times 10^{26} \text{ J}$$
