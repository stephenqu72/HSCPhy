### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Calculation:**
1.  **Identify the total distance:** The light must travel from Galileo to the assistant and back to Galileo. Since the distance between them is $1\text{ km}$, the total distance $d$ is:
    $$d = 1\text{ km} + 1\text{ km} = 2\text{ km} = 2000\text{ m}$$
2.  **Identify the time:** Galileo’s measured time is dominated by the human reaction time. Since there are two human reactions (the assistant seeing the light and opening their lantern, and Galileo seeing the assistant's light and stopping his timer), the total time interval $t$ is:
    $$t = 0.25\text{ s} + 0.25\text{ s} = 0.50\text{ s}$$
3.  **Calculate speed:** Using $v = \frac{d}{t}$:
    $$v = \frac{2000\text{ m}}{0.50\text{ s}} = 4000\text{ m/s} = 4\text{ km/s}$$

**Conclusion:** The fastest speed Galileo could have measured is $4\text{ km/s}$.

---

#### Part (b)
**Evaluation:**
To evaluate the accuracy, we compare the calculated value to the accepted speed of light ($c \approx 3 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$ or $300,000\text{ km/s}$).

*   **Significant Error:** Galileo’s result of $4\text{ km/s}$ is extremely inaccurate, differing from the actual speed of light by several orders of magnitude. 
*   **Limitation of Method:** The primary limitation is that the human reaction time is millions of times larger than the time it actually takes for light to traverse $2\text{ km}$ ($t = \frac{2000}{3 \times 10^8} \approx 6.7 \times 10^{-6}\text{ s}$). 
*   **Verdict:** Because the reaction time of the observers is so slow compared to the speed of light, this method is fundamentally incapable of measuring light speed. It instead measures the latency of the human nervous system.
