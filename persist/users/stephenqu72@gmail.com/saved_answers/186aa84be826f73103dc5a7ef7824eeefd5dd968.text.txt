To solve this problem, we must apply the physics of radioactive decay. The decay law is expressed as:
$$N_t = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$
Taking the natural logarithm of both sides gives the linear form:
$$\ln\left(\frac{N_t}{N_0}\right) = -\lambda t$$
This is in the form of $y = mx + c$, where $y = \ln(N_t/N_0)$, $x = t$ (time), and the gradient $m = -\lambda$.

### Part (b): Determine the decay constant ($\lambda$)
1. **Calculate the gradient:** Choose two points on your line of best fit, $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$. The gradient is:
   $$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
   Using the data points $(2, 0)$ and $(24, -1.10)$:
   $$m = \frac{-1.10 - 0}{24 - 2} = \frac{-1.10}{22} \approx -0.05 \text{ s}^{-1}$$
2. **Relate to $\lambda$:** Since $m = -\lambda$, we have:
   $$-\lambda = -0.05$$
   $$\lambda = 0.05 \text{ s}^{-1}$$

**Explanation:**
The decay constant $\lambda$ represents the probability per unit time that a nucleus will decay. By transforming the exponential decay equation into a linear equation, we can determine $\lambda$ by finding the negative of the slope of the line.
