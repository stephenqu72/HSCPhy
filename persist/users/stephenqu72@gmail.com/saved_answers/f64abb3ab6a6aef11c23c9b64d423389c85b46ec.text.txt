To find the energy of a photon, we use the Planck-Einstein relation:

$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

### Given data:
*   Wavelength ($\lambda$) = $475\text{ nm} = 475 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}$
*   Planck’s constant ($h$) $\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
*   Speed of light ($c$) $\approx 3.00 \times 10^{8}\text{ m/s}$

### Calculation steps:
1.  **Substitute the values into the equation:**
    $$E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J}\cdot\text{s}) \times (3.00 \times 10^{8}\text{ m/s})}{475 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}}$$

2.  **Calculate the numerator:**
    $$(6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.00 \times 10^{8}) \approx 1.9878 \times 10^{-25}$$

3.  **Divide by the wavelength:**
    $$E = \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{475 \times 10^{-9}}$$
    $$E \approx 4.1848 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$$

Rounding to three significant figures, we get $4.18 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$.

Comparing this to the given options, the correct answer is **(B)**.

### How to solve:
1.  **Identify the constants:** Always ensure your units match. Since the wavelength is in nanometers (nm), convert it to meters (m) by multiplying by $10^{-9}$.
2.  **Apply the formula:** Remember that energy is inversely proportional to wavelength. If you have frequency ($f$) instead, you would use $E = hf$.
3.  **Check your order of magnitude:** A single photon of visible light should typically have an energy in the range of $10^{-19}$ Joules. Options (C) and (D) are physically impossible for a single photon, and (A) is far too large, which serves as a good reality check for your calculation.
