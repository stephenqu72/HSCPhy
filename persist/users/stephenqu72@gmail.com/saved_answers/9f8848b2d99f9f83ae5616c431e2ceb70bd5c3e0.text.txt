### Answer
The correct answer is **(C) the wavefunction squared**.

### Explanation
In quantum mechanics, the state of an electron in a hydrogen atom is described by a mathematical function known as the **wavefunction**, denoted by the Greek letter psi ($\psi$). 

1.  **The Nature of the Wavefunction ($\psi$):** The wavefunction itself does not represent a physical observable. It can contain complex numbers and its value at a specific point in space does not directly correspond to a physical quantity like "likelihood."
2.  **The Born Interpretation:** Max Born proposed that the physical significance of the wavefunction lies in its square. Specifically, the probability density of finding an electron at a specific location is given by the square of the absolute value of the wavefunction, represented as $|\psi|^2$.
3.  **Why (C) is the answer:** In the context of the Schrödinger model of the atom, the "likelihood" or probability density of locating an electron within a given volume of space is directly proportional to $|\psi|^2$. This is often referred to as the "wavefunction squared." 

**Distinction from other options:**
*   Option (A) is incorrect because $\psi$ can be negative or complex, while probability must be a real, positive number.
*   Option (B) is conceptually vague, as the amplitude *is* the value of the wavefunction.
*   Option (D) is redundant or potentially misleading terminology, as $|\psi|^2$ is the standard definition of the probability density.
