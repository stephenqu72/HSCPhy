The correct answer is **D**.

### Explanation
To understand why quantised energy levels exist in atoms, we look to the De Broglie hypothesis and the Bohr model of the atom.

1.  **De Broglie Hypothesis:** Louis de Broglie proposed that matter, including electrons, exhibits wave-like properties. The wavelength $\lambda$ of an electron is given by $\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$, where $h$ is Planck's constant, $m$ is the mass, and $v$ is the velocity.
2.  **Standing Waves:** When an electron moves around the nucleus, it can be thought of as a wave confined to a circular orbit. For the wave to be stable (not interfere with itself destructively), it must form a **standing wave**. 
3.  **Quantisation:** A standing wave can only exist if the circumference of the orbit is an integer multiple of the wavelength ($2\pi r = n\lambda$, where $n = 1, 2, 3...$). Because only specific wavelengths are allowed to form these stable standing waves, the electron's momentum and, consequently, its energy, are restricted to discrete (quantised) values.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** Classical particle physics cannot explain why energy is quantised; it assumes electrons can have any amount of energy.
*   **B:** This is a vague description that does not identify the underlying physical mechanism (the wave nature of matter).
*   **C:** While electrons do diffract, energy levels are not "diffraction patterns." They are stable standing wave states.
