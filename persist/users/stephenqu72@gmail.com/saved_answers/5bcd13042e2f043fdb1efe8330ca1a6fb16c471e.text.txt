### Answer

**(a)** According to Newton’s second law of motion, the momentum $p$ of an object is given by the formula $p = mv$, where $m$ is mass and $v$ is velocity. The graph plots $p$ on the y-axis and $v$ on the x-axis. Comparing this to the linear equation $y = mx + c$ (where $c=0$), the gradient is the ratio $\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta v}$. Since $p = mv$, the gradient is $\frac{mv}{v} = m$. Therefore, for the Newtonian model, the slope of the line is equivalent to the constant mass of the object.

**(b)** The differences arise from the fundamental assumptions of each theory regarding the nature of mass and the speed limit of the universe:

*   **Newtonian Mechanics:** Assumes mass is an invariant scalar quantity. As an object's velocity increases, its momentum increases linearly ($p = mv$). This model does not account for a universal speed limit, so it incorrectly predicts that momentum can increase infinitely as velocity increases beyond $c$.
*   **Special Relativity:** Einstein’s theory introduces the Lorentz factor, $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$. Relativistic momentum is defined as $p = \gamma mv = \frac{mv}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$. As the velocity $v$ approaches the speed of light $c$, the denominator approaches zero, causing the momentum $p$ to approach infinity. This explains the vertical asymptote at $v = c$ in the graph, confirming that it is impossible for an object with mass to reach or exceed the speed of light.
