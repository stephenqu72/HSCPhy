To solve this problem, we need to understand the relationship between **binding energy**, **mass defect**, and **nuclear stability**.

### 1. Analysis of Binding Energy
The binding energy of a nucleus is defined as the energy required to completely separate the nucleus into its individual protons and neutrons (nucleons).
*   Binding energy of He-4 = $28.3\text{ MeV}$
*   Binding energy of Be-6 = $26.9\text{ MeV}$

Since $28.3 > 26.9$, He-4 requires **more energy** to be separated into its individual nucleons than Be-6.

### 2. Analysis of Mass and Binding Energy
According to Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$, a system with higher binding energy has a larger **mass defect** ($\Delta m$). The mass of a nucleus ($M_{nucleus}$) is related to the mass of its individual constituents ($\sum m_{nucleons}$) by the following equation:
$$M_{nucleus} = \sum m_{nucleons} - \frac{\text{Binding Energy}}{c^2}$$

A **higher** binding energy means more mass is "lost" as energy when the nucleus is formed (greater mass defect). Consequently, the nucleus with the higher binding energy will have a **smaller** actual mass compared to a nucleus with lower binding energy, assuming the total number of nucleons is comparable or considering the specific mass-energy states.

More simply: He-4 ($28.3\text{ MeV}$) is more tightly bound than Be-6 ($26.9\text{ MeV}$). Because He-4 releases more energy upon formation, it has a larger mass defect, making it **less massive** than Be-6.

### 3. Conclusion
*   He-4 requires **more energy** to separate into individual protons and neutrons.
*   He-4 is **less massive** than Be-6.
