To solve this physics problem, we use the principles of Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation and the definition of a gravitational field.

### Part (a)
The magnitude of the gravitational field strength $g$ at a distance $r$ from a mass $M$ is given by:
$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$
where $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$.

1. **Calculate the field from Jupiter ($g_J$):**
   $$g_J = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(1.9 \times 10^{27})}{(4.2 \times 10^{11})^2} \approx 7.18 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{N/kg}$$

2. **Calculate the field from Saturn ($g_S$):**
   $$g_S = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.7 \times 10^{26})}{(3.9 \times 10^{11})^2} \approx 2.50 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{N/kg}$$

3. **Total Gravitational Field Strength:**
   Since the probe is between the two planets, the vectors point in opposite directions. The net magnitude is the difference:
   $$g_{net} = |g_J - g_S| = |7.18 \times 10^{-7} - 2.50 \times 10^{-7}| = 4.68 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{N/kg}$$

### Part (b)
Since $g_J > g_S$ ($7.18 \times 10^{-7} > 2.50 \times 10^{-7}$), the net gravitational field vector points toward the planet with the greater pull, which is **Jupiter**. On your figure, you would draw an arrow pointing from the Cassini probe towards Jupiter.

### Part (c)
A 'stationary' orbit (geostationary equivalent for Saturn) requires the orbital period to match the rotation period of the planet.
Given the Saturn day is $10.7$ hours:
$$\text{Period} = 10.7 \, \text{hours} \times 60 \, \text{minutes/hour} \times 60 \, \text{seconds/minute}$$
$$\text{Period} = 38,520 \, \text{seconds}$$
