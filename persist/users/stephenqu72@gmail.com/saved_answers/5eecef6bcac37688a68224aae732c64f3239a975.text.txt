### Explanation
To determine the correct answer, we must analyze the physics of the CNO cycle as presented in the diagram and known in stellar nucleosynthesis:

1.  **Analyze the Physics:** The CNO cycle (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle) is a catalytic process where Carbon, Nitrogen, and Oxygen nuclei facilitate the fusion of four hydrogen nuclei (protons) into one helium-4 nucleus.
2.  **Evaluate Option (A):** While the CNO cycle is highly efficient, "releasing more energy" is not the reason it is the "likely process." In fact, the total energy released from fusing four protons to one helium nucleus is the same regardless of whether the proton-proton chain or the CNO cycle is used.
3.  **Evaluate Option (B):** This is factually incorrect. The CNO cycle is dominant in stars **more massive** than the Sun (where core temperatures exceed roughly 17 million Kelvin), not small mass stars.
4.  **Evaluate Option (C):** This is false. The CNO cycle is a classic example of a sequence of nuclear fusion reactions involving beta-plus decay and gamma-ray emission.
5.  **Evaluate Option (D):** This is the correct physical description. At every step in the cycle shown in the diagram—such as $^{12}\text{C} + ^{1}\text{H} \rightarrow ^{13}\text{N} + \gamma$—the process involves the interaction between a nucleus and a single proton. Because the density of the stellar core is manageable and the probability of three-body collisions is astronomically low, fusion occurs via binary collisions (two particles colliding at any given time). This allows the reaction to proceed in discrete, manageable stages.
