To solve this physics problem, we need to apply the principle of **Conservation of Mechanical Energy** and the requirements for **Circular Motion**.

### Step 1: Analyze Circular Motion at the Top of the Loop
To safely perform the stunt, the rider must maintain contact with the track at the very top of the loop. This means the centripetal force is provided solely by gravity at the critical minimum speed (where the normal force $F_N \approx 0$).

The condition for the minimum speed $v$ at the top of the loop (radius $r = 4\text{ m}$) is:
$$mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Solving for $v^2$:
$$v^2 = gr$$

### Step 2: Use Conservation of Energy
The total energy at the platform height ($h$) must equal the total energy at the top of the loop.
*   **At the platform:** The system has gravitational potential energy ($mgh$). (Kinetic energy is $0$ as it starts from rest).
*   **At the top of the loop:** The system has kinetic energy ($\frac{1}{2}mv^2$) and potential energy ($mg \cdot 2r$ because the height is the diameter).

Setting them equal:
$$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mg(2r)$$
*Note: The mass $m = 80\text{ kg} + 5\text{ kg} = 85\text{ kg}$ cancels out.*

Divide by $mg$:
$$h = \frac{v^2}{2g} + 2r$$

### Step 3: Substitute and Calculate
Substitute $v^2 = gr$ into the equation:
$$h = \frac{gr}{2g} + 2r$$
$$h = \frac{r}{2} + 2r = 2.5r$$

Given $r = 4\text{ m}$:
$$h = 2.5 \times 4\text{ m}$$
$$h = 10\text{ m}$$

**Answer:** The minimum platform height is **$10\text{ m}$**.

***

### Explanation of the approach:
1.  **Identify the physics principles:** We use circular motion to determine the minimum speed required to stay on the track and the Law of Conservation of Energy to link the starting height to that speed.
2.  **Recognize the "critical condition":** The rider must have just enough speed at the top of the loop so that gravity provides the necessary centripetal force.
3.  **Simplify:** Notice that the mass of the rider and bike cancels out in the energy equations, meaning the final answer is independent of the total mass.
4.  **Final Calculation:** By deriving the ratio $h = 2.5r$, we easily arrive at $10\text{ m}$ by multiplying the given radius by $2.5$.
