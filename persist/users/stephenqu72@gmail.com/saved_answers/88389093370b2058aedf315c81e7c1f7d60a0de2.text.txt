To determine how the gravitational potential energy changes, we must look at the formula for gravitational potential energy ($U$) between two masses:

$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$

where:
- $G$ is the gravitational constant
- $M$ is the mass of the planet
- $m$ is the mass of the satellite
- $r$ is the distance between the centers of the masses

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Initial State:** The satellite is at distance $r$. Its initial potential energy is:
    $$U_1 = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$

2.  **Final State ($X$):** The diagram shows $X$ is at a distance of $2r$ from the center of the planet. Therefore, its new potential energy is:
    $$U_2 = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$

3.  **Calculate the change:** Comparing $U_2$ to $U_1$:
    $$U_2 = \frac{1}{2} \left( -\frac{GMm}{r} \right) = \frac{1}{2} U_1$$

4.  **Interpret the result:** Gravitational potential energy is negative. By moving the satellite from $r$ to $2r$, the value moves from a "larger" negative number (e.g., $-1$) to a "smaller" negative number (e.g., $-0.5$). 
    In physics, moving from a more negative value to a less negative value (closer to zero) is an **increase** in potential energy. Mathematically, the magnitude of the potential energy is halved, but because it is negative, dividing by 2 makes the value "less negative" (closer to 0), which is an increase.
