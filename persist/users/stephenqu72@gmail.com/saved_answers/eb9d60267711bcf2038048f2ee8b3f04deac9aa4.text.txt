### Explanation
This question tests your understanding of one of the fundamental postulates of Einstein's **Special Theory of Relativity**.

1.  **The Second Postulate:** Albert Einstein proposed that the speed of light in a vacuum, denoted as $c$ (approximately $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$), is a universal constant. This means that $c$ is the same for all inertial observers, regardless of their state of motion or the state of motion of the light source.
2.  **Evaluating the options:** 
    *   **Option A** is correct because it directly states the Principle of the Constancy of the Speed of Light. No matter how fast a source is moving toward or away from you, or how fast you are moving toward or away from the source, you will always measure the speed of light in a vacuum to be exactly $c$.
    *   **Options B, C, and D** all suggest that the speed of light can change based on relative motion (Galilean relativity). While the *frequency* and *wavelength* of the light may change due to the Doppler effect, the *speed* of the light remains constant.

In the context of the HSC Physics syllabus (Module 8: From the Universe to the Atom), remembering that the constancy of the speed of light is a postulate that defies classical (Galilean) addition of velocities is crucial for answering questions about special relativity.
