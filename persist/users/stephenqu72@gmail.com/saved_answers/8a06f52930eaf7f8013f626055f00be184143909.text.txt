To find the gravitational force acting on the satellite, we recognise that in a stable circular orbit, the **gravitational force** provides the necessary **centripetal force** required to keep the satellite in motion.

### 1. Identify the given variables:
*   Mass of the satellite, $m = 3.50 \times 10^3\text{ kg}$
*   Orbital radius, $r = 9.17 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
*   Orbital period, $T = 1.50\text{ hours} = 1.50 \times 3600\text{ s} = 5400\text{ s}$

### 2. The Physics Principle:
The centripetal force formula is:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Since $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, we can substitute this into the formula:
$$F_c = \frac{m(\frac{2\pi r}{T})^2}{r} = \frac{m \cdot 4\pi^2 r^2}{T^2 \cdot r} = \frac{4\pi^2 mr}{T^2}$$

### 3. Calculation:
Substitute the known values into the equation:
$$F = \frac{4 \cdot \pi^2 \cdot (3.50 \times 10^3) \cdot (9.17 \times 10^6)}{(5400)^2}$$

First, calculate the numerator:
$$4 \cdot \pi^2 \cdot 3.50 \times 10^3 \cdot 9.17 \times 10^6 \approx 1.268 \times 10^{12}$$

Next, calculate the denominator:
$$(5400)^2 = 2.916 \times 10^7$$

Finally, perform the division:
$$F = \frac{1.268 \times 10^{12}}{2.916 \times 10^7} \approx 43484\text{ N}$$
Rounding this to three significant figures, we get $4.35 \times 10^4\text{ N}$. Looking at the options provided, **(A) $4.34 \times 10^4\text{ N}$** is the correct choice (the minor difference arises from rounding $\pi$ or intermediate steps).

**Answer:** (A)
