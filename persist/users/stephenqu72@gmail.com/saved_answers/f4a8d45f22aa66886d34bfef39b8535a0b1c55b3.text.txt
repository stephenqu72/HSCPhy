To "account for" in an HSC Physics context means to explain *why* something is the case. You must link the physical nature of the radiation (mass, charge) to its observed properties (penetrating power and ionising ability).

### Model Answer
The properties of nuclear radiation are determined by their mass, charge, and nature:

*   **Alpha ($\alpha$) radiation:** Consists of helium nuclei ($^4_2He^{2+}$). Due to their relatively large mass and $+2$ charge, they interact strongly with matter, resulting in **high ionising ability** but **very low penetrating power** (stopped by a sheet of paper or a few centimetres of air).
*   **Beta ($\beta$) radiation:** Consists of high-speed electrons ($^0_{-1}e^-$) or positrons. They have much less mass than alpha particles and a $-1$ charge. Consequently, they have **moderate ionising ability** and **moderate penetrating power** (can pass through paper but are stopped by a thin sheet of aluminium).
*   **Gamma ($\gamma$) radiation:** Consists of high-energy electromagnetic photons. Being massless and uncharged, they have **very low ionising ability** but **very high penetrating power**, requiring thick lead or concrete shielding to be significantly attenuated.

### How to solve this
1.  **Understand the Verb:** "Account for" requires a cause-and-effect relationship. Do not just list the properties; you must explain *why* those properties exist (e.g., "because it is charged/massive, it does X").
2.  **Structure for Marks:** Since this is a 3-mark question, aim to provide a clear distinction for all three types. Providing a structured comparison (as shown above) ensures you cover all bases efficiently.
3.  **The Physics Link:** Remember this fundamental rule of HSC Physics:
    *   **Ionising ability** is inversely related to **penetrating power**.
    *   **Mass and charge** are the primary factors: Higher mass/charge leads to more frequent collisions with atoms, which increases ionisation but slows the particle down (decreasing penetration).
