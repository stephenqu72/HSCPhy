### Answer
To minimize issues with torque and back EMF in a DC motor, several design modifications can be implemented:

**1. Improving Torque:**
*   **Increasing the number of turns ($N$):** Torque is directly proportional to the number of loops of wire in the coil ($\tau = nIAB \sin \theta$). Increasing the number of turns increases the total force experienced by the coil.
*   **Using a soft iron core:** Placing an iron core inside the coil increases the magnetic flux density ($B$) due to its high magnetic permeability, which significantly boosts the motor's torque.
*   **Radial Magnetic Field:** Using curved pole pieces ensures that the magnetic field lines are always radial (perpendicular to the plane of the coil) for a wider rotation range. This ensures that $\sin \theta$ remains at its maximum value ($1$), providing constant, maximum torque throughout the rotation.

**2. Managing Back EMF and Efficiency:**
*   **Laminated Armature:** To minimize eddy current losses, which cause heating and oppose the motor's rotation, the armature core should be laminated. This increases electrical resistance to eddy currents without affecting the magnetic circuit.
*   **Increasing Back EMF at Operating Speed:** Back EMF ($\varepsilon$) opposes the supply voltage ($V$), which limits the current. By designing the motor to rotate faster, the back EMF increases, preventing the motor from drawing excessive current that could overheat the windings.
*   **Use of Multiple Coils and Commutator Segments:** Using a motor with more coils and more segments on the commutator reduces the "ripple" in torque, ensuring smoother operation and more consistent back EMF, which prevents sudden current surges that could damage the motor.
