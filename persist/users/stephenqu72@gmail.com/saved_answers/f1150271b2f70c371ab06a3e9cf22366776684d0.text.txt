### Answer
Two reasons for the broadening of spectral lines in a star are:

1.  **Stellar Rotation:** As a star rotates, one side moves toward the observer (blueshift) and the opposite side moves away (redshift). This range of Doppler shifts across the stellar disk causes the spectral lines to smear or broaden.
2.  **Pressure (Collisional) Broadening:** High pressure in a star's atmosphere causes frequent collisions between particles. These frequent interactions disrupt the energy levels of atoms, causing a spread in the emitted photon frequencies and resulting in broader spectral lines.
    *(Alternatively: **Thermal/Doppler Broadening** can be cited, where the random thermal motion of atoms leads to varying Doppler shifts, broadening the lines.)*

### Explanation
To solve this HSC-style physics question, you need to identify the physical mechanisms that alter the frequency or wavelength of light emitted by the star's atoms. 

*   **Step 1:** Recognize that a spectral line represents a specific transition in an atom. If the line is "broadened," it means light is being detected at wavelengths slightly above and below the theoretical central wavelength of that transition.
*   **Step 2:** Apply the Doppler effect principle. The most common cause in an observational context is **rotation**. Because the star is a sphere, the Doppler shift varies across its surface. If the star is rotating rapidly, the light collected from the entire disk is a superposition of blueshifted light (from the side rotating toward us) and redshifted light (from the side rotating away).
*   **Step 3:** Consider the environment of the gas. The density and temperature of the star's atmosphere also play a huge role. Under high pressure, the atoms are crowded, and their energy levels are perturbed by neighbors. This is known as **Pressure Broadening**. High temperatures also cause **Thermal Broadening**, as the atoms move at different velocities due to random kinetic energy, creating a distribution of Doppler shifts even in a non-rotating gas.
