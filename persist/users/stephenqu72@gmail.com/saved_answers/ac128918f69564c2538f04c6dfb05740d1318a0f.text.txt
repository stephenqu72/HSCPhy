### Explanation
To solve this, we must analyse the components of projectile motion (assuming air resistance is negligible):

1.  **Horizontal Motion:** In the absence of air resistance, there are no forces acting in the horizontal direction. According to Newton's First Law, the horizontal acceleration is zero ($a_x = 0$). Therefore, the horizontal component of velocity ($v_x$) remains constant throughout the entire flight. Thus, the initial horizontal velocity equals the final horizontal velocity. **Statement (D) is correct.**

2.  **Evaluating other options:**
    *   **(A) Incorrect:** At point $Y$ (the maximum height), the *vertical* component of velocity ($v_y$) is zero, but the *horizontal* component ($v_x$) remains non-zero and unchanged. Therefore, the total instantaneous velocity at $Y$ is equal to $v_x$, which is not zero.
    *   **(B) Incorrect:** The vertical component of velocity is subject to the constant acceleration of gravity ($g$), which causes $v_y$ to change continuously ($v_y = u_y - gt$).
    *   **(C) Incorrect:** While the *magnitude* of the vertical velocity at launch is often the same as at landing (assuming the launch and landing heights are the same), the *direction* is different (upwards at launch, downwards at landing). In physics, velocity is a vector, so these are not the same.
