### Answer and Explanation

#### Part a)
To find the battery voltage that produces the same average brightness as the AC alternator, we must calculate the **Root Mean Square (RMS) voltage** of the AC signal. The average brightness of a light globe is determined by the power it dissipates, which relates to the RMS voltage, not the peak voltage.

1.  **Identify the peak voltage ($V_p$):** From Figure 5, the peak voltage is $4\text{ V}$.
2.  **Calculate the RMS voltage ($V_{rms}$):** The formula for the RMS voltage of a sinusoidal AC waveform is:
    $$V_{rms} = \frac{V_p}{\sqrt{2}}$$
3.  **Substitute the values:**
    $$V_{rms} = \frac{4}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 2.828\text{ V}$$

Rounding to a reasonable number of significant figures, the battery should have a voltage of **$2.83\text{ V}$**.

#### Part b)
When the rate of rotation of the loop is doubled, two physical changes occur based on Faraday's Law of Induction ($ \mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi}{dt} $):
1.  **Frequency ($f$):** Doubling the rotation speed doubles the frequency. This means the period ($T = 1/f$) is halved. You must draw two full cycles in the time previously taken by one cycle (or four cycles in the space of the original two).
2.  **Peak Voltage ($V_p$):** The rate of change of magnetic flux ($\frac{d\Phi}{dt}$) also doubles. Therefore, the peak voltage doubles from $4\text{ V}$ to $8\text{ V}$.
