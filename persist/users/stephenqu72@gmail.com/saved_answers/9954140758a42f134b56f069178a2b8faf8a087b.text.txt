### Explanation

To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental nature of ultrasound waves and the physical properties of waves in general:

1.  **Nature of Ultrasound:** Ultrasound waves are **longitudinal mechanical waves**. This means the particles of the medium oscillate in the same direction that the wave propagates (parallel to the direction of energy transfer).
2.  **Polarisation:** Polarisation is a property exclusively associated with **transverse waves**, where oscillations occur perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation (e.g., light waves or electromagnetic waves). Because longitudinal waves (like sound) do not have oscillations perpendicular to their direction of travel, they cannot be polarised. Therefore, polarisation is not a property that can be employed when using ultrasound.
3.  **Other Options:**
    *   **A. Diffraction:** This is the bending of waves around obstacles. It is a key factor in ultrasound imaging, as it influences the resolution and the spread of the beam.
    *   **B. Interference:** This occurs when waves superimpose. It is fundamental in ultrasound technology, particularly in phased-array transducers where constructive and destructive interference are used to steer and focus the beam.
    *   **D. Reflection:** This is the core principle behind most ultrasound applications (like pulse-echo imaging and echolocation), where the time taken for a reflected wave to return from a boundary is measured to determine distance or create an image.

Since longitudinal waves do not exhibit polarisation, option C is the property that is not employed.
