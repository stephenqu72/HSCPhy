To solve this problem, we need to calculate the period ($T$) for each entry and propagate the uncertainty associated with the time for 20 oscillations.

### 1. Calculation of the Period ($T$)
The period of a single oscillation is defined as the total time ($t$) divided by the number of oscillations ($n$):
$$T = \frac{t}{20}$$

### 2. Propagation of Uncertainty
When multiplying or dividing a measurement by a constant (in this case, dividing by 20, which is an exact number with no uncertainty), the absolute uncertainty of the result is equal to the original absolute uncertainty divided by that constant:
$$\Delta T = \frac{\Delta t}{20}$$

### Worked Examples:
*   **For Student A ($0.100\text{ kg}$):**
    *   $T = 7.7 / 20 = 0.385\text{ s}$
    *   $\Delta T = 0.4 / 20 = 0.02\text{ s}$
    *   Result: $0.39 \pm 0.02\text{ s}$ (rounding to match decimal places of uncertainty).

*   **For Student B ($0.250\text{ kg}$):**
    *   $T = 13.0 / 20 = 0.65\text{ s}$
    *   $\Delta T = 0.5 / 20 = 0.025\text{ s} \approx 0.03\text{ s}$
    *   Result: $0.65 \pm 0.03\text{ s}$.
