### Answer
The correct answer is **B: the friction between the tyres and the road.**

---

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must identify which physical force acts as the "net centripetal force" for a car turning on a flat circular path.

1.  **Understanding Centripetal Force:** Centripetal force is not a new or separate force; it is the name given to the *net force* acting toward the center of a circular path. For a car to change its direction (to go around a bend), it must undergo centripetal acceleration, which requires a horizontal force directed toward the center of the turn.
2.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **A (Engine force):** This force provides the longitudinal acceleration or maintenance of speed, not the lateral force required to change direction.
    *   **B (Friction):** When a car turns, the tyres are turned to point toward the center of the circle. This creates a static frictional force between the road surface and the tyres directed toward the center of the curve. This lateral friction is the only force capable of providing the necessary centripetal force to keep the car moving in a circle.
    *   **C (Weight):** The weight of the car acts vertically downward. It is countered by the normal force from the road and does not contribute to horizontal circular motion.
    *   **D (Steering wheel force):** This is an internal force exerted by the driver on the car’s mechanism. While it initiates the turn by angling the tyres, it is not the external force that actually pushes the car toward the center of the bend.

Thus, the external force providing the centripetal acceleration is the **frictional force**.
