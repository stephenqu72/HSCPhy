### Explanation

To find the mass of an electron, physicists required two key pieces of information. 

1.  **J.J. Thomson’s Experiment (Option C):** Using a cathode ray tube, Thomson was able to measure the **charge-to-mass ratio** ($q/m$) of the electron. While this was a monumental discovery, it did not provide the absolute mass of the electron because he did not know the individual values of charge ($q$) or mass ($m$) separately; he only knew their ratio.
2.  **Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment (Option D):** Robert Millikan performed the famous oil drop experiment, which allowed him to determine the elementary charge of a single electron ($q \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$).

Once the value for the charge ($q$) was known from Millikan’s work, scientists could substitute this value into the charge-to-mass ratio determined by Thomson:

$$m = \frac{q}{(q/m)}$$

By dividing the known charge by the known ratio, the absolute mass of the electron could finally be calculated. Therefore, Millikan's experiment was the development that allowed the mass to be isolated and found.
