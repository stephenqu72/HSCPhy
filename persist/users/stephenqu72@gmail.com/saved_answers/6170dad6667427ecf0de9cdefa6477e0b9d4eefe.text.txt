### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) Romer**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the historical progression of methods used to determine the speed of light, which is a key topic in the HSC Physics module "Advanced Mechanics" (or formerly "Ideas to Implementation").

1.  **Galileo (A):** Galileo attempted to measure the speed of light using lanterns on hilltops. However, because light travels so fast, he could not measure the time delay over the short terrestrial distances available, concluding that light was likely instantaneous.
2.  **Romer (B):** In 1676, the Danish astronomer Ole Romer made the first quantitative measurement of the speed of light. He observed the eclipses of Jupiter's moon, **Io**. He noticed that the timing of these eclipses varied depending on the Earth's position in its orbit relative to Jupiter. When Earth was further from Jupiter, the light from the moon took longer to reach Earth. By using the known diameter of Earth's orbit and the observed time difference in the eclipses, he provided the first evidence that light has a finite speed and calculated a value for it.
3.  **Foucault (C) and Fizeau (D):** Both Foucault and Fizeau used terrestrial methods involving rotating mirrors or toothed wheels to measure the speed of light. While these methods were more precise than Romer's, they were developed later and did not rely on the movement of solar system bodies.

Since the question specifies the "movement of bodies in our solar system," Ole Romer is the correct historical figure.
