### Answer and Explanation

**Part a) Justify Chloe's statement.**
The motor effect occurs when a current-carrying conductor experiences a force when placed in an external magnetic field. 
1. Conductor X carries a current $I_1$, which generates a magnetic field $B_1$ in the space around it (Ampère's Law). 
2. Conductor Y is situated within this magnetic field $B_1$. Because conductor Y also carries a current $I_2$, it experiences a force due to the motor effect ($F = IlB\sin\theta$). 
3. The same logic applies reciprocally to conductor X being in the magnetic field generated by conductor Y. Since both conductors experience a force because they are current-carrying wires located within a magnetic field generated by the other, Chloe's statement is correct.

**Part b) Prove the formula $F = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2 l}{2 \pi r}$.**
To derive this, we combine the formula for the magnetic field created by a long straight wire and the formula for the motor effect force.

1. The magnetic field $B_1$ produced by conductor X at a distance $r$ is given by Ampère’s Law:
   $$B_1 = \frac{\mu_0 I_1}{2 \pi r}$$
2. The force $F$ experienced by a length $l$ of conductor Y carrying current $I_2$ in this magnetic field $B_1$ is given by the motor effect formula ($F = BIl\sin\theta$). Since the wire is perpendicular to the field ($\theta = 90^\circ, \sin(90^\circ) = 1$):
   $$F = B_1 I_2 l$$
3. Substitute the expression for $B_1$ into the force equation:
   $$F = \left( \frac{\mu_0 I_1}{2 \pi r} \right) I_2 l$$
   Rearranging this gives the required proof:
   $$F = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2 l}{2 \pi r}$$
