### Explanation

To solve this question, we need to understand the interaction between light and atoms, specifically regarding spectral lines:

1.  **Nature of the light source:** White light contains a continuous range of all visible wavelengths (a continuous spectrum).
2.  **Interaction with the gas:** When this white light passes through a cool, gaseous sample of an element, the atoms in the gas absorb specific photons of energy. The energy of these photons corresponds exactly to the energy difference between the ground state of the electrons and higher energy levels (excited states).
3.  **Resulting spectrum:** Because specific wavelengths are "stolen" or absorbed from the original continuous white light to promote electrons to higher energy levels, those specific colors are missing from the resulting spectrum. When viewed through a spectrometer, this appears as a continuous colored background (the remaining light) interrupted by **dark lines** at the specific wavelengths that were absorbed. This is known as an **absorption spectrum**.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **B and C:** An **emission spectrum** is produced when excited atoms emit light as electrons drop to lower energy states, resulting in colored lines on a dark background. This is not what happens when white light passes through a gas.
*   **D:** This option incorrectly identifies the process. An absorption spectrum is represented by dark lines on a colored background, not an emission spectrum.

Therefore, the only correct description is that it produces an absorption spectrum shown as a series of dark lines on a coloured background.
