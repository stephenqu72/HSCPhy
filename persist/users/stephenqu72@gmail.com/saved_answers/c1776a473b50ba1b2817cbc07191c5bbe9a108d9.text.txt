To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of an ideal transformer.

### 1. Identify the Transformer Ratio
The ratio of the turns in the primary coil ($N_p$) to the secondary coil ($N_s$) determines how the voltage changes:
$$N_p = 450, \quad N_s = 1800$$
The turns ratio is:
$$\frac{N_s}{N_p} = \frac{1800}{450} = 4$$
For an ideal transformer, the voltage ratio is equal to the turns ratio:
$$\frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p} = 4$$
This means the secondary voltage ($V_s$) must be exactly **4 times** the primary voltage ($V_p$).

### 2. Check the Voltage Ratios
Let's test each option to see if $\frac{V_s}{V_p} = 4$:
*   **(A)** $V_s = 92.0\text{ V}, V_p = 23.0\text{ V}$. Ratio: $92/23 = 4$. (Correct)
*   **(B)** $V_s = 92.0\text{ V}, V_p = 23.0\text{ V}$. Ratio: $92/23 = 4$. (Correct)

*   **(D)** $V_s = 69.0\text{ V}, V_p = 46.0\text{ V}$. Ratio: $69/46 = 1.5$. (Incorrect)

### 3. Check for Conservation of Energy (Ideal Transformer)
For an ideal transformer, power in must equal power out ($P_p = P_s$), where $P = VI$.
$$V_p \times I_p = V_s \times I_s$$
Since $V_s = 4 V_p$, then $I_s$ must be $\frac{1}{4}$ of $I_p$ for the equation to hold:
$$I_s = \frac{I_p}{4}$$

Now, let's test the remaining options:
*   **(A)** $I_p = 5.00\text{ A}, I_s = 2.00\text{ A}$. $\frac{5.00}{4} = 1.25\text{ A} \neq 2.00\text{ A}$. (Incorrect)
*   **(B)** $I_p = 5.00\text{ A}, I_s = 1.25\text{ A}$. $\frac{5.00}{4} = 1.25\text{ A}$. (**Correct**)
*   **(C)** $I_p = 10.0\text{ A}, I_s = 5.00\text{ A}$. $\frac{10.0}{4} = 2.50\text{ A} \neq 5.00\text{ A}$. (Incorrect)

**Conclusion:** Option (B) is the only one that satisfies both the voltage transformation ratio and the conservation of power for an ideal transformer.
