To determine which graph represents the speed of the projectile, let's break down the motion using physics principles:

### 1. Analysis of Velocity Components
A projectile launched with initial speed $v$ at an angle $\theta$ has two velocity components:
*   **Horizontal component ($v_x$):** Since there is no air resistance, $v_x$ remains constant throughout the flight: $v_x = v \cos(\theta)$.
*   **Vertical component ($v_y$):** This component changes due to gravity: $v_y(t) = v \sin(\theta) - gt$.

### 2. The Equation for Speed
The speed $S$ of the projectile at any time $t$ is the magnitude of the velocity vector:
$$S(t) = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y(t)^2}$$
Substituting our components:
$$S(t) = \sqrt{(v \cos \theta)^2 + (v \sin \theta - gt)^2}$$

### 3. Evaluating the Shape
*   **At launch ($t=0$):** $S = \sqrt{v^2 \cos^2 \theta + v^2 \sin^2 \theta} = v$.
*   **At the peak of flight:** The vertical velocity $v_y$ is $0$. Therefore, the speed is at its minimum, equal to $v \cos \theta$. Because $\cos \theta \neq 0$ (unless launched vertically, which is not the case here), the **minimum speed must be greater than zero.**
*   **Mathematical Curve:** The expression is in the form $\sqrt{a + (b-ct)^2}$, which describes a hyperbola-like curve that is smooth (differentiable) at the vertex, rather than a sharp point (V-shape).
