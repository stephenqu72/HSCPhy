### Solution

**Part (a): Calculate the minimum escape velocity**

To escape the electric field of the proton, the electron must have enough kinetic energy to overcome its electrostatic potential energy. At the point of "escaping" (reaching infinity with zero velocity), the total energy of the system is zero.

The electrostatic potential energy of an electron (charge $q = -e$) near a proton (charge $Q = +e$) is:
$$U_E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(-e)(e)}{r} = -\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{e^2}{r}$$

Setting the total energy ($E_{total} = K + U_E$) to zero for escape:
$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{e^2}{r} = 0 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{2e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 mr}}$$

Given constants:
- $k = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \approx 8.99 \times 10^9 \text{ N m}^2/\text{C}^2$
- $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$
- $m_e = 9.109 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
- $r = 9 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m}$

Substitute the values:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (8.99 \times 10^9) \times (1.602 \times 10^{-19})^2}{(9.109 \times 10^{-31}) \times (9 \times 10^{-15})}}$$
$$v \approx \sqrt{\frac{4.61 \times 10^{-28}}{8.198 \times 10^{-45}}} \approx \sqrt{5.62 \times 10^{16}} \approx 2.37 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$$

**Part (b): Explain why the electron cannot escape**

An electron can only escape if it has a velocity $v \ge c$ (the speed of light). According to Special Relativity, no particle with mass can reach or exceed the speed of light. Furthermore, at the distance of $5 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m}$, the escape velocity required would exceed $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$. Since the electron cannot be accelerated to the speed of light, it remains bound to the proton in this specific theoretical framework.
