The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
To arrive at this answer, we must evaluate the historical and scientific purpose of the Michelson-Morley experiment:

1.  **The Context:** At the time, physicists believed that light waves required a medium to propagate through, similar to sound waves needing air. This medium was called the **luminiferous aether**.
2.  **The Purpose:** The experiment was specifically designed to detect the "aether wind" by measuring the difference in the speed of light in two perpendicular directions as the Earth moved through the assumed aether.
3.  **Why it was "Valid":** In the scientific method, an experiment is considered **valid** if it effectively tests the hypothesis it was designed to investigate. Even though the experiment produced a "null result" (they found no evidence of the aether), it was a highly precise and well-designed experiment. It effectively demonstrated that the aether did not exist, which was a fundamental turning point in physics, eventually leading to Einstein’s Special Theory of Relativity.
4.  **Why other options are incorrect:**
    *   **A:** The experiment wasn't attempting to account for "all properties of light"; it was specifically looking for a medium (aether).
    *   **B & D:** These options label the experiment "invalid." In physics, a result that contradicts a hypothesis does not make an experiment "invalid." On the contrary, the experiment was a masterpiece of precision and is considered one of the most significant experiments in the history of science precisely *because* its results were reliable and accurate, even if they were unexpected. The experiment did account for the Earth's motion; it simply failed to detect the expected effect of an aether wind.

By confirming the absence of the aether, the experiment provided the empirical foundation for the principle of relativity—specifically, that the speed of light is constant in all inertial frames of reference.
