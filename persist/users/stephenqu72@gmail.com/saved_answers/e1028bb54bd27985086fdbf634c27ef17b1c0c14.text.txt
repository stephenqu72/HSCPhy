### Analysis

To achieve full marks for a 9-mark "Analyse" question, you must demonstrate a deep understanding of how these three scientists contributed to the paradigm shift from classical to quantum physics.

**1. Max Planck (The Quantum Hypothesis)**
*   **The Problem:** Classical physics (the Rayleigh-Jeans law) failed to explain black-body radiation, leading to the "ultraviolet catastrophe."
*   **The Work:** Planck proposed that energy is not continuous but emitted or absorbed in discrete packets called "quanta." He introduced his constant $h$ via the relation $E = hf$.
*   **Impact:** This fundamentally broke the classical assumption of continuous energy exchange and provided the foundation for the quantum model of the atom.

**2. Niels Bohr (The Quantum Atom)**
*   **The Problem:** Classical physics predicted that an orbiting electron would spiral into the nucleus due to the emission of electromagnetic radiation, making atoms unstable.
*   **The Work:** Bohr combined Planck’s quanta with Rutherford’s nuclear model. He proposed that electrons exist in stable "stationary states" with quantized angular momentum ($L = \frac{nh}{2\pi}$). Electrons jump between these levels, emitting or absorbing a photon with energy $\Delta E = hf$ matching the difference between orbits.
*   **Impact:** Bohr’s model successfully explained the stability of the atom and the spectral lines of hydrogen (Rydberg formula), proving that quantum rules govern atomic structure.

**3. Louis de Broglie (Wave-Particle Duality)**
*   **The Problem:** Bohr’s model "postulated" stable orbits without explaining *why* they were quantized.
*   **The Work:** De Broglie proposed the radical hypothesis that if light behaves as both a wave and a particle, then matter (specifically electrons) should exhibit wave-like properties. He derived the wavelength formula $\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$.
*   **Impact:** This explained Bohr's quantization: electrons form standing waves around the nucleus, where only integer numbers of wavelengths fit ($n\lambda = 2\pi r$). This unified the understanding of matter and radiation, directly leading to the development of wave mechanics and modern quantum theory.
