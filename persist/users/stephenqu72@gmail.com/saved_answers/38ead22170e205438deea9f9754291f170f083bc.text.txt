To solve this problem, we must analyze the physics of circular orbits using Newton's law of universal gravitation and centripetal force. Let $r$ be the orbital radius (distance from the center of the Earth). From the diagram, it is clear that $r_B > r_A$.

### 1. Orbital Velocity ($v$)
The centripetal force is provided by gravity:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Since $v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}$, the satellite at a smaller radius (A) must travel faster than the satellite at a larger radius (B). Therefore, **A has a greater orbital velocity**.

### 2. Orbital Period ($T$)
Using Kepler’s Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$):
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM}}$$
Since $T \propto \sqrt{r^3}$, a larger radius results in a longer orbital period. Therefore, **B has a greater orbital period**.

### 3. Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
The centripetal acceleration is equivalent to the local gravitational field strength:
$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$
Since $a_c \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$, the satellite closer to the Earth experiences a stronger gravitational pull and thus a higher acceleration. Therefore, **A has a greater centripetal acceleration**.

### Conclusion
Comparing our findings:
*   Orbital velocity: A > B
*   Orbital period: B > A
*   Centripetal acceleration: A > B
