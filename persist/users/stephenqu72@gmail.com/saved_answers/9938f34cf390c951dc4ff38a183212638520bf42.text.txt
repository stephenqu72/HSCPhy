### Explanation
To understand why banked tracks are used, we must analyze the physics of circular motion. When a car travels around a flat corner, the only force providing the necessary centripetal force is static friction acting horizontally between the tyres and the road surface. At high speeds, this friction may not be sufficient, leading to the car sliding off the track.

When a track is banked at an angle $\theta$:
1. The **normal force ($N$)** acts perpendicular to the surface of the track.
2. Because the track is tilted, the normal force $N$ is no longer purely vertical. It can be resolved into two perpendicular components:
   - A vertical component: $N \cos(\theta)$, which balances the weight of the car ($mg$).
   - A horizontal component: $N \sin(\theta)$, which points toward the center of the circular path.

This horizontal component, $N \sin(\theta)$, provides (or assists in providing) the **centripetal force** required to keep the car moving in a circle. By using the normal force to supply this centripetal force, the car relies significantly less on friction. This allows vehicles to navigate bends at much higher speeds than would be possible on a flat surface, as they are not solely dependent on the coefficient of friction between the rubber and the asphalt.

*   **A is incorrect:** Banking does not necessarily increase the magnitude of the frictional force; in fact, it often reduces the *reliance* on it.
*   **B is incorrect:** The goal of racing is to go fast; the banking is designed to *enable* higher speeds, not prevent them.
*   **C is incorrect:** While it is true that the car relies less on friction, "tyre longevity" is not the primary physics principle explaining why banking is used for high-speed motion. Option D provides the precise mechanical explanation.
