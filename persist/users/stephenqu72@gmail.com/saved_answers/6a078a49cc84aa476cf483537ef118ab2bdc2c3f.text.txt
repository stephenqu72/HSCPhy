To calculate the work function ($\Phi$) for a metal given its threshold frequency ($f_0$), we use the photoelectric equation:
$$\Phi = hf_0$$
where $h$ is Planck's constant.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Identify the given values:**
*   Threshold frequency $f_0 = 1.0 \times 10^9 \text{ MHz}$.
*   Convert MHz to Hz: $1 \text{ MHz} = 10^6 \text{ Hz}$.
*   Therefore, $f_0 = 1.0 \times 10^9 \times 10^6 \text{ Hz} = 1.0 \times 10^{15} \text{ Hz}$.
*   Planck's constant $h \approx 4.136 \times 10^{-15} \text{ eV}\cdot\text{s}$.

**2. Perform the calculation:**
Using the value of $h$ in units of electronvolts-seconds ($\text{eV}\cdot\text{s}$), we get:
$$\Phi = (4.136 \times 10^{-15} \text{ eV}\cdot\text{s}) \times (1.0 \times 10^{15} \text{ Hz})$$
$$\Phi = 4.136 \text{ eV}$$

**3. Analyze the result:**
Rounding the result to two significant figures gives approximately $4.1 \text{ eV}$.
Looking at the options provided:
*   (A) $4.1 \text{ eV}$
*   (B) $4.1 \text{ MeV}$
*   (C) $3.9 \text{ eV}$
*   (D) $3.9 \text{ MeV}$

The unit is $\text{eV}$ (electronvolts), which is the standard unit for work function, not $\text{MeV}$ (mega-electronvolts), which would be far too large for a typical metal.

**Answer:** (A)
