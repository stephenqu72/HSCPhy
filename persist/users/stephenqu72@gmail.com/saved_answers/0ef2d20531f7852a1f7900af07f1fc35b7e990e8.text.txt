### Answer
The correct answer is **B**. Increasing both the bank angle $\theta$ and the coefficient of friction $\mu$ will result in the greatest increase in the motorcyclist's maximum cornering speed.

---

### Explanation
To understand why, we look at the physics of a vehicle on a banked track. The maximum speed $v$ a vehicle can maintain on a banked curve without sliding up the track is governed by the forces of gravity, the normal force, and friction. 

The general formula for the maximum speed $v$ on a banked track with friction is:
$$v = \sqrt{rg \left( \frac{\tan\theta + \mu}{1 - \mu\tan\theta} \right)}$$

Where:
*   $r$ is the radius of the turn.
*   $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity.
*   $\theta$ is the angle of the bank.
*   $\mu$ is the coefficient of static friction.

**Analysis of the variables:**
1.  **Effect of Bank Angle ($\theta$):** As the angle $\theta$ increases, $\tan\theta$ increases. Looking at the numerator inside the square root, an increase in $\tan\theta$ directly contributes to an increase in $v$. Essentially, a steeper bank allows the normal force to provide a larger component of the centripetal force required for turning.
2.  **Effect of Friction ($\mu$):** Friction acts to prevent the motorcycle from sliding. A higher coefficient of friction $\mu$ allows for more grip, which increases the maximum speed at which the motorcycle can traverse the curve before skidding outwards. In the formula, increasing $\mu$ increases the value of the expression inside the square root.

**Conclusion:**
Since both $\theta$ and $\mu$ have a positive correlation with the maximum cornering speed $v$, increasing both parameters simultaneously will yield the highest possible increase in speed. Therefore, **Option B** is the correct choice.
