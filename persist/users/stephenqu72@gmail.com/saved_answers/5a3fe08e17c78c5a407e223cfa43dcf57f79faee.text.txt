To solve this problem, we use Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation:

$$F = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

Where:
*   $F = 1.09 \times 10^5 \text{ N}$ (gravitational force)
*   $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2 \text{ kg}^{-2}$ (gravitational constant)
*   $M = 8.5 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$ (mass of the planet)
*   $m = 1 \times 10^4 \text{ kg}$ (mass of the rocket)
*   $r$ is the distance from the center of the planet to the rocket.

### Step 1: Solve for $r$
Rearrange the formula to solve for $r$:
$$r^2 = \frac{GMm}{F}$$
$$r = \sqrt{\frac{GMm}{F}}$$

Substitute the values:
$$r = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (8.5 \times 10^{24}) \times (1 \times 10^4)}{1.09 \times 10^5}}$$
$$r = \sqrt{\frac{5.6695 \times 10^{18}}{1.09 \times 10^5}}$$
$$r = \sqrt{5.201 \times 10^{13}} \approx 7.212 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$$

### Step 2: Convert to km
Since the radius is given in km, convert $r$ to km:
$$r \approx 7212 \text{ km}$$

### Step 3: Find the altitude
The distance $r$ is the sum of the planet's radius ($R$) and the altitude ($h$):
$$r = R + h$$
$$7212 \text{ km} = 7200 \text{ km} + h$$
$$h = 7212 \text{ km} - 7200 \text{ km} = 12 \text{ km}$$

**Correct Answer:** **(A)**

### Explanation
The most common mistake in these problems is confusing the distance from the center of the planet ($r$) with the altitude ($h$). Always remember that $r = R_{planet} + \text{altitude}$. Once you calculate the total orbital radius, subtract the planet's radius to find how high the rocket is above the surface.
