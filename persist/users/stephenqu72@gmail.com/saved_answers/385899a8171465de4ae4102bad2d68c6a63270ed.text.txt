### Teacher's Solution and Explanation

#### Part (a)
To show the formula for maximum height ($h_{max}$), we focus on the vertical motion of the projectile. 

1.  **Resolve initial velocity:** The initial vertical component of the velocity is $v_{y0} = u \sin\theta$.
2.  **Condition at maximum height:** At the peak of the trajectory, the vertical velocity becomes zero ($v_y = 0$).
3.  **Use equations of motion:** We use the kinematic equation $v_y^2 = v_{y0}^2 + 2ay$, where $a = -g$ and $y = h_{max}$.
    $$0 = (u \sin\theta)^2 - 2g h_{max}$$
4.  **Rearrange for $h_{max}$:**
    $$2g h_{max} = u^2 \sin^2\theta$$
    $$h_{max} = \frac{u^2 \sin^2\theta}{2g}$$

***

#### Part (b)
We need to determine if the projectile lands on, short of, or beyond the 140.0 m mark. We calculate the horizontal range ($R$) using the projectile range formula.

1.  **Given values:** 
    - Initial velocity $u = 40.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$
    - Angle $\theta = 30^\circ$
    - Gravity $g \approx 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$
2.  **Range formula:** The horizontal range for a projectile on level ground is given by $R = \frac{u^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$.
3.  **Calculation:**
    $$R = \frac{40.0^2 \times \sin(2 \times 30^\circ)}{9.8}$$
    $$R = \frac{1600 \times \sin(60^\circ)}{9.8}$$
    $$R = \frac{1600 \times 0.866}{9.8} \approx 141.4\text{ m}$$
4.  **Conclusion:** Since the calculated range ($141.4\text{ m}$) is greater than the target distance ($140.0\text{ m}$), the projectile **travels further than the target**.
