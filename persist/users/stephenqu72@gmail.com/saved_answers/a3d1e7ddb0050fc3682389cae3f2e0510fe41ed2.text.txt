To solve this problem, we use **Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) is directly proportional to the cube of the orbital radius ($r$):

$$\frac{T_1^2}{r_1^3} = \frac{T_2^2}{r_2^3}$$

### Given data:
*   Satellite 1: $r_1 = 2.3 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$, $T_1 = 7.6 \times 10^4 \text{ s}$
*   Satellite 2: $T_2 = 1.3 \times 10^5 \text{ s}$, $r_2 = ?$

### Step-by-step solution:
1.  **Rearrange the formula to solve for $r_2^3$:**
    $$r_2^3 = r_1^3 \times \left( \frac{T_2}{T_1} \right)^2$$

2.  **Substitute the known values:**
    $$r_2^3 = (2.3 \times 10^6)^3 \times \left( \frac{1.3 \times 10^5}{7.6 \times 10^4} \right)^2$$

3.  **Calculate the ratio of periods:**
    $$\frac{1.3 \times 10^5}{7.6 \times 10^4} \approx 1.7105$$
    $$(1.7105)^2 \approx 2.9259$$

4.  **Calculate $r_1^3$:**
    $$(2.3 \times 10^6)^3 = 12.167 \times 10^{18} \text{ m}^3$$

5.  **Multiply to find $r_2^3$:**
    $$r_2^3 = 12.167 \times 10^{18} \times 2.9259 \approx 35.6 \times 10^{18} \text{ m}^3$$

6.  **Find the cube root to determine $r_2$:**
    $$r_2 = \sqrt[3]{35.6 \times 10^{18}} \approx 3.29 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$$

Rounding to two significant figures (as per the data provided), we get **$3.3 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$**.
