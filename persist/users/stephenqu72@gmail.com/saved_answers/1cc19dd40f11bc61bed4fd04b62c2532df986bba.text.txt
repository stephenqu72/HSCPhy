### Answer
The rotational speed of a DC motor is primarily determined by the balance between the applied terminal voltage, the internal resistance of the armature, and the magnetic flux.

The fundamental mathematical model for a DC motor is given by the voltage equation:
$$V = E_b + I_a R_a$$
Where:
- $V$ is the supply voltage
- $E_b$ is the back electromotive force (back EMF)
- $I_a$ is the armature current
- $R_a$ is the armature resistance

The back EMF $E_b$ is directly proportional to the rotational speed ($\omega$) and the magnetic flux ($\phi$) produced by the field:
$$E_b = k\phi\omega$$
Substituting this into the voltage equation gives:
$$V = k\phi\omega + I_a R_a$$
Rearranging to solve for the angular velocity ($\omega$):
$$\omega = \frac{V - I_a R_a}{k\phi}$$

**Factors affecting speed:**
1. **Supply Voltage ($V$):** Increasing the supply voltage increases the motor speed, as $\omega$ is directly proportional to $V$.
2. **Armature Resistance ($R_a$):** An increase in load leads to a higher current ($I_a$), increasing the "voltage drop" $I_a R_a$ across the internal resistance. This reduces the effective EMF, thereby decreasing the rotational speed.
3. **Magnetic Flux ($\phi$):** The speed is inversely proportional to the magnetic flux. Reducing the flux (field weakening) will increase the motor speed, whereas increasing the flux will decrease the speed.
