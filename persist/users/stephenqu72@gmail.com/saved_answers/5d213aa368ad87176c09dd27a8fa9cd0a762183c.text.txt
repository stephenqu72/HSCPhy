To solve this problem, we need to apply the principles of relative motion and inertial vs. non-inertial frames of reference.

### Answer
The correct answer is **D**.

### Explanation
1.  **The Physics of the Mass (Inertial Frame):** Once the string is cut, gravity is the only force acting on the mass (neglecting air resistance). To an observer outside the bus (an inertial frame), the mass will undergo projectile motion. Because the bus was moving forward at a constant velocity, the mass retains that initial horizontal velocity. As the bus decelerates underneath it, the mass continues forward at its original speed while accelerating downwards due to gravity.

2.  **The Frame of Reference of the Bus (Non-Inertial Frame):** Inside the bus, the frame is accelerating (decelerating at $3\text{ m s}^{-2}$ in the direction of travel). When you are in an accelerating frame of reference, you must introduce a "fictitious" or "pseudo" force to explain the motion of objects.
    *   Since the bus is slowing down (decelerating), the fictitious force acts in the **forward** direction of the bus (opposite to the direction of acceleration).
    *   The mass, therefore, experiences two components of acceleration within the bus's frame:
        *   **Vertical:** The gravitational acceleration $g$ acting downwards.
        *   **Horizontal:** The fictitious acceleration $a = 3\text{ m s}^{-2}$ acting towards the **front** of the bus.

3.  **Determining the Path:** Since the mass has a constant downward acceleration ($g$) and a constant forward acceleration ($a$) relative to the bus, the resultant path follows a parabolic trajectory. Because the horizontal acceleration is directed towards the front of the bus, the mass will appear to move in a parabola that curves downward and forward relative to its starting position.

Thus, relative to the bus, the mass accelerates both downwards and forwards, resulting in a parabolic trajectory towards the front.
