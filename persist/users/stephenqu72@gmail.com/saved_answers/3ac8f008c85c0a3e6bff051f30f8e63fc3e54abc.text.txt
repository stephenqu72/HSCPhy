To solve this problem, we must understand what happens during gamma decay and how it relates to the stability of the nucleus.

### Explanation

1.  **The Process:** Gamma decay involves an excited nucleus ($^{137m}\text{Ba}$) transitioning to a lower energy state (the ground state, $^{137}\text{Ba}$) by emitting a high-energy photon (a gamma ray, $\gamma$). 
2.  **Binding Energy Definition:** The binding energy of a nucleus is the energy required to disassemble a nucleus into its constituent protons and neutrons. The **binding energy per nucleon** is a measure of the stability of the nucleus. A higher binding energy per nucleon indicates a more stable, lower-energy configuration of the nucleus.
3.  **The Change:** 
    *   The excited state $^{137m}\text{Ba}$ has excess energy and is less stable.
    *   By emitting a gamma photon, the nucleus loses energy and moves to a lower, more stable energy state ($^{137}\text{Ba}$).
    *   Because the total energy of the nucleus decreases while the number of nucleons remains constant, the binding energy (the "missing" mass equivalent holding the nucleus together) relative to its total mass-energy increases.
    *   Essentially, the ground state is more tightly bound than the excited state. Therefore, the binding energy per nucleon must increase as the nucleus transitions to a more stable state.

### Conclusion
The release of the gamma photon indicates that the nucleus has transitioned to a more stable state. Consequently, the binding energy per nucleon increases.
