### Answer
The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the physical mechanism behind "orbital decay."

1.  **Understanding Orbital Decay:** Orbital decay is the gradual decrease in the distance between an orbiting body and the primary body it orbits. For a satellite, this occurs primarily due to **atmospheric drag**.
2.  **Atmospheric Density:** Earth's atmosphere is not a sharp cutoff; it thins out gradually with increasing altitude. Satellites in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) occupy an altitude range where there are still residual particles of the Earth's atmosphere (the thermosphere/exosphere).
3.  **The Mechanism:** As a satellite travels through these residual gas molecules at orbital velocities (approximately $7.8\text{ km/s}$), the particles collide with the satellite. These collisions create a small but constant drag force that opposes the satellite's motion. This drag causes the satellite to lose kinetic energy. As kinetic energy decreases, the satellite drops to a lower orbital altitude (according to the relationship between orbital energy and radius), where the atmosphere is even denser, further increasing the drag. This feedback loop is known as orbital decay.
4.  **Why other options are incorrect:**
    *   **A:** While gravity is stronger in LEO, gravity itself is responsible for keeping the satellite in orbit (providing the centripetal force). It does not cause decay; it causes the orbit.
    *   **B:** While LEO satellites do travel faster than geostationary satellites to maintain their orbit, their high speed is a requirement for the orbit, not the *cause* of the decay.
    *   **D:** This statement is factually incorrect; LEO satellites travel faster than geostationary satellites because they are closer to the Earth's center of mass.

Therefore, the defining factor that causes the difference in decay rates is the presence of the atmosphere at low altitudes.
