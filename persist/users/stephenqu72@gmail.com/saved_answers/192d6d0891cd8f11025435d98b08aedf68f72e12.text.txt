### Answer

**Comparison of Discharge Tubes and Stellar Spectra:**

*   **Similarities:** Both types of spectra are produced by the transition of electrons between energy levels in atoms. They both exhibit discrete spectral lines at characteristic frequencies (wavelengths) that act as "fingerprints" for specific elements.
*   **Differences:**
    *   **Nature of Spectra:** A discharge tube typically produces an **emission spectrum** (bright lines on a dark background) because the gas is hot and low-density, emitting photons. A star produces an **absorption spectrum** (dark lines on a continuous rainbow background) because the hot, dense core (photosphere) produces a continuous spectrum, and the cooler outer atmosphere (chromosphere) absorbs specific wavelengths.
    *   **Complexity:** Discharge tubes usually contain a single or few gases, resulting in relatively simple spectra. Stellar spectra are extremely complex due to the presence of many elements, various ionization states, and Doppler shifting.

**Features of a Star Revealed by Stellar Spectra:**

1.  **Chemical Composition:** By comparing the position of absorption lines with known emission spectra from laboratories, astronomers identify the specific elements present in the star's atmosphere.
2.  **Surface Temperature:** The intensity of specific absorption lines (e.g., hydrogen Balmer lines vs. ionized metal lines) depends on the temperature, allowing stars to be classified into spectral types (O, B, A, F, G, K, M).
3.  **Radial Velocity/Motion:** The **Doppler effect** causes spectral lines to shift. A redshift indicates the star is moving away, while a blueshift indicates it is moving towards Earth.
4.  **Rotational Speed:** Rapid rotation causes "line broadening" (the Doppler shift of light from the approaching vs. receding sides of the star).
5.  **Magnetic Fields:** The **Zeeman effect** causes spectral lines to split into multiple components in the presence of strong magnetic fields.
