To solve this problem, we apply the physics of forces between current-carrying conductors.

### 1. Identifying the Relevant Physics
The force between two parallel current-carrying wires is given by the formula:
$$F = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2 L}{2 \pi r}$$
where:
*   $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ T}\cdot\text{m/A}$ (permeability of free space)
*   $I_1 = 10 \text{ A}$ (current in Wire X)
*   $I_2 = 2.5 \text{ A}$ (current in the parallel segment of Wire Y)
*   $L = 4.0 \text{ cm} = 0.04 \text{ m}$ (length of the parallel segment of Wire Y)
*   $r = 1.0 \text{ cm} = 0.01 \text{ m}$ (distance between the wires)

*Note: Even though Wire X is 16 cm long, the force is only exerted on the segment of Wire X that is adjacent to the parallel portion of Wire Y (which is 4.0 cm long).*

### 2. Calculating the Magnitude
Substitute the values into the formula:
$$F = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}) \times 10 \times 2.5 \times 0.04}{2 \pi \times 0.01}$$
Simplify the expression:
$$F = \frac{2 \times 10^{-7} \times 10 \times 2.5 \times 0.04}{0.01}$$
$$F = \frac{2 \times 10^{-7} \times 1}{0.01}$$
$$F = 2 \times 10^{-5} \text{ N}$$

### 3. Determining the Direction
We use the Right-Hand Grip Rule and the motor effect:
1.  **Currents in same direction:** Wires attract.
2.  **Currents in opposite direction:** Wires repel.

Looking at the diagram, the current in the top part of Wire Y flows to the left, while the current in Wire X flows to the right. Since the currents are in **opposite directions**, the wires will **repel** each other. Therefore, the force on Wire X will be directed **away from Wire Y**.
