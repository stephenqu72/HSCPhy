### Answer

**(a)** In the process of nuclear fusion, four hydrogen nuclei (protons) are fused to form one helium nucleus. The total mass of the resulting helium nucleus is slightly less than the sum of the masses of the four initial hydrogen nuclei. This "missing" mass, known as the **mass defect** ($\Delta m$), is converted into energy according to Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, defined by the equation:
$$E = \Delta mc^2$$
Because the speed of light ($c$) is a very large constant ($3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$), even a tiny amount of mass defect results in the release of a massive amount of energy.

**(b)** Because the probability of three or more particles colliding simultaneously is extremely low, main sequence stars utilize specific multi-step pathways to achieve fusion:

1.  **Proton-Proton (p-p) Chain:** This is the primary process in stars like our Sun. It occurs through a series of two-body interactions where protons fuse to form deuterium, then helium-3, and eventually helium-4. Each step involves only two particles colliding at a time, overcoming the electrostatic repulsion through high temperatures and quantum tunneling.
2.  **CNO Cycle (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle):** This process occurs in more massive stars. It acts as a catalytic cycle where carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen nuclei act as catalysts to fuse four hydrogen nuclei into one helium nucleus. Like the p-p chain, every individual reaction within the CNO cycle involves the collision of only two particles, successfully avoiding the requirement for impossible three-body interactions.
