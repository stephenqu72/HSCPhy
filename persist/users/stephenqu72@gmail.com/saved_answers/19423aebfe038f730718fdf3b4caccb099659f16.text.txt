### Answer
The apparatus functions as an **accelerometer**. When the car moves at a constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line), the angle $\theta$ will be $0^\circ$ because there is no net horizontal force. If the car accelerates or moves in a circular path, the string swings to a constant angle $\theta$ relative to the vertical. The angle $\theta$ allows us to determine the horizontal acceleration ($a$) of the car.

**Derivation:**
Consider the free-body diagram of the mass $m$. Two forces act on the mass:
1. The tension in the string, $T$.
2. The gravitational force, $mg$, acting downwards.

When the car has a horizontal acceleration $a$, the net force is $F_{net} = ma$ acting horizontally. Resolving the tension into components:
- Vertical component: $T \cos \theta = mg$  (Equation 1)
- Horizontal component: $T \sin \theta = ma$  (Equation 2)

Dividing Equation 2 by Equation 1:
$$\frac{T \sin \theta}{T \cos \theta} = \frac{ma}{mg}$$
$$\tan \theta = \frac{a}{g}$$
$$a = g \tan \theta$$

Thus, the car's acceleration can be determined by measuring the angle $\theta$ and multiplying the tangent of that angle by the gravitational acceleration $g$.

### How to solve it
1. **Identify the Physics Principles:** Recognise that this is a problem of non-inertial frames or net force in a dynamic system. Newton’s Second Law ($F_{net} = ma$) is the core principle.
2. **Draw a Force Diagram:** Visualise the forces acting on the mass. The tension acts along the string, and gravity acts vertically. The resultant force must point in the direction of the car's horizontal acceleration.
3. **Resolve Vectors:** Break the tension force into horizontal and vertical components. The vertical component must balance the weight ($mg$) to keep the mass at a constant height. The horizontal component provides the force required to accelerate the mass along with the car.
4. **Relate Components:** Use trigonometry ($\tan \theta = \text{opposite} / \text{adjacent}$) to relate the ratio of the horizontal force to the weight. 
5. **Final Expression:** Isolate the acceleration $a$ to show how the measurement ($\theta$) directly corresponds to the car's physical motion.
