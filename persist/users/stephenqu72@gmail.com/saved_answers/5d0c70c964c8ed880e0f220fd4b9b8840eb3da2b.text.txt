### Answer
The spectrum of a star provides vital data regarding its physical properties:

1.  **Determining Temperature:** A star's surface temperature is determined by analyzing its continuous blackbody radiation curve. According to **Wien’s Law**, the peak wavelength of the emitted radiation, $\lambda_{max}$, is inversely proportional to the temperature $T$ of the star ($\lambda_{max} T = \text{constant}$). A "bluer" star has a shorter peak wavelength, indicating a higher temperature, while a "redder" star has a longer peak wavelength, indicating a lower temperature. Additionally, the presence and strength of specific absorption lines (e.g., hydrogen vs. ionized metals) indicate the excitation state of atoms, which is directly dependent on the star's surface temperature.

2.  **Determining Chemical Composition:** The composition is determined through **absorption spectroscopy**. As the star's continuous light passes through its cooler outer atmosphere, specific elements absorb photons at characteristic discrete wavelengths. These appear as dark "absorption lines" in the spectrum. By comparing the position of these spectral lines to known laboratory spectra of elements (fingerprinting), astronomers can identify the specific chemical elements present in the star's atmosphere.
