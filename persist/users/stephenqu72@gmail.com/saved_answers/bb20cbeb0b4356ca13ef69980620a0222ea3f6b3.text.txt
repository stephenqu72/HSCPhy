### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** Lucy would know the force of gravity is equal to the centripetal force when the balls maintain a **stable, circular orbit** at a constant radius without drifting inward or outward. 

**Explanation:** In a system where gravitational attraction between two objects provides the necessary centripetal force for circular motion, the objects will maintain a constant orbital distance. If the centripetal force were greater than gravity, the objects would spiral inward; if less, they would drift apart.

---

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
To derive the relationship, we equate the gravitational force ($F_g$) to the centripetal force ($F_c$) required for the circular motion of a mass $m$ at distance $r$ from the center of mass (assuming a two-body system where the other mass is large, or simplifying the balance of forces):

1. The gravitational force between two masses is $F_g = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$.
2. The centripetal force required for circular motion is $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$.
3. Equating them:
   $$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
4. Divide both sides by $m$ and multiply by $r$:
   $$v^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$$
5. Taking the square root:
   $$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
*(Note: In the specific case of two identical balls rotating around a common pivot, the force is dictated by the interaction between them, which leads to the derivation of the orbital speed.)*

---

#### Part (c)
**Answer:** Lucy would not observe measurable results because the universal gravitational constant ($G$) is extremely small ($6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2$).

**Explanation:** Even for objects of significant mass (e.g., $100 \text{ kg}$), the gravitational force between them is negligible compared to other forces like friction, air resistance, or even the slight vibrations of the apparatus. Because $G$ is so small, the resulting velocity $v$ required to maintain this gravitational orbit would be infinitesimally slow or require masses so large that they would not be "balls" in a laboratory setting. Consequently, the force of gravity is too weak to be the primary driver of motion compared to local environmental interference.
