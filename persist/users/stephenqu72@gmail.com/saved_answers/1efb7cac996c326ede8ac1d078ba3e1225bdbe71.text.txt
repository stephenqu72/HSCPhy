### Answer
Magnetic braking relies on the principle of electromagnetic induction. As the metal disc rotates through the region of the external magnetic field, the free electrons within the metal experience a changing magnetic flux. According to **Faraday's Law**, this change in flux induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the disc, which drives circulating currents known as **eddy currents**.

According to **Lenz’s Law**, these eddy currents generate their own magnetic field that opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced them. This interaction creates a magnetic force (a Lorentz force) that acts in the opposite direction to the rotation of the disc, thereby exerting a torque that slows the disc down.

Regarding the **conservation of energy**, the mechanical kinetic energy of the rotating disc is not lost; rather, it is transformed. Due to the electrical resistance of the metal, the eddy currents dissipate energy as thermal energy (heat) within the disc ($P = I^2R$). Thus, the initial mechanical kinetic energy of the system is converted into internal thermal energy, bringing the disc to a stop.
