### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the classification of particles within the Standard Model of particle physics:

1.  **Fundamental Particles:** These are particles that are not composed of smaller sub-components. According to the Standard Model, these are divided into two main groups:
    *   **Fermions:** These include **Quarks** (which make up composite particles) and **Leptons** (such as the electron).
    *   **Bosons:** These are force-carrier particles (e.g., the photon, gluon, $W$ and $Z$ bosons, and the Higgs boson).
    *   Therefore, the set **Quarks, Leptons and Bosons** correctly categorizes all fundamental particles in the Standard Model.

2.  **Why the other options are incorrect:**
    *   **B (Hadrons, Baryons and Mesons):** These are **composite** particles. Hadrons are made of quarks; Baryons are three-quark states (like protons and neutrons), and Mesons are quark-antiquark pairs.
    *   **C (Photons, Gluons and Electrons):** While all three are indeed fundamental particles, this is a specific list of examples rather than the broad *categories* that define the structural framework of the Standard Model. Option A is the superior answer as it encompasses the entirety of the particle families.
