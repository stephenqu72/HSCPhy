To solve this problem, we must understand the relationship between the motor speed and the torque produced by a DC motor powered by a constant external EMF ($V$).

### Physics Principles
1.  **Back EMF:** As a DC motor spins, the coil induces a back EMF ($\varepsilon$) that opposes the supply voltage. The back EMF is directly proportional to the angular velocity ($\omega$) of the motor: $\varepsilon = k\omega$.
2.  **Armature Current:** The net voltage across the armature resistance ($R$) is $V - \varepsilon$. By Ohm’s Law, the current ($I$) is:
    $$I = \frac{V - \varepsilon}{R} = \frac{V - k\omega}{R}$$
3.  **Torque:** The torque ($\tau$) produced by the motor is proportional to the current flowing through the coil: $\tau = kI$. 
4.  **Combining the Equations:**
    $$\tau = k \left( \frac{V - k\omega}{R} \right)$$
    $$\tau = \frac{kV}{R} - \frac{k^2}{R}\omega$$

### Analysis
The equation $\tau = \frac{kV}{R} - \frac{k^2}{R}\omega$ shows that the relationship between torque ($\tau$) and angular velocity ($\omega$) is **linear** with a negative gradient.
*   At **stationary** ($\omega = 0$), the torque is at its maximum value: $\tau_{max} = \frac{kV}{R}$.
*   At **maximum operating speed**, the motor reaches a point where the back EMF almost equals the supply voltage, causing the current (and thus the torque) to drop toward zero.
