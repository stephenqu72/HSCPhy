### Answer
In 1930, Bothe and Becker observed that bombarding beryllium with alpha particles produced highly penetrating, neutral radiation. While initially thought to be high-energy gamma rays, James Chadwick later proved this radiation consisted of particles with approximately the mass of a proton but no electric charge: the **neutron**.

The discovery relied on the laws of **conservation of mass-energy** and **conservation of momentum**. When the unknown radiation struck light targets (like paraffin wax, which is rich in protons), the protons were ejected at very high velocities. If the radiation had been gamma rays (massless), the energy required to eject the protons at those observed speeds would have been physically impossible under the laws of conservation of momentum and energy. Chadwick used the kinematics of the collision to show that the incident particle must have a mass comparable to the proton, confirming the existence of the neutron.
