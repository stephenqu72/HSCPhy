### Explanation
To solve this problem, we need to analyze the gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy of a satellite in a circular orbit using the following standard physics formulas:

**1. Potential Energy ($U$):**
The gravitational potential energy for an object of mass $m$ at a distance $r$ from the center of the Earth (mass $M$) is given by:
$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$
As the satellite moves to an orbit "closer to Earth," the orbital radius $r$ decreases. Since $r$ is in the denominator and the value is negative, as $r$ becomes smaller, the fraction $\frac{GMm}{r}$ becomes larger, making the negative value $U$ "more negative." Therefore, the potential energy **decreases**.

**2. Kinetic Energy ($K$):**
The orbital velocity $v$ of a satellite is given by:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
The kinetic energy $K$ is given by $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Substituting the expression for $v$:
$$K = \frac{1}{2}m\left(\frac{GM}{r}\right) = \frac{GMm}{2r}$$
As the orbital radius $r$ decreases, the denominator becomes smaller, which means the kinetic energy $K$ **increases**. 

**Summary:**
*   When a satellite moves closer to the Earth, it loses gravitational potential energy (it becomes more negative).
*   To maintain a stable circular orbit at a lower altitude, the satellite must travel at a higher velocity, resulting in an increase in kinetic energy.

This corresponds to the values provided in option **A**.
