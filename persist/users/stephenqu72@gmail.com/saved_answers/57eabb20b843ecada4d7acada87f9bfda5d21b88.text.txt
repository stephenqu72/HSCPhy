To find the decay constant $\lambda$ for the radioisotope, we follow these steps:

### 1. Identify the half-life ($t_{1/2}$) from the graph
The half-life is the time it takes for the decay rate (activity) to decrease by half.
*   At 8:00 pm (10/04), the count is $16\,000 \text{ min}^{-1}$.
*   Half of $16\,000$ is $8\,000$. Looking at the graph, $8\,000$ counts occur exactly halfway between 10:00 pm and 12:00 am (11:00 pm).
*   Alternatively, look at the value at 10:00 pm ($10\,000$) and find when it drops to $5\,000$. It drops to $5\,000$ at roughly 2:00 am.
*   Let's check the interval: From 8:00 pm to 2:00 am is $6$ hours. At 8:00 pm, count = $16\,000$. At 2:00 am (6 hours later), count = $4\,000$.
*   Since the count dropped from $16\,000$ to $4\,000$ ($1/4$ of the original value) in $6$ hours, this represents two half-lives ($16 \to 8 \to 4$).
*   Therefore, $2 \times t_{1/2} = 6 \text{ hours}$, meaning the half-life $t_{1/2} = 3 \text{ hours}$.

### 2. Convert units
The decay constant $\lambda$ is requested in $\text{s}^{-1}$. We must convert the half-life from hours to seconds:
$$t_{1/2} = 3 \text{ hours} \times 60 \text{ minutes/hour} \times 60 \text{ seconds/minute} = 10\,800 \text{ s}$$

### 3. Calculate the decay constant $\lambda$
We use the relationship between the half-life and the decay constant:
$$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{0.693}{10\,800 \text{ s}}$$
$$\lambda \approx 6.416 \times 10^{-5} \text{ s}^{-1}$$
