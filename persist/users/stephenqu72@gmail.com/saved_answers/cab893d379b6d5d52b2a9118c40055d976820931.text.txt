### Explanation
To solve this problem, keep these two core principles in mind:

1.  **Direction of the Electric Field:** By convention, electric field lines represent the path a positive test charge would take. Therefore, field lines always point **away from positive charges** and **toward negative charges**. Since the top plate is positive ($+$) and the bottom plate is negative ($-$), the field lines must point downwards, from the top plate to the bottom plate.
2.  **Uniformity of the Field:** Between two parallel, oppositely charged plates, the electric field is essentially uniform (constant in both magnitude and direction). This is represented by parallel, equally spaced field lines. 
3.  **Fringing Effect:** At the edges of the plates, the field is no longer perfectly uniform; the field lines "bulge" outward. This is known as the fringing effect.

**Evaluating the options:**
*   **(A)** shows field lines pointing from positive to negative, which is the correct direction, and includes the expected "fringing" (curving) at the edges.
*   **(B)** and **(D)** show field lines moving horizontally, which is incorrect for vertical plates. 
*   **(C)** shows field lines pointing from negative to positive, which is the reverse of the standard convention.

By identifying that the field lines must originate at the positive plate and terminate at the negative plate with a uniform distribution, we conclude that (A) is the only physically accurate representation.
