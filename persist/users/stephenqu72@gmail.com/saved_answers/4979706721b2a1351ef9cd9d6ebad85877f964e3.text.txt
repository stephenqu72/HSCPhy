The correct answer is **B. The Geiger-Marsden experiment**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to recall the history of atomic physics and the specific contributions of key scientists:

*   **Rutherford’s Gold Foil Experiment:** In 1909, Ernest Rutherford supervised Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden in an experiment where they fired alpha particles at a thin sheet of gold foil. They observed that while most alpha particles passed straight through, a small percentage were deflected at large angles, and some even bounced directly back. This unexpected result led Rutherford to propose the **nuclear model of the atom**, which states that the atom consists of a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space. This is widely referred to as the **Geiger-Marsden experiment**.
*   **Millikan’s oil drop experiment (A):** This was used to determine the elementary charge ($e$) of a single electron.
*   **Chadwick’s discovery of the neutron (C):** James Chadwick discovered the neutron in 1932, which helped explain the existence of isotopes and the mass of the nucleus, but this happened long after Rutherford's initial nuclear model.
*   **Thomson’s charge to mass ratio experiment (D):** J.J. Thomson used cathode ray tubes to determine the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$) of the electron, which led to the discovery of the electron and his own "plum pudding" model of the atom, which Rutherford’s work eventually disproved.

By understanding the historical context of the nuclear model, you can identify the Geiger-Marsden experiment as the definitive evidence used by Rutherford.
