### 1. Determining the mass of the bolt ($m$)
First, we linearize the given formula for terminal velocity:
$$v_{terminal} = \sqrt{\frac{2Nmg}{\rho A C_d}}$$
Squaring both sides gives:
$$v_{terminal}^2 = \left( \frac{2mg}{\rho A C_d} \right) N$$
This is in the form of $y = mx$, where $y = v_{terminal}^2$ and $x = N$. The slope of the graph is equal to $\frac{2mg}{\rho A C_d}$.

**Calculating the slope:**
Using the data points $(N=6, v^2 \approx 0.72)$ and $(N=1, v^2 \approx 0.12)$:
$$\text{Slope} \approx \frac{0.72 - 0.12}{6 - 1} = \frac{0.60}{5} = 0.12 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2 \text{ per bolt}$$

$$0.12 = \frac{19.6m}{0.504}$$

### 2. Critical Analysis
**Effectiveness in modelling Millikan’s experiment:**
*   **Strengths:** Like Millikan’s, this experiment uses terminal velocity to balance forces (drag vs. gravity/buoyancy). It demonstrates quantization, as $N$ (the number of bolts) must be an integer, mimicking the quantization of electric charge ($q = ne$).
*   **Weaknesses/Limitations:**
    *   **Scale and Precision:** Millikan’s experiment dealt with sub-microscopic droplets where Brownian motion and Stokes' Law drag (viscous force) are dominant. This macro-scale experiment uses turbulent drag ($v^2$ dependence), which is not representative of the original physics.
    *   **Variables:** In Millikan's, the electric field is tuned to hold droplets stationary. Here, the objects are in constant motion, making it a "dynamic" rather than "static" balance experiment.
    *   **Consistency:** The spread in the data points on the graph (e.g., at $N=3$ and $N=5$) suggests experimental error or variations in the containers, which undermines the "identical container" assumption required to isolate $N$ as the sole variable.
