To solve this, let's evaluate each option based on the postulates of Special Relativity:

*   **A is incorrect:** According to the second postulate of Special Relativity, the speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) is constant for all inertial observers, regardless of their motion or the motion of the source. Neither observer will measure light traveling "faster" than the other.
*   **B is incorrect:** Length contraction occurs only when an object is in motion relative to the observer. An observer on the rocket is in the same reference frame as the rocket; therefore, they measure the **proper length** ($L_0$) of the rocket. The observer on Earth, who is in motion relative to the rocket, will perceive the rocket as being shorter (length contraction).
*   **C is incorrect:** Time dilation is relative. The observer on the Earth will see the clock on the rocket running slow. However, the observer on the rocket will see the Earth's clocks running slow. They do not "agree" on whose time is passing slower; it depends entirely on the reference frame of the observer.
*   **D is correct:** This is a statement of the **Principle of Relativity** (the first postulate of Special Relativity). It states that the laws of physics (including Newton’s First Law) are the same in all inertial frames of reference. Whether you are on the rocket or on Earth (both being inertial frames), an object subject to zero net force will continue to move at a constant velocity.
