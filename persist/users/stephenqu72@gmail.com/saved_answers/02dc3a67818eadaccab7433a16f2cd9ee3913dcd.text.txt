### Suggested Answer

The evolution of the atomic model reflects a transition from classical mechanical views to quantum mechanics:

1.  **Dalton:** Proposed the indivisible "billiard ball" model, stating atoms were solid, indestructible spheres.
2.  **Thomson:** Following his discovery of the electron, he proposed the "plum pudding" model—a sphere of positive charge with negatively charged electrons embedded throughout.
3.  **Rutherford:** Conducted the gold foil experiment, which revealed that the positive charge and most mass were concentrated in a dense central nucleus. He proposed a "planetary model" where electrons orbited the nucleus.
4.  **Bohr:** Refined Rutherford’s model by introducing quantized energy levels. He suggested that electrons orbit at fixed distances from the nucleus, explaining atomic emission spectra by proposing that electrons absorb or emit specific amounts of energy (photons) when jumping between these levels.
5.  **Schrödinger (Quantum Model):** While Bohr’s model worked well for hydrogen, it failed for multi-electron atoms. Schrödinger introduced wave mechanics. He proposed that electrons do not orbit in fixed paths but exist in "orbitals"—regions of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron. This is represented as an "electron cloud," shifting the view from deterministic orbits to probabilistic zones defined by wave functions.

### How to solve this question
*   **Structure:** Since this is an 8-mark question, treat it as a mini-essay. Use a chronological structure to show the "cause and effect" of scientific advancements.
*   **Key Scientists:** Ensure you explicitly name the scientists (Dalton, Thomson, Rutherford, Bohr, Schrödinger).
*   **The "Why":** Don't just list them; explain *why* the model changed. For example, mention that Rutherford's experiment disproved the plum pudding model, and Bohr's model was a reaction to the need to explain spectral lines.
*   **Synthesis:** Conclude by highlighting the paradigm shift from the "planetary/deterministic" view to the "probabilistic/wave" view inherent in Schrödinger's model.
