To solve this problem, we must break the vertical motion of the projectile into two distinct segments. We will assume standard gravity $g = 9.8\text{ ms}^{-2}$.

### Step 1: Initial Parameters
The initial vertical velocity is:
$$u_y = u \sin(\theta) = 50 \sin(30^\circ) = 50 \times 0.5 = 25\text{ ms}^{-1}$$

The peak of a standard trajectory occurs when $v_y = 0$. The height to the peak ($H_{peak}$) is:
$$H_{peak} = \frac{u_y^2}{2g} = \frac{25^2}{2 \times 9.8} \approx 31.89\text{ m}$$

### Step 2: Analyze the two segments
The problem states the external force starts acting **midway to the peak**. 
- The height of the midpoint is $h_1 = \frac{H_{peak}}{2} = \frac{31.89}{2} = 15.945\text{ m}$.
- We need the vertical velocity at this midpoint ($v_{mid}$). Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$:
$$v_{mid}^2 = u_y^2 - 2g(h_1) = 25^2 - 2(9.8)(15.945) = 625 - 312.5 = 312.5$$
$$v_{mid} = \sqrt{312.5} \approx 17.68\text{ ms}^{-1}$$

### Step 3: Calculate the additional height ($h_2$)
At the midpoint, an upward force is applied such that the net vertical force is halved. The new net acceleration is $a_{new} = -\frac{g}{2} = -4.9\text{ ms}^{-2}$.
The projectile continues to rise until its velocity becomes zero. Using $v_f^2 = v_{mid}^2 + 2a_{new}h_2$:
$$0 = 312.5 + 2(-4.9)h_2$$
$$9.8h_2 = 312.5$$
$$h_2 = \frac{312.5}{9.8} \approx 31.89\text{ m}$$

### Step 4: Total Maximum Height
The total maximum height $H_{max} = h_1 + h_2$:
$$H_{max} = 15.945 + 31.89 = 47.835\text{ m}$$

Rounding to the nearest whole number, we get **48 m**.

D. 49 m

The correct option is **C**.
