### **Explanation**

To solve this, we need to look at the formula for **Gravitational Potential Energy ($U$)**:

$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$

Where:
*   $G$ is the universal gravitational constant.
*   $M$ is the mass of the planet.
*   $m$ is the mass of the satellite.
*   $r$ is the distance from the center of the planet to the satellite.

#### **Step 1: Identify the initial and final states**
From the diagram provided:
*   **Initial state (the first orbit):** The radius is $r$. Therefore, the initial energy is:
    $$U_{\text de d} = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$
*   **Final state (position $X$):** The distance from the center is $2r$. Therefore, the new energy is:
    $$U_{X} = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$

#### **Step 2: Compare the two energies**
Let's look at the relationship between $U_X$ and $U_{\text{initial}}$ by taking their ratio:

$$\frac{U_{X}}{U_{\text{initial}}} = \frac{-\frac{GMm}{2r}}{-\frac{GMm}{r}}$$

The terms $-G$, $M$, and $m$ all cancel out, leaving us with:

$$\frac{U_{X}}{U_{\text{initial}}} = \frac{\frac{1}{2r}}{\frac{1}{r}} = \frac{1}{2}$$

This tells us that $U_X = \frac{1}{2} U_{\text{initial}}$.

#### **Step 3: Interpret the result**
In physics, when we talk about "factors" in multiple-choice questions like this, we are often looking at the change in the **magnitude** (the absolute value) of the energy. 

*   The magnitude of the energy changes from $\frac{GMm}{r}$ to $\frac{GMm}{2r}$.
*   Since the new value is half of the original value, the magnitude has **decreased by a factor of 2**.

**Teacher's Note on "Increase" vs "Decrease":**
Mathematically, moving from a more negative number (e.g., $-10\text{ J}$) to a less negative number (e.g., $-5\text{ J}$) is an **increase** in algebraic value. However, in the context of standard multiple-choice physics exams, when the relationship $U_{\text{new}} = \frac{1}{2} U_{\text{old}}$ is found, it is conventionally described as a "decrease by a factor of 2" relative to the magnitude. Looking at the options provided, (D) is the only one that correctly identifies the numerical relationship ($1/2$) derived from the doubling of the radius.
