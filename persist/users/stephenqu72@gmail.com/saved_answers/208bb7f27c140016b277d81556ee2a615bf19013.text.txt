### Answer

**a) Explain how this is possible with such different eccentricities.**
Kepler’s Third Law states that the square of the orbital period ($T^2$) is proportional to the cube of the semimajor axis ($a^3$), expressed as:
$$\frac{T^2}{a^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$$
Since the orbital period depends only on the mass of the central star ($M$) and the semimajor axis ($a$), the eccentricity of the orbit does not affect the period. As long as both planets share the same semimajor axis ($a$) and orbit the same star, their periods will be identical, regardless of whether the orbit is a perfect circle or a highly elongated ellipse.

**b) Describe how you can verify that Kepler’s Third Law is universal. In your answer, include the expected results.**
To verify universality, one must collect data for multiple satellite systems orbiting different central bodies (e.g., planets orbiting the Sun, moons orbiting Jupiter, or artificial satellites orbiting Earth). For each system, plot the square of the orbital period ($T^2$) against the cube of the semimajor axis ($a^3$).
*   **Expected Results:** For each distinct system, the plot should yield a straight line passing through the origin. The gradient of this line will be equal to $\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$. Because the gradient is constant for a specific central mass, it confirms the mathematical relationship $T^2 \propto a^3$ holds true for any gravitationally bound system in the universe.

### How to solve this
1.  **Part (a):** Focus on the definition of Kepler’s Third Law. Many students mistakenly think the "radius" determines the period. You must clarify that for an elliptical orbit, the distance parameter used in the law is specifically the **semimajor axis**, not the average distance or the radius of a circle. Once you establish that the period formula only contains $a$, $M$, and $G$, it becomes clear that eccentricity is an independent variable.
2.  **Part (b):** Think like a scientist conducting an experiment. To prove a law is "universal," you cannot just look at one system. You need to demonstrate that the *relationship* holds across different gravitational environments. Mentioning the graph of $T^2$ vs $a^3$ is the standard HSC expectation because it demonstrates the linear relationship derived from the formula. Always mention that the slope depends on the central mass, which is why you have to compare systems with different central masses to confirm the law's broad applicability.
