### Explanation
To solve this, we need to consider how air resistance (drag) acts on an object moving through a fluid:

1.  **Horizontal Motion:** Without air resistance, the horizontal velocity ($v_x$) is constant. With air resistance, a drag force acts opposite to the direction of motion, causing the horizontal velocity to decrease continuously throughout the flight. Consequently, the projectile will travel a shorter horizontal distance (range) than it would in a vacuum.
2.  **Vertical Motion:** 
    *   **On the way up:** Both gravity and air resistance act downwards. This causes the projectile to decelerate faster than it would under gravity alone, resulting in a **lower maximum height** than the ideal path.
    *   **On the way down:** Gravity acts downwards, but air resistance acts upwards (opposing the downward motion). This reduces the net acceleration, making the descent "flatter" or less steep towards the end compared to the initial steep climb.
3.  **The Shape:** Because of the combined effects:
    *   The peak is lower (ruling out B).
    *   The range is shorter (ruling out C).
    *   The trajectory becomes asymmetric. The descending part of the path is steeper than the ascending part due to the loss of horizontal velocity, which is represented in **Diagram D**. Specifically, the path falls more "vertically" at the end of the flight compared to the ideal parabola, which is characteristic of motion with air resistance.

Diagram D correctly captures the reduced range, reduced peak height, and the characteristic asymmetry of the path caused by the constant removal of kinetic energy via drag.
