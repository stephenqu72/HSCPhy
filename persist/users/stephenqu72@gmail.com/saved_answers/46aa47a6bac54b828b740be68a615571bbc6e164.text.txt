### Explanation
1.  **Identify the Star's Location:** Looking at the HR diagram, star **X** is located on the Main Sequence. Specifically, it is positioned towards the upper-left of the Main Sequence, corresponding to a high surface temperature (roughly $20,000 \text{ K}$) and high luminosity (significantly greater than $10^2 L_{sun}$).
2.  **Relate Temperature to Mass:** On the Main Sequence, stars with higher temperatures and higher luminosities are more massive. Because star X is very hot, it is a massive, early-type star (likely an O or early B-type star).
3.  **Nucleosynthesis Processes:**
    *   **Proton-proton (p-p) chain:** This is the dominant energy source for lower-mass stars (like our Sun) with lower core temperatures.
    *   **CNO cycle (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen):** This process is much more temperature-sensitive than the p-p chain. As the core temperature increases (which occurs in higher-mass stars), the efficiency of the CNO cycle increases rapidly. In massive stars (typically those significantly more massive than the Sun), the CNO cycle becomes the dominant pathway for hydrogen fusion.
4.  **Conclusion:** Since star X is a high-mass, high-temperature main-sequence star, the CNO cycle is its primary energy production mechanism. However, as the temperature is still within the range where the p-p chain can occur, it contributes a smaller, supplementary amount to the energy output.

Therefore, the dominant reaction is the CNO cycle, with a smaller contribution from the proton-proton chain.
