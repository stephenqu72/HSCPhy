To determine the age of a star cluster using an H-R diagram, we look at the **main-sequence turn-off point**. 

### Explanation:
1.  **The Concept:** Stars in a cluster are formed at approximately the same time. Massive, hot (high temperature $T$, high luminosity $L$) stars exhaust their hydrogen fuel much faster than smaller, cooler stars. As a cluster ages, the most massive stars evolve off the main sequence first, followed by progressively less massive stars. Therefore, the "turn-off point" (where stars leave the main sequence to become giants/supergiants) moves down the main sequence towards lower temperatures and luminosities as the cluster ages.
2.  **Evaluating the Clusters:**
    *   **Cluster Y:** The main sequence is still very full, extending far toward the top-left (hot, luminous stars are still present). This indicates that very few stars have left the main sequence. This is the **youngest** cluster.
    *   **Cluster X:** The turn-off point is further down the main sequence than in Y. Some of the more massive stars have already evolved off the main sequence. This is of **intermediate age**.
    *   **Cluster Z:** The turn-off point is the lowest of the three, with a significant number of stars having already moved into the giant branch. This cluster has had the most time for its stars to evolve, making it the **oldest** cluster.

3.  **Ordering:** Ordering from youngest to oldest, we get **Y, X, Z**.

Matching this with the options provided, the correct answer is **B**.
