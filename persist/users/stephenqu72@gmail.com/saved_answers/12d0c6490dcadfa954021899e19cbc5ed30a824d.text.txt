### Answer

Einstein’s model of light as discrete quanta (photons) was necessary to resolve the failure of the classical wave theory of light to explain the **photoelectric effect**.

**Why the classical model was insufficient:**
According to classical electromagnetic theory, light was considered a continuous wave. It predicted that the energy of the emitted electrons should depend on the *intensity* (amplitude) of the light, regardless of its frequency. It also predicted that if light of any frequency were shone on a metal surface for long enough, it would eventually provide enough energy for an electron to be ejected. However, experimental observations showed:
1.  **Threshold Frequency:** No electrons are ejected below a specific minimum frequency ($f_0$), regardless of the light's intensity.
2.  **Instantaneous Emission:** Emission occurs instantly if the frequency is high enough, with no time lag for energy accumulation.
3.  **Kinetic Energy dependence:** The maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons depends only on the frequency of the incident light, not its intensity.

**How Einstein’s model accounted for observations:**
Einstein proposed that light consists of packets of energy called photons, where the energy of each photon is given by $E = hf$ (where $h$ is Planck's constant and $f$ is frequency).
*   **Threshold frequency:** A single photon must have enough energy to overcome the work function ($\Phi$) of the metal (the energy required to release an electron). If $hf < \Phi$, no emission occurs, regardless of how many photons (intensity) are present.
*   **Instantaneous emission:** Energy is transferred in a discrete "one-to-one" interaction between a single photon and a single electron, meaning no time is needed to "accumulate" energy.
*   **Kinetic energy:** By the law of conservation of energy, the incident photon energy is split between the work function and the kinetic energy ($K_{max}$) of the electron:
    $$hf = \Phi + K_{max}$$
    Increasing the frequency increases the energy of the individual photons, thereby increasing the kinetic energy of the ejected electrons.
