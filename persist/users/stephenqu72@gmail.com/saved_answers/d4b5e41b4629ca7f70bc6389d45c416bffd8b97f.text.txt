To solve this problem, we need to combine the photoelectric effect equation with the physics of charged particles in a magnetic field.

### Step 1: Find the kinetic energy of the photoelectrons
According to Einstein's photoelectric equation, the maximum kinetic energy $K_{max}$ of a photoelectron is given by:
$$K_{max} = E_{photon} - \phi$$
For the initial state, $E_{photon} = 2\phi$:
$$K_{max,1} = 2\phi - \phi = \phi$$

### Step 2: Relate kinetic energy to the radius of the path
When a charged particle moves in a uniform magnetic field $B$, the magnetic force provides the centripetal force:
$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Solving for the radius $r$:
$$r = \frac{mv}{qB} = \frac{p}{qB}$$
Since kinetic energy $K = \frac{p^2}{2m}$, we have $p = \sqrt{2mK}$. Substituting this into the radius formula:
$$r = \frac{\sqrt{2mK}}{qB}$$
This shows that $r \propto \sqrt{K}$.

### Step 3: Analyze the change when photon energy is doubled
If the photon energy is doubled, the new energy is $E_{photon, 2} = 2 \times (2\phi) = 4\phi$.
The new maximum kinetic energy $K_{max, 2}$ is:
$$K_{max, 2} = 4\phi - \phi = 3\phi$$

Since $r \propto \sqrt{K}$, we can set up a ratio:
$$\frac{r_2}{r_1} = \sqrt{\frac{K_{max, 2}}{K_{max, 1}}}$$
$$\frac{r_2}{r} = \sqrt{\frac{3\phi}{\phi}} = \sqrt{3}$$
$$r_2 = \sqrt{3}r$$
