### Part (a)
**Answer:** The pointer will move to the right (assuming standard galvanometer orientation where current enters the left terminal). 

**Explanation:**
To solve this, use the **Right-Hand Palm Rule** (or Fleming’s Left-Hand Rule for motors).
1.  **Identify the magnetic field ($B$):** The field lines travel from the North pole to the South pole.
2.  **Identify the current ($I$):** Determine the direction of the current flow through the coil.
3.  **Apply the Motor Effect:** The force $F = BIL \sin(\theta)$ acts on the sides of the coil. The current flowing "up" on one side and "down" on the other creates a couple (torque), causing the coil to rotate.
4.  **Direction:** The pointer is attached to the coil; therefore, the torque generated by the interaction of the current-carrying coil within the radial magnetic field causes the pointer to deflect in the direction determined by the current's polarity.

### Part (b)
**Answer:** The student’s suggestion is highly effective and scientifically sound.

**Evaluation:**
*   **The Physics Principle:** When the coil of a galvanometer rotates within the permanent magnetic field, it cuts magnetic flux lines. If the terminals are connected (shorted), the motion of the coil induces an electromotive force (EMF) according to **Faraday’s Law** ($ \varepsilon = -N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} $).
*   **Lenz's Law:** Because the circuit is closed, this induced EMF drives an induced current through the coil. According to **Lenz’s Law**, this current will create a magnetic field that opposes the original motion (the rotation) of the coil.
*   **Damping effect:** This generates a "back-torque" that opposes the rotation of the coil, effectively providing **electromagnetic damping**.
*   **Conclusion:** By shorting the terminals, any kinetic energy gained by the coil during transport is rapidly dissipated as thermal energy due to the induced currents. This significantly reduces the oscillation and "free rotation" of the coil, thereby protecting the delicate suspension system from damage. 

This is a classic application of electromagnetic braking.
