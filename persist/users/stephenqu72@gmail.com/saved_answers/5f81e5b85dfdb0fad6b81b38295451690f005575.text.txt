To "assess" a contribution in an HSC Physics context means to make a judgment about the value, success, or impact of these scientific breakthroughs on our current atomic model. With 9 marks allocated, you need to provide a balanced, detailed, and logical progression of how each scientist corrected or refined the model of the previous one.

### Answer

The evolution of the atomic model was a cumulative process where each scientist addressed the limitations of their predecessor.

**1. Ernest Rutherford (The Nuclear Model)**
Rutherford’s 1909 Gold Foil Experiment provided the first evidence for a small, dense, positively charged nucleus. By observing alpha particles reflecting back at large angles, he disproved the "plum pudding" model. His contribution was fundamental: he established that the atom is mostly empty space with mass concentrated in the nucleus. However, his model was unstable; classical physics predicted that orbiting electrons should lose energy via electromagnetic radiation and spiral into the nucleus, causing the atom to collapse.

**2. Niels Bohr (The Quantised Model)**
Bohr addressed the instability of Rutherford’s model by applying Planck’s quantum theory. He proposed that electrons occupy stable, "stationary" energy levels (shells) and do not radiate energy unless they transition between levels. By equating the centripetal force to the electrostatic force, he defined these orbits using the angular momentum postulate $mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi}$. Bohr’s contribution successfully explained the emission spectra of hydrogen (Balmer series), providing a mathematical basis for atomic stability. While revolutionary, his model struggled to explain the spectra of multi-electron atoms and the Zeeman effect.

**3. James Chadwick (The Discovery of the Neutron)**
By the 1930s, there was a discrepancy between the atomic number and atomic mass. Chadwick solved this by discovering the neutron in 1932. Through the bombardment of beryllium with alpha particles, he identified a neutral particle with mass approximately equal to a proton. This was the final piece of the puzzle: it explained the existence of isotopes (atoms of the same element with different neutron counts) and provided the strong nuclear force necessary to overcome the electrostatic repulsion of protons within the nucleus, finally making the atomic model physically consistent.

*   **Mark Allocation:** Aim for roughly 3 marks per scientist. Each section should cover: The Experiment/Discovery $\rightarrow$ The Improvement to the Model $\rightarrow$ The Remaining Limitation (or finalization).
