### Answer
The correct answer is **C. To increase the linkage of the flux.**

---

### Explanation
To understand why an iron core is used in a transformer, we must look at the principle of electromagnetic induction:

1.  **Magnetic Permeability:** Iron is a ferromagnetic material, meaning it has a very high magnetic permeability. When an alternating current flows through the primary coil, it creates a changing magnetic field. Because the iron core is highly permeable, it significantly concentrates the magnetic field lines (magnetic flux) within itself.
2.  **Flux Linkage:** A transformer works by transferring energy from the primary coil to the secondary coil via a changing magnetic field. Without a core (i.e., an air-core transformer), much of the magnetic flux produced by the primary coil would "leak" into the surrounding air and fail to pass through the secondary coil.
3.  **Efficiency:** The iron core acts as a path of least resistance (low reluctance) for the magnetic flux. By guiding and confining the flux, it ensures that almost all the magnetic field lines generated by the primary coil pass through the turns of the secondary coil. This maximizes the **magnetic flux linkage** between the two coils, which is essential for efficient electromagnetic induction and the operation of the transformer.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** Using a solid iron core can actually *increase* heat generation due to eddy currents if it is not laminated.
*   **B:** The goal is to *couple* (link) the magnetic fields of the two coils, not separate them.
*   **D:** We limit eddy currents by using **laminated** silicon steel sheets (insulating layers), not by the act of using an iron core itself. In fact, iron cores are specifically designed to minimize the negative side effects of their conductivity while maximizing their magnetic benefits.
