To solve this problem, we use the principles of orbital mechanics, specifically the formula for the total mechanical energy ($E$) of a satellite in a circular orbit:
$$E = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$

**Constants used:**
*   $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2$
*   $M_{\text{Earth}} = 5.972 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$
*   $m = 920 \text{ kg}$

---

### (a) Find the total mechanical energy
The current radius $r_1 = 44,428 \text{ km} = 4.4428 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$.
$$E_1 = -\frac{(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})(920)}{2(4.4428 \times 10^7)}$$
$$E_1 \approx -4.12 \times 10^9 \text{ J}$$

### (b) Work done to change radius
Work required is the change in total mechanical energy: $W = E_2 - E_1$.
The new radius $r_2 = 42,164 \text{ km} = 4.2164 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$.
$$E_2 = -\frac{(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})(920)}{2(4.2164 \times 10^7)} \approx -4.34 \times 10^9 \text{ J}$$
$$W = (-4.34 \times 10^9) - (-4.12 \times 10^9) = -2.2 \times 10^8 \text{ J}$$

### (c) Justification
The work done is negative ($W < 0$), which means the satellite must **lose energy**. Moving from a higher orbit to a lower orbit requires a decrease in total mechanical energy. The satellite must perform a burn (retro-burn) to reduce its kinetic energy sufficiently to descend to the lower geostationary orbital radius.

---

### How to solve this type of problem:
1.  **Identify the system:** Recognize that gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy combine to form the total mechanical energy $E = -GMm/2r$.
2.  **Unit conversion:** Always convert kilometers to meters ($1 \text{ km} = 1000 \text{ m}$) before calculation.
3.  **Work-Energy Theorem:** Understand that work done on a system is the change in the state of its energy ($\Delta E$).
4.  **Directionality:** Note that as $r$ decreases, the denominator in the energy formula decreases, making the value "more negative" (lower energy). Therefore, moving inward requires a loss of total energy.
