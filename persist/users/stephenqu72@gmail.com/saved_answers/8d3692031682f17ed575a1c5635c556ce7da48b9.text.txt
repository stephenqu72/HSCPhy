### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) Beta decay**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we must understand the nuclear reactions involved in the **CNO cycle** (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle). The CNO cycle is a catalytic fusion process that converts four hydrogen nuclei ($^1H$) into one helium nucleus ($^4He$). 

The cycle consists of a series of proton captures followed by radioactive decay steps. Let's look at the specific decay steps within the cycle:

1.  $^{12}C + ^1H \rightarrow ^{13}N + \gamma$
2.  $^{13}N \rightarrow ^{13}C + e^+ + \nu_e$ (**Beta-plus decay**)
3.  $^{13}C + ^1H \rightarrow ^{14}N + \gamma$
4.  $^{14}N + ^1H \rightarrow ^{15}O + \gamma$
5.  $^{15}O \rightarrow ^{15}N + e^+ + \nu_e$ (**Beta-plus decay**)
6.  $^{15}N + ^1H \rightarrow ^{12}C + ^4He$

In the complete cycle, there are two instances where a nucleus undergoes **beta-plus decay** ($^{13}N \rightarrow ^{13}C$ and $^{15}O \rightarrow ^{15}N$). While gamma radiation is emitted during the proton capture reactions, the actual "radioactive decay" steps that convert the proton-rich intermediate nuclei into stable or semi-stable isotopes are the beta-plus decays. Therefore, beta decay is the primary mechanism of radioactive decay occurring within the cycle's sequence.
