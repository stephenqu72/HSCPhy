To determine the correct graph for the induced electromotive force (emf), we use **Faraday's Law of Induction**, which states that the induced emf ($\varepsilon$) in a circuit is proportional to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux ($\Phi$) with respect to time ($t$):

$$\varepsilon = - \frac{d\Phi}{dt}$$

This means the value of the emf at any point in time is equal to the **negative gradient** (slope) of the magnetic flux-time graph. Let's analyze the flux graph in three distinct stages:

1.  **First stage (increasing gradient):** The flux is increasing with an increasing slope (it is concave up). The gradient is positive and increasing. Since $\varepsilon = - \text{gradient}$, the emf must be negative and becoming increasingly negative (moving further away from zero in the negative direction).
2.  **Second stage (constant flux):** The flux is constant (a flat horizontal line). The gradient is $0$. Therefore, $\varepsilon = 0$.
3.  **Third stage (constant negative gradient):** The flux is decreasing linearly (a straight line with a constant negative slope). Since the gradient is constant and negative, the emf must be a constant positive value (because $\varepsilon = -(\text{negative value}) = \text{positive value}$).
