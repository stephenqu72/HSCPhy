### Analysis of Options:

*   **A and D:** Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation is indeed a primary pillar of evidence for the Big Bang. It represents the "afterglow" of the early, hot universe. Option D correctly identifies that as the universe expanded, the high-energy radiation from the early universe underwent cosmological redshift (stretching of wavelength) to its current microwave state. Option A refers to variations in temperature (anisotropies), which supports the Big Bang but the reason given is slightly less direct in explaining the *expansion* mechanism compared to the wavelength stretching.
*   **B:** While plotting velocity against distance (Hubble’s Law) does allow us to determine the Hubble constant and estimate the age of the universe, this is a method derived *from* the observed expansion, rather than a fundamental "reason" for the evidence itself in the context of this specific curriculum.
*   **C:** Redshift is the observation that light from distant galaxies is shifted toward longer (redder) wavelengths. While it confirms galaxies are receding, the reason "forces acting on them from the Big Bang" is physically incorrect. The expansion of the universe is not caused by an explosion pushing galaxies through space, but rather the expansion of space itself.

### The Correct Answer:
**D** is the most accurate statement. The expansion of the universe caused the light waves (photons) emitted shortly after the Big Bang to stretch significantly over billions of years, transitioning from high-energy radiation into the microwave spectrum we observe today ($2.73 \, \text{K}$). This provides direct evidence of a cooling, expanding history.
