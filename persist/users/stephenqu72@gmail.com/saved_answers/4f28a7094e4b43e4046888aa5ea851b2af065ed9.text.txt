To solve this problem, we must derive the relationship between the speed of light ($c$), the frequency of the rotating mirror ($f$), the distance between the mirrors ($D$), and the displacement of the reflected beam ($\Delta x$).

### 1. Derivation of the Formula
The time taken for light to travel to the fixed mirror and back is $t = \frac{2D}{c}$.
During this time, the mirror rotates by an angle $\Delta \theta$. If the mirror rotates at $f$ rotations per second, the angular velocity is $\omega = 2\pi f$. Thus:
$$\Delta \theta = \omega t = (2\pi f) \times \left(\frac{2D}{c}\right) = \frac{4\pi f D}{c}$$

The light is deflected by $2\Delta \theta$. Given the approximation $\tan(2\Delta \theta) = 2\Delta \theta = \frac{\Delta x}{r}$, we substitute $\Delta \theta$:
$$\frac{\Delta x}{r} = 2 \times \left(\frac{4\pi f D}{c}\right) = \frac{8\pi f D}{c}$$
Rearranging for $c$:
$$c = \frac{8\pi f D r}{\Delta x} = \left(\frac{8\pi D r}{\Delta x}\right) f$$
Converting $\Delta x$ from mm to m ($\Delta x \times 10^{-3}$):
$$c = \frac{8\pi \times 20 \times 5}{\Delta x \times 10^{-3}} \times f = \frac{8000\pi}{\Delta x} \times f$$

### 2. Calculation
To find $c$, we plot $f$ (y-axis) vs $\Delta x$ (x-axis in m). However, it is easier to calculate $c$ for each data point and take the average or perform a linear regression.
Converting $\Delta x$ to meters:
*   $0.0009\text{ m}, 95\text{ Hz} \rightarrow c = \frac{8 \times \pi \times 20 \times 5 \times 95}{0.0009} \approx 2.65 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
*   $0.0011\text{ m}, 115\text{ Hz} \rightarrow c \approx 2.62 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
*   $0.0014\text{ m}, 135\text{ Hz} \rightarrow c \approx 2.42 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
*   $0.0017\text{ m}, 180\text{ Hz} \rightarrow c \approx 2.66 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
*   $0.0022\text{ m}, 230\text{ Hz} \rightarrow c \approx 2.63 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$

Taking the average of these values gives $c \approx 2.60 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.

### 3. Comment on Validity
The calculated value ($2.60 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$) is significantly lower than the accepted value of $299,792,458 \text{ m/s}$.
*   **Error Analysis:** The percentage error is approximately $13\%$. 
*   **Validity:** The result is **not highly valid** as a precise measurement of the speed of light. Possible sources of error include:
    1.  **Parallax/Measurement Error:** Small errors in measuring $\Delta x$ with a microscope lead to large variations in $c$.
    2.  **Assumption Validity:** The small-angle approximation $\tan(2\Delta \theta) \approx 2\Delta \theta$ becomes less accurate at higher mirror frequencies.
    3.  **Experimental Precision:** Friction in the rotating mirror or vibrations could affect the frequency consistency.
