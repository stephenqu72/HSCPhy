To solve this question, we must understand the core discovery made by Edwin Hubble in the 1920s regarding the nature of the universe.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) Observation of redshifts dominating the spectra of observable galaxies.**

### Explanation
1.  **The Doppler Effect in Astronomy:** When an object emitting light moves away from an observer, the light waves are "stretched," shifting the spectral lines toward the red end of the electromagnetic spectrum (longer wavelengths). This is known as **redshift**.
2.  **Hubble's Discovery:** By measuring the spectra of distant galaxies, Hubble observed that almost all of them exhibited a redshift. Furthermore, he observed that the degree of this redshift was proportional to their distance from Earth, a relationship now known as Hubble's Law ($v = H_0d$).
3.  **Why (D) is the best answer:** While (A) mentions redshifts in "distant" galaxies, (D) is more precise because the defining evidence for the *expansion of the universe as a whole* was the systematic observation that redshifts dominated the spectra of galaxies across the observable universe. This showed that, on a cosmic scale, objects were receding from us in every direction, which is the hallmark of space itself expanding.
4.  **Why the others are incorrect:** 
    *   (B) is incorrect because blueshifts indicate galaxies moving *toward* us (e.g., the Andromeda galaxy), which contradicts an expanding universe model.
    *   (C) is factually true—Hubble did use Cepheid variables to prove that galaxies were outside our Milky Way—but this proved the *scale* of the universe, not the *expansion* of the universe. The expansion was inferred specifically from the redshift data.
