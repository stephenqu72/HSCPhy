To determine the correct answer, we must examine the limitations of Huygens' wave theory of light.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) Photo-electric effect**.

### Explanation
Huygens proposed that light behaves as a mechanical wave propagating through an all-encompassing medium called the "luminiferous aether." While his model was highly successful in explaining wave-like properties, it failed to account for quantum phenomena:

1.  **Interference, Diffraction, and Refraction (A, B, and C):** These are all wave phenomena. Huygens' principle—which states that every point on a wavefront is a source of secondary wavelets—successfully explains how light bends around obstacles (diffraction), how it combines to form patterns (interference), and how its speed changes at a boundary (refraction).
2.  **Photo-electric effect (D):** This phenomenon involves the emission of electrons from a material when struck by light. Classical wave theory (including Huygens' model) predicts that the energy of the emitted electrons should depend on the intensity (brightness) of the light. However, experiments showed that electron emission depends on the *frequency* of light, not the intensity. This could only be explained by Einstein’s particle model of light, where light is quantized into discrete packets called photons, described by the equation:
    $$E = hf$$
    where $E$ is the energy, $h$ is Planck's constant, and $f$ is the frequency. Since Huygens' model relied purely on continuous wave propagation, it could not account for this particle-like behavior.
