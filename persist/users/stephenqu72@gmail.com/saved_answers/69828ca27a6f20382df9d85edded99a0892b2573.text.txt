To solve this problem, we use the principles of double-slit interference.

### Given Data:
*   Wavelength ($\lambda$) = $854\text{ nm} = 854 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}$
*   Slit separation ($d$) = $0.200\text{ mm} = 0.200 \times 10^{-3}\text{ m} = 2.00 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}$
*   Screen distance ($L$) = $2.5\text{ m}$
*   Order ($n$) = $1$

---

### (a) Determine the angle of the first-order maximum

The condition for constructive interference (maxima) in a double-slit experiment is given by:
$$d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$$

Rearranging to solve for $\theta$:
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{n\lambda}{d}$$
$$\sin(\theta) = \frac{1 \times 854 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}}{2.00 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}}$$
$$\sin(\theta) = 0.00427$$
$$\theta = \arcsin(0.00427) \approx 0.2446^\circ$$

**Answer:** The angle is approximately **$0.245^\circ$**.

---

### (b) Calculate the distance on the screen between the first-order maximum and the central maximum

The distance ($y$) on the screen can be found using the relationship:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{L}$$
$$y = L \tan(\theta)$$

Using the angle found in part (a):
$$y = 2.5 \times \tan(0.2446^\circ)$$
$$y \approx 2.5 \times 0.00427003 \approx 0.010675\text{ m}$$

Converting to millimetres:
$$y \approx 10.7\text{ mm}$$

**Answer:** The distance is approximately **$10.7\text{ mm}$**.

---

### How to solve it:
1.  **Identify the physics principle:** Since we are dealing with double-slit interference, use the formula $d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$ for maxima.
2.  **Unit conversion:** Always ensure your units are in SI base units (metres) before plugging them into the formula.
3.  **Use trigonometry:** For part (b), remember that while small-angle approximation ($\sin(\theta) \approx \tan(\theta)$) is often valid for these problems, using the explicit tangent function ensures higher precision.
