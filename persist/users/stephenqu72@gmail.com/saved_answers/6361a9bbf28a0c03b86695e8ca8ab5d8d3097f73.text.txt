To solve this problem, we must apply the principle of conservation of energy: the electrical potential energy gained by a charged particle moving through a potential difference $V$ is converted into kinetic energy.

### Part (b)
**Justification:**
The electrical work done on the particle is $W = qV$, and the kinetic energy gained is $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Equating these:
$$qV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \implies v^2 = \left( \frac{2q}{m} \right) V$$
This indicates a linear relationship where the gradient $m_{grad} = \frac{2q}{m}$.

**1. Calculate the gradient:**
Using the points $(0,0)$ and $(500, 5.2 \times 10^9)$:
$$m_{grad} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{5.2 \times 10^9 - 0}{500 - 0} = 1.04 \times 10^7 \text{ C kg}^{-1} \text{ V}^{-1}$$

**2. Check the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$):**
$$1.04 \times 10^7 = \frac{2q}{m} \implies \frac{q}{m} = \frac{1.04 \times 10^7}{2} = 5.2 \times 10^6 \text{ C kg}^{-1}$$

**3. Compare with the accepted value for an electron:**
The accepted charge-to-mass ratio for an electron is:
$$\frac{e}{m_e} = \frac{1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}}{9.109 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}} \approx 1.76 \times 10^{11} \text{ C kg}^{-1}$$

**Conclusion:**
Since the calculated value ($5.2 \times 10^6 \text{ C kg}^{-1}$) is significantly lower than the accepted value for an electron ($1.76 \times 10^{11} \text{ C kg}^{-1}$), the student's hypothesis is **incorrect**. The particles have a much higher mass than an electron.
