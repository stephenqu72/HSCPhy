### Answer
The correct answer is **(B)**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must consider the physics of circular motion on a banked track. 

1.  **Ideal Banking:** On a frictionless banked track, a car can travel around a curve at a specific "design speed" where the horizontal component of the normal force ($N \sin \theta$) provides the necessary centripetal force. In this ideal case, the speed is determined solely by the radius of the turn and the angle of the bank.
2.  **The Role of Friction:**
    *   If the car travels **faster** than the design speed, it has a tendency to slide *up* the bank. Friction acts down the slope to oppose this motion, effectively contributing to the centripetal force required to keep the car on the track.
    *   If the car travels **slower** than the design speed, it has a tendency to slide *down* the bank. Friction acts up the slope to prevent this.
3.  **Maximum Speed:** Friction allows the car to exceed the design speed that would be possible on a frictionless surface. By providing an additional component of force directed toward the center of the turn, friction increases the net centripetal force available, which allows for a higher maximum speed before the car loses traction and slides outward.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A)** is incorrect because friction is a significant factor in determining the upper limit of the speed range.
*   **(C)** is a distracter; banking is always oriented toward the center of the turn by definition in this context.
*   **(D)** is incorrect because, as established in point 1, a car can turn on a banked track without friction at the exact design speed.
