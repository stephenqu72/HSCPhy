To solve this problem, we must determine the half-life of the radioactive source from the provided graph and then use that to calculate the remaining mass after $7$ hours and $20$ minutes.

### 1. Determine the half-life ($t_{1/2}$)
The initial mass ($N_0$) at $t = 0$ is $120\text{ g}$.
The half-life occurs when the mass is reduced to half of the initial value, which is:
$$\frac{120\text{ g}}{2} = 60\text{ g}$$
Looking at the graph, the mass is $60\text{ g}$ when the time $t = 2\text{ hours}$.
Therefore, the half-life $t_{1/2} = 2\text{ hours}$.

### 2. Calculate the number of half-lives elapsed
We need to find the mass remaining at $7\text{ hours}$ and $20\text{ minutes}$.
First, convert the total time into hours:
$$7\text{ hours and } 20\text{ minutes} = 7 + \frac{20}{60}\text{ hours} = 7.333...\text{ hours} = \frac{22}{3}\text{ hours}$$
Now, find the number of half-lives ($n$):
$$n = \frac{\text{total time}}{t_{1/2}} = \frac{22/3}{2} = \frac{11}{3} \approx 3.6667$$

### 3. Calculate the remaining mass ($N_t$)
Using the radioactive decay formula:
$$N_t = N_0 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n$$
Substitute the values:
$$N_t = 120 \times \left(0.5\right)^{3.6667}$$
$$N_t = 120 \times 0.078745...$$
$$N_t \approx 9.449\text{ g}$$

Rounding to two decimal places, we get **$9.45\text{ g}$**.
