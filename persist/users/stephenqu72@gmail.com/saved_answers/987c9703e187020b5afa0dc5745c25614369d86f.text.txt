To find the energy released in the nuclear reaction, we need to calculate the mass defect and then convert that mass into energy using the mass-energy equivalence principle.

### Step 1: Calculate the mass defect ($\Delta m$)
The mass defect is the difference between the total mass of the reactants and the total mass of the products:
$$\Delta m = (\text{mass of reactants}) - (\text{mass of products})$$

*   **Mass of reactants:** 
    $m(^{2}H) + m(^{3}H) = 2.014102 \, u + 3.016049 \, u = 5.030151 \, u$
*   **Mass of products:** 
    $m(^{4}He) + m(^{1}n) = 4.002602 \, u + 1.008665 \, u = 5.011267 \, u$

$$\Delta m = 5.030151 \, u - 5.011267 \, u = 0.018884 \, u$$

### Step 2: Convert mass defect to energy
We know that $1 \, u$ is equivalent to approximately $931.5 \, MeV/c^2$. Therefore, the energy released ($E$) is:
$$E = \Delta m \times 931.5 \, MeV/u$$
$$E = 0.018884 \, u \times 931.5 \, MeV/u$$
$$E \approx 17.59 \, MeV$$

Rounding to one decimal place, we get **$17.6 \, MeV$**.
