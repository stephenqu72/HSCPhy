### Part (a)
**Answer:** The torque due to $F_1$ is $100\text{ Nm}$ (clockwise).

**Explanation:**
Torque ($\tau$) is defined as the product of the force and the perpendicular distance from the pivot point: $\tau = F \cdot d_\perp$.
In the diagram, force $F_1$ is applied $1.0\text{ m}$ away from the centre $O$ along a horizontal radius. Since the force is acting straight down (vertically) and the distance is measured along the horizontal radius, the force is perfectly perpendicular to the lever arm.
$$\tau_1 = 100\text{ N} \times 1.0\text{ m} = 100\text{ Nm}$$
Because this force pushes the left side of the disk downwards, it creates a **clockwise** torque.

### Part (b)
**Answer:** The magnitude of $F_2$ is approximately $133.3\text{ N}$.

**Explanation:**
For the disk to remain stationary (in rotational equilibrium), the net torque must be zero:
$$\sum \tau = 0 \implies \tau_{clockwise} = \tau_{anticlockwise}$$
We know the clockwise torque is $100\text{ Nm}$. Therefore, $F_2$ must produce an anticlockwise torque of $100\text{ Nm}$.

The torque formula is $\tau = r F \sin(\theta)$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the position vector and the force vector.
1. The distance from the centre is $r = 1.5\text{ m}$.
2. The angle between the vertical axis and the position vector of $F_2$ is $120^\circ$. Since $F_2$ acts vertically downwards, we need the angle between the lever arm and the force vector.
3. If we look at the right side of the disk, the angle between the horizontal and the position vector is $180^\circ - 120^\circ = 60^\circ$ below the horizontal. The angle between the vertical force and the position vector is therefore $90^\circ - 60^\circ = 30^\circ$ (or you can use the perpendicular distance method $r_\perp = r \sin(60^\circ)$).
4. Using $\tau_2 = F_2 \cdot r \sin(\phi)$:
$$100 = F_2 \times 1.5 \times \sin(60^\circ)$$
$$F_2 = \frac{100}{1.5 \times 0.866}$$
$$F_2 = \frac{100}{1.299} \approx 76.96 \text{ (if angle used was 60)}...$$

**Wait, let's look closer at the geometry:**
The angle between the position vector and the vertical is $120^\circ$. The lever arm $d_\perp$ for $F_2$ is $r \sin(180^\circ - 120^\circ) = 1.5 \sin(60^\circ)$.
$$100 = F_2 \times 1.5 \times \sin(60^\circ)$$
$$F_2 = \frac{100}{1.5 \times 0.866} \approx 76.98\text{ N}$$
