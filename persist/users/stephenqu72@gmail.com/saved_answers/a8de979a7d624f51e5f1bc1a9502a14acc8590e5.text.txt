### Explanation

To solve this problem, we need to consider two key physics relationships: the relationship between power, voltage, and current, and the formula for power loss in a transmission line.

**1. The Relationship between Power, Voltage, and Current**
Electrical power is given by the formula:
$$P = VI$$
If the power $P$ remains constant and the voltage $V$ is doubled ($2V$), the current $I$ must be halved to maintain the same power output:
$$P = (2V) \times I_{new} \implies I_{new} = \frac{I}{2}$$

**2. The Power Loss Equation**
Energy lost as heat in the transmission lines (due to electrical resistance $R$) is calculated using the Joule heating formula:
$$P_{loss} = I^2R$$

**3. Applying the Change**
If the current is halved ($I_{new} = 0.5I$), we substitute this into the power loss formula:
$$P_{loss, new} = (0.5I)^2 \times R$$
$$P_{loss, new} = 0.25 \times I^2R$$
$$P_{loss, new} = 0.25 \times P_{loss, original}$$

**Conclusion**
The new power loss is $25\%$ of the original power loss. This represents a reduction of:
$$100\% - 25\% = 75\%$$
Therefore, doubling the voltage results in a $75\%$ reduction in energy wasted due to resistance.
