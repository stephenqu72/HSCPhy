### Model Answer

The evolution of the universe from the Big Bang to its current state is governed by the interplay of fundamental forces, specifically the **strong nuclear force** and **gravity**.

**1. The Role of the Strong Nuclear Force (Nucleosynthesis):**
After the Big Bang, as the universe cooled, the strong nuclear force became the critical mechanism for building matter. It acts as the "glue" that overcomes the electrostatic repulsion between positively charged protons. Within the first few minutes (Big Bang Nucleosynthesis), the strong force allowed protons and neutrons to bind together to form the nuclei of light elements, primarily helium ($^4He$) and trace amounts of lithium. Later, within the cores of stars, the strong force continued to fuse lighter nuclei into heavier elements, enabling the chemical complexity of the universe we see today. Without the strong force, atomic nuclei would not exist, and the universe would consist only of isolated protons (hydrogen).

**2. The Role of Gravity (Structural Formation):**
While the strong force builds nuclei, gravity acts on a cosmic scale to organize matter into celestial objects. Following the "recombination" era—where atoms formed and the universe became transparent—matter was distributed in a nearly uniform, yet slightly "clumpy," state. Gravity acted on these density fluctuations, drawing matter together into clouds of gas. As these clouds became massive, gravity caused them to collapse and increase in temperature, leading to the ignition of the first stars. On larger scales, gravity orchestrated the assembly of these stars into galaxies, and galaxies into clusters and superclusters, forming the "cosmic web" that structures the current universe.
