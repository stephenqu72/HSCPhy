### Teacher's Guide and Solutions

To solve these problems, we use Kepler’s Third Law, which states that the square of the orbital period $T$ is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius $r$ ($T^2 \propto r^3$). Specifically:
$$\frac{r_1^3}{T_1^2} = \frac{r_2^3}{T_2^2}$$

---

#### (a) Calculate the ratio of the orbital radii
We need the ratio $r_E / r_J$. We are given:
*   Earth's period ($T_E$): $1\text{ year} = 365.25\text{ days}$
*   Jupiter's synodic period ($S$): $400\text{ days}$

First, find Jupiter's sidereal period ($T_J$):
$$\frac{1}{S} = \frac{1}{T_E} - \frac{1}{T_J} \implies \frac{1}{400} = \frac{1}{365.25} - \frac{1}{T_J}$$
$$ \frac{1}{T_J} \approx 0.002738 - 0.0025 = 0.000238 \implies T_J \approx 4200\text{ days}$$
Ratio of radii:
$$\frac{r_E^3}{r_J^3} = \frac{T_E^2}{T_J^2} = \left(\frac{365.25}{4200}\right)^2 \approx (0.08696)^2 \approx 0.00756$$
$$\frac{r_E}{r_J} = \sqrt[3]{0.00756} \approx 0.196 \approx 1:5.1$$

#### (b) Explain the variation of the apparent duration
Rømer realized that because light has a finite speed, the time taken for the light from Io to reach Earth depends on the distance between Earth and Jupiter. As Earth moves from point A (closest to Jupiter) to the opposite side of its orbit (farthest from Jupiter), the light has a greater distance to travel. Consequently, the eclipses appear delayed (longer duration between observations) as Earth moves away, and appear to "accelerate" or occur earlier as Earth moves closer back toward point A.

#### (c) Explain the slanting effect
The "slanting" occurs because the distance between Earth and Jupiter is not purely changing based on Earth's motion alone; Jupiter is also moving in its own orbit. The "fitted sine curve" assumes a circular orbit where the distance variation is perfectly symmetrical. However, because Jupiter moves while Earth orbits, the Earth-Jupiter distance function is slightly skewed (asymmetric), leading to the "slanted" actual curve.

#### (d) Maximum deviation
The maximum deviation occurs when the rate of change of the distance between Earth and Jupiter is zero (i.e., when the Earth-Jupiter distance is at its extreme). In a simplified circular model, this happens at $1/4$ and $3/4$ of the synodic period. Given a $400$-day synodic period:
$$t = \frac{400}{4} = 100\text{ days}$$
The maximum deviation occurs approximately **100 days** after Earth is at point A.
