To solve this problem, we must apply Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$, where the power output $P$ is the rate of energy release over time: $P = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t}c^2$.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Identify the given values:**
*   Rate of mass decrease ($\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t}$): $4.0 \times 10^{-6} \text{ kg hour}^{-1}$
*   Speed of light ($c$): $3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

**2. Convert the time unit to SI base units (seconds):**
The rate is given per hour. Since there are $3600 \text{ seconds}$ in an hour ($60 \text{ minutes} \times 60 \text{ seconds}$), we divide the mass rate by $3600$:
$$\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t} = \frac{4.0 \times 10^{-6} \text{ kg}}{3600 \text{ s}} \approx 1.11 \times 10^{-9} \text{ kg s}^{-1}$$

**3. Calculate the Power ($P$):**
Using $P = (\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t})c^2$:
$$P = (1.11 \times 10^{-9} \text{ kg s}^{-1}) \times (3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m s}^{-1})^2$$
$$P = (1.11 \times 10^{-9}) \times (9.0 \times 10^{16} \text{ m}^2 \text{ s}^{-2})$$
$$P \approx 10 \times 10^7 \text{ Watts}$$
$$P \approx 100 \times 10^6 \text{ Watts} = 100 \text{ MW}$$

**Conclusion:**
Comparing our result to the options provided, the correct power output is $100 \text{ MW}$.

The correct option is **(C)**.
