### Explanation
To solve this question, we need to understand the core contribution of Louis de Broglie to quantum physics:

1.  **The De Broglie Hypothesis:** In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that matter, specifically electrons, exhibits wave-like properties. He extended the concept of wave-particle duality (previously applied to light by Einstein) to all particles with momentum.
2.  **The Relationship:** He formulated this mathematically as:
    $$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$
    where $\lambda$ is the wavelength, $h$ is Planck's constant, $m$ is the mass of the particle, and $v$ is its velocity.
3.  **Application to the Atom:** De Broglie used this wave nature to explain why electrons in Bohr’s model are restricted to specific, stable orbits. He proposed that an electron can only orbit the nucleus if its path forms a **standing wave**. This means the circumference of the orbit must be an integer multiple of the electron's wavelength ($2\pi r = n\lambda$).

**Why the other options are incorrect in the context of "De Broglie's ideas":**
*   **B and C:** These concepts relate more to the later work of Schrödinger (wave mechanics) and Born (statistical interpretation of the wavefunction). While they built upon de Broglie's work, the "cloud" and "probability" interpretations are distinct from de Broglie's initial proposal of the wave-nature of matter itself.
*   **D:** While electrons have wave properties, they are not "electromagnetic waves" in the sense of classical light (photons); they are matter waves.

Therefore, the statement that best summarizes his fundamental contribution is that electrons exhibit wave-like characteristics.
