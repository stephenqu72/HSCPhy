To solve this problem, we must apply the right-hand grip rule to determine the direction of the magnetic field and then use the motor effect (Lorentz force) principles to infer the charge distribution.

### 1. Determining the Direction of the Magnetic Field
Look at the two solenoids and their battery connections:
*   **Left solenoid:** Current flows into the coil such that it comes over the top and down the front faces. Using the right-hand grip rule, curling your fingers in the direction of the current, your thumb points to the **right**.
*   **Right solenoid:** Current flows into the coil such that it comes over the top and down the front faces. Your thumb also points to the **right**.
*   Since both solenoids generate magnetic fields pointing to the right, the net magnetic field $B$ between them is directed to the **right**.

### 2. Determining the Charge at P
Inside a conductor moving through a magnetic field, the free electrons experience a magnetic force given by the Lorentz force law: $F = qv \times B$.
*   The cylinder is moving **downward** ($v$).
*   The magnetic field $B$ points to the **right**.
*   Using the right-hand rule for the force on a **positive** charge ($q$ is positive):
    *   Point your fingers in the direction of $v$ (down).
    *   Curl your fingers toward $B$ (right).
    *   Your thumb points **out of the page** (towards you).
*   However, in a metal conductor, it is the **electrons** (negative charges) that move. The magnetic force on a negative charge is in the opposite direction of the force on a positive charge. Therefore, the electrons are pushed **into the page**.
*   As electrons accumulate at the end of the cylinder that is "into the page," the end P (which is "out of the page") is left with a deficiency of electrons, making it **positive**.

### Conclusion
*   Magnetic field direction: **Right**
*   Charge at P: **Positive**
