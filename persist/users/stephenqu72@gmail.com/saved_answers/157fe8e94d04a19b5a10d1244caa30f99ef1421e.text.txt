### Answer

**The Electron: J.J. Thomson’s Cathode Ray Experiment**
In 1897, J.J. Thomson investigated cathode rays using a vacuum tube. By applying both electric and magnetic fields, he measured the deflection of the rays. He found that the ratio of charge to mass ($q/m$) was constant regardless of the cathode material, suggesting that these rays were composed of universal subatomic particles, which he named electrons. By determining the $q/m$ ratio and later, in conjunction with Millikan’s Oil Drop experiment (which determined the charge $e$), the mass of the electron was accurately calculated, confirming it as a fundamental constituent of matter.

**The Neutron: Chadwick’s Beryllium Bombardment**
In 1932, James Chadwick investigated anomalous radiation produced when alpha particles bombarded beryllium. This radiation was highly penetrating and unaffected by electric or magnetic fields, indicating it was neutral. By observing the impact of this "neutral radiation" on paraffin wax, he used the laws of conservation of momentum and energy to analyze the resulting recoil of protons. Chadwick concluded that the radiation consisted of particles with a mass approximately equal to the proton but with zero charge, leading to the discovery of the neutron. This solved the mystery of the "missing mass" in the nucleus.
