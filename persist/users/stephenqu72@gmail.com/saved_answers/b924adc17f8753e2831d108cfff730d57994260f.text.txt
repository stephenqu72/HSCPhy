To determine the composition of the star, we use the principle of **spectroscopic analysis**. Each element has a unique "fingerprint" of absorption lines. If a star contains a specific element, the characteristic dark absorption lines of that element will be present at the exact same positions in the star’s absorption spectrum.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Analyze the Star's Spectrum:** Look at the bottom spectrum labeled "absorption spectrum from a star." It contains several distinct vertical dark lines.
2.  **Compare Element by Element:**
    *   **Calcium:** The calcium spectrum has lines in the blue, green, and red regions. If you scan vertically from the star's spectrum, you will see that these lines align perfectly with the dark lines in the star's spectrum. **(Calcium is present)**
    *   **Sodium:** The sodium spectrum has two characteristic dark lines close together in the yellow-orange region. Looking at the star's spectrum, those same two lines appear in the corresponding position. **(Sodium is present)**
    *   **Iron:** The iron spectrum has a series of lines in the purple and blue regions. When comparing these to the star's spectrum, they align with the dark lines present in the star. **(Iron is present)**
    *   **Magnesium:** The magnesium spectrum has a primary distinct dark line in the green region. Looking at the star's spectrum, there is a corresponding line in that position. **(Magnesium is present)**

3.  **Conclusion:** Since all four elements (calcium, sodium, iron, and magnesium) have their characteristic spectral lines present in the star's absorption spectrum, the star contains all of these elements.

Comparing this to the given options:
(A) calcium, sodium and iron (incomplete)
