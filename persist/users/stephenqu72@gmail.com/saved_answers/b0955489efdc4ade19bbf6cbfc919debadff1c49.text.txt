### EXPLANATION
To solve this problem, we must understand the fundamental relationships in the photoelectric effect:

1.  **Stopping Voltage ($V_s$):** This is the negative voltage required to stop even the most energetic photoelectrons. It is determined by the maximum kinetic energy of the electrons, which depends solely on the frequency of the incident light ($K_{max} = hf - \Phi$). Because the stopping voltage occurs at different values for the curves in **Experiment A**, this indicates that the **frequencies of the light were varied**.
2.  **Saturation Current:** This is the maximum current reached when all emitted photoelectrons are collected by the detector. The number of photoelectrons emitted per second is directly proportional to the intensity of the incident light. Because the saturation current occurs at different values for the curves in **Experiment B**, this indicates that the **intensities of the light were varied**.

**Breakdown of the Graphs:**
*   **Experiment A:** We see different $x$-intercepts (different stopping voltages). Since stopping voltage is frequency-dependent, this experiment tests the effect of varying the **frequency**.
*   **Experiment B:** All curves share the same $x$-intercept (same stopping voltage), meaning the light frequency is constant, but they reach different maximum current levels. This experiment tests the effect of varying the **intensity**.

Therefore, option (B) correctly identifies that Experiment A varies frequency and Experiment B varies intensity.
