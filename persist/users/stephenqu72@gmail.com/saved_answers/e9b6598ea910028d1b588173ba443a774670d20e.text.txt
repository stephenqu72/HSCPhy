To solve this problem, we must apply **Malus's Law**, which states that the intensity of light passing through a polarising filter is given by:

$$I = I_0 \cos^2(\phi)$$

where $I_0$ is the intensity of the light incident on the second filter, and $\phi$ is the angle between the plane of polarisation of the incident light and the axis of the second filter.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Identify the angles:**
    *   The first filter is parallel to the $y$-axis. Therefore, the light emerging from the first filter has a polarisation direction along the $y$-axis.
    *   The second filter is at an angle $\theta$ with the $x$-axis. Since the $y$-axis is at $90^\circ$ to the $x$-axis, the angle $\phi$ between the incident light ($y$-axis) and the second filter axis is:
        $$\phi = 90^\circ - \theta$$

2.  **Determine the intensity constant:**
    *   The question provides a reference: when $\theta = 90^\circ$, the intensity is $210\text{ lux}$.
    *   When $\theta = 90^\circ$, the angle between the light ($y$-axis) and the filter ($90^\circ$ from $x$-axis) is $\phi = 90^\circ - 90^\circ = 0^\circ$.
    *   Applying Malus's Law: $210 = I_0 \cos^2(0^\circ) = I_0(1)^2 \implies I_0 = 210\text{ lux}$.

3.  **Calculate the intensity for $\theta = 35^\circ$:**
    *   The angle between the light ($y$-axis) and the filter axis ($\theta = 35^\circ$ from $x$-axis) is:
        $$\phi = 90^\circ - 35^\circ = 55^\circ$$
    *   Now, calculate the final intensity $I$:
        $$I = 210 \times \cos^2(55^\circ)$$
        $$I = 210 \times (0.5736)^2$$
        $$I = 210 \times 0.329$$
        $$I \approx 69.1\text{ lux}$$
