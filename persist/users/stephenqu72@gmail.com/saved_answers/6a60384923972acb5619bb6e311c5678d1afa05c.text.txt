To solve this problem, we use Kepler's Third Law of planetary motion, which relates the orbital period ($T$) of a satellite to its orbital radius ($r$):

$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

### 1. Identify the Constants
*   Gravitational constant $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$
*   Mass of Earth $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \, \text{kg}$
*   Orbital period $T = 3 \, \text{hours} = 3 \times 3600 \, \text{s} = 10,800 \, \text{s}$

### 2. Calculate the Orbital Radius ($r$)
Rearranging the formula to solve for $r$:
$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}}$$

Substitute the values:
$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(10800)^2}{4\pi^2}}$$
$$r \approx \sqrt[3]{1.168 \times 10^{22}}$$
$$r \approx 22,689,000 \, \text{m}$$

### 3. Calculate the Altitude ($h$)
The radius $r$ is the distance from the **center** of the Earth. To find the altitude $h$ above the Earth's surface, we must subtract the Earth's radius ($R_e \approx 6.37 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$):
$$h = r - R_e$$
$$h = 22,689,000 \, \text{m} - 6,370,000 \, \text{m}$$
$$h \approx 16,319,000 \, \text{m}$$
