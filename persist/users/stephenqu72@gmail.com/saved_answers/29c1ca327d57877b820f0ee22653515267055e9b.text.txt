### Answer
The operation of a mass spectrometer relies on the interaction between charged particles and electromagnetic fields:

1.  **Acceleration:** Once ionised, positive ions are accelerated by an **electric field**. The potential difference ($V$) does work on the ion, converting electrical potential energy into kinetic energy ($E_k = qV$). This ensures all ions of a given charge reach a consistent velocity.
2.  **Deflection:** As the ions enter the magnetic field ($B$), they experience a Lorentz force ($F = qvB \sin\theta$) perpendicular to their path. This force acts as a centripetal force ($F = \frac{mv^2}{r}$), causing the ions to travel in a circular path.
3.  **Separation:** The radius of the path ($r$) is determined by the mass-to-charge ratio ($m/q$) of the ion, where $r = \frac{mv}{qB}$. Because particles with different masses (or charges) experience different radii of curvature in the same magnetic field, they are physically separated, allowing for detection based on their specific $m/q$ value.
