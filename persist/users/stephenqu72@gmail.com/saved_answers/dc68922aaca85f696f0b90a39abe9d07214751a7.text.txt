To assess the man's statement, we must analyze the forces acting on him at the top of the hump-backed bridge.

### Answer
The man's statement is **correct**. At the top of a circular bridge, the normal force ($N$)—which is what the scales measure as "apparent weight"—decreases as velocity $v$ increases. If the car travels at a specific critical speed, the normal force becomes zero, meaning the man experiences weightlessness.

### Explanation and Derivation
To solve this, we use Newton’s Second Law for circular motion. 

1.  **Identify the forces:** At the top of the bridge, the two vertical forces acting on the man are:
    *   Gravity ($mg$) acting downwards.
    *   Normal force ($N$) acting upwards (the reading on the scales).

2.  **Apply Newton's Second Law:** For circular motion, the net centripetal force ($F_c$) must be directed towards the center of the circle (downwards):
    $$F_{net} = mg - N = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

3.  **Solve for the normal force ($N$):** Rearranging the equation to solve for $N$ gives the apparent weight:
    $$N = mg - \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

4.  **Analyze the "Zero Reading" condition:** The scales register zero when $N = 0$. Setting the equation to zero:
    $$0 = mg - \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    $$mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    $$g = \frac{v^2}{r} \implies v = \sqrt{gr}$$

**Conclusion:** 
The man is correct because the expression $N = mg - \frac{mv^2}{r}$ shows that as $v$ increases, $N$ decreases. If the man drives at a speed $v = \sqrt{gr}$, the centripetal force required to keep him in circular motion is provided entirely by gravity. At this speed, the scales no longer need to exert an upward normal force, and they will register zero. This is the condition of "apparent weightlessness."
