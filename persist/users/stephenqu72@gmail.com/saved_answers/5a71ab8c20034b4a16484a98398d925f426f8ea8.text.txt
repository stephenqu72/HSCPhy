This experiment depicts the landmark discovery of the neutron by **James Chadwick in 1932**. 

### Answer
When alpha particles ($\alpha$) strike the beryllium (Be) target, they emit a highly penetrating, neutral "unknown radiation." When this radiation strikes a block of paraffin wax (which is rich in hydrogen/protons), it knocks protons out of the wax, which are then detected by the ionization chamber.

By analyzing the kinetic energy and momentum of these ejected protons, Chadwick concluded that the unknown radiation could not be high-energy gamma rays (as previously hypothesized), because gamma rays lacked the mass/momentum to eject protons with such high velocities. Instead, the radiation had to consist of neutral particles with a mass approximately equal to that of a proton. This led to the discovery of the **neutron** and changed the model of the atom to include neutrons within the nucleus alongside protons, resolving the discrepancy between atomic number and atomic mass.
