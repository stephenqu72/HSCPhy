To solve this problem, we use Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation, which states that the gravitational field strength (or acceleration due to gravity, $g$) exerted by an object of mass $M$ at a distance $r$ is given by:

$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

where $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2 \text{ kg}^{-2}$.

### Step 1: Determine the distance
The object is located midway between the Earth and the Moon. Therefore, the distance $r$ from either body is:
$$r = \frac{3.84 \times 10^8 \text{ m}}{2} = 1.92 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$$

### Step 2: Calculate acceleration due to Earth ($g_E$)
$$g_E = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(6.0 \times 10^{24})}{(1.92 \times 10^8)^2}$$
$$g_E = \frac{4.002 \times 10^{14}}{3.6864 \times 10^{16}} \approx 1.0856 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m s}^{-2} \text{ (towards Earth)}$$

### Step 3: Calculate acceleration due to the Moon ($g_M$)
$$g_M = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(7.35 \times 10^{22})}{(1.92 \times 10^8)^2}$$
$$g_M = \frac{4.902 \times 10^{12}}{3.6864 \times 10^{16}} \approx 1.33 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m s}^{-2} \text{ (towards the Moon)}$$

### Step 4: Calculate the net acceleration
Since the forces are in opposite directions, we subtract the smaller acceleration from the larger one:
$$g_{net} = g_E - g_M$$
$$g_{net} = 1.0856 \times 10^{-2} - 0.0133 \times 10^{-2} = 1.0723 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m s}^{-2}$$

Because $g_E > g_M$, the net acceleration is directed **towards Earth**. Rounding to the appropriate significant figures, we get $1.07 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m s}^{-2}$ towards Earth.
