### Answer

The statement is accurate; energy losses in transmission and distribution are significant due to physical constraints in the electrical grid. The primary source of this energy loss is **Joule heating (resistive heating)** in transmission lines. 

Because transmission lines have a non-zero resistance ($R$), as current ($I$) flows through them, electrical energy is dissipated as heat according to the formula:
$$P_{loss} = I^2 R$$
To minimize these losses, electricity is stepped up to very high voltages using transformers, which decreases the current required to transmit the same amount of power ($P = VI$). However, resistance cannot be entirely eliminated, and heating persists.

Additional sources of energy loss include:
*   **Transformer inefficiency:** Magnetic hysteresis and eddy currents in the cores of transformers cause energy to be lost as heat.
*   **Corona discharge:** At very high voltages, the electric field strength can ionize the surrounding air, resulting in energy loss as sound, light, and heat.
*   **Infrastructure aging:** Older components have higher resistance and lower efficiency compared to modern, superconducting, or smart-grid technologies.

While high-voltage transmission mitigates the issue, the inherent physics of conductors and the practical limitations of equipment ensure that 8–15% losses are a standard reality of modern grids.
