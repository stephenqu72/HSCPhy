### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
To calculate the force between two parallel current-carrying wires, we use the formula derived from Ampère's Force Law:
$$F = \frac{k' \cdot 2 \cdot I_1 \cdot I_2 \cdot L}{r}$$
Alternatively, using the magnetic constant $\mu_0$:
$$F = \frac{\mu_0 \cdot I_1 \cdot I_2 \cdot L}{2\pi r}$$

**Given values:**
*   $I_1 = I_2 = 5\text{ A}$
*   $L = 1\text{ m}$
*   $r = 5\text{ mm} = 0.005\text{ m}$
*   $k' = 2 \times 10^{-7}\text{ N/A}^2$ (or $\frac{\mu_0}{2\pi} = 2 \times 10^{-7}$)

**Calculation:**
$$F = \frac{(2 \times 10^{-7}) \cdot 2 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 1}{0.005}$$
$$F = \frac{2 \times 10^{-7} \cdot 50}{0.005}$$
$$F = \frac{1 \times 10^{-5}}{0.005}$$
$$F = 2 \times 10^{-3}\text{ N}$$

The size of the force is **$2 \times 10^{-3}\text{ N}$** (or $2\text{ mN}$).

---

#### Part (b)
Measuring such a small force (milli-newton range) requires high sensitivity. A practical way is to **suspend one of the rods using a sensitive electronic micro-balance or a force sensor.**

*   **Explanation:** One rod is fixed, while the other is mounted on a very sensitive top-loading balance. As the current flows through both rods, the magnetic force (either attractive or repulsive) will cause a change in the reading of the balance. The change in the mass reading ($\Delta m$) is converted to a force using $F = mg$, where $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$. 
*   **Alternative:** Using a cantilever beam or a strain gauge setup calibrated to measure small displacements or stresses.
