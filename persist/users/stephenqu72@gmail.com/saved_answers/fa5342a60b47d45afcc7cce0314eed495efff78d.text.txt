To answer an 8-mark "Analyse" question in Physics, you must demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of the historical progression of scientific thought, identifying the relationship between experimental evidence and the shift in theoretical models.

### Answer
The understanding of the nature of light has evolved through a paradigm shift from a particle model to a wave model, and finally to the contemporary wave-particle duality.

1. **The Particle (Corpuscular) Model:** Initially, Isaac Newton proposed the corpuscular theory, arguing that light consisted of streams of tiny particles. This successfully explained rectilinear propagation, reflection, and refraction. However, it struggled to explain diffraction and interference phenomena.
2. **The Wave Model:** Christiaan Huygens proposed a wave theory of light. This gained prominence in the 19th century following Thomas Young’s Double Slit Experiment, which produced interference patterns—a phenomenon exclusive to waves. Later, James Clerk Maxwell’s work on electromagnetism identified light as a transverse electromagnetic wave, where the speed of light $c$ is related to vacuum permeability $\mu_0$ and permittivity $\epsilon_0$ by $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}}$. This model effectively explained reflection, refraction, interference, and diffraction.
3. **The Crisis of the Wave Model:** The wave theory failed to explain the Photoelectric Effect, where the kinetic energy of emitted electrons depends on the frequency of incident light rather than intensity.
4. **Quantum Theory and Wave-Particle Duality:** Albert Einstein resolved this by proposing that light behaves as discrete packets of energy called photons, where energy $E = hf$ ($h$ is Planck's constant). This revived the particle-like behavior of light. Subsequent experiments, such as the Compton effect and de Broglie's hypothesis of matter waves ($\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$), established that light exhibits a dual nature: it propagates as a wave but interacts with matter as a particle. This synthesis remains the bedrock of modern quantum mechanics.

2.  **Focus on "Why":** Don't just list the models; mention the *evidence* that caused the shift (e.g., "Young’s experiment led to the adoption of the wave model"). This is critical for high-range marks.
3.  **Include Key Equations:** Explicitly referencing equations like $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}}$ or $E = hf$ adds depth and scientific rigour, demonstrating your grasp of the physics behind the theory.
4.  **Use Terminology:** Ensure you use specific terms like "diffraction," "interference," "photoelectric effect," and "wave-particle duality."
5.  **Address the Command Verb:** "Analyse" requires you to break down the development into parts and describe how they relate to one another. Ensure you conclude by summarizing how these theories are reconciled today.
