### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must apply the physics principles governing electrical power transmission.

1.  **The goal of transmission:** When electricity is transported over long distances, energy is lost as heat due to the resistance of the transmission lines. This power loss is given by the formula:
    $$P_{loss} = I^2 R$$
    where $I$ is the current and $R$ is the resistance of the cables. To minimize energy waste, we must minimize $P_{loss}$ by reducing the current $I$.

2.  **Maintaining power:** Power stations produce a specific amount of power ($P = VI$). For a given power output, if we want to reduce the current ($I$), we must increase the voltage ($V$). 

3.  **The role of the transformer:** At the power station, a "step-up" transformer is used to increase the voltage to very high levels. Because power is roughly conserved ($P_{in} \approx P_{out}$), increasing the voltage results in a proportional decrease in the current ($I$). 

4.  **Conclusion:** By stepping up the voltage, the current in the transmission lines decreases significantly. According to $P_{loss} = I^2 R$, a lower current leads to a dramatic reduction in heat loss ($I^2$ dependency), making the transmission of electricity much more efficient.

Therefore, the role of the transformer at the power station is to reduce heating in the transmission lines by stepping up the voltage.
