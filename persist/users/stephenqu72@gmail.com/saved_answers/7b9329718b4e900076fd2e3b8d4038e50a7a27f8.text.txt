### (b) Use the line of best fit to determine the mass of the car.
The physics governing this experiment is the centripetal force equation:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Rearranging this to match the linear form $y = mx + c$:
$$F = \left( \frac{m}{r} \right) v^2$$
Here, $F$ is on the $y$-axis and $v^2$ is on the $x$-axis. The gradient of the line is $\frac{m}{r}$.
1. **Find the gradient:** Select two points on your line of best fit (e.g., at $v^2 = 5$ and $v^2 = 25$). 
2. Using the data provided, the points trend toward a gradient of approximately:
   $$\text{Gradient} \approx \frac{16 - 0}{25 - 1} = \frac{16}{24} \approx 0.667 \, \text{kg/m}$$
3. Since $\text{gradient} = \frac{m}{r}$, then $m = \text{gradient} \times r$.
   $$m = 0.667 \times 0.11 \approx 0.073 \, \text{kg}$$

### (c) Identify possible errors and how to reduce their effects.
*   **Systematic Error (Calibration):** The force sensor might not be zeroed correctly (the graph shows an intercept at $v^2 = 1.0$ where $F=0$, suggesting the sensor wasn't calibrated at rest). **Reduction:** Zero the sensor before starting measurements.
*   **Random Error (Reaction time):** Timing the period using a stopwatch is subject to human reaction time. **Reduction:** Use a light gate or a motion sensor to measure the period automatically and accurately.
*   **Systematic Error (Radius measurement):** The car's centre of mass may shift, or the tether might not be perfectly horizontal. **Reduction:** Use a rigid rod instead of a string to maintain a constant $r$ and ensure the car is centered securely.
*   **Random Error (Friction):** Friction between the car wheels and the turntable acts against the centripetal force. **Reduction:** Use a low-friction surface or a bearing-mounted car to ensure that the tension in the sensor is the only force providing the centripetal acceleration.
