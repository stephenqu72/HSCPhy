### Explanation
To solve this, we must evaluate the historical progression of atomic models:

1.  **Evaluate Option C:** This refers to the **Rutherford model**, based on his gold foil experiment, which proposed a small, dense, positively charged nucleus with electrons orbiting randomly like planets.
2.  **Evaluate Option B:** This refers to the **Bohr model**. Bohr refined the Rutherford model by proposing that electrons exist in discrete, stable energy levels (or shells) to explain atomic spectra.
3.  **Evaluate Option D:** While this sounds sophisticated, it is a slight misinterpretation of the **de Broglie hypothesis**. De Broglie proposed that matter (like electrons) has a wave nature, and Bohr later used this to explain why his energy levels were stable (treating electrons as standing waves). However, this is not the primary definition of the Schrödinger model.
4.  **Evaluate Option A:** Erwin Schrödinger developed the **Quantum Mechanical Model** of the atom. He formulated the Schrödinger equation, which describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes with time. Instead of "orbits" (defined paths), his model describes electrons as having a wave nature defined by a probability distribution, which we call **orbitals** (regions of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron). 

Schrödinger’s key contribution was shifting our understanding from the electron as a particle in a fixed path to a wave-function represented by a probability cloud (orbital).
