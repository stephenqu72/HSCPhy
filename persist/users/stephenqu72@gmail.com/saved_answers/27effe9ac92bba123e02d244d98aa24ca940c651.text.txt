### Explanation
To understand why the velocity remains constant, we look at the nature of the magnetic force acting on the moving charge.

1.  **The Magnetic Force Law:** When a charged particle moves through a magnetic field, it experiences a magnetic force ($F_B$) given by the formula:
    $$F_B = qvB \sin(\theta)$$
    where $q$ is the charge, $v$ is the velocity, $B$ is the magnetic field strength, and $\theta$ is the angle between the velocity vector and the magnetic field vector.

2.  **Direction of the Force:** The magnetic force is always directed perpendicular to both the velocity vector ($\vec{v}$) and the magnetic field vector ($\vec{B}$). According to the right-hand rule (adjusted for the negative charge of the electron), the force acts as a **centripetal force**.

3.  **Work and Kinetic Energy:** Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the direction of motion (the velocity vector), it does **zero work** on the electron. The work done ($W$) is defined as:
    $$W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(\phi)$$
    Since the force is perpendicular to the displacement ($d$), the angle $\phi$ is $90^\circ$, and $\cos(90^\circ) = 0$.

4.  **Conclusion:** Since the magnetic field does no work on the electron, there is no change in its kinetic energy. According to the work-energy theorem ($W = \Delta K$), if the work done is zero, the change in kinetic energy is zero. Therefore, the magnitude of the velocity (speed) remains constant throughout the particle's path in the magnetic field.

The electron will follow a curved path (circular motion), but its speed upon exiting the field will be identical to its speed upon entering.
