To determine the correct answer, we must categorize the historical methods used to measure the speed of light ($c$):

1.  **Romer:** Ole Romer made the first successful quantitative measurement of the speed of light in 1676 by observing the eclipses of Jupiter's moon, Io. This is an **astronomical method**.
2.  **Bradley:** James Bradley discovered the aberration of starlight in 1728. By observing the change in the apparent position of stars due to the Earth's orbital motion, he calculated the speed of light. This is also an **astronomical method**.
3.  **Fizeau:** Hippolyte Fizeau performed the first successful terrestrial measurement of $c$ in 1849 using a **rotating cogwheel** (a rotating component) to time light traveling to a distant mirror and back.
4.  **Foucault:** Léon Foucault refined terrestrial measurements in 1862 by replacing the cogwheel with a **rotating mirror** (a rotating component) to measure the speed of light more accurately, even showing that light travels slower in water than in air.

### Explanation Strategy
When approaching HSC Physics questions regarding the history of determining $c$:
*   **Divide into Eras:** Think of the history of this measurement in two broad stages: the "Astronomical" era (observing celestial bodies) and the "Terrestrial" era (using mechanical apparatus in a laboratory setting).
*   **Identify Mechanisms:** If you see "rotating" in a name, it almost always refers to the terrestrial experiments (Fizeau/Foucault) where mechanical parts were used to chop or reflect light beams. If you see names like Romer, Bradley, or Galileo, associate them with observational/astronomical measurements.
