To solve this problem, we use the photoelectric equation, which describes the relationship between the maximum kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons ($E_{k \text{ max}}$) and the frequency of the incident light ($f$):

$$E_{k \text{ max}} = hf - \Phi$$

In this equation:
- $h$ is Planck’s constant.
- $f$ is the frequency of the incident light.
- $\Phi$ (phi) is the **work function** of the metal, which represents the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the metal surface.

### How to solve:
1.  **Analyze the graph:** The x-intercept of these lines occurs when $E_{k \text{ max}} = 0$. Setting the equation to zero gives $hf = \Phi$, or $f_0 = \frac{\Phi}{h}$, where $f_0$ is the **threshold frequency**.
2.  **Understand the relationship:** Since $h$ is a constant, the work function $\Phi$ is directly proportional to the threshold frequency $f_0$. Therefore, a higher threshold frequency indicates a higher work function.
3.  **Identify the values from the graph:** Looking at the x-axis intercepts for each metal:
    *   **nickel:** $f_0 \approx 5.1 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$
    *   **potassium:** $f_0 \approx 5.5 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$
    *   **sodium:** $f_0 \approx 5.7 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$
    *   **lithium:** $f_0 = 6.2 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$
4.  **Rank the values:** Ranking them from highest to lowest threshold frequency (and thus highest to lowest work function):
    *   Lithium ($6.2$) > Sodium ($5.7$) > Potassium ($5.5$) > Nickel ($5.1$).
