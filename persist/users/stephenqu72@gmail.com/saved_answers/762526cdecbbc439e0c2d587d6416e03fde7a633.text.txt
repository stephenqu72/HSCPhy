To solve this problem, we need to determine the energy of the photon being absorbed and see which transition in the hydrogen atom matches that energy difference.

### 1. Calculate the energy of the photon
The energy $E$ of a photon is given by the formula:
$$E = hf$$
Where:
- $h$ is Planck's constant: $6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$
- $f$ is the frequency: $2.34 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$

$$E = (6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (2.34 \times 10^{14}) \approx 1.55 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

Now, convert this energy from Joules to electron-volts (eV) using the conversion factor $1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$:
$$E_{\text{eV}} = \frac{1.55 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 0.97 \text{ eV}$$

### 2. Compare with energy levels in the table
The table provides the energy required to remove an electron (the ionization energy from that level). This means the energy level $E_n$ is the negative of the value in the table (e.g., $E_2 = -3.40 \text{ eV}$, $E_4 = -0.85 \text{ eV}$).

We are looking for a transition where the change in energy $\Delta E = |E_{\text{final}} - E_{\text{initial}}|$ equals approximately $0.97 \text{ eV}$. Since the atom **absorbs** a photon, the electron must move from a lower level to a higher level.

Let's test the options:
*   **A. $2 \rightarrow 4$**: $\Delta E = |-0.85 - (-3.40)| = 2.55 \text{ eV}$ (Incorrect)
*   **B. $3 \rightarrow 5$**: $\Delta E = |-0.54 - (-1.51)| = 0.97 \text{ eV}$ (Matches our calculation!)
*   **C. $4 \rightarrow 2$**: This is an emission (moving to a lower state), not absorption.
*   **D. $5 \rightarrow 3$**: This is an emission, not absorption.

The absorption of this photon causes the electron to transition from energy level 3 to energy level 5.

**Correct Answer: B**
