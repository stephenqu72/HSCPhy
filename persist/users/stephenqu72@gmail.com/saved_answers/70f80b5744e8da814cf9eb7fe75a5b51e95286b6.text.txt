### Explanation
To solve this, we must consider the core principles of classical physics, which were the foundation of Rutherford's atomic model, and identify where they contradicted observed reality.

1.  **The Classical Physics Problem:** According to classical electromagnetism (Maxwell's equations), any charged particle undergoing acceleration must radiate energy. An electron orbiting the nucleus in a circular path is constantly accelerating (centripetal acceleration).
2.  **The Predicted Outcome:** As the electron radiates energy, it should lose kinetic energy and spiral inward toward the nucleus. This would cause the atom to collapse in a fraction of a second.
3.  **The Reality:** Atoms are stable and do not collapse. Therefore, Rutherford’s model—which proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus in stable circular paths—was fundamentally unable to explain why atoms are stable.
4.  **Eliminating other options:**
    *   **B:** While Rutherford did not know about neutrons (the composition of the nucleus), this was considered a secondary detail compared to the fundamental breakdown of the model's structural stability.
    *   **C and D:** These statements actually describe the limitations of the **Bohr** model (which was specifically designed to address the hydrogen spectrum), not the Rutherford model. The Rutherford model did not account for the hydrogen spectrum at all, as it didn't even address discrete energy levels.

Thus, the failure to account for the stability of the atom due to classical electromagnetic theory is the most significant flaw of the Rutherford model.
