### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**How an absorption spectrum is formed:**
An absorption spectrum is produced when light from a hot, dense source (such as the interior of a star) passes through a cooler, lower-density gas (such as the star's atmosphere). Atoms in the cooler gas absorb specific wavelengths of light that correspond exactly to the energy transitions required to excite their electrons from lower energy levels to higher ones. These absorbed wavelengths appear as dark lines against the continuous rainbow background of the star's thermal emission.

**Determining the direction of movement:**
Scientists compare the observed wavelengths of the spectral lines from the star to the known laboratory wavelengths of the same elements (at rest). Due to the **Doppler Effect**:
*   If a star is moving **away** from Earth, the light waves are stretched, causing the spectral lines to shift toward longer wavelengths (the red end of the spectrum). This is called **redshift**.
*   Because the observed spectral lines are shifted towards the red (longer wavelengths) compared to their rest positions, scientists can conclude the star is moving away.

#### Part (b)
To draw the lines for a planet orbiting the star, you must represent the "rest" state—the state where there is no relative motion between the source and the observer.
*   **The logic:** When on a planet orbiting the star, the relative velocity between the observer and the star is essentially zero. Therefore, there is no Doppler shift. 
*   **The action:** You must draw the two lines back to their original "rest" positions. Based on the diagram provided in (a), the lines appear to be at approximately $580\text{ nm}$ and $590\text{ nm}$. You should draw two vertical lines on the bottom diagram at these positions (or slightly to the left of the original shifted position) to indicate they have moved back toward the blue/shorter wavelength side to account for the lack of redshift.
