To solve this problem, we apply Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion, which states that for orbiting bodies, $\frac{T^2}{R^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$.

### Part (a): Plotting the Graph
To plot the graph, place $R^3$ ($10^{24} \text{ m}^3$) on the $x$-axis and $T^2$ ($10^{10} \text{ s}^2$) on the $y$-axis. 
- The $x$-axis range should go from $0$ to $160 \times 10^{24} \text{ m}^3$.
- The $y$-axis range should go from $0$ to $16 \times 10^{10} \text{ s}^2$.
- Plot the coordinates: $(6.4, 0.66)$, $(13.5, 1.39)$, $(25.7, 2.66)$, $(53.6, 5.57)$, and $(146, 15.2)$.
- Draw a straight line of best fit that passes as close as possible to all points, ideally near the origin $(0,0)$.

### Part (b): Calculating the Gradient
Pick two points on your line of best fit. Using the data points (e.g., $(146, 15.2)$ and $(6.4, 0.66)$):
$$\text{Gradient} (m) = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{(15.2 - 0.66) \times 10^{10}}{(146 - 6.4) \times 10^{24}}$$
$$m = \frac{14.54 \times 10^{10}}{139.6 \times 10^{24}} \approx 1.04 \times 10^{-16} \text{ s}^2 \text{ m}^{-3}$$

### Part (c): Determining the Mass of Saturn
From Kepler’s Third Law:
$$\frac{T^2}{R^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$$
The gradient of our graph represents $\frac{T^2}{R^3}$. Thus, $m = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$.
Rearranging for $M$:
$$M = \frac{4\pi^2}{G \times m}$$
Using $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3 \text{ kg}^{-1} \text{ s}^{-2}$ and $m \approx 1.04 \times 10^{-16} \text{ s}^2 \text{ m}^{-3}$:
$$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (1.04 \times 10^{-16})}$$
$$M \approx \frac{39.478}{6.9368 \times 10^{-27}} \approx 5.69 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$$

*(Note: The actual mass of Saturn is approximately $5.68 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$, confirming the calculation is accurate.)*
