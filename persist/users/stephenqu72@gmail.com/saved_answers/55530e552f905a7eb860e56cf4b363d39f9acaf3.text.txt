### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:**
The magnitude of the EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux through the coil, as described by Faraday’s Law ($|\varepsilon| = N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$). When the amplitude of the ground movement increases, the magnet undergoes greater displacement and moves with a higher average velocity as it oscillates. Since the EMF is proportional to the speed of the magnet relative to the coil, a larger amplitude leads to higher instantaneous speeds, resulting in a larger rate of change of magnetic flux and, consequently, a greater induced EMF.

**Explanation:**
To solve this, you must link two concepts: simple harmonic motion and electromagnetic induction.
1. Recall that for an object oscillating on a spring, the maximum velocity is proportional to the amplitude ($v_{max} = A\omega$).
2. Identify that the induced EMF is generated by the changing magnetic field cutting through the coil.
3. Conclude by linking the increased motion (velocity) to Faraday's Law, where the rate of flux change is directly related to the speed of the magnet.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
Using Faraday's Law of induction:
$$|\varepsilon| = N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$
Where:
- $|\varepsilon| = 65 \text{ mV} = 65 \times 10^{-3} \text{ V}$
- $N = 150 \text{ turns}$

Rearranging for the rate of flux change ($\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$):
$$\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} = \frac{|\varepsilon|}{N}$$
$$\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} = \frac{65 \times 10^{-3} \text{ V}}{150}$$
$$\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} \approx 4.33 \times 10^{-4} \text{ Wb/s}$$

**Explanation:**
This is a straightforward application of the formula for electromagnetic induction. The key is to ensure the EMF is converted from millivolts to volts before calculation to maintain SI units (Webers per second).
