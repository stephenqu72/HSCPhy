### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
To relate $v_{TG}$, $v_{RG}$, and $\theta$, we consider the vector addition of velocities. The velocity of the rain relative to the train ($\vec{v}_{RT}$) is given by:
$$\vec{v}_{RT} = \vec{v}_{RG} - \vec{v}_{TG}$$
Since the rain falls vertically ($v_{RG}$) and the train moves horizontally ($v_{TG}$), these two vectors form the legs of a right-angled triangle. The angle $\theta$ is the angle between the vertical (the direction of $\vec{v}_{RG}$) and the resultant vector $\vec{v}_{RT}$. Using trigonometry:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} = \frac{v_{TG}}{v_{RG}}$$
Thus, the equation is:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v_{TG}}{v_{RG}}$$

#### Part (b)
This is an application of the aberration of starlight. The angle given is $\theta = 0^\circ 0' 20.5''$. We must convert this into degrees:
$$\theta = \frac{20.5}{3600} \text{ degrees}$$
Converting to radians for calculation:
$$\theta = \left(\frac{20.5}{3600}\right) \times \left(\frac{\pi}{180}\right) \approx 9.938 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad}$$
For small angles, $\tan(\theta) \approx \theta$. The Earth's orbital speed ($v_E$) is:
$$v_E = \frac{2\pi r_E}{T}$$
Where $T = 365.25 \times 24 \times 3600 = 31,557,600 \text{ s}$.
$$v_E = \frac{2 \times \pi \times 1.5 \times 10^{11}}{31,557,600} \approx 29,864 \text{ m/s}$$
Using the relation from part (a), where $c$ is the speed of light:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v_E}{c} \implies c = \frac{v_E}{\tan(\theta)}$$
Since $\tan(\theta) \approx \theta$ in radians:
$$c = \frac{29,864}{9.938 \times 10^{-5}} \approx 3.005 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$$
