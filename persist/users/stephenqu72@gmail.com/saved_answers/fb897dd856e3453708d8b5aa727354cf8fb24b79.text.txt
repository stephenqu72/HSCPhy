To solve this problem, we apply Malus's Law and the rule for unpolarised light passing through a polariser.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Passing through the first filter:**
When unpolarised light of intensity $I_0$ passes through an ideal polariser, the intensity of the transmitted light $I_1$ is exactly half of the initial intensity:
$$I_1 = \frac{I_0}{2} = \frac{128\text{ W m}^{-2}}{2} = 64\text{ W m}^{-2}$$

**2. Passing through the second filter:**
The light leaving the first filter is now linearly polarised at $0^\circ$. It then passes through the second filter, which is oriented at an angle $\theta$. According to Malus's Law ($I = I_{max} \cos^2\theta$):
$$I_2 = I_1 \cos^2\theta = 64 \cos^2\theta$$

**3. Passing through the third filter:**
The light leaving the second filter is polarised at angle $\theta$. The third filter is at $0^\circ$. The relative angle between the light incident on the third filter and its axis is $(0 - \theta) = -\theta$. Applying Malus's Law again:
$$I_3 = I_2 \cos^2\theta = (64 \cos^2\theta) \cos^2\theta = 64 \cos^4\theta$$

**4. Solve for $\theta$:**
We are given that the final intensity $I_3 = 16\text{ W m}^{-2}$.
$$16 = 64 \cos^4\theta$$
$$\frac{16}{64} = \cos^4\theta$$
$$\frac{1}{4} = \cos^4\theta$$
Taking the square root of both sides:
$$\sqrt{\frac{1}{4}} = \cos^2\theta \implies \frac{1}{2} = \cos^2\theta$$
Taking the square root again:
$$\cos\theta = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$
$$\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = 45^\circ$$

**Answer:** The angle $\theta$ is $45^\circ$.
