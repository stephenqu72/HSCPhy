### Explanation
To solve this problem, we use the principle that parallel current-carrying wires exert forces on each other based on Ampere's Force Law. The magnitude of the force per unit length between two wires is given by:
$$F = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2 L}{2\pi r}$$
Where $r$ is the distance between the wires. Since the wires are identical and carry the same current magnitude $I$, the force $F$ is inversely proportional to the distance $r$ ($F \propto \frac{1}{r}$).

1.  **Analyze the interaction between $W_1$ and $W_2$:**
    *   $W_1$ carries current downwards, and $W_2$ carries current upwards (opposite directions).
    *   Wires with currents in opposite directions **repel** each other.
    *   Therefore, the force exerted by $W_1$ on $W_2$ (given as $F$) pushes $W_2$ to the **right**.

2.  **Analyze the interaction between $W_3$ and $W_2$:**
    *   $W_3$ carries current downwards, and $W_2$ carries current upwards (opposite directions).
    *   Wires with currents in opposite directions **repel** each other.
    *   Therefore, the force exerted by $W_3$ on $W_2$ pushes $W_2$ to the **left**.

3.  **Calculate the magnitude of the force from $W_3$:**
    *   The distance between $W_3$ and $W_2$ is $2d$, which is twice the distance between $W_1$ and $W_2$ ($d$).
    *   Since $F \propto \frac{1}{r}$, doubling the distance halves the force.
    *   The magnitude of the force from $W_3$ on $W_2$ is $\frac{1}{2}F$.

4.  **Determine the resultant force:**
    *   Force from $W_1$ on $W_2 = F$ (to the right).
    *   Force from $W_3$ on $W_2 = \frac{1}{2}F$ (to the left).
    *   Resultant force = $F (\text{right}) - \frac{1}{2}F (\text{left}) = \frac{1}{2}F$ (to the right).

Thus, the magnitude is $\frac{1}{2}F$ and the direction is to the **right**.
