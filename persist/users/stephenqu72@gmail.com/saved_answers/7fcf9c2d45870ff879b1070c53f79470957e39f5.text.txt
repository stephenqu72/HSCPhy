### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) back EMF produced in the motor.**

### Explanation
To understand why this is the correct answer, we need to look at the relationship between supply voltage ($V_{supply}$), the motor's internal resistance ($R$), the current flowing through it ($I$), and the back EMF ($\varepsilon$).

1.  **The Governing Equation:** The current flowing through the motor is given by:
    $$I = \frac{V_{supply} - \varepsilon}{R}$$
    Where $\varepsilon$ is the back EMF generated by the rotation of the motor's coil within the magnetic field (following Faraday’s Law of Induction).

2.  **How the Motor Speeds Up:** When the motor starts, the speed is zero, so the back EMF ($\varepsilon$) is zero. At this point, the current is at its maximum ($I = \frac{V_{supply}}{R}$). As the motor spins faster, it generates an increasing back EMF.

3.  **Reaching the Limit:** As $\varepsilon$ increases, the numerator ($V_{supply} - \varepsilon$) decreases, which causes the current ($I$) to decrease. Eventually, the motor reaches a "no-load" steady speed where the back EMF almost equals the supply voltage ($V_{supply} \approx \varepsilon$). At this state, the net current is just enough to overcome internal friction and air resistance. 

4.  **Conclusion:** Because the back EMF opposes the applied voltage, it limits the amount of current that can flow as the motor speeds up. When the back EMF approaches the value of the supply voltage, the acceleration stops, thus determining the maximum speed the motor can reach under no-load conditions.
