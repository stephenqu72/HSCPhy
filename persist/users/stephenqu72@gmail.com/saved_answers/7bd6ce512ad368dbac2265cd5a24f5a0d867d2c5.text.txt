To answer this question effectively, you need to address two distinct parts: the **discovery of the neutron** (the particle responsible for isotopes) and **why this discovery was delayed** compared to other subatomic particles.

### Answer
The particle responsible for the variation in mass between isotopes is the **neutron**.

**The Discovery:**
In 1932, James Chadwick discovered the neutron. He bombarded a thin target of beryllium with alpha particles ($\alpha$ radiation). This process produced a highly penetrating, electrically neutral radiation that was initially thought to be gamma rays. However, when Chadwick directed this radiation at paraffin wax, it ejected protons. By measuring the velocities and energies of the ejected protons and applying the laws of conservation of momentum and energy, Chadwick deduced that the neutral radiation consisted of particles with a mass approximately equal to that of a proton.

**Why the discovery was delayed:**
1.  **Lack of Charge:** Because the neutron carries no net electric charge, it does not interact with electromagnetic fields. Early detection methods for subatomic particles (like cloud chambers or electrical deflection) relied heavily on detecting the ionization caused by charged particles. Since neutrons do not ionize matter directly, they remained "invisible" to these conventional detection techniques.
2.  **Interaction Limitation:** Neutrons can only be detected via their secondary effects—specifically, through collisions with atomic nuclei (such as protons in paraffin) that result in charged particles which *can* then be detected. This indirect method required more sophisticated experimental setups that were not available until the 1930s.
