To solve this problem, we use **Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) is directly proportional to the cube of the orbital radius ($r$):

$$\frac{T_1^2}{r_1^3} = \frac{T_2^2}{r_2^3}$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1. **Identify the variables:**
   - Let Ganymede be subscript 1: $T_1 = 7.2$ days.
   - Let Europa be subscript 2: $r_2 = 0.63 \times r_1$.
   - We need to find $T_2$.

2. **Rearrange the formula to solve for $T_2$:**
   $$T_2^2 = T_1^2 \times \left( \frac{r_2}{r_1} \right)^3$$

3. **Substitute the known values:**
   $$T_2^2 = (7.2)^2 \times (0.63)^3$$
   $$T_2^2 = 51.84 \times 0.250047$$
   $$T_2^2 \approx 12.96$$

4. **Calculate $T_2$:**
   $$T_2 = \sqrt{12.96}$$
   $$T_2 = 3.6 \text{ days}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, the correct answer is **(B)**.

### Explanation:
By applying Kepler's Third Law, we establish the relationship between the period and the radius of objects orbiting the same central body. Since Europa is closer to Jupiter than Ganymede ($0.63r$), it must have a shorter orbital period. By cubing the ratio of the radii and taking the square root of that value multiplied by the square of Ganymede's period, we find the orbital period of Europa to be $3.6$ days.
