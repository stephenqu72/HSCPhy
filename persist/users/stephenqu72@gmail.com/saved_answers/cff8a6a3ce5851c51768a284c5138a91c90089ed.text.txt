### Explanation
The diagram shows the classic apparatus for **Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment**. Here is how to solve it and the historical context you need to remember for your exams:

1.  **Identify the Experiment:** The image shows an atomizer spraying oil droplets into a chamber, where they pass through a small hole between two charged plates. This is the hallmark of Robert Millikan’s famous experiment performed in 1909.
2.  **Understand the Physics:** Millikan adjusted the strength of the electric field ($E$) between the plates to balance the gravitational force acting on the tiny oil droplets. By adjusting the voltage, he could hold a charged droplet suspended in mid-air, meaning the electric force ($F_e = qE$) equalled the gravitational force ($F_g = mg$).
3.  **The Goal:** By measuring the terminal velocity of the droplets and the voltage required to suspend them, Millikan was able to calculate the charge ($q$) on individual oil drops. 
4.  **The Conclusion:** He discovered that the charges on the drops were always integer multiples of a specific value: approximately $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ Coulombs. This value is known as the **elementary charge ($e$)**, which is the magnitude of the charge on a single electron.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** Electrons are far too small to be observed directly with an optical microscope.
*   **B:** The experiment used oil droplets, not alpha particles.
*   **C:** The sign of the charge (positive or negative) was already known from J.J. Thomson's previous work; Millikan’s specific goal was to find the *magnitude* of that charge.
