### Answers and Explanations

**a. Explain how the students applied constant force and maintained radius.**
*   **Constant Force:** The centripetal force is provided by the tension in the string, which is equal to the weight of the hanging slotted masses ($Mg$). By keeping the number of slotted masses constant, the tension remains constant.
*   **Constant Radius:** The students maintain the radius by ensuring a reference mark on the string (usually just below the tube) remains at a fixed position relative to the bottom of the tube throughout the rotation.

**b. How did measuring 10 rotations improve accuracy?**
Measuring 10 rotations reduces the impact of human reaction time errors. If the reaction time error is $\Delta t$, the error in a single period is $\Delta t$, but the error per period when measuring 10 cycles is $\frac{\Delta t}{10}$, significantly reducing the percentage uncertainty.

**c. Do these results support their hypothesis?**
Yes. The hypothesis states that as mass ($m$) increases, speed ($v$) decreases. Looking at the data, as mass increases from $0.20$ to $0.45$ kg, the period ($T$) increases from $0.82$ to $1.26$ s. Since speed $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, an increase in the period $T$ (with constant $r$) necessitates a decrease in the speed $v$. Thus, the data supports the hypothesis.

**d. Explain a valid procedure used when calculating the period.**
The students calculated the average time for 10 rotations from three trials to reduce random error, then divided this average time by 10 to find the period of a single rotation ($T = \frac{t_{avg}}{10}$).

**e. Draw a suitable graph.**
To verify the relationship $T^2 = (\frac{4\pi^2 r}{Mg}) m$, the students should plot $T^2$ (on the y-axis) against mass $m$ (on the x-axis). This will result in a straight line passing through the origin (or intercepting near it), confirming a linear relationship between $T^2$ and $m$.

**f. Assess the reliability of their results.**
The reliability is high. The students performed three trials for each mass, allowing them to calculate an average and observe the consistency of the data. The close proximity of the individual trial times (e.g., for $m=0.30$ kg, values are $9.97, 9.95, 9.96$) indicates high precision and consistent technique.

### How to solve questions like this:
1.  **Identify the Physics:** This is a classic uniform circular motion experiment. Remember that the centripetal force $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{m(2\pi r / T)^2}{r} = \frac{4\pi^2 mr}{T^2}$.
2.  **Relate Variables:** When a question asks about "maintaining constant force," always look for the hanging mass ($Mg$). 
3.  **Data Trends:** When analyzing hypotheses, explicitly link the variables mentioned in the hypothesis to the variables in the table. If $T$ goes up and $r$ is constant, $v$ *must* go down.
4.  **Reliability vs. Accuracy:** Remember that *reliability* refers to the consistency of results (repeat trials), while *validity* refers to whether the experiment actually tests the hypothesis (using controlled variables).
