### Answer
The image models the success of the Bohr model in explaining the discrete emission spectra of hydrogen, where electrons transition between quantized energy levels ($n$) to emit photons of specific energies ($E = \Delta E = hf$). It correctly predicts the Rydberg series (Lyman, Balmer, etc.) by showing transitions terminating at specific levels. However, the limitation is that it only models the atom as a series of simple, one-dimensional energy levels. It fails to account for fine structure splitting, the relative intensities of these spectral lines, or the behavior of multi-electron atoms, as it treats the electron simply as a particle in a fixed orbit rather than a wave-function (as described by quantum mechanics).

### How to solve this
To secure full marks (3 marks), you need to address three key components:

1.  **The Advantage (1 mark):** Identify what the diagram is showing—quantized energy levels. Bohr’s model successfully explained why hydrogen emits light only at specific wavelengths (the spectral lines) because electrons can only exist in stable, discrete orbits. When they drop from $n_{high}$ to $n_{low}$, they release a photon with energy equal to the difference between those levels.
2.  **The Limitation (1 mark):** Explain what is missing. The model is essentially a "map" that suggests fixed, circular orbits. It does not explain *why* these levels exist (the wave nature of the electron) or why some transitions are more probable than others (intensity).
3.  **The Breadth/Complexity (1 mark):** Mention that the model is restricted to hydrogen-like (one-electron) systems. It cannot accurately predict the spectral lines of complex atoms (multi-electron) because it fails to account for electron-electron repulsion or the "fine structure" (splitting of lines under high-resolution spectroscopy due to relativistic effects and electron spin).
