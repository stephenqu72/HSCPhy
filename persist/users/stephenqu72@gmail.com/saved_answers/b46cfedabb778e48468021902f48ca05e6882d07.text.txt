To determine the correct identities of the particles, we must trace the nuclear reactions in the CNO cycle, ensuring that the mass numbers ($A$) and atomic numbers ($Z$) are conserved at each step.

### Step-by-Step Analysis:

1.  **Particle (iii):** 
    Looking at the step involving $^{13}N$, it undergoes beta-plus decay ($\beta^+$), emitting a positron ($e^+$) and a neutrino ($\nu$). This transforms the nucleus into carbon-13.
    The reaction is: $^{13}N \rightarrow ^{13}C + e^+ + \nu$.
    Therefore, **(iii) is $^{13}C$**.

2.  **Particle (i):** 
    Look at the final step where $^{15}N$ interacts with a proton ($^{1}H$ or $p$). This reaction produces $^{12}C$ and an alpha particle ($^{4}He$). 
    The reaction is: $^{15}N + ^{1}H \rightarrow ^{12}C + ^{4}He$.
    Therefore, **(i) is $^{4}He$**.

3.  **Particle (ii):** 
    Looking at the step where $^{15}O$ decays, we see it emits a neutrino ($\nu$) and a positron ($e^+$) to become $^{15}N$.
    The reaction is: $^{15}O \rightarrow ^{15}N + e^+ + \nu$.
    Therefore, **(ii) is $e^+$**.

### Conclusion:
By checking our findings against the options:
- i = $^{4}He$
- ii = $e^+$
- iii = $^{13}C$

This corresponds exactly to **Option (C)**.

### How to solve this:
When solving nuclear reaction diagrams, always perform a **"Nucleon/Charge Balance Check"**:
*   **Conservation of Mass Number ($A$):** The sum of the superscripts on the left side of the arrow must equal the sum on the right side.
*   **Conservation of Atomic Number ($Z$):** The sum of the subscripts (or protons) on the left must equal the sum on the right.
*   By applying these two conservation laws to the specific nodes labeled i, ii, and iii, you can deduce the missing particle even if you haven't memorized the entire CNO cycle.
