To answer this question, we need to analyze how the acceleration due to gravity ($g$) affects the vertical and horizontal components of projectile motion.

### Analysis
Let a projectile be launched with an initial velocity $v$ at an angle $\theta$. The initial vertical velocity is $v_y = v \sin \theta$ and the initial horizontal velocity is $v_x = v \cos \theta$.

1.  **Maximum Height ($H$):**
    At the maximum height, the vertical velocity is zero ($v_f = 0$). Using the kinematic equation $v_f^2 = v_y^2 - 2gH$:
    $$0 = (v \sin \theta)^2 - 2gH \implies H = \frac{v^2 \sin^2 \theta}{2g}$$
    Since $g$ is in the denominator, an increase in $g$ results in a **decreased maximum height**.

2.  **Range ($R$):**
    The total time of flight ($T$) is the time taken to return to the launch height, where vertical displacement is zero ($0 = v_y T - \frac{1}{2}gT^2$). This gives $T = \frac{2v \sin \theta}{g}$.
    The range is the horizontal distance covered during this time:
    $$R = v_x \times T = (v \cos \theta) \left( \frac{2v \sin \theta}{g} \right) = \frac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$$
    Since $g$ is in the denominator, an increase in $g$ results in a **decreased range**.

### Conclusion
Because both the range and the maximum height equations have $g$ in the denominator, an increase in the value of $g$ will cause both to decrease.
