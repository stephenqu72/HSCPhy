To determine the new radius of the satellite's orbit, we must first establish the relationship between the kinetic energy ($K$) and the orbital radius ($r$) for a satellite in a circular orbit.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Derive the Kinetic Energy formula:**
    For a satellite in a circular orbit, the gravitational force provides the centripetal force:
    $$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    Multiplying both sides by $r/2$, we get:
    $$\frac{GMm}{2r} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
    Since kinetic energy $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, it follows that:
    $$K = \frac{GMm}{2r}$$
    This shows that kinetic energy is inversely proportional to the radius: $K \propto \frac{1}{r}$.

2.  **Set up the ratios:**
    Let the initial state be $K_1$ and $r_1 = r$.
    Let the final state be $K_2$ and $r_2 = r'$.
    The problem states the kinetic energy is 25% larger, so:
    $$K_2 = 1.25 K_1 = \frac{5}{4} K_1$$

3.  **Relate the variables:**
    Because $K \propto \frac{1}{r}$, we can write the ratio:
    $$\frac{K_2}{K_1} = \frac{r_1}{r_2}$$
    Substituting the known values:
    $$\frac{5}{4} = \frac{r}{r'}$$

4.  **Solve for $r'$:**
    $$r' = \frac{4}{5}r$$

### Conclusion:
Comparing our result $r' = \frac{4}{5}r$ to the given options, it matches option (B).
