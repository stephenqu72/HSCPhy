### Answer
**J.J. Thomson (Charge-to-mass ratio, $q/m$):**
Thomson used a cathode ray tube involving crossed electric and magnetic fields. He balanced the electric force ($F_E = qE$) and the magnetic force ($F_B = qvB$) to ensure the electron beam traveled in a straight line, allowing him to determine the velocity of the electrons. By subsequently turning off the magnetic field, he measured the deflection of the electrons caused by the electric field alone. By analyzing the circular path (centripetal force $F_c = mv^2/r$ equated to $F_E$), he was able to calculate the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$) of the electron.

**Robert Millikan (Elementary charge, $q$):**
Millikan used the oil-drop experiment, which involved charged oil droplets suspended between two horizontal parallel metal plates. He used a uniform electric field ($E = V/d$) to create an upward electric force ($F_E = qE$) that balanced the downward gravitational force ($F_g = mg$) acting on the drop. By precisely adjusting the voltage to keep a droplet stationary, he could calculate the charge $q$ on the droplet. By observing many droplets, he determined that all charges were integer multiples of a fundamental elementary charge, confirming the quantization of electric charge.
