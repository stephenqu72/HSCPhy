To solve this problem, we look at the relationship between the work done by the electric field and the kinetic energy of the particle.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Work-Energy Principle:** The work done ($W$) by the electric force on the particle is equal to the change in its kinetic energy ($\Delta K$).
    $$W = \Delta K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$$
    Since the particle starts from rest, $u = 0$, so:
    $$W = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

2.  **Work in an Electric Field:** The work done by a uniform electric field $E$ on a charge $q$ over a distance $d$ is given by:
    $$W = Fd = qEd$$

3.  **Relating Velocity to Distance:**
    For the first case (distance $d$):
    $$qEd = \frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 \quad \text{--- (Equation 1)}$$

    For the second case (distance $2d$):
    Let the new velocity be $v'$.
    $$qE(2d) = \frac{1}{2}m(v')^2 \quad \text{--- (Equation 2)}$$

4.  **Solving for $v'$:**
    Notice that Equation 2 is exactly double the value of Equation 1:
    $$2(qEd) = 2 \left( \frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 \right)$$
    $$\frac{1}{2}m(v')^2 = m v_0^2$$
    $$(v')^2 = 2v_0^2$$
    Taking the square root of both sides:
    $$v' = \sqrt{2}v_0$$
