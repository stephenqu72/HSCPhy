### Explanation

To solve this, we must evaluate which of these observations are standard pieces of evidence for **Special Relativity** (which deals with constant velocity and inertial frames) versus **General Relativity** (which deals with gravity and acceleration).

*   **A. Observation of cosmic-muons:** This is a classic example of **time dilation**. Muons created in the upper atmosphere have a very short half-life. Without time dilation, they would decay before reaching the Earth's surface. Observing them at sea level is strong evidence for Special Relativity.
*   **B. The desynchronisation of atomic clocks:** While atomic clocks *do* experience time dilation, the primary "desynchronisation" observed in high-precision experiments (like the Hafele-Keating experiment or GPS satellite systems) involves accounting for **General Relativity** (gravitational time dilation) alongside Special Relativity (velocity-based time dilation). Because this phenomenon is fundamentally linked to gravity and general frames of reference rather than being exclusive to Special Relativity, it is the "least" correct choice as pure evidence for Special Relativity in this context.
*   **C. Momentum dilation:** As particles in accelerators approach the speed of light $c$, their relativistic momentum $p = \gamma mv$ increases. Experiments show that this momentum increases according to the Lorentz factor $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$, which is a direct consequence of Special Relativity.
*   **D. No change in the velocity of light (The Michelson-Morley Experiment):** This is the foundational evidence for Special Relativity. By showing that the speed of light remained constant regardless of the Earth's motion through the "aether," it provided the empirical basis for Einstein’s second postulate: that the speed of light is constant in all inertial frames.

**Conclusion:** Options A, C, and D are direct confirmations of the postulates of Special Relativity. Option B typically involves corrections for both Special and General Relativity, making it the correct choice for "NOT evidence" exclusively for Special Relativity.
