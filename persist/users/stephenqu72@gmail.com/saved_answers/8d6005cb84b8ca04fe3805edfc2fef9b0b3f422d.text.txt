To solve this question, we must apply our understanding of stellar spectroscopy and the Doppler effect.

### Answer
1. **The shift (Doppler Effect):** The spectral lines are shifted toward shorter wavelengths (a "blueshift," indicated here by the "shift downwards" in numerical wavelength values). This indicates that the star is moving **towards** the observer (Earth).
2. **The broadening (Stellar Rotation):** The broadening of the spectral lines is caused by the **rotation** of the star. As the star rotates, one side moves towards the observer (causing a blueshift) and the other side moves away (causing a redshift). This smears the spectral lines, resulting in a broader appearance compared to a stationary, non-rotating source.

### Teacher's Explanation
When answering HSC Physics questions regarding stellar spectra, you need to identify the physical phenomena behind specific spectral modifications:

*   **For the "Shift" (1 mark):** In astronomy, a shift in wavelength is governed by the **Doppler Effect**. 
    *   If lines shift to longer wavelengths (higher numbers), it is a redshift (moving away).
    *   If lines shift to shorter wavelengths (lower numbers, often described as shifting "downwards"), it is a **blueshift**, meaning the object is approaching the observer. Always link the direction of the shift to the relative motion of the source.

*   **For the "Broadening" (1 mark):** Spectral line broadening is a classic topic in the Astrophysics module. While broadening can be caused by high pressure (pressure broadening) or high temperature (thermal broadening), in the context of stars, **rotation** is the most common cause tested. Because the star is a sphere, different parts of its surface have different line-of-sight velocities relative to the observer. The integration of these different Doppler-shifted light sources across the stellar disk results in a widened spectral profile.
