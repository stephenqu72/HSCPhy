### Answer
The concept of energy quantisation emerged as a solution to the failure of classical physics to explain the behaviour of radiation and matter at the atomic scale. 

1. **Blackbody Radiation (Planck):** Classical physics predicted the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe," where an object should emit infinite energy at high frequencies. Max Planck resolved this in 1900 by proposing that energy is emitted or absorbed in discrete "packets" or quanta, defined by $E = nhf$. This was the foundational moment of quantum theory.

2. **The Photoelectric Effect (Einstein):** Einstein extended Planck's idea to light itself, proposing the photon model. Experimental evidence showed that photoelectric emission depends on frequency, not intensity. This confirmed that light behaves as quantised particles (photons) with energy $E = hf$, providing empirical validation for quantisation.

3. **Atomic Models (Bohr):** Bohr applied quantisation to matter by proposing that electrons in atoms exist in stationary, quantised energy levels. He postulated that electrons can only transition between these levels by emitting or absorbing a photon of energy equal to the difference between the levels: $\Delta E = |E_2 - E_1| = hf$. This explained the discrete spectral lines observed in hydrogen emission spectra, which classical mechanics could not account for.

4. **Matter Waves (de Broglie):** Louis de Broglie hypothesised that if light (a wave) could behave as a particle, then matter (particles) could behave as a wave. He proposed the relationship $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$. This linked quantisation to matter, as the stability of Bohr's orbits can be explained by electrons forming standing waves around the nucleus, where the circumference must be an integer multiple of the wavelength ($2\pi r = n\lambda$).
