To solve this problem, we must analyze the forces acting on the car when it is travelling at the "design speed" (where no friction is required).

### Part (a): Derivation of the design speed equation

1. **Identify the forces:** The two forces acting on the car are the gravitational force ($mg$) acting downwards and the normal force ($N$) acting perpendicular to the banked road.
2. **Resolve the normal force:** The normal force $N$ can be resolved into two components:
   - The vertical component that balances gravity: $N \cos \theta = mg$
   - The horizontal component that provides the centripetal force ($F_c$) required for circular motion: $N \sin \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$
3. **Form the ratio:** Divide the horizontal component equation by the vertical component equation:
   $$\frac{N \sin \theta}{N \cos \theta} = \frac{\frac{mv^2}{r}}{mg}$$
4. **Simplify:** The $N$ and $m$ terms cancel out, and $\frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} = \tan \theta$:
   $$\tan \theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$
5. **Rearrange for $v$:**
   $$v^2 = rg \tan \theta \implies v = \sqrt{rg \tan \theta}$$

### Part (b): Explain what occurs if the car travels slower than the design speed

If the car travels slower than the design speed, the required centripetal force ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$) decreases. Since the normal force is still acting at angle $\theta$, the horizontal component of the normal force ($N \sin \theta$) becomes greater than the required centripetal force. 

Consequently, the car will have a tendency to slide down the incline. To prevent this, the tyres must exert an upward (uphill) component of static friction. Therefore, at speeds slower than the design speed, the car relies on **sideway friction acting up the slope** to keep it on its circular path.
