### Explanation
To arrive at this answer, we must differentiate between "errors" (which include mistakes) and "uncertainty":

1.  **Understanding Uncertainty:** In physics and chemistry, experimental uncertainty represents the range within which the "true value" of a quantity is expected to lie. It acknowledges that no measurement is perfect due to the limits of our equipment or the nature of the physical system. It is usually expressed as $x \pm \Delta x$, where $\Delta x$ is the uncertainty.
2.  **Why B and C are incorrect:** Options B and C describe **mistakes** (or blunders). In scientific practice, a mistake is an avoidable error (like misreading a scale or using the wrong chemical). Mistakes should be eliminated from data sets entirely, whereas uncertainty is an inherent, unavoidable aspect of measurement that is quantified and reported alongside the result.
3.  **Why A is incorrect:** Validity refers to whether an experiment actually tests the hypothesis or research question (e.g., controlling all variables to ensure a fair test). Uncertainty relates to the **precision** and **accuracy** of the numerical data, not the validity of the experimental design itself.
4.  **Why D is correct:** Uncertainty defines the interval around the measured value that accounts for the potential deviation from the true value. By reporting a value with an uncertainty, we are essentially stating our confidence in the "likely difference" between our result and the theoretical "true" value.
