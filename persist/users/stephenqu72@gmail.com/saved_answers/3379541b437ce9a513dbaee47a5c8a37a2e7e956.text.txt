### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the scope of James Clerk Maxwell’s work in the context of the history of physics:

1.  **Maxwell’s Equations:** Published in the 1860s, these equations unified electricity and magnetism into the theory of electromagnetism. They successfully predicted that light is an electromagnetic wave travelling at a constant speed, defined by the permittivity ($\epsilon_0$) and permeability ($\mu_0$) of free space, represented by the relationship:
    $$c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$$
    This addresses options **(A)**, **(C)**, and **(D)**. Maxwell's equations confirmed the wave nature of light and implied that electromagnetic waves could exist at any frequency, predicting the existence of the electromagnetic spectrum beyond the visible range.

2.  **The Photoelectric Effect:** This phenomenon describes the emission of electrons when light shines on a material. Classical electromagnetic theory (Maxwell’s equations) predicts that the energy of the emitted electrons should depend on the *intensity* (amplitude) of the light. However, experimental observations showed that the energy of the electrons depends on the *frequency* of the light, regardless of intensity. 

3.  **Conclusion:** The photoelectric effect could not be explained by classical wave theory. It required Albert Einstein’s 1905 hypothesis that light is quantized into discrete packets (photons). Therefore, the photoelectric effect represents a particle-like nature of light that is not addressed by the classical wave-based framework of Maxwell’s equations.
