The correct answer is **(B)**.

### Explanation
This question tests your understanding of the **photoelectric effect**, which provided critical evidence that light behaves as particles (photons) rather than purely as a continuous wave.

1.  **The Wave Theory Prediction:** According to the classical wave theory of light, the energy of a light wave is determined by its **intensity** (amplitude). If light were purely a wave, even if the frequency was low, increasing the intensity should eventually provide enough energy to "knock" an electron off the metal surface given enough time.
2.  **The Observation:** The experiment shows that even when the intensity is increased, no electrons are emitted. This indicates that the energy of the individual photons ($E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$) is insufficient to overcome the work function ($\Phi$) of the metal. 
3.  **The Conclusion:** Because increasing the intensity (which increases the number of photons) does not result in emission, we know that emission depends on the **frequency (or wavelength)** of the light, not its intensity. This contradicts the classical wave model of light, which suggests that intensity should be the governing factor for energy transfer in this context. Therefore, this experimental result is evidence that light is **not behaving as a wave** in this interaction; it is exhibiting its particle nature.

*   (A) is incorrect because the result explicitly contradicts wave model predictions.
*   (C) and (D) are incorrect because light clearly carries energy; the energy per photon is simply too low to overcome the threshold required for the photoelectric effect in this specific metal.
