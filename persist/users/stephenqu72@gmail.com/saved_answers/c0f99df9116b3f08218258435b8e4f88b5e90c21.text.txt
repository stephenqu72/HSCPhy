To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of **Special Relativity**, specifically **length contraction** and **time dilation**.

### Step-by-Step Explanation

**1. Identify the given values:**
*   Proper distance ($L_0$) = $5.963 \text{ light years}$ (measured by an observer on Earth).
*   Velocity ($v$) = $0.15c$ (where $c$ is the speed of light).

**2. Calculate the contracted distance ($L$):**
From the astronaut's perspective (moving at $0.15c$ relative to Earth and the star), the distance is shortened due to length contraction. The formula is:
$$L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$
Substituting $v = 0.15c$:
$$L = 5.963 \times \sqrt{1 - (0.15)^2}$$
$$L = 5.963 \times \sqrt{1 - 0.0225}$$
$$L = 5.963 \times \sqrt{0.9775} \approx 5.963 \times 0.988686 \approx 5.8955 \text{ light years}$$

**3. Calculate the time of travel ($\Delta t$):**
The astronaut experiences "proper time" as they see the star moving toward them across the contracted distance $L$ at a speed of $0.15c$.
$$\Delta t = \frac{L}{v}$$
$$\Delta t = \frac{5.8955 \text{ light years}}{0.15c}$$
Since $1 \text{ light year} = c \times 1 \text{ year}$, the $c$ values cancel out:
$$\Delta t = \frac{5.8955}{0.15} \text{ years} \approx 39.303 \text{ years}$$

**Conclusion:**
Rounding to one decimal place, the time of travel for the astronaut is approximately **39.3 years**.
