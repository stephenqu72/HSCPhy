To solve this problem, we look at the formula for the magnetic force per unit length ($F/L$) between two parallel, long, straight current-carrying wires:

$$\frac{F}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi r}$$

Where:
- $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space.
- $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the currents in the two wires.
- $r$ is the separation distance between the wires.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the relationship:** From the formula, we can see that the force per unit length is inversely proportional to the separation distance $r$. That is, $F \propto \frac{1}{r}$.
2.  **Apply the change:** The question states that the separation distance is doubled. If we replace $r$ with $2r$ in our relationship:
    $$F_{new} \propto \frac{1}{2r}$$
3.  **Compare to original:** Since the original force was $F \propto \frac{1}{r}$, replacing $r$ with $2r$ results in the force being divided by 2:
    $$F_{new} = \frac{1}{2} \times F = \frac{F}{2}$$

Therefore, when the separation distance is doubled, the force per unit length is halved.
