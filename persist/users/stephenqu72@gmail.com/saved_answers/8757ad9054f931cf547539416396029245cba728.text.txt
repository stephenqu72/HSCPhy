To solve this problem, we need to equate the useful output power of the motor to the change in gravitational potential energy of the mass over time.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Calculate the total electrical power input ($P_{in}$):**
The motor draws $2\text{ A}$ at $12\text{ V}$. Power is calculated using $P = VI$:
$$P_{in} = 12\text{ V} \times 2\text{ A} = 24\text{ W}$$

**2. Calculate the useful mechanical power output ($P_{out}$):**
The motor is $30\%$ efficient, meaning only $30\%$ of the electrical energy is converted into gravitational potential energy:
$$P_{out} = 0.30 \times 24\text{ W} = 7.2\text{ W}$$

**3. Relate power to work and height:**
Mechanical power is defined as the rate of doing work ($P = \frac{W}{t}$). The work done in raising the mass is the change in gravitational potential energy ($\Delta U = mgh$):
$$P_{out} = \frac{mgh}{t}$$
Where:
*   $m = 3\text{ kg}$
*   $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$ (standard gravity)
*   $t = 5\text{ s}$
*   $h = \text{height}$

**4. Solve for $h$:**
Rearrange the equation for $h$:
$$h = \frac{P_{out} \times t}{m \times g}$$
$$h = \frac{7.2\text{ W} \times 5\text{ s}}{3\text{ kg} \times 9.8\text{ m/s}^2}$$
$$h = \frac{36}{29.4}$$
$$h \approx 1.22\text{ m}$$

*(Note: If using $g = 10\text{ m/s}^2$ for simplicity, $h = \frac{36}{30} = 1.2\text{ m}$)*.

**Final Answer:**
The motor raises the mass to a height of approximately **$1.22\text{ m}$**.
