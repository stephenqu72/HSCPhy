### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the historical context of classical physics at the turn of the 20th century:

1.  **Classical Conservation Laws:** Before Einstein’s 1905 paper, physics was governed by the separate principles of the **Conservation of Mass** (as formulated by Lavoisier) and the **Conservation of Energy** (the First Law of Thermodynamics). Scientists believed that mass and energy were two distinct, immutable entities that could not be converted into one another.
2.  **The Impact of $E = mc^2$:** Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle proposed that mass and energy were not independent, but were actually different forms of the same thing. This meant that mass could be "lost" or "gained" in a reaction if it were converted into energy, which appeared to directly contradict the long-held Law of Conservation of Mass (which stated that mass cannot be created or destroyed).
3.  **Evaluating the distractors:**
    *   **(B)** is incorrect because the theory actually *extended* the law of conservation of energy to include mass-energy, rather than contradicting it.
    *   **(C)** is a vague statement. While it did challenge the *foundation* of classical physics, (A) is a more specific and accurate description of the immediate conceptual hurdle it presented to scientists at the time.
    *   **(D)** is incorrect because, while it is true that there was little experimental evidence at the time, the *controversy* stemmed primarily from the fundamental theoretical conflict with the well-established principle of conservation of mass.

In the HSC Physics curriculum, it is vital to understand that Einstein’s work unified these two separate conservation laws into a single, comprehensive law: the **Law of Conservation of Mass-Energy**.
