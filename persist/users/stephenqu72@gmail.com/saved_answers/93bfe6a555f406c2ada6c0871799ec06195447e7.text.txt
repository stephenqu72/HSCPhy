To solve this problem, we need to analyze the vertical motion of the ball to determine how the "time in the air" changes when the height of the throw is increased.

### Step 1: Analyze the vertical motion
The ball is thrown vertically upward and caught at the same level. The time the ball spends in the air ($t$) is given by the kinematic equation for vertical displacement being zero:
$$s = u_y t + \frac{1}{2} a_y t^2$$
$$0 = u_y t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2$$
Solving for $t$:
$$t = \frac{2 u_y}{g}$$
The maximum height ($h$) reached by the ball is given by the formula:
$$h = \frac{u_y^2}{2g}$$
This shows that the maximum height is proportional to the square of the initial vertical velocity: $h \propto u_y^2$. Consequently, the initial vertical velocity is proportional to the square root of the height: $u_y \propto \sqrt{h}$.

### Step 2: Determine the change in time
Since $t = \frac{2 u_y}{g}$, the time in the air is directly proportional to the initial vertical velocity: $t \propto u_y$.
Combining these relationships, we find that the time the ball is in the air is proportional to the square root of the height:
$$t \propto \sqrt{h}$$

### Step 3: Calculate the new distance
In the first case, the car travels $100\text{ m}$ while the ball is in the air. Assuming the car moves at a constant velocity $v$, the distance $d$ is:
$$d = v \times t_1 = 100\text{ m}$$
In the second case, the height is $4$ times higher ($h_2 = 4h_1$). The new time in the air $t_2$ will be:
$$t_2 \propto \sqrt{4h_1} = 2\sqrt{h_1} = 2 t_1$$
Since the time in the air doubles, and the car moves at the same velocity $v$, the new distance $d_2$ is:
$$d_2 = v \times t_2 = v \times (2t_1) = 2 \times (v \times t_1) = 2 \times 100\text{ m} = 200\text{ m}$$
