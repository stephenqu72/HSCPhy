### Answer
The Big Bang primarily produced hydrogen, helium, and trace amounts of lithium. Elements heavier than these are created through stellar nucleosynthesis:

1.  **Main Sequence Stars:** These stars primarily fuse hydrogen into helium via the proton-proton chain or the CNO cycle. This provides the primary energy source and converts light elements into helium.
2.  **Post-Main Sequence (Red Giant/Supergiant):** As a star exhausts its hydrogen, the core contracts and heats up, allowing for the fusion of helium into carbon, and subsequently oxygen, neon, and magnesium. In more massive stars, advanced burning stages continue this progression, creating elements up to iron ($^{56}Fe$) via successive alpha-capture processes.
3.  **Supernova Nucleosynthesis:** Elements heavier than iron cannot be created through fusion because the process becomes endothermic. These elements (e.g., gold, uranium) are created via the **r-process** (rapid neutron capture) during the extreme conditions of a supernova explosion. 
4.  **Distribution:** The death of these stars through planetary nebulae or supernova explosions disperses these newly synthesized elements into the interstellar medium, where they enrich subsequent generations of stars and form the 94 naturally occurring elements.
