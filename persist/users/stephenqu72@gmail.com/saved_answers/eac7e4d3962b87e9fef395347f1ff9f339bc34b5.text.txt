### Part (a)
**Answer:**
Bohr justified his postulates by addressing the deficiencies in Rutherford’s model using empirical evidence:
1. **Rutherford’s Nuclear Model:** Provided the evidence for a dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by empty space, which Bohr incorporated as the center of his atom.
2. **Atomic Spectra (Balmer/Rydberg):** Bohr used the mathematical patterns discovered by Balmer and Rydberg to explain why electrons exist in discrete energy levels. He postulated that electrons do not radiate energy while in stable "stationary states," only emitting or absorbing a photon of energy $E = hf$ when transitioning between levels. This directly accounted for the observed line emission spectra of hydrogen.

**How to solve:** Focus on the "why." You must link the *evidence* (the unstable nature of orbiting electrons and the observed discrete spectral lines) to Bohr's *solutions* (quantized orbits and stationary states).

### Part (c)
**Answer:**
1. **Multi-electron atoms:** Bohr’s model could only accurately predict the spectra of hydrogen and hydrogen-like ions (single electron). It failed to account for electron-electron repulsions in more complex atoms.
2. **Relative intensity of spectral lines:** The model could predict the *frequency* of spectral lines, but it could not predict the *intensity* or probability of different transitions occurring.
3. **Hyperfine structure/Zeeman/Stark effects:** The model could not explain why spectral lines split into multiple finer lines when the atom is placed in a strong magnetic field (Zeeman effect) or electric field (Stark effect).
*(Bonus: It also failed to incorporate the wave-particle duality and the Uncertainty Principle).*

**How to solve:** List three distinct, specific failures. Using technical terms like "Zeeman effect" or "multi-electron interactions" will ensure you receive full marks.
