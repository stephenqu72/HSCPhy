To determine the number of photons emitted per second, we must relate the total power of the laser to the energy of an individual photon.

### Step-by-Step Solution

**1. Identify the given information:**
*   Power ($P$) = $0.5\text{ mW} = 0.5 \times 10^{-3}\text{ W} = 5 \times 10^{-4}\text{ J/s}$
*   Wavelength ($\lambda$) = $610\text{ nm} = 610 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m} = 6.1 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$
*   Planck's constant ($h$) $\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J s}$
*   Speed of light ($c$) $\approx 3.0 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$

**2. Calculate the energy of a single photon ($E$):**
Using the formula $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$:
$$E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J s}) \times (3.0 \times 10^8\text{ m/s})}{6.1 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}}$$
$$E \approx \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{6.1 \times 10^{-7}}\text{ J}$$
$$E \approx 3.258 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$$

**3. Calculate the number of photons per second ($n$):**
The total power $P$ is the energy per second, which equals the number of photons per second ($n$) multiplied by the energy of one photon ($E$):
$$P = n \times E \implies n = \frac{P}{E}$$
$$n = \frac{5 \times 10^{-4}\text{ J/s}}{3.258 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}}$$
$$n \approx 1.53 \times 10^{15}$$

Rounding to two significant figures, we get $1.5 \times 10^{15}$ photons per second.
