To solve this problem, we must understand the fundamental process of **beta-minus ($\beta^-$) decay**.

### Explanation

In beta-minus decay, a neutron within the nucleus is converted into a proton, an electron (which is ejected as a beta particle), and an electron antineutrino. This process can be represented by the following nuclear equation:

$$n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

Let's look at how this changes the composition of the nucleus:

1.  **Neutrons:** Because one neutron is converted into a proton, the total number of neutrons in the nucleus decreases by $1$.
2.  **Protons:** Because a new proton is created from that neutron, the total number of protons in the nucleus increases by $1$.
3.  **Mass Number:** Since the total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons) remains constant, the mass number does not change.
4.  **Atomic Number:** Since the number of protons increases by $1$, the atomic number increases by $1$, which changes the identity of the element.

Comparing this to the provided options:
*   (A) describes alpha decay (loss of 2 protons and 2 neutrons).
*   (B) describes alpha decay (loss of 2 protons and 2 neutrons).
*   **(C) matches our derivation: the nucleus has one more proton and one less neutron.**
*   (D) describes positron emission ($\beta^+$ decay).

Therefore, the correct answer is **(C)**.
