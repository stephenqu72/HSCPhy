### Answer

**(a)**
To calculate the wavelength, we use the de Broglie equation:
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$$

**Values:**
- Planck's constant ($h$) = $6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$
- Mass of an electron ($m$) = $9.109 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
- Speed of the electron ($v$) = $2.188 \times 10^6 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

**Calculation:**
$$\lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{(9.109 \times 10^{-31}) \times (2.188 \times 10^6)}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{1.993 \times 10^{-24}}$$
$$\lambda \approx 3.32 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m}$$

**(b)**
Bohr postulated that electrons move in stable orbits but could not provide a physical reason for why only certain orbits were "allowed." De Broglie’s hypothesis that all matter exhibits wave-like properties provided the theoretical justification. 

De Broglie proposed that an electron in a stable orbit behaves as a **standing wave**. For a standing wave to exist around the nucleus without interfering with itself, the circumference of the orbit must be an integer multiple of the electron's wavelength:
$$2\pi r = n\lambda$$
Substituting the de Broglie wavelength ($\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$), we get $2\pi r = \frac{nh}{mv}$, which rearranges to $mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi}$. This matches Bohr’s condition for **quantized angular momentum**. Thus, electrons are stable because they exist in resonant standing-wave patterns, preventing them from spiraling into the nucleus.
