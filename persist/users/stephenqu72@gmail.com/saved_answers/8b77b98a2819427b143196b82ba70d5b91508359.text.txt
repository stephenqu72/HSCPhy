### Answer
The correct answer is **A. Cathode rays travel in straight lines.**

### Explanation
This experiment is a classic demonstration involving a **Maltese Cross tube** (often associated with the work of early cathode ray tube researchers like William Crookes). 

1.  **Observation:** In the image, we can see a clear, sharp shadow of the metal Maltese cross projected onto the phosphor-coated end of the vacuum tube.
2.  **Physics Principle:** The shadow is formed because the "rays" (now known to be a stream of electrons) emitted from the cathode travel in straight lines and are blocked by the opaque object (the cross). Because the rays cannot bend around the corners of the object, they create a sharp, well-defined shadow on the screen behind it. 
3.  **Evaluating other options:**
    *   While cathode rays *are* negatively charged (Option B), this is determined by experiments involving electric or magnetic fields (e.g., J.J. Thomson’s deflection experiments), not a simple shadow experiment.
    *   While they have mass (Option C), this was determined by observing the mechanical effect of rays hitting a paddle wheel or through precise measurement of $e/m$ ratios.
    *   While they travel from cathode to anode (Option D), the shadow experiment specifically highlights the *rectilinear propagation* (straight-line motion) of the rays, rather than the specific electrode origins.

Therefore, the defining property demonstrated by the casting of a sharp shadow is that they travel in straight lines.
