### Teacher's Explanation and Answer

To solve this question, we must look at the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram to identify the characteristics of the stars and apply our knowledge of stellar nucleosynthesis.

**Part (a): Comparing Nucleosynthesis**
*   **Identification:** Star X is located on the Main Sequence at a lower temperature and luminosity (similar to the Sun), while Star Y is a high-luminosity star (a giant/supergiant, located above the main sequence).
*   **Star X (Main Sequence):** Since it is a lower-mass/medium-mass main sequence star, the primary process for converting hydrogen to helium is the **proton-proton (p-p) chain**.
*   **Star Y (High Luminosity/Mass):** Because Star Y is significantly more luminous and hotter (or an evolved giant), it has a higher core temperature. It utilizes the **CNO cycle** (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle) to fuse hydrogen into helium, which is much more efficient at higher temperatures.

**Answer (a):**
Star X is a lower-mass main sequence star and primarily uses the proton-proton (p-p) chain to fuse hydrogen into helium. Star Y, being a more massive or evolved star with a higher core temperature, utilizes the CNO cycle to fuse hydrogen into helium. The CNO cycle is a much more temperature-sensitive process than the p-p chain, allowing the more massive Star Y to achieve higher rates of energy production.

***

**Part (b): Formation of elements up to Iron**
*   **Concepts:** Once a star leaves the main sequence, it exhausts its hydrogen core. In stars massive enough to reach the fusion of elements up to iron, the star undergoes successive shell burning.
*   **The Process:** After hydrogen exhaustion, the star contracts until core temperatures allow for **helium burning (the triple-alpha process)** to create carbon. As the star evolves, it develops an "onion-skin" structure. Subsequent gravitational contraction raises the core temperature, allowing the fusion of heavier nuclei (e.g., carbon, neon, oxygen, silicon). This process continues sequentially until iron is formed in the core. Iron is the limit of fusion because it has the highest binding energy per nucleon; fusing iron consumes energy rather than releasing it, leading to the end of the star's stable life.

**Answer (b):**
After hydrogen is exhausted, Star Y undergoes gravitational contraction, heating the core enough to initiate helium fusion (the triple-alpha process) to form carbon. As the star continues to evolve and contract, it reaches temperatures high enough for subsequent stages of nuclear fusion, involving the fusion of heavier elements such as neon, oxygen, and silicon. This creates an "onion-skin" structure of concentric shells of burning elements, eventually leading to the synthesis of iron in the core. Fusion stops at iron because iron has the highest binding energy per nucleon; therefore, further fusion does not release energy.
