To determine the most likely value of the radioactive decay constant $\lambda$, we must first identify the half-life ($t_{1/2}$) of the isotope from the provided graph.

### Step-by-step Solution:

1.  **Find the half-life ($t_{1/2}$):** 
    The half-life is the time it takes for the quantity of radioactive nuclei to reduce to 50% of its initial value.
    - Initial amount at $t = 0$ is $100\%$.
    - Look at the graph for when the remaining nuclei equal $50\%$.
    - Tracking the curve from $50\%$ on the y-axis down to the x-axis, we see that the point falls exactly between $100$s and $150$s. Let's look for a clearer point to verify.
    - At $t = 200$s, the value is $32\%$.
    - At $t = 0$s, $N = 100$. After one half-life ($t_{1/2}$), $N = 50$. After two half-lives ($2 \times t_{1/2}$), $N = 25$. 
    - Looking at the graph, the value is $25\%$ at $t = 250$s.
    - If $2 \times t_{1/2} = 250$s, then $t_{1/2} = 125$s.

2.  **Calculate the decay constant ($\lambda$):**
    The relationship between the decay constant and the half-life is given by the formula:
    $$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$$
    Substitute the half-life value:
    $$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{125}$$
    $$\lambda \approx \frac{0.6931}{125}$$
    $$\lambda \approx 0.005545 \text{ s}^{-1}$$

3.  **Compare with the given options:**
    - (A) 125 (This is the half-life, not the decay constant)
    - (B) 50
    - (C) 0.005658
    - (D) 0.58
    
    The calculated value $0.005545$ is extremely close to $0.005658$. Minor discrepancies in reading the graph manually often lead to slight rounding differences, making (C) the correct choice.

**Answer:** (C) 0.005658
