To solve this problem, we need to apply the principle of mass-energy equivalence based on the proton-proton chain reaction occurring in the Sun, where four hydrogen nuclei ($^1H$) fuse to form one helium nucleus ($^4He$).

### Step 1: Calculate the mass defect
The reaction is effectively: $4 \times (^1H) \rightarrow ^4He + \text{energy}$.
- Mass of a proton ($^1H$) $\approx 1.007276 \text{ amu}$
- Mass of a helium nucleus $= 4.002602 \text{ amu}$

The mass defect ($\Delta m$) for the formation of one helium nucleus is:
$$\Delta m = (4 \times 1.007276) - 4.002602$$
$$\Delta m = 4.029104 - 4.002602 = 0.026502 \text{ amu}$$

### Step 2: Convert mass defect to energy (Joules)
Using the conversion factor $1 \text{ amu} = 1.6605 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$:
$$\Delta m (\text{kg}) = 0.026502 \times 1.6605 \times 10^{-27} \approx 4.4006 \times 10^{-29} \text{ kg}$$

Now, use Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence formula $E = \Delta m c^2$:
$$E = (4.4006 \times 10^{-29} \text{ kg}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})^2$$
$$E \approx 3.9605 \times 10^{-12} \text{ J per helium nucleus}$$

### Step 3: Determine the number of nuclei produced per second
The Sun produces a power of $3.85 \times 10^{26} \text{ W}$ (Joules per second). To find the number of helium nuclei ($N$) produced per second, divide the total power by the energy produced per nucleus:
$$N = \frac{\text{Total Power}}{\text{Energy per nucleus}}$$
$$N = \frac{3.85 \times 10^{26} \text{ J/s}}{3.9605 \times 10^{-12} \text{ J/nucleus}}$$
$$N \approx 9.72 \times 10^{37} \text{ nuclei/s}$$
