### Explanation
To solve this, we need to evaluate the properties of each field (gravitational, electric, and magnetic) against the statements provided:

*   **A. All three fields can be approximately uniform in a relatively small region:** This is **correct**. For example, the Earth's gravitational field near the surface is roughly constant ($g \approx 9.8 \, \text{ms}^{-2}$), the electric field between parallel plates is uniform, and the magnetic field inside a long solenoid or between the poles of a strong permanent magnet is often considered uniform.
*   **B. All three fields are vector quantities:** This is **correct**. Each of these fields has both a magnitude and a direction at any point in space, which is the definition of a vector field.
*   **C. All three fields follow the inverse square law:** This is **NOT correct**. While gravitational fields (Newton’s Law of Gravitation: $F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$) and electrostatic fields (Coulomb’s Law: $F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$) follow the inverse square law, **magnetic fields do not**. Magnetic fields are generated by dipoles (or moving charges/currents). The field of a magnetic dipole falls off as $1/r^3$ along the axis, which is a different mathematical relationship than the inverse square law.
*   **D. All three fields can be static:** This is **correct**. We can have static mass distributions, static charge distributions (electrostatics), and static magnetic fields produced by permanent magnets or constant DC currents (magnetostatics).

Since the question asks for the statement that is **NOT** correct, C is the clear choice.
