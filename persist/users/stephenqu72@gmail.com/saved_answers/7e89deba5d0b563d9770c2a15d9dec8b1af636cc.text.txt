To solve this problem, we need to calculate the energy of the emitted photon and then convert that energy into a wavelength using the relationship between energy, Planck's constant, and the speed of light.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

**1. Calculate the energy difference ($\Delta E$):**
When an electron transitions from a higher energy level ($n_i = 5$) to a lower energy level ($n_f = 2$), the energy of the emitted photon is equal to the difference between these two energy levels:
$$\Delta E = E_5 - E_2$$
From the diagram provided:
$$E_5 = -0.54 \text{ eV}$$
$$E_2 = -3.40 \text{ eV}$$
$$\Delta E = -0.54 \text{ eV} - (-3.40 \text{ eV}) = 2.86 \text{ eV}$$

**2. Convert the energy from eV to Joules:**
Since $1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$:
$$\Delta E = 2.86 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} \approx 4.582 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

**3. Calculate the wavelength ($\lambda$):**
We use the formula $\Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$, rearranged to solve for $\lambda$:
$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E}$$
Where:
- $h$ (Planck's constant) $\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
- $c$ (speed of light) $\approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$

$$\lambda = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{4.582 \times 10^{-19}}$$
$$\lambda \approx 4.337 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$$

**4. Convert to nanometers (nm):**
Since $1 \text{ nm} = 10^{-9} \text{ m}$:
$$\lambda \approx 433.7 \text{ nm} \approx 434 \text{ nm}$$
