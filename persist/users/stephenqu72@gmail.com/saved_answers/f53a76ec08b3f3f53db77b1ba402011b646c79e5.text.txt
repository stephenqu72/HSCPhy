### Answer
The investigation effectively models the expansion of the universe, specifically demonstrating **Hubble’s Law**. The observations align with the cosmological principle that space itself is expanding, which leads to the proportionality between a galaxy's distance and its recessional velocity.

### Explanation and Justification
To earn full marks, you must link the physical observations of the elastic band to the astronomical evidence observed by Hubble.

1.  **Uniform Expansion:** In the investigation, every distance ($d_1$, $d_2$, $d_3$) doubles when the elastic is pulled. This represents the homogeneous and isotropic expansion of space. In the universe, this means that space is stretching uniformly in all directions.
2.  **Recessional Velocity:** Velocity is defined as $v = \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}$. If every point on the elastic moves to a new position in the same time interval, points further from the fixed end must move a greater distance than points closer to the fixed end. Consequently, points further away move at a higher velocity.
3.  **Hubble's Law ($v = H_0 d$):** Hubble observed that galaxies are moving away from us at speeds proportional to their distance. The elastic model demonstrates this:
    *   Let the initial positions be $d_1, d_2, d_3$.
    *   After stretching, the new positions are $2d_1, 2d_2, 2d_3$.
    *   The change in position (displacement) is $\Delta d_1 = d_1$, $\Delta d_2 = d_2$, and $\Delta d_3 = d_3$.
    *   Since $v \propto \Delta d$, it follows that $v_1 < v_2 < v_3$. This confirms that the further an object is from the observer (the fixed end), the faster it appears to be receding.
4.  **Limitations (Evaluation):** To provide a high-level response, note that while the model captures the *kinematics* of expansion, it is limited because the galaxies themselves are not "stretching" like the elastic; rather, the space *between* the galaxies is increasing while the galaxies remain gravitationally bound.
