### Answer

Here are three components of the electricity grid and their operational explanations:

**1. Step-up Transformers (at the Power Station)**
Step-up transformers are used to increase the voltage ($V$) of electricity generated at the power station before transmission. According to the power equation $P = VI$, if power remains constant, increasing the voltage necessitates a proportional decrease in current ($I$). By reducing the current, energy loss due to resistive heating in the transmission lines—calculated by $P_{loss} = I^2R$—is significantly minimized. This allows for long-distance, efficient transmission with minimal thermal energy dissipation.

**2. Transmission Lines**
Transmission lines serve as the transport medium for electrical energy over vast distances. These conductors are typically made of materials with high conductivity (like aluminum) to keep resistance ($R$) low. Because the electricity is transmitted at very high voltages (up to 500 kV), the current is kept low. This ensures that the potential difference is maintained over long distances, reducing the energy lost as heat to the environment, thereby maintaining the efficiency of the grid.

**3. Step-down Transformers (at Distribution Sites)**
Step-down transformers are located near residential and industrial areas to safely convert high-voltage electricity into a lower, usable voltage (e.g., 240 V in Australia). These devices operate on the principle of electromagnetic induction. An alternating current in the primary coil creates a changing magnetic flux in the iron core, which induces a voltage in the secondary coil. The ratio of the number of turns in the coils ($N_p/N_s = V_p/V_s$) allows the grid to transform the high-voltage transmission power into a safe, manageable voltage for consumer appliances.

### How to solve this
1.  **Allocate Time and Marks:** Since this is a 9-mark question, aim for approximately 3 marks per component. You need depth, not just a list.
2.  **Use the "Cause and Effect" structure:** For each component, identify the physics principle involved (e.g., Electromagnetic Induction, Joule heating/Ohmic losses).
3.  **Use correct terminology:** Use terms like *magnetic flux*, *induced EMF*, *resistive heating*, *potential difference*, and *current*.
4.  **Connect to the Grid:** Always link the function of the component back to the broader goal: the efficient transfer of energy from the source to the load.
