To solve this problem, we must analyze the forces acting on a car moving in a circular path on a flat road.

### Answer
The new maximum velocity is $\frac{v}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707v$ $\text{km h}^{-1}$.

### Explanation
**1. Understand the physics of the initial state:**
When a car turns on a flat road, the centripetal force required to keep the car moving in a circle is provided entirely by static friction.
The centripetal force is given by:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
The maximum frictional force is given by:
$$f_{max} = \mu N = \mu mg$$
Where $\mu$ is the coefficient of friction, $m$ is the mass, and $g$ is gravity. Equating these for the maximum speed $v$:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \mu mg$$
$$v^2 = \mu rg$$

**2. Analyze the change in conditions:**
The problem states that the oil spill halves the friction. Let the new coefficient of friction be $\mu' = \frac{1}{2}\mu$. We want to find the new maximum velocity $v'$:
$$\frac{m(v')^2}{r} = \mu' mg$$
$$\frac{m(v')^2}{r} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\mu\right)mg$$
$$(v')^2 = \frac{1}{2} \mu rg$$

**3. Relate the new state to the original state:**
Since we know from the first step that $\mu rg = v^2$, we can substitute this into our equation for $(v')^2$:
$$(v')^2 = \frac{1}{2}v^2$$
Taking the square root of both sides:
$$v' = \sqrt{\frac{v^2}{2}} = \frac{v}{\sqrt{2}}$$

Thus, the new maximum velocity is $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ of the original velocity.
