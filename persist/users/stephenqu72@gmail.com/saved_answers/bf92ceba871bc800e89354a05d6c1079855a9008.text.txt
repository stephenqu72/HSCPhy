To find the magnetic field strength ($B$), we use the definition of magnetic flux ($\Phi$):

$$\Phi = B \cdot A$$

Where:
*   $\Phi$ is the magnetic flux (in Webers, Wb)
*   $B$ is the magnetic field strength (in Tesla, T)
*   $A$ is the cross-sectional area (in square meters, $m^2$)

### Step-by-Step Calculation:

**1. Identify the given values:**
*   $\Phi = 80 \text{ mWb} = 80 \times 10^{-3} \text{ Wb} = 0.08 \text{ Wb}$
*   The dimensions are $5 \text{ cm} \times 10 \text{ cm}$. We must convert these to meters first.
    *   $5 \text{ cm} = 0.05 \text{ m}$
    *   $10 \text{ cm} = 0.10 \text{ m}$

**2. Calculate the area ($A$):**
$$A = 0.05 \text{ m} \times 0.10 \text{ m} = 0.005 \text{ m}^2$$

**3. Rearrange the formula to solve for $B$:**
$$B = \frac{\Phi}{A}$$

**4. Substitute the values:**
$$B = \frac{0.08 \text{ Wb}}{0.005 \text{ m}^2}$$
$$B = 16 \text{ T}$$

Comparing our result to the options provided, the correct answer is **(D)**.

***

### How to approach this in an exam:
*   **Units:** Always check units! HSC examiners love to catch students out by using cm instead of meters or mWb (milli-Webers) instead of Webers. Always convert to base SI units ($m$ and $Wb$) before calculating.
*   **Formula:** Remember that $B$ is the "flux density," so $B = \Phi / A$ is essentially calculating how much flux is packed into every square meter.
