### How to solve this question

To tackle this question, you need to understand the definitions and characteristics of the two main types of experimental errors:

1.  **Random Errors:** These are unpredictable fluctuations in readings that occur due to factors like human reaction time, variations in experimental conditions (e.g., slight temperature changes), or limitations of the measuring instrument. Because these errors are "random" (they fluctuate above and below the true value), if you take multiple readings and calculate the **mean (average)**, the positive and negative fluctuations tend to cancel each other out. Thus, repeated readings significantly improve the precision of your results.

2.  **Systematic Errors:** These are consistent, repeatable errors usually associated with faulty equipment or a flawed experimental method. For example, if a scale is not "zeroed" correctly (it always reads $0.5\text{ g}$ too high), every single measurement will be off by that same amount. Because the error is consistent, taking more readings will not change the result—you will just get the same biased average over and over. Systematic errors can only be reduced by recalibrating equipment or improving the experimental design.

**Evaluating the options:**
*   **(A) is correct:** By calculating the mean of repeated trials, we minimize the impact of random variations.
*   **(B) and (C) are incorrect:** Systematic errors remain constant regardless of how many times you repeat the measurement.
*   **(D) is incorrect:** Repeated readings are a standard technique specifically used to minimize random error.
