### Answer and Explanation

To explain why the car on the banked curve can travel at a higher speed, we must analyze the forces acting on the vehicles.

#### 1. Additions to the diagram (Conceptual)
*   **Horizontal road:** Draw a weight vector $mg$ downwards and a normal force $N$ upwards (equal to $mg$). Draw a horizontal centripetal force vector $F_c$ towards the left, provided entirely by static friction $f_s$ between the tires and the road.
*   **Banked road:** Draw a weight vector $mg$ downwards. Draw the normal force $N$ perpendicular to the road surface. Resolve $N$ into two components: $N \cos \theta$ (acting vertically to balance $mg$) and $N \sin \theta$ (acting horizontally towards the center of the curve).

#### 2. Explanation
The safety of a car taking a corner depends on the available net horizontal force (centripetal force) directed towards the center of the turn.

*   **On a flat road:** The only force providing the centripetal force is friction ($f_s$). The maximum speed is limited by the coefficient of friction ($\mu$): $v_{max} = \sqrt{\mu rg}$. Once the required centripetal force exceeds the maximum static friction, the car will skid.
*   **On a banked road:** The normal force $N$ is no longer strictly vertical. When the road is banked at an angle $\theta$, the horizontal component of the normal force, $N \sin \theta$, acts as an **additional** source of centripetal force. 
*   **Conclusion:** Because the normal force contributes to the centripetal acceleration ($F_c = N \sin \theta + f_s \cos \theta$), the reliance on friction is reduced. Even without friction, the car can negotiate the curve at a specific "design speed" where $v = \sqrt{rg \tan \theta}$. Consequently, the total available inward force is greater on the banked road, allowing for a significantly higher safe speed compared to the flat road.

2.  **Compare components:** Clearly distinguish that on a flat surface, the horizontal force is restricted to friction, whereas on a bank, the geometry of the normal force inherently provides a horizontal component toward the center of the circle.
3.  **Relate to Physics Principles:** Use the concept of circular motion ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$) to justify why increasing the inward force allows for an increase in velocity $v$.
4.  **Structure the answer:** Use a point-by-point format to ensure you hit all marking criteria (Diagram, Force identification, Comparison, and Conclusion).
