To solve this physics problem regarding projectile motion, we must analyze the behavior of the horizontal and vertical velocity components independently.

### 1. Horizontal Velocity ($v_x$)
In ideal projectile motion (ignoring air resistance), there are no horizontal forces acting on the ball after it is struck. According to Newton's First Law, an object in motion will maintain a constant velocity unless acted upon by an external force. Therefore, the horizontal velocity $v_x$ remains constant throughout the flight.
*   **Graph:** A constant value over time is represented by a horizontal straight line.
*   **Matching Graph:** Graph **X** shows a constant, non-zero value.

### 2. Vertical Velocity ($v_y$)
The only force acting on the ball in the vertical direction is gravity. This produces a constant downward acceleration ($g$). The vertical velocity is given by the equation:
$$v_y(t) = v_{0y} - gt$$
This is a linear equation with a negative slope ($-g$). 
*   Initially, the ball is traveling upwards ($v_y > 0$).
*   At the peak of the trajectory, the vertical velocity becomes zero.
*   After the peak, the ball travels downwards ($v_y < 0$).
*   **Graph:** This corresponds to a straight line starting at a positive value, crossing the time axis at the peak, and continuing into negative values.
*   **Matching Graph:** Graph **Z** represents this linear decrease of vertical velocity.
