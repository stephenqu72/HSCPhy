### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental definition of an inertial frame of reference in classical and relativistic mechanics:

1.  **Definition:** An inertial frame of reference is a frame in which Newton's First Law of motion holds true. This means an object in this frame will remain at rest or continue to move at a constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force.
2.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **A is correct:** For a frame to be "inertial," it must not be accelerating. Moving at a constant speed (including zero speed) implies that the velocity is constant, which satisfies the requirement for an inertial frame.
    *   **B is incorrect:** Special relativity applies even when objects are moving at relativistic speeds (near the speed of light $c$). An inertial frame can certainly move at $0.9c$; the speed itself does not disqualify it from being inertial.
    *   **C is incorrect:** The expansion of the universe is a cosmological concept and is not a requirement or a defining property for a frame to be considered inertial in the context of Einstein's postulates.
    *   **D is incorrect:** This is a distractor. While it is true that an inertial frame has no acceleration, the phrasing "No observer... can detect any acceleration" is better associated with the *Equivalence Principle* in General Relativity (which states that observers cannot distinguish between gravity and acceleration). In an inertial frame, if there were any acceleration, it would be physically detectable (e.g., via fictitious forces like the Coriolis force or centrifugal force); thus, it is the *absence* of acceleration that defines the frame, not the inability to detect it.

Therefore, the defining characteristic is that the frame is not accelerating, which is synonymous with moving at a constant velocity (constant speed and constant direction).
