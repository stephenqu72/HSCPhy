To assess the efficiency of these two proposals, we must calculate the power loss due to heat in the transmission lines.

### 1. Calculations

**Parameters:**
- Power produced ($P$): $100.0\text{ MW} = 1.00 \times 10^8\text{ W}$
- Distance ($d$): $15\text{ km} = 15,000\text{ m}$. Since transmission lines include two wires (out and return), the total length ($L$) is $30,000\text{ m}$.
- Resistance per unit length: $3.21 \times 10^{-4}\text{ }\Omega\text{ m}^{-1}$
- Total resistance ($R$): $30,000 \times 3.21 \times 10^{-4} = 9.63\text{ }\Omega$

**Proposal 1 (DC Transmission):**
- Transmission Voltage ($V$): $25,000\text{ V}$
- Current ($I$): $I = \frac{P}{V} = \frac{10^8}{25,000} = 4,000\text{ A}$
- Power Loss ($P_{\text{loss}}$): $P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R = (4,000)^2 \times 9.63 = 1.54 \times 10^8\text{ W}$
- *Observation:* The power loss ($154\text{ MW}$) exceeds the power produced ($100\text{ MW}$). This proposal is physically impossible and highly inefficient.

**Proposal 2 (AC Transmission):**
- Transmission Voltage ($V$): $500,000\text{ V}$
- Current ($I$): $I = \frac{P}{V} = \frac{10^8}{500,000} = 200\text{ A}$
- Power Loss ($P_{\text{loss}}$): $P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R = (200)^2 \times 9.63 = 385,200\text{ W} = 0.385\text{ MW}$
- *Observation:* Very low power loss ($\approx 0.39\%$). This is highly efficient.

### 2. Assessment and Suggestions

*   **Proposal 1 Efficiency:** Extremely poor. The high current leads to excessive resistive heating ($I^2 R$ losses). By the Law of Conservation of Energy, the energy dissipated as heat in the lines comes at the expense of the energy reaching the factory.
    *   *Improvement:* Increase the thickness of the cables (lower resistance) or use a higher transmission voltage (requires conversion technology).
*   **Proposal 2 Efficiency:** High. The use of transformers allows high-voltage, low-current transmission, drastically reducing energy dissipation.
    *   *Improvement:* Use superconducting materials for lines (eliminating $R$) or ensure transformers are operating at near-ideal conditions to minimize eddy current/hysteresis losses, adhering to the Law of Conservation of Energy (input power $\approx$ output power minus small losses).
