### Explanation
To solve this problem, you need to apply the **Law of Conservation of Mechanical Energy** and **Kepler’s Second Law** (or the principle of gravitational potential energy).

1.  **Conservation of Energy**: For a satellite in an elliptical orbit, the total mechanical energy $E_{total} = E_k + E_p$ is constant.
    *   $E_k$ is kinetic energy.
    *   $E_p$ is gravitational potential energy, defined by the formula: $E_p = -\frac{GMm}{r}$, where $r$ is the distance from the center of the Earth.

2.  **Analyze Potential Energy**:
    *   The gravitational potential energy is highest (least negative) when the distance $r$ is greatest and lowest (most negative) when the distance $r$ is smallest.
    *   Looking at the diagram, satellite **P** is at the **perigee** (the point closest to the Earth). Therefore, at position P, the value of $r$ is at its minimum. This makes the gravitational potential energy at P the lowest (most negative) compared to the other positions.

3.  **Analyze Kinetic Energy**:
    *   Since $E_{total} = E_k + E_p$ is constant, as the satellite moves closer to the Earth (decreasing $r$), its potential energy decreases. To keep the total energy constant, the kinetic energy must increase.
    *   Conversely, as the satellite moves further away (as seen at point R, the apogee), it trades kinetic energy for potential energy, causing the satellite to slow down.
    *   Therefore, the satellite must have its **maximum velocity** and **maximum kinetic energy** at the point where it is closest to the Earth (the perigee).

Satellite **P** is closest to the Earth; therefore, it has the greatest kinetic energy.
