### Answer
The correct answer is **B. Reflection spectra**.

### Explanation
To arrive at this answer, we must examine how the device interacts with the rock sample:

1.  **Source of Light:** The device emits light of a "known wavelength" *onto* the sample. This is an active form of spectroscopy, meaning it is not waiting for the rock to spontaneously emit its own light (which would be emission spectroscopy).
2.  **Interaction:** When this light hits the rock surface at room temperature, the atoms and molecules in the rock will absorb specific wavelengths of that light (corresponding to their electronic or vibrational energy levels) and reflect or scatter the rest.
3.  **Detection:** The device detects the "returned wavelengths." Since the light is being reflected off the surface of the rock back into the detector, the resulting data is a record of which wavelengths were reflected versus those that were absorbed.
4.  **Terminology:**
    *   **Emission spectra** occur when excited atoms return to lower energy states and release photons.
    *   **Absorption spectra** occur when light passes *through* a medium, and specific wavelengths are removed.
    *   **Reflection spectra** specifically measure the light that is reflected or back-scattered from the surface of a solid sample. Because the spectrometer is designed for field use on rock surfaces, it analyzes how the material reflects the incident light source, making it a reflection spectroscopy technique.

When you are identifying the elemental or mineral composition of a solid surface by shining light on it, you are analyzing the light that bounces back (reflectance).
