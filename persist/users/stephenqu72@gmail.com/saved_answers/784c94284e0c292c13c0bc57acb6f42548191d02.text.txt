To analyze this system, we must examine the energy transformations and the relationship between electrical and mechanical power in each stage.

### Analysis of Physics and Energy Conservation

**Stage 1: Electrical to Mechanical + Potential Energy**
*   **Qualitative:** The power supply is switched on, driving the motor to lift the mass. Electrical energy is converted into kinetic energy (acceleration) and gravitational potential energy ($GPE = mgh$). Some energy is dissipated as heat due to the internal resistance of the motor.
*   **Quantitative:** At $t \approx 0.6 \text{ s}$, velocity $v \approx 1.0 \text{ m/s}$. The input electrical power is $P_{elec} = VI$. From the graphs, $V \approx 2.1 \text{ V}$ and $I \approx 2.0 \text{ A}$, so $P_{in} \approx 4.2 \text{ W}$. The mechanical power output is $P_{mech} = Fv = mgv$. Given $m = 0.09 \text{ kg}$ and $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, $P_{mech} \approx 0.09 \times 9.8 \times 1.0 \approx 0.88 \text{ W}$. The discrepancy ($4.2 - 0.88 = 3.32 \text{ W}$) accounts for kinetic energy gain, internal resistance heating, and frictional losses.

**Stage 2: Electrical to Thermal Energy**
*   **Qualitative:** The mass is stationary. The motor is stalled or holding the load. Since $v = 0$, mechanical work done ($W = Fd$) is zero. The electrical input is now entirely dissipated as heat due to the motor's internal resistance ($P = I^2R$).
*   **Quantitative:** From the graphs, $V \approx 2.0 \text{ V}$ and $I \approx 2.7 \text{ A}$. The power dissipated as heat is $P = VI \approx 5.4 \text{ W}$. This stage demonstrates the "stall current" phenomenon where the absence of back-EMF (since the motor isn't rotating) results in higher current draw and significant heating.

**Stage 3: Mechanical (GPE) to Electrical (Back-EMF) / Thermal**
*   **Qualitative:** The power supply is off (short circuit). Gravity accelerates the mass downwards. The motor now acts as a generator. The GPE of the mass is converted into electrical energy (driving current through the short circuit) and rotational kinetic energy of the motor shaft.
*   **Quantitative:** As the mass falls, it gains kinetic energy ($KE = 0.5mv^2$). At $t \approx 1.7 \text{ s}$, $v \approx 2.5 \text{ m/s}$, so $KE \approx 0.5 \times 0.09 \times (2.5)^2 \approx 0.28 \text{ J}$. The current $I$ increases as the motor speeds up, indicating that the induced EMF (Faraday's Law) is effectively transforming the mechanical gravitational energy into electrical energy, which is subsequently dissipated as heat in the motor's windings ($I^2R$ losses).
