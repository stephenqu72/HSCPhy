### Part (a) Answer: Assessing the Limitations of the Rutherford and Bohr Models

**Rutherford's Model:**
The Rutherford model (planetary model) successfully proposed a small, dense, positively charged nucleus. However, it was fundamentally limited by classical electrodynamics. According to Maxwell’s equations, an accelerating charged particle (the electron) must continuously emit electromagnetic radiation. Consequently, the electron would lose energy and spiral into the nucleus in a fraction of a second, meaning atoms should be inherently unstable. Furthermore, the model could not explain the origin of discrete spectral lines in emission spectra.

**Bohr's Model:**
Bohr addressed the stability issue by proposing that electrons exist in stationary, quantized orbits where no energy is radiated. While this successfully explained the hydrogen spectrum, its limitations were significant:
1. It could not explain the spectra of multi-electron atoms.
2. It failed to account for the "fine structure" of spectral lines (the splitting of lines under high resolution).
3. It offered no theoretical justification for why orbits were quantized, essentially postulating an *ad hoc* rule to fit the experimental data.
4. It could not explain the Zeeman effect (splitting of spectral lines in a magnetic field).

**Assessment:** While both models were vital milestones in the development of atomic theory, they are now considered obsolete. Rutherford's model failed to account for atomic stability, and Bohr's model, though useful for hydrogen-like systems, lacked a physical basis for its postulates and could not explain complex atomic behaviors.

### Part (b) Answer: Why Schrödinger’s Quantum Mechanics Improved the Model

Schrödinger improved our understanding by shifting the focus from particles moving in fixed orbits to a probabilistic wave-based approach. His model introduced the concept of the **wave function** ($\psi$), where the electron is treated as a standing wave. 

Crucially, this replaced the "fixed orbit" with the **orbital**—a region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron. By treating the electron as a wave, Schrödinger’s equation could accurately predict the energy levels and spectral lines for complex, multi-electron atoms and account for the fine structure of spectra, which Bohr's model could not. It effectively moved atomic physics from deterministic "classical" trajectories to a probabilistic quantum framework.
