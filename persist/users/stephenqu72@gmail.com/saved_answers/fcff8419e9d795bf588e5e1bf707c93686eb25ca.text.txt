### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** The positron is antimatter. When it encounters an electron (its antiparticle) within the star, the two particles undergo **annihilation**. In this process, their combined mass is converted entirely into energy in the form of two gamma-ray photons, which equates to much more energy than the positron's initial kinetic energy.

**Explanation:** In HSC Physics, you must recognise that mass-energy equivalence ($E=mc^2$) applies to matter-antimatter interactions. A positron has a rest mass energy of approximately $0.511\text{ MeV}$. When it annihilates with an electron, the total mass ($2 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$) is converted into radiant energy.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
1. Calculate the mass defect ($\Delta m$):
   $$\Delta m = (\text{Mass of reactants}) - (\text{Mass of products})$$
   $$\Delta m = (2.014102\text{ u} + 1.007276\text{ u}) - 3.016029\text{ u}$$
   $$\Delta m = 3.021378\text{ u} - 3.016029\text{ u} = 0.005349\text{ u}$$

2. Convert the mass defect to energy in MeV ($1\text{ u} \approx 931.5\text{ MeV/c}^2$):
   $$E = 0.005349 \times 931.5\text{ MeV}$$
   $$E \approx 4.9826\text{ MeV}$$

**Explanation:** To solve this, you calculate the "missing mass" (the difference between the mass of the reactants and the product). This mass difference is converted into energy according to Einstein's mass-energy equivalence. In nuclear physics problems, it is standard practice to use the conversion factor $1\text{ u} = 931.5\text{ MeV}$ to find the energy released.
