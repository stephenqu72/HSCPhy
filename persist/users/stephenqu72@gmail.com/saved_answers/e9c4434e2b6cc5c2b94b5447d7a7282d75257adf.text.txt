To solve this question, we must apply the formal definition of **proper time** ($t_0$) within the framework of Special Relativity.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
In Special Relativity, **proper time** is defined as the time interval between two events as measured by an observer who is at rest relative to those events. 

*   If two events occur at the same spatial location in a specific reference frame (e.g., a clock ticking on a desk), the time interval measured by a clock in that same frame is the proper time.
*   **Why B is correct:** Because the clock is "not moving" relative to the location of the events, it records the interval at the same spatial coordinate, which is the defining characteristic of proper time.
*   **Why the others are incorrect:** 
    *   **A:** The "event observer" is ambiguous; if the observer is moving relative to the event, they will measure **dilated time** ($t$), not proper time.
    *   **C:** Observers in different frames will disagree on the duration of events due to time dilation; there is no universal "agreed on" time in this context.
    *   **D:** Any clock moving at a constant velocity will measure a dilated time interval if it is moving relative to the events occurring at a single location in space.

When solving relativity questions, always look for the frame of reference where the two events occur at the **same spatial position** ($x_1 = x_2$). The time measured by a clock in that specific frame is the proper time.
