To solve this problem, we use the fundamental physics principles of electrical power transmission, specifically Ohm's Law ($V = IR$) and the power dissipation formula ($P_{loss} = I^2 R$).

### Part a: Showing the total resistance
We are given:
- Power loss ($P_{loss}$) = $20\text{ MW} = 20 \times 10^6\text{ W}$
- Current ($I$) = $700\text{ A}$

The formula for power loss in a resistor is:
$$P_{loss} = I^2 R$$

Rearranging to solve for resistance ($R$):
$$R = \frac{P_{loss}}{I^2} = \frac{20 \times 10^6}{700^2}$$
$$R = \frac{20,000,000}{490,000} \approx 40.816\dots \Omega$$
Rounding to the nearest whole number as requested by the prompt, $R \approx 41\ \Omega$.

---

### Part b: Determining if 480 kV is available
First, we calculate the voltage drop ($V_{drop}$) across the transmission lines using Ohm's Law:
$$V_{drop} = I \times R$$
$$V_{drop} = 700\text{ A} \times 40.816\ \Omega = 28,571.2\text{ V} \approx 28.6\text{ kV}$$

The voltage available at Town B is the source voltage minus the voltage drop:
$$V_{Town B} = V_{source} - V_{drop}$$
$$V_{Town B} = 500\text{ kV} - 28.6\text{ kV} = 471.4\text{ kV}$$

Since $471.4\text{ kV} < 480\text{ kV}$, the required $480\text{ kV}$ **will not** be available at Town B.

---

### Part c: Impact of lowering voltage
If the transmission voltage is reduced to $50\text{ kV}$ while keeping power output constant, the current ($I = P/V$) must increase significantly (by a factor of 10). 

Because power loss is proportional to the **square** of the current ($P_{loss} = I^2 R$), a tenfold increase in current results in a 100-fold increase in power loss ($10^2 = 100$). This would lead to massive energy waste as heat and likely melt or destroy the transmission lines, making the transmission of the original $350\text{ MW}$ of power impossible.
