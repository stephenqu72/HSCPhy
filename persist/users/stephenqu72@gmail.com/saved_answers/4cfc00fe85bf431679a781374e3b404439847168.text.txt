To solve this problem, we must account for two physical phenomena: the **Inverse Square Law** of light and the effect of a **polarising filter** on unpolarised light.

### Step 1: Apply the Inverse Square Law
The intensity of a point light source follows the inverse square law: $I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$.
We are given an initial intensity $I_1 = 120$ units at distance $r_1 = 0.5\text{ m}$. We need to find the intensity $I_2$ at distance $r_2 = 3.0\text{ m}$.

$$I_2 = I_1 \times \left( \frac{r_1}{r_2} \right)^2$$
$$I_2 = 120 \times \left( \frac{0.5}{3.0} \right)^2$$
$$I_2 = 120 \times \left( \frac{1}{6} \right)^2 = 120 \times \frac{1}{36}$$
$$I_2 \approx 3.333\text{ units}$$

### Step 2: Apply the Polarisation Rule
When unpolarised light passes through a polarising filter, the intensity of the transmitted light is exactly half of the incident intensity:
$$I_{transmitted} = \frac{1}{2} I_{incident}$$

Using our result from Step 1:
$$I_{final} = \frac{1}{2} \times 3.333 \approx 1.667\text{ units}$$

### Conclusion
Rounding to two significant figures, we get $1.7$. Comparing this to the provided options, the correct choice is **(C)**.
