### Answer
The correct answer is **c) It couldn’t explain the emission spectra of atoms with multiple electrons in their valence shell.**

---

### Explanation

To solve this question, we must understand the successes and failures of the Bohr model of the atom:

*   **Option a is incorrect:** One of the greatest successes of Bohr's model was its ability to accurately predict the wavelengths of light emitted by the Hydrogen atom (the Balmer series) using his postulates.
*   **Option b is incorrect:** While Bohr couldn't provide a *fundamental* physical reason for why electrons didn't emit Electromagnetic Radiation (EMR), he simply **postulated** that electrons exist in "stationary states" where they do not radiate energy. This was a premise of his model, not a failure to explain the spectra itself.
*   **Option c is correct:** The Bohr model relies on the interaction between a single nucleus and a single electron. When applied to multi-electron atoms, the model fails because it cannot account for the complex electrostatic repulsions between multiple electrons. Consequently, it cannot predict the fine structure or the specific emission spectra of atoms larger than Hydrogen or ionized Helium ($He^+$).
*   **Option d is incorrect:** The Geiger-Marsden experiment (the gold foil experiment) was performed by Rutherford's team to determine the structure of the nucleus. The observation that alpha particles deflected by angles $> 90^\circ$ was the evidence used to *support* the existence of a small, dense, positive nucleus, which was then adopted as the foundation for the Bohr model.

**Study Tip:** In HSC Physics, remember that Bohr's model is highly effective for single-electron systems (Hydrogen-like atoms) but breaks down for anything more complex. Its failure to account for electron-electron interactions is a key limitation you should be able to cite in an exam.
