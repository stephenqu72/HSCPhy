To solve this problem, we apply **Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) is directly proportional to the cube of the average orbital radius ($r$):

$$\frac{T^2}{r^3} = k$$

This means that for two different orbits around the same central body, the relationship is:

$$\frac{T_1^2}{r_1^3} = \frac{T_2^2}{r_2^3}$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1. **Identify the knowns:**
   - Initial radius: $r_1 = 50\,000\text{ km}$
   - Initial period: $T_1 = 60\text{ minutes}$
   - New radius: $r_2 = 2 \times r_1 = 100\,000\text{ km}$

2. **Set up the ratio:**
   We want to find $T_2$. Rearranging the formula:
   $$\frac{T_2^2}{T_1^2} = \frac{r_2^3}{r_1^3}$$
   $$\frac{T_2^2}{T_1^2} = \left( \frac{r_2}{r_1} \right)^3$$

3. **Substitute the values:**
   Since $r_2 = 2r_1$, the ratio $\frac{r_2}{r_1} = 2$.
   $$\frac{T_2^2}{60^2} = 2^3$$
   $$\frac{T_2^2}{3600} = 8$$

4. **Calculate $T_2$:**
   $$T_2^2 = 8 \times 3600$$
   $$T_2^2 = 28\,800$$
   $$T_2 = \sqrt{28\,800}$$
   $$T_2 \approx 169.7\text{ minutes}$$

Rounding to the nearest whole number gives us approximately $170\text{ minutes}$. Comparing this to the given options, **(B)** is the correct choice.
