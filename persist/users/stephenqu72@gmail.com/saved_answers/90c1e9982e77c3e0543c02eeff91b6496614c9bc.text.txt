### Answer

**a) Explain what is happening from point X to point Y.**
From point X, the satellite is experiencing **orbital decay** due to atmospheric drag. As the satellite passes through the thin upper layers of the Earth's atmosphere, it experiences friction, which causes it to lose kinetic energy. Consequently, the satellite drops to a lower altitude. Upon reaching the low point, an onboard propulsion system performs an **orbital station-keeping maneuver** (a thruster burn), which increases the satellite's altitude back to its original height, as seen at point Y.

**b) Outline how geostationary satellites are currently being used.**
Geostationary satellites maintain a fixed position relative to a point on the Earth's surface by orbiting above the equator with a period of 24 hours. They are primarily used for:
*   **Telecommunications:** Relaying television, radio, and telephone signals globally.
*   **Weather Monitoring:** Providing continuous observation of weather patterns and cloud movements over a specific region of the Earth.

*   **For part (a):** Notice the graph pattern. The downward slope represents a continuous loss of altitude. In Low Earth Orbit (LEO), there is still trace atmospheric gas; collisions with these particles cause a drag force that saps the satellite's energy. When you see the vertical spike, recognize that this is an artificial adjustment. A satellite cannot increase its own altitude spontaneously; it requires a "burn" from its thrusters to push it back into a higher orbit. Mentioning "atmospheric drag" and "station-keeping maneuvers" is key to getting both marks.
*   **For part (b):** This is a recall-based question. Remember the defining characteristics of geostationary orbits: they remain stationary over one spot. This makes them ideal for satellite TV dishes (which don't have to move to track the satellite) and weather monitoring (where you want to watch the same patch of Earth constantly).
