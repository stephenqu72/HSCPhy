To determine the variation of kinetic energy $E_k$ with time $t$, we can analyze the physics of the motion step-by-step.

### 1. Physics Analysis
A ball thrown vertically upwards undergoes constant acceleration due to gravity, $g$, acting downwards. 
The velocity of the ball at any time $t$ is given by the equation of motion:
$$v(t) = u - gt$$
where $u$ is the initial upward velocity.

The kinetic energy $E_k$ is defined as:
$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
Substituting the expression for $v(t)$:
$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}m(u - gt)^2$$

### 2. Interpreting the Equation
*   **Quadratic Nature:** The expression for $E_k$ is a quadratic function of time $t$ (of the form $E_k = at^2 + bt + c$). This means the graph must be a **parabola**, not a straight line. This immediately rules out options A and B.
*   **Minimum Value:** At the peak of the flight, the velocity $v = 0$. Since $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, the kinetic energy must be $0$ at the peak. 
*   **Direction of the Parabola:** Because the square of a real number is always non-negative ($v^2 \ge 0$), kinetic energy can never be negative. The graph must represent a parabola that opens upwards, starting at some initial positive value, decreasing to zero at the peak of the flight, and increasing again as the ball falls back down.

### 3. Conclusion
*   Option C shows a downward-opening parabola, which would imply $E_k$ becomes negative, which is impossible.
*   Option D shows an upward-opening parabola that hits $0$ at the vertex. This correctly represents the ball losing kinetic energy as it rises, reaching zero at the peak, and gaining kinetic energy as it returns to the ground.

The correct graph is **D**.
