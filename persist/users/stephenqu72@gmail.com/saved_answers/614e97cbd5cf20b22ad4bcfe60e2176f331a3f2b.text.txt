### Answer
This experiment demonstrates the **transverse wave nature of light**, which is a fundamental requirement of Maxwell’s theory of electromagnetism. 

1.  **Polarization by Reflection:** Light from the incandescent source is initially unpolarized. Upon reflecting off the surface of the water, the light becomes partially polarized. The horizontal components of the electric field reflect more efficiently than the vertical components at this angle.
2.  **Transverse Nature:** Maxwell proposed that light consists of oscillating perpendicular electric and magnetic fields that propagate as a transverse electromagnetic wave. Only transverse waves can be polarized. If light were a longitudinal wave (like sound), rotating an analyser would have no effect on the intensity.
3.  **Experimental Evidence:** The significant drop in intensity from $2,000\text{ lux}$ to $7\text{ lux}$ when the analyser is rotated by $90^\circ$ confirms that the light has a specific orientation of oscillation. This confirms that light is an electromagnetic wave, as Maxwell predicted that these waves must oscillate perpendicularly to the direction of propagation.

### How to solve this
When you see a 6-mark "Explain" question in Physics, you need to follow a logical flow:

*   **Step 1: Identify the phenomenon.** Start by explicitly stating that the experiment demonstrates **polarization**. Mention that the water acts as a polariser by reflection.
*   **Step 2: Connect to the wave type.** You must explicitly state that **only transverse waves can be polarized**. Explain that longitudinal waves oscillate parallel to the direction of travel and thus cannot be blocked by an analyser filter in this manner.
*   **Step 3: Link to Maxwell.** Maxwell’s theory unified electricity and magnetism into a single field theory, predicting that light is a self-propagating **transverse electromagnetic wave**.
*   **Step 4: Analyze the data.** Use the numbers provided ($2,000$ vs $7\text{ lux}$). The near-total extinction of light upon rotating the analyser proves that the light reflected off the water has a preferred plane of oscillation. This physical observation provides the empirical evidence required to support Maxwell’s mathematical prediction that light consists of oscillating $E$ and $B$ fields perpendicular to the direction of wave motion.
