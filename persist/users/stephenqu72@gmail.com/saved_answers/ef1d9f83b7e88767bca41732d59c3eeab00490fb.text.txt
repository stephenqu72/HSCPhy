To solve this problem, we must apply Malus's Law and consider the relative rotation of the polarisers.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Light passing through Polariser A:** 
    When unpolarised light of intensity $I_0$ passes through the first polariser (A), the intensity is halved. Therefore, the intensity of light exiting Polariser A is:
    $$I_A = \frac{1}{2}I_0$$

2.  **Light passing through Polariser B (Malus's Law):**
    Malus's Law states that the intensity of light after passing through a second polariser is $I = I_A \cos^2(\theta)$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the transmission axes of the two polarisers. 
    We want the final intensity $I = \frac{1}{2}I_0$. Substituting $I_A = \frac{1}{2}I_0$:
    $$\frac{1}{2}I_0 = (\frac{1}{2}I_0) \cos^2(\theta)$$
    This implies $\cos^2(\theta) = 1$, which occurs when $\theta = 0^\circ, 180^\circ, \dots$ or $n\pi$ radians.

3.  **Determining the relative angle $\theta$:**
    *   Initial angle: $\theta_0 = 45^\circ = \frac{\pi}{4}$ radians.
    *   Polariser A rotates at $\omega_A = 2\pi$ rad s$^{-1}$ (clockwise).
    *   Polariser B rotates at $\omega_B = \frac{3}{4}\pi$ rad s$^{-1}$ (clockwise).
    Since both are rotating clockwise, the relative angular velocity is the difference between their velocities:
    $$\Delta\omega = |\omega_A - \omega_B| = |2\pi - \frac{3}{4}\pi| = \frac{5}{4}\pi \text{ rad s}^{-1}$$
    The angle at any time $t$ is given by:
    $$\theta(t) = \theta_0 + \Delta\omega t = \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{5}{4}\pi t$$

4.  **Solving for $t$:**
    We set $\theta(t) = n\pi$. For the **first** instance, we look for the smallest $t > 0$:
    $$\frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{5}{4}\pi t = \pi$$
    Subtract $\frac{\pi}{4}$ from both sides:
    $$\frac{5}{4}\pi t = \frac{3}{4}\pi$$
    $$5t = 3$$
    $$t = \frac{3}{5} = 0.6 \text{ seconds}$$
