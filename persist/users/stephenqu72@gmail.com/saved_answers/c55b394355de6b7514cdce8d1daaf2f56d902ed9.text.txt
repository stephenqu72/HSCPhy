To solve this question, we must understand the core limitations of the Rutherford model and the specific evidence that drove Niels Bohr to propose his quantum model of the atom.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
1.  **Analyze the context:** Rutherford's model suggested that electrons orbited the nucleus like planets around the sun. However, classical electrodynamics states that an accelerating charged particle (like an electron in a circular orbit) must constantly emit electromagnetic radiation. This loss of energy would cause the electron to spiral into the nucleus, meaning Rutherford's atom should have been unstable, which contradicts the reality that matter exists.
2.  **Evaluate the options:**
    *   **A:** This is the result of the Geiger-Marsden experiment. While this provided the evidence for the *existence* of the nucleus (Rutherford's model), it does not explain why Bohr had to modify it.
    *   **B:** This is the correct answer. The observation of **atomic emission spectra** (distinct colors/wavelengths of light) provided the empirical evidence that electrons exist in stable, discrete energy levels ($E_n$). When an electron transitions between these levels, it emits a photon with energy equal to the difference between the levels: $\Delta E = E_{higher} - E_{lower} = hf$. Bohr's model successfully accounted for these stable orbits and the resulting spectral lines.
    *   **C:** This is actually a *limitation* of the Rutherford model, not evidence that supported Bohr's modification. Bohr had to propose a "postulate" (the stationary state postulate) to explicitly forbid this classical prediction.
    *   **D:** While spectral lines were observed, the "brightness" (intensity) was not the primary evidence that supported Bohr's model; rather, it was the *specific wavelengths* (positions) of these lines that confirmed his theory of quantized energy levels.

By observing the discrete nature of light emitted by atoms, Bohr was able to construct a model that defined stationary states where electrons do not radiate energy, thus solving the instability problem inherent in Rutherford’s model.
