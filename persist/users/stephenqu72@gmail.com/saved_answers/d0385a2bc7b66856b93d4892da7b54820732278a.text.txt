To answer this question, you need to identify the factors that are excluded in "ideal" projectile motion models but exist in real-world scenarios.

### Answer
The primary reason for the difference between theoretical and practical projectile motion is the presence of **air resistance (drag)**. 

Air resistance acts as a non-conservative force opposing the motion of the projectile in both the horizontal and vertical directions. This results in:
1. **Reduced horizontal velocity:** Unlike theoretical models, where horizontal velocity $v_x$ is constant, air resistance causes $v_x$ to decrease over time, leading to a shorter range.
2. **Reduced vertical height:** Air resistance acts downward during the ascent (adding to gravity) and upward during the descent, leading to a lower maximum height and an asymmetric trajectory.

### How to solve this question
1. **Identify the core concept:** The "theoretical" model assumes a vacuum (no air resistance) and a constant gravitational field. You must identify what happens when you remove the "vacuum" assumption.
2. **Address the two marks:** To secure both marks, you need to provide a clear causal link. 
    * Mark 1: Identify the main factor—Air resistance/drag.
    * Mark 2: Explain the physical consequence—It dissipates the mechanical energy of the projectile and changes its velocity components, directly impacting range and height.
3. **Draft your response:** Start by explicitly naming air resistance, then describe how it affects the motion. Mentioning that the trajectory is no longer a perfect parabola is also a sophisticated touch that examiners look for.
