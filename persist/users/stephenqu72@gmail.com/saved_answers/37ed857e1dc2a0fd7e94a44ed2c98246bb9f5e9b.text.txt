### Answer

Scientific progress is an evolutionary process where the failures of one model identify the limitations that the next model must overcome.

**1. Rutherford and the Nuclear Model**
Rutherford’s gold foil experiment (1909) successfully identified that the positive charge and most of the mass of an atom were concentrated in a dense, central nucleus. However, his model was limited by classical electromagnetic theory: an orbiting electron undergoing centripetal acceleration should constantly radiate energy, lose speed, and spiral into the nucleus, causing atomic collapse. His model could not explain why atoms were stable or why they produced discrete emission spectra.

**2. Bohr and Quantized Orbits**
Bohr addressed the "stability problem" by building on Rutherford’s nucleus while proposing that electrons exist in stable, stationary orbits where they do not radiate energy. He introduced the postulate of angular momentum quantization:
$$mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi}$$
Bohr’s success was in explaining the Rydberg formula and the discrete spectral lines of hydrogen as transitions between these energy levels. However, his model was limited because it could not explain the spectra of multi-electron atoms or the phenomenon of "fine structure" (split spectral lines) and lacked a physical justification for why orbits were quantized.

**3. de Broglie and Wave-Particle Duality**
Louis de Broglie addressed the limitations of Bohr’s arbitrary quantization by proposing that particles have wave-like properties: $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$. By applying this to an electron confined in a circular orbit, he showed that stable orbits only occur where the circumference is an integer multiple of the electron's wavelength ($2\pi r = n\lambda$). This provided a physical, fundamental basis for Bohr’s quantization, transforming the "orbit" into a standing wave, which naturally explained why electrons do not radiate energy in these allowed states.
