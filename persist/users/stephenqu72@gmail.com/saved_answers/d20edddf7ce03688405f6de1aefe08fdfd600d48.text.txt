### Answer

**Part a) Outline the experimental evidence that supports Newton’s theory of light.**
Newton’s corpuscular (particle) theory of light was supported by his observation of the **rectilinear propagation of light** (light travelling in straight lines), which explains the formation of sharp shadows. Furthermore, he used the corpuscular model to explain **reflection** (particles bouncing off a surface like an elastic ball) and **refraction** (particles speeding up as they enter a denser medium, which Newton incorrectly predicted, but which provided a mathematical consistency for Snell’s Law at the time).

**Part b) Determine the gravitational force between the Earth and the Moon.**

To solve this, we use Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation:
$$F = \frac{G M_1 M_2}{r^2}$$
However, since the mass of the Moon and the Earth are not provided in the prompt, and the force of the Sun on the Moon is given, we must look at the centripetal force required to keep the Moon in orbit. The force of the Earth on the Moon ($F_{EM}$) is the primary gravitational force providing the centripetal acceleration ($a_c$) for the Moon’s orbit around the Earth.

Using $F = m_m \omega^2 r$ or $F = \frac{m_m v^2}{r}$:
1. Orbital period of the Moon ($T$) $\approx 27.3$ days $= 2.358 \times 10^6$ s.
2. Radius of orbit ($r$) $= 3.8 \times 10^8$ m.
3. Using the standard mass of the Earth ($M_e \approx 5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg) and Moon ($M_m \approx 7.35 \times 10^{22}$ kg):
$$F_{EM} = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (7.35 \times 10^{22})}{(3.8 \times 10^8)^2}$$
$$F_{EM} \approx 2.03 \times 10^{20} \text{ N}$$
