To determine the correct answer, we must look at the mathematical relationship governing the interference pattern produced in Young's double-slit experiment.

### Explanation
The spacing between adjacent bright fringes (the fringe width, denoted as $\Delta x$) in a Young’s double-slit experiment is given by the formula:

$$\Delta x = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$$

Where:
- $\Delta x$ is the distance between adjacent bright fringes.
- $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light source.
- $L$ is the distance from the slits to the screen.
- $d$ is the separation distance between the two slits.

In this scenario:
1. The slit separation $d$ remains constant.
2. The distance to the screen $L$ remains constant.
3. The wavelength $\lambda$ is increased from $532\text{ nm}$ (green) to $633\text{ nm}$ (red).

Since $\Delta x$ is directly proportional to the wavelength ($\Delta x \propto \lambda$), increasing the wavelength will result in an increase in the spacing between the fringes. Therefore, the alternating bands of bright light will become more spread out (more separated).

Comparing this to the given options:
(A) Incorrect: The fringe width depends on the wavelength.
(B) Incorrect: Brightness is related to the intensity/power of the laser, not the wavelength.
(C) Incorrect: This would happen if the wavelength were decreased.
(D) **Correct**: The increase in wavelength results in a larger fringe separation.

**Answer:** (D)
