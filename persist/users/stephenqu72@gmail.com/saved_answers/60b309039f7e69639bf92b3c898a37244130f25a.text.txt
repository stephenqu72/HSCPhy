### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** The student's model is **invalid**. 
**Justification:** A proportional model $\tau = k\theta$ implies that if $\theta = 0$, then $\tau$ must be $0$, and the graph should be a straight line passing through the origin $(0,0)$. If we calculate $k$ using the data points, we see inconsistency:
*   At $\theta = 5^\circ$, $\tau = 8 \text{ Nm}$, so $k = 8/5 = 1.6 \text{ Nm/degree}$.
*   At $\theta = 25^\circ$, $\tau = 42 \text{ Nm}$, so $k = 42/25 = 1.68 \text{ Nm/degree}$.
Since the ratio $k = \tau/\theta$ is not constant, the relationship is not linear, and the student's linear model does not accurately fit the experimental data.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:** The accuracy of the model will significantly decrease as the angle increases beyond $25^\circ$.
**Justification:** The physical reality of torque is governed by the formula $\tau = rF\sin(\theta)$. As $\theta$ increases toward $90^\circ$, the torque should follow a sine curve, not a linear one. 
*   In the linear model ($\tau = 1.7\theta$), at $\theta = 90^\circ$, the predicted torque would be $1.7 \times 90 = 153 \text{ Nm}$.
*   Using the correct physical model $\tau = rF\sin(\theta)$, with $r = 0.25 \text{ m}$ and $F = 400 \text{ N}$, the maximum torque at $\theta = 90^\circ$ is $0.25 \times 400 \times \sin(90^\circ) = 100 \text{ Nm}$.
The linear model fails to account for the trigonometric nature of the force component perpendicular to the spanner, causing the model to overestimate the torque and diverge significantly from physical reality as the angle increases.

### How to solve this type of question:
1.  **Analyze the Model:** When a student proposes a model like $\tau = k\theta$, immediately think "Linear." Check if the graph passes through the origin $(0,0)$ and if the gradient is constant.
2.  **Test the Data:** Use the provided values to calculate the constant $k$ at different points. If $k$ changes, the model is not a direct proportion.
3.  **Use Physics Principles:** For part (b), always fall back on the standard formula taught in the syllabus. Torque is defined as $\tau = rF\sin(\theta)$. Recognize that the student has mistakenly tried to fit a linear trend to a small segment of a trigonometric curve.
4.  **Structure your argument:** Always use "Evidence (from the graph) $\rightarrow$ Analysis (calculation/comparison) $\rightarrow$ Conclusion" to ensure you gain full marks for justification.
