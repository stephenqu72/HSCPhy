### Solution and Explanation

#### Part (A): Derivation
To derive the motional emf formula, we begin with Faraday's Law of Induction, which states that the induced emf ($\varepsilon$) is equal to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux ($\Phi$):
$$\varepsilon = -\frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}$$

1. Magnetic flux is defined as $\Phi = BA$, where $A$ is the area perpendicular to the magnetic field.
2. Consider a wire of length $L$ moving at velocity $v$. In a small time interval $\Delta t$, the wire moves a distance $\Delta x = v\Delta t$.
3. The change in the area swept by the wire is $\Delta A = L \cdot \Delta x = L \cdot v \cdot \Delta t$.
4. The change in flux is therefore $\Delta\Phi = B \cdot \Delta A = B \cdot L \cdot v \cdot \Delta t$.
5. Substituting this into Faraday's Law (taking the magnitude):
$$\varepsilon = \frac{B \cdot L \cdot v \cdot \Delta t}{\Delta t} = BLv$$

#### Part (B): Calculation
Given the parameters:
* Magnetic field strength ($B$) = $2\text{ T}$
* Length of wire ($L$) = $3\text{ m}$
* Velocity ($v$) = $4\text{ ms}^{-1}$

Using the derived formula:
$$\varepsilon = BLv$$
$$\varepsilon = 2 \times 3 \times 4$$
$$\varepsilon = 24\text{ V}$$

The potential difference generated across the wire is $24\text{ V}$.
