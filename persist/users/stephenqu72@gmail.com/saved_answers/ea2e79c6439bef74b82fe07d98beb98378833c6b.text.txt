To solve this physics problem, we must apply the principles of Malus's Law and the rule for unpolarised light passing through a polariser.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Pass through the first filter:**
When unpolarised light of intensity $I_0$ passes through an ideal polariser, the intensity of the transmitted light $I_1$ is reduced by half.
$$I_1 = \frac{I_0}{2} = \frac{128\text{ Wm}^{-2}}{2} = 64\text{ Wm}^{-2}$$

**2. Pass through the second filter:**
The light leaving the first filter is plane-polarised at $0^\circ$. It then strikes the second filter, which is oriented at an angle $\theta$ relative to the first. According to Malus's Law ($I = I_{incident} \cos^2\theta$):
$$I_2 = I_1 \cos^2\theta = 64 \cos^2\theta$$

**3. Pass through the third filter:**
The light leaving the second filter is at an angle $\theta$ relative to the vertical axis. The third filter is at $0^\circ$. Therefore, the angle between the light incident on the third filter and the third filter's axis is $(0 - \theta)$, or simply $\theta$. Applying Malus's Law again:
$$I_3 = I_2 \cos^2\theta$$
Substituting the expression for $I_2$:
$$I_3 = (64 \cos^2\theta) \cos^2\theta = 64 \cos^4\theta$$

**4. Solve for $\theta$:**
We are given that the final intensity $I_3 = 16\text{ Wm}^{-2}$.
$$16 = 64 \cos^4\theta$$
$$\cos^4\theta = \frac{16}{64} = \frac{1}{4}$$
Taking the square root of both sides:
$$\cos^2\theta = \sqrt{\frac{1}{4}} = \frac{1}{2}$$
Taking the square root again:
$$\cos\theta = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}} = \pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$
$$\theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = 45^\circ$$

**Final Answer:**
The angle of rotation $\theta$ is $45^\circ$.
