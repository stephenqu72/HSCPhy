### Teacher's Solution and Explanation

To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of static equilibrium. For the oil drop to remain at rest, the upward electric force must perfectly balance the downward gravitational force:
$$F_E = F_g \implies qE = mg$$

---

#### (a) Magnitude of the charge on the drop
Using $q = \frac{mg}{E}$:
- $m = 3.27 \times 10^{-16}\text{ kg}$
- $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$
- $E = 10,000\text{ N/C}$

$$q = \frac{(3.27 \times 10^{-16}\text{ kg}) \times (9.8\text{ m/s}^2)}{10,000\text{ N/C}} = 3.2046 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$$
**Answer:** The magnitude of the charge is approximately $3.20 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$.

#### (b) Explain why the electric force is upward
The electric force is given by $F_E = qE$. By definition, the direction of an electric field $E$ is the direction a positive charge would move. If the electric field is directed downward but the force on the drop is upward, the oil drop must have a **negative charge**. The force on a negative charge is always in the opposite direction to the electric field lines.

#### (c) Potential difference between the plates
Using the relationship $E = \frac{V}{d}$:
$$V = E \times d = 10,000\text{ N/C} \times 0.01\text{ m} = 100\text{ V}$$
**Answer:** The potential difference is $100\text{ V}$.

#### (d) Motion of the drop after evaporation
If the oil evaporates, the mass ($m$) of the drop decreases. The gravitational force $F_g = mg$ decreases. Since the charge ($q$) and the electric field ($E$) remain constant, the upward electric force $F_E = qE$ remains unchanged.
Because the upward force is now greater than the downward gravitational force, the net force is upward, and the **drop moves upward.**
