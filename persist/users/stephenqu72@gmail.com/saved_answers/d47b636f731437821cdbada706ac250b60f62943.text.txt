To solve this problem, we must analyze the physics of circular satellite motion under the influence of gravity. Let $r$ be the orbital radius (distance from the center of the Earth). From the diagram, we can see that satellite $A$ is closer to the Earth than satellite $B$, meaning $r_A < r_B$.

### 1. Orbital Velocity ($v$)
The orbital velocity is derived by equating the gravitational force to the centripetal force:
$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Since $v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}$, a smaller radius results in a higher velocity. Because $r_A < r_B$, **$A$ has a greater orbital velocity than $B$**.

### 2. Orbital Period ($T$)
Using Kepler's Third Law, $T^2 \propto r^3$, or $T \propto \sqrt{r^3}$.
Because $r_A < r_B$, it follows that $T_A < T_B$. Therefore, **$B$ has a greater orbital period than $A$**.

### 3. Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
The centripetal acceleration for an orbiting body is provided by gravity:
$$a_c = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$
Since $a_c \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$, a smaller radius results in a larger acceleration. Because $r_A < r_B$, **$A$ has a greater centripetal acceleration than $B$**.

### Summary of Comparisons:
*   **Orbital velocity:** $A > B$
*   **Orbital period:** $B > A$
*   **Centripetal acceleration:** $A > B$
