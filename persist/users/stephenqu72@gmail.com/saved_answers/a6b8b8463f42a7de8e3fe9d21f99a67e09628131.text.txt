To solve this question, we must understand the historical significance of the specific experiments conducted using Crookes tubes to determine the nature of cathode rays.

### Explanation

William Crookes used various modifications of the discharge tube to investigate the properties of cathode rays. Here is a breakdown of the two experiments mentioned in the options:

1.  **The Paddle Wheel Experiment:** When cathode rays were directed at a small paddle wheel mounted on a track inside the vacuum tube, the wheel rotated. Because the wheel moved, it demonstrated that cathode rays were able to transfer kinetic energy and exert force upon the blades. In physics, an object that can move another mass through collisions must possess mass and velocity, and thus it must possess **momentum** ($p = mv$). This experiment was crucial in proving that cathode rays were particles rather than electromagnetic waves.
2.  **The Maltese Cross Experiment:** This experiment involved placing a metal cross in the path of the rays, which cast a sharp shadow on the phosphorescent glass at the end of the tube. This demonstrated that cathode rays travel in **straight lines** (rectilinear propagation), not that they reflect or diffract.

### Evaluating the Options
*   **Option A:** While the rays must have velocity to move the wheel, the scientific conclusion drawn from the motion of the wheel is that the rays have **momentum** (they have mass and are moving).
*   **Option B:** The Maltese Cross demonstrates straight-line propagation, not reflection.
*   **Option C:** This correctly identifies that the rotation of the paddle wheel proves the rays possess momentum.
*   **Option D:** The Maltese Cross experiment was not used to demonstrate diffraction.

Therefore, the correct association is the paddle wheel experiment leading to the conclusion that cathode rays have momentum.

### Answer
