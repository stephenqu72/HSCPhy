To solve this problem, we use Einstein's photoelectric equation:
$$E_k = hf - \Phi$$
where $E_k$ is the maximum kinetic energy, $h$ is Planck's constant, $f$ is the frequency, and $\Phi$ is the work function. This equation takes the linear form $y = mx + c$, where $y = E_k$, $x = f$, $m = h$, and $c = -\Phi$.

### Part a) Derive Planck's constant and the work function
To derive these values, we find the slope of the line of best fit from the provided data.

1.  **Calculate the slope ($h$):** Using two points, e.g., $(5.6 \times 10^{14}, 0.01)$ and $(8.8 \times 10^{14}, 1.31)$:
    $$h \approx \frac{1.31 - 0.01}{(8.8 - 5.6) \times 10^{14}} = \frac{1.30}{3.2 \times 10^{14}} \approx 4.06 \times 10^{-15} \text{ eV}\cdot\text{s}$$
    *(Note: Using eV in the calculation provides $h$ in $\text{eV}\cdot\text{s}$. Converting to SI units ($J\cdot\text{s}$), $4.06 \times 10^{-15} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \approx 6.5 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$.)*

2.  **Calculate the work function ($\Phi$):** Using $E_k = hf - \Phi \rightarrow \Phi = hf - E_k$:
    $$\Phi = (4.06 \times 10^{-15} \times 5.6 \times 10^{14}) - 0.01 \approx 2.27 - 0.01 \approx 2.26 \text{ eV}$$

### Part b) Maximum speed of ejected electrons
1.  **Calculate photon energy ($E$):**
    $$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(4.14 \times 10^{-15} \text{ eV}\cdot\text{s})(3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})}{300 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}} = 4.14 \text{ eV}$$

2.  **Calculate maximum kinetic energy ($E_k$):**
    $$E_k = E - \Phi = 4.14 \text{ eV} - 4.33 \text{ eV} = -0.19 \text{ eV}$$
    Since $E < \Phi$ (the photon energy is less than the work function), the energy is negative. This means the incident radiation has insufficient energy to overcome the work function of the metal. 
    **Conclusion:** No electrons will be ejected; therefore, the maximum speed is $0 \text{ m/s}$.
