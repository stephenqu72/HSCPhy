To answer this question, we must understand the principle of electromagnetic induction.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A)**.

### Explanation
The deflection in the galvanometer is caused by an induced electromotive force (EMF) in the secondary coil, governed by **Faraday’s Law of Induction**:
$$\mathcal{E} = -N_s \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$
Where:
- $\mathcal{E}$ is the induced EMF.
- $N_s$ is the number of turns in the secondary coil.
- $\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$ is the rate of change of magnetic flux through the coils.

When the switch is closed, a current begins to flow in the primary coil, creating a magnetic field that changes rapidly as the current grows from zero. This changing magnetic flux is carried by the iron core to the secondary coil.

To increase the size of the deflection (the induced current), we need to increase the induced EMF. We can do this by increasing the rate of change of magnetic flux ($\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$). The rate of change of flux is directly proportional to the current in the primary coil and the number of primary turns ($N_p$), as $\Phi \propto N_p I_p$. If we **decrease the number of primary coils ($N_p$)**, the self-inductance of the primary circuit decreases. This allows the current to rise to its final value faster upon closing the switch, thereby increasing $\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$ and resulting in a larger induced EMF in the secondary coil.

**Why other options are incorrect:**
- **(B)** Decreasing $N_s$ directly reduces the induced EMF according to Faraday's law ($ \mathcal{E} \propto N_s $).
- **(C)** Replacing the iron core with a copper core would drastically reduce the magnetic permeability of the core, significantly weakening the magnetic flux linkage and reducing the induced EMF.
- **(D)** Placing a resistor in series increases the total resistance of the secondary circuit, which, according to Ohm’s Law ($I = \frac{V}{R}$), would decrease the current flowing through the galvanometer.
