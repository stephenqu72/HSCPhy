The correct answer is **A. Brushes**.

### Explanation:
To understand why, let's look at the construction and principles of an AC induction motor:

1.  **How an induction motor works:** The motor operates via electromagnetic induction. An AC current is supplied to the **stator windings**, which creates a rotating magnetic field. This rotating field induces an EMF in the rotor conductors (often a "squirrel cage"), causing current to flow in the rotor. The interaction between this induced rotor current and the stator's magnetic field creates a torque that causes the rotor to spin.
2.  **Why "Brushes" are not required:** Brushes are used in DC motors or universal motors to maintain electrical contact with a rotating commutator to reverse current direction. In an AC induction motor, the rotor is "induced"—the current in the rotor is generated by the changing magnetic flux from the stator. There is no physical electrical connection (like brushes) required to supply current to the rotor; the rotor is entirely "brushless."
3.  **Why the others are required:**
    *   **B. Stator winding:** Essential to carry the AC current that generates the rotating magnetic field.
    *   **C. Magnetic fields:** The fundamental mechanism of induction relies on the interaction between the stator's magnetic field and the induced current in the rotor.
    *   **D. Current applied to the rotor:** While not "applied" via wires or brushes, current is *induced* in the rotor (as the name "induction motor" suggests). Some curricula simplify this to state that current in the rotor is a necessary component of the operation, distinguishing it from the physical supply mechanism (brushes).

In summary, because induction motors work on the principle of electromagnetic induction rather than direct electrical conduction to the rotor, they are specifically designed to operate without brushes.
