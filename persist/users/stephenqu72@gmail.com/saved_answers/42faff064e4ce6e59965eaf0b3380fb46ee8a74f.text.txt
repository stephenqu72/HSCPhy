To determine the correct answer, we must consider the orbital mechanics of a **Low Earth Orbit (LEO)** satellite.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B: Orbits multiple times per day.**

### Explanation
1.  **Understanding LEO:** A "near Earth satellite" is typically in Low Earth Orbit (LEO). These satellites orbit at an altitude between approximately $160\text{ km}$ and $2,000\text{ km}$ above the Earth's surface.
2.  **Orbital Velocity:** According to the formula for orbital velocity, $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$, a smaller radius $r$ (being close to Earth) requires a very high orbital velocity to maintain a stable circular orbit.
3.  **Orbital Period:** Because they travel at such high speeds in a relatively small orbit, their period of revolution is quite short. A typical LEO satellite takes about $90$ to $100$ minutes to complete one full orbit around the Earth.
4.  **Frequency:** Since a day has $1,440$ minutes, a satellite with a $90$-minute period will complete approximately $16$ orbits in a single day. This confirms that they orbit the Earth **multiple times per day**.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** A satellite that orbits exactly once per day ($24$ hours) is in a **Geosynchronous Orbit**, which is much further away than a "near Earth" orbit.
*   **C:** An orbit taking less than an hour would require an orbital radius inside the Earth itself, which is physically impossible for a satellite.
*   **D:** A satellite that stays above the same place on Earth is in a **Geostationary Orbit**. This requires a very specific altitude (approx. $35,786\text{ km}$), which is not considered "near Earth."
