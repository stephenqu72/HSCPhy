To solve this problem, we must apply Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$, specifically relating the energy produced by nuclear fusion to the mass defect of the helium nuclei produced.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

**1. Determine the energy produced by the fusion process:**
In the Sun, the net reaction is essentially $4 \times {}^1\text{H} \rightarrow {}^4\text{He} + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + \text{energy}$.
The mass of four protons is $4 \times 1.007825\text{ amu} = 4.031300\text{ amu}$.
The mass of a helium nucleus is given as $4.002602\text{ amu}$.
The mass defect ($\Delta m$) per helium nucleus produced is:
$$\Delta m = 4.031300 - 4.002602 = 0.028698\text{ amu}$$

**2. Convert the mass defect to energy:**
Using the conversion $1\text{ amu} = 1.6605 \times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}$ and $c = 3.00 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$:
$$\Delta E = \Delta m \cdot c^2 = (0.028698 \times 1.6605 \times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8\text{ m/s})^2$$
$$\Delta E \approx 4.29 \times 10^{-12}\text{ J}$$
*(Note: Using more precise values, this is approximately $26.7\text{ MeV}$, which converts to $4.28 \times 10^{-12}\text{ J}$)*.

**3. Calculate the number of nuclei produced per second:**
The Sun produces $3.85 \times 10^{26}\text{ W}$ (Joules per second). The number of nuclei ($N$) produced per second is:
$$N = \frac{\text{Total Power}}{\text{Energy per nucleus}} = \frac{3.85 \times 10^{26}\text{ J/s}}{4.28 \times 10^{-12}\text{ J/nucleus}}$$
$$N \approx 8.995 \times 10^{37}$$

Rounding to significant figures provided in the options, the result corresponds to **(B)**.

The correct answer is **(B)**.
