### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) Atoms emit discrete spectral lines.**

### Explanation
To solve this, we must evaluate the capabilities and limitations of the Rutherford atomic model (often called the "planetary model"):

1.  **Understanding the Rutherford Model:** Rutherford’s model was developed based on his famous alpha particle scattering experiment. He concluded that an atom consists of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons orbiting at a distance, implying that the atom is mostly empty space.
2.  **Evaluating Options (A), (B), and (C):** 
    *   **(A)** The fact that most alpha particles passed through the foil directly confirmed that the atom is **mostly empty space (C)**.
    *   **(B)** The large-angle deflections were explained by the existence of a concentrated, positively charged nucleus that exerts a strong electrostatic repulsive force on the positively charged alpha particles. 
    *   Since Rutherford's model successfully explains these three points, options (A), (B), and (C) are incorrect in the context of the question (which asks what *could not* be explained).
3.  **Evaluating Option (D):**
    *   According to classical physics (which Rutherford’s model relied upon), an accelerating charged particle (like an electron orbiting a nucleus) should constantly lose energy as electromagnetic radiation. This energy loss would cause the electron to spiral inward and crash into the nucleus, meaning the atom would be unstable.
    *   Furthermore, this classical prediction implies that an atom should emit a continuous spectrum of light as the electron spirals inward. However, experimental evidence (atomic spectroscopy) shows that atoms emit **discrete (line) spectra**. 
    *   The Rutherford model could not account for this stability or the existence of discrete spectral lines. It wasn't until Niels Bohr introduced quantized energy levels that this observation was successfully explained.

Therefore, because the Rutherford model predicts the collapse of the atom and a continuous spectrum, it fails to explain the discrete spectral lines observed in reality.
