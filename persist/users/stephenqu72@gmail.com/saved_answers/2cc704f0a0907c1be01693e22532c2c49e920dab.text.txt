### Answer

**1. The Second Postulate:**
The second postulate of Einstein’s theory of special relativity states that the speed of light in a vacuum ($c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$) is constant for all inertial observers, regardless of the motion of the light source or the motion of the observer.

**2. Difference from Classical Physics:**
In classical (Galilean) physics, velocities are additive. According to the principle of Galilean relativity, if a source moves at velocity $v$ and emits light, an observer would measure the speed of that light as $c \pm v$ depending on their relative motion. Einstein’s postulate rejects this, asserting that the speed of light is an absolute constant that does not change through the addition or subtraction of relative velocities.
