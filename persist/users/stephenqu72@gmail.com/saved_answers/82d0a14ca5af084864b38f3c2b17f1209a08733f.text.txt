### Answer
The statement is **valid**. Unpolarised light consists of electromagnetic waves vibrating in all possible directions perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Any arbitrary, complex orientation of these vibrations can be mathematically decomposed into two orthogonal components (which we can label as $x$ and $y$ polarisations).

**Experimental Support:**
This can be demonstrated using two polarising filters (polaroids). 
1. **Procedure:** Place an unpolarised light source (such as an incandescent bulb) in front of a polarising filter. Observe that rotating the filter does not change the intensity of the light transmitted through it, confirming that the initial light contains a uniform mix of all polarisations.
2. **Analysis:** When a second polarising filter (analyser) is placed behind the first, the light passing through the first filter becomes linearly polarised. If the axis of the second filter is at an angle $\theta$ relative to the first, the intensity $I$ of the transmitted light follows Malus’s Law: $I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$.
3. **Conclusion:** If the original light were not a mixture of orthogonal components, we would not observe the consistent reduction in intensity dictated by the projection of light vectors onto the transmission axis of the polariser. By decomposing the unpolarised light into orthogonal $x$ and $y$ components, Malus’s Law successfully explains why we observe a smooth intensity gradient from maximum to zero as we rotate the analyser.
