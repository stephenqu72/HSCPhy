### Answer
The correct answer is **(A)**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental differences between the Bohr model and the Schrödinger (quantum mechanical) model of the atom:

1.  **Analyze the Bohr Model:** Bohr proposed that electrons move in fixed, circular orbits around the nucleus at discrete energy levels ($n=1, 2, 3, ...$). While Bohr's model successfully explained the emission spectrum of the hydrogen atom (Option C is a *strength* of Bohr's model, not a difference), it was limited because it treated electrons as particles moving in precise paths (making Option D incorrect, as Bohr *did* try to predict location, while Schrödinger rejected this).
2.  **Analyze the Schrödinger Model:** Erwin Schrödinger used wave mechanics to describe electrons. Instead of fixed orbits, his model describes electrons in terms of **probability distributions** called **orbitals**. This mathematical approach introduced the concept of energy sublevels (subshells) defined by quantum numbers ($s, p, d, f$). 
3.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **(A) Allowing for the existence of subshells:** This is correct. The quantum mechanical model introduced the idea of orbitals and subshells ($l$ quantum number) to describe the region where an electron is likely to be found, which Bohr's model did not account for.
    *   **(B) Assuming constant speed/acceleration:** This is incorrect. Bohr's model required centripetal acceleration (changing velocity), and quantum mechanics abandons the classical "trajectory" view entirely.
    *   **(C) Explaining the visible hydrogen spectrum:** This is incorrect because this was the *primary success* of Bohr's model, not a differentiator that showed where Schrödinger's model differed in a way that replaced Bohr's original limitation.
    *   **(D) Making firm predictions as to location:** This is incorrect. Schrödinger's model explicitly *refutes* the idea of "firm" (precise) locations, replacing them with probability clouds (Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle).

Therefore, the introduction of subshells is the defining advancement of the Schrödinger model over the Bohr model.
