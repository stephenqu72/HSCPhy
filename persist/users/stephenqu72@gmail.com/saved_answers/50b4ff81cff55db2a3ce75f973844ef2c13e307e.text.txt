To determine where the electric field strength is zero between two positive point charges $P$ and $Q$, we must consider the vector nature of the electric field.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B. Y only**.

### Explanation
The electric field strength $E$ due to a point charge is a vector quantity defined by the formula:
$$E = \frac{k|q|}{r^2}$$
where $k$ is Coulomb's constant, $q$ is the magnitude of the charge, and $r$ is the distance from the charge. The direction of the electric field from a positive charge is always **away** from the charge.

1.  **Region Y (between the charges):**
    *   At any point between $P$ and $Q$, the electric field from $P$ points to the right (away from $P$).
    *   The electric field from $Q$ points to the left (away from $Q$).
    *   Because the two fields point in opposite directions, they can subtract from each other. There will be a specific point between the two charges where the magnitudes are equal, causing the net electric field to be **zero**.

2.  **Regions X and Z (outside the charges):**
    *   **Region X:** Both charges $P$ and $Q$ are to the right. Both electric fields point to the left. Since the vectors point in the same direction, they will always add together and can never result in a net field of zero.
    *   **Region Z:** Both charges $P$ and $Q$ are to the left. Both electric fields point to the right. Again, the fields add together, so the net field cannot be zero.

Therefore, the only location where the electric field strength can be zero is in the region between the two charges, which is denoted as point $Y$.
