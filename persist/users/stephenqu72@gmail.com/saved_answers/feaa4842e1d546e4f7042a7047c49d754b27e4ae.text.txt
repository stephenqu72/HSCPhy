To solve this problem, we must account for two physical phenomena: the **Inverse Square Law** (for the spread of light over distance) and **Malus's Law** (specifically the reduction of unpolarised light by a single ideal polarising filter).

### Step 1: Apply the Inverse Square Law
The intensity of a point source of light decreases as the square of the distance from the source. The relationship is given by:
$$I_1 d_1^2 = I_2 d_2^2$$

Where:
*   $I_1 = 120\text{ lumens}$
*   $d_1 = 0.5\text{ m}$
*   $d_2 = 3.0\text{ m}$
*   $I_2$ is the intensity at $3.0\text{ m}$ before hitting the filter.

Rearranging for $I_2$:
$$I_2 = I_1 \times \left(\frac{d_1}{d_2}\right)^2$$
$$I_2 = 120 \times \left(\frac{0.5}{3.0}\right)^2$$
$$I_2 = 120 \times \left(\frac{1}{6}\right)^2$$
$$I_2 = 120 \times \frac{1}{36} \approx 3.333\text{ lumens}$$

### Step 2: Apply the Polarising Filter effect
When unpolarised light passes through an ideal polarising filter, the intensity of the transmitted light is exactly half of the incident intensity:
$$I_{transmitted} = \frac{1}{2} I_2$$

Substituting our value from Step 1:
$$I_{transmitted} = \frac{3.333}{2} \approx 1.666\text{ lumens}$$

Rounding to two significant figures (consistent with the inputs), we get approximately **$1.7\text{ lumens}$**.
