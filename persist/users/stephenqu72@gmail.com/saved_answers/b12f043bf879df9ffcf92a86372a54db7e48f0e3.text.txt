### Teacher's Explanation

To solve this problem, we rely on the principle of **magnetic levitation**, where the upward magnetic force ($F_B$) must perfectly balance the downward gravitational force ($F_g$) acting on the rod.

#### (a) Diagrammatic Representation
To levitate the rod, the magnetic force must point upwards. According to the right-hand rule for the force on a current-carrying wire ($F = IlB\sin\theta$), if we assume the magnetic field ($B$) is directed **into the page**, the current ($I$) must flow from **left to right**. 
*   **Diagram components:** Draw a rod, a vector pointing downwards labeled $F_g = mg$, an upward vector labeled $F_B = IlB$, indicate $B$-field crosses (into page), and current $I$ flowing to the right.

#### (b) Calculation and Explanation
Levitation occurs when the net force is zero:
$$F_B = F_g$$
$$IlB = mg$$

We are asked for the mass per unit length, which is defined as $\frac{m}{L}$. We can rearrange the equation above:
$$\frac{m}{L} = \frac{IB}{g}$$

Using the given values:
- Current $I = 2.3 \text{ A}$
- Magnetic field $B = 1.2 \text{ T}$
- Acceleration due to gravity $g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

$$\frac{m}{L} = \frac{2.3 \times 1.2}{9.8}$$
$$\frac{m}{L} \approx \frac{2.76}{9.8} \approx 0.28163... \text{ kg/m}$$

Rounding to three significant figures, we get $0.282 \text{ kg m}^{-1}$. 
**Explanation:** If the mass per unit length exceeds this value, the gravitational force will exceed the maximum possible upward magnetic force generated by the current, causing the rod to fall. Thus, $0.282 \text{ kg m}^{-1}$ is the limiting value for equilibrium.
