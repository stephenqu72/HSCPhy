### Explanation

1.  **Original State (Young's Double-Slit Experiment):** When light passes through two slits, it creates an **interference pattern** due to the superposition of waves. This results in a series of bright fringes (maxima) and dark fringes (minima) across the screen. The spacing of these maxima is determined by the slit separation distance $d$.

2.  **Modified State (Single-Slit Diffraction):** When you cover one of the slits, you are no longer performing a double-slit interference experiment. Instead, the light passes through a single slit. This changes the physical phenomenon from **interference** to **diffraction**.

3.  **Comparing the Patterns:**
    *   In a double-slit pattern, you have a series of equally spaced maxima.
    *   In a single-slit pattern, the light spreads out to form a broad central maximum with much lower intensity side maxima.
    *   Because the wave interference that created the multiple sharp maxima is removed, the distinct, sharp interference maxima disappear. You are left with a single, wide diffraction pattern. Therefore, the "fringe" pattern is replaced by a diffraction pattern, resulting in **fewer observable maxima** (specifically, the loss of the high-frequency interference fringes).

4.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **A is correct:** The multiple sharp interference fringes are replaced by a single, wide diffraction pattern.
    *   **B is incorrect:** The intensity of the central region actually decreases because you have removed half of the light source (one of the two slits).
    *   **C and D are incorrect:** The single-slit diffraction pattern is characterized by a central maximum that is significantly wider than the individual maxima in a double-slit experiment.

**Correct Answer: A**

### How to solve it
*   Identify the transition from **Double-Slit Interference** to **Single-Slit Diffraction**.
*   Recall that interference relies on the path difference between two coherent sources. Removing one source destroys the interference condition.
*   Conclude that the numerous, narrow interference maxima observed in the double-slit setup will cease to exist, leaving behind only the single-slit diffraction pattern.
