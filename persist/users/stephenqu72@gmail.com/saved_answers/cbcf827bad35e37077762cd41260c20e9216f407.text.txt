### Answer
The absence of "green stars" is due to the nature of black-body radiation and the physiological response of the human eye. According to **Wien’s Displacement Law**, as a star's surface temperature increases, its peak wavelength of emission shifts toward shorter wavelengths. A star that would have a peak emission in the green part of the visible spectrum ($~500\text{--}550\text{ nm}$) would actually emit a broad range of wavelengths across the entire visible spectrum. Because the black-body curve is relatively broad, a star peaking in green also emits significant amounts of red and blue light. When these wavelengths are combined, the human eye perceives the resulting light as **white** rather than green.

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to apply three key HSC Physics concepts:

1.  **Wien’s Displacement Law:** This law states that $\lambda_{peak} \propto \frac{1}{T}$. As a star gets hotter, the peak of its radiation curve shifts to the left (toward blue). 
2.  **Broad Spectrum Emission:** Look at the top graph provided. Even at $5000\text{ K}$, the curve does not emit *only* at the peak wavelength; it covers a wide range of the visible spectrum. A star that peaks in the "green" region is essentially emitting a mix of all visible colors.
3.  **Human Perception (Color Addition):** When our eyes receive a combination of all visible colors with relatively uniform intensity across the spectrum, the brain interprets this mixture as white light.
