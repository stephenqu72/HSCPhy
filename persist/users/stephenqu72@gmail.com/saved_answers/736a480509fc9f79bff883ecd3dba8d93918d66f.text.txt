### Answer
The speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) is defined as an exact constant, specifically $299,792,458$ meters per second. Because $c$ is now a fixed universal constant, the SI unit of length (the metre) is defined as the distance light travels in a vacuum during a time interval of exactly $1/299,792,458$ of a second. This approach links the measurement of distance directly to the precise measurement of time, ensuring that distance measurements are universal and unchanging.

To solve this question, you need to demonstrate an understanding of the shift in metrology—moving from physical artifacts (like metal bars) to fundamental physical constants. Here is the breakdown for the 3 marks:

1.  **State the universal status of $c$:** Mention that the speed of light is a fundamental constant of the universe. Because it does not vary, it serves as a perfect "anchor" for measurement.
2.  **Define the metre:** Explain the current definition: $1$ metre is the distance light travels in a vacuum in $1/299,792,458$ seconds.
3.  **Explain the logic:** Emphasize that because time can be measured with extreme precision (using atomic clocks), defining distance based on $c$ and time provides a significantly higher level of accuracy and reproducibility than using physical objects that could degrade or change over time.
