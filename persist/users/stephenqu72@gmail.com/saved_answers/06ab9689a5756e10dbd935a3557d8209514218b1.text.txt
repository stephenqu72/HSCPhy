### Explanation
To solve this problem, let's analyze the properties of the three main types of nuclear radiation:

1.  **Alpha ($\alpha$) particles:** These are helium nuclei ($^4_2He^{2+}$). They have a high mass and a $+2$ charge. Because they are large and charged, they interact very strongly with matter, giving them the **highest ionising ability** but the **lowest penetrating power** (they can be stopped by a sheet of paper).
2.  **Beta ($\beta$) particles:** These are high-energy electrons (or positrons). They have a very small mass and a charge of $-1$ (or $+1$). They have moderate ionising ability and moderate penetrating power (they can be stopped by a thin sheet of aluminium).
3.  **Gamma ($\gamma$) rays:** These are high-energy electromagnetic waves. They have no mass and no charge. Because they have no charge, they have the **lowest ionising ability** but the **highest penetrating power** (they can pass through most materials, requiring lead or thick concrete to stop).

**Evaluating the options:**
*   **A & B:** Deflection in magnetic or electric fields depends on the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$). Beta particles have a much higher charge-to-mass ratio than alpha particles, meaning beta particles are deflected *more* than alpha particles. Gamma rays have no charge and are not deflected at all. These options are false.
*   **C:** As established, alpha particles have the *lowest* penetrating power, not the highest. This option is false.
*   **D:** Because alpha particles are the most massive and highly charged, they cause the most ionisation per centimetre of path in matter. Thus, the order of ionising ability from highest to lowest is: Alpha > Beta > Gamma. This statement is **true**.
