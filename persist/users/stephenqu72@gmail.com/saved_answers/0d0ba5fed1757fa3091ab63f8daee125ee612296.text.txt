To determine the points where the electric potential is zero, we must use the principle of superposition for electric potential.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) At all points along the horizontal and vertical axis.**

---

### Explanation

The electric potential $V$ due to a point charge $q$ at a distance $r$ is given by the formula:
$$V = k \frac{q}{r}$$
where $k$ is Coulomb's constant. The total potential at any point is the scalar sum of the potentials from each individual charge: $V_{total} = \sum k \frac{q_i}{r_i}$.

Let the side length of the square be $2a$. The charges are located at coordinates:
- Top-left: $(-a, a)$ with charge $+q$
- Top-right: $(a, a)$ with charge $-q$
- Bottom-left: $(-a, -a)$ with charge $-q$
- Bottom-right: $(a, -a)$ with charge $+q$

**1. Testing the horizontal axis ($y = 0$):**
Any point on the horizontal axis has coordinates $(x, 0)$. The distance $r$ from this point to either the left charges or the right charges is the same.
- Distance to the left charges ($x, \pm a$): $r_L = \sqrt{(x+a)^2 + a^2}$
- Distance to the right charges ($x, \pm a$): $r_R = \sqrt{(x-a)^2 + a^2}$

The total potential is:
$$V = k \left( \frac{+q}{r_L} + \frac{-q}{r_R} + \frac{-q}{r_L} + \frac{+q}{r_R} \right)$$
Rearranging terms:
$$V = k \left( \frac{q - q}{r_L} + \frac{-q + q}{r_R} \right) = 0$$
Since the potential sums to zero for any value of $x$, all points on the horizontal axis have zero potential.

**2. Testing the vertical axis ($x = 0$):**
Any point on the vertical axis has coordinates $(0, y)$. 
- Distance to the top charges ($\pm a, a$): $r_{top} = \sqrt{a^2 + (y-a)^2}$
- Distance to the bottom charges ($\pm a, -a$): $r_{bot} = \sqrt{a^2 + (y+a)^2}$

Wait, let's look at the symmetry: For any point on the vertical axis, the distance to the left charge is equal to the distance to the right charge. 
- Top-left charge ($+q$) and top-right charge ($-q$) at $(0, y)$: $V_{top} = k \frac{q}{r} + k \frac{-q}{r} = 0$.
- Bottom-left charge ($-q$) and bottom-right charge ($+q$) at $(0, y)$: $V_{bot} = k \frac{-q}{r} + k \frac{q}{r} = 0$.
Thus, $V_{total} = 0 + 0 = 0$.

Since the potential is zero everywhere on both the horizontal and vertical axes, option (D) is correct.
