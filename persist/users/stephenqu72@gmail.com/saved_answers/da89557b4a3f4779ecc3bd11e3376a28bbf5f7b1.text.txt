### Answer
In a photoelectric effect apparatus, the stopping voltage ($V_s$) is the minimum negative potential applied to the anode relative to the cathode that is required to prevent even the most energetic photoelectrons from reaching the anode.

When electrons are emitted from the cathode, they possess a range of kinetic energies, with the most energetic having a maximum kinetic energy ($E_{k,max}$). By applying a stopping voltage, an electric field is created that does work on the electrons, opposing their motion. The work done by this electric field to bring the fastest electrons to a stop is equal to their initial maximum kinetic energy.

The relationship is defined by the equation:
$$E_{k,max} = qV_s$$

Where:
- $E_{k,max}$ is the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted electrons (in Joules).
- $q$ is the elementary charge of an electron ($1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$).
- $V_s$ is the magnitude of the stopping voltage (in Volts).

Thus, by measuring the value of the stopping voltage $V_s$ at which the photoelectric current drops to zero, we can directly calculate $E_{k,max}$ by multiplying the stopping voltage by the elementary charge.
