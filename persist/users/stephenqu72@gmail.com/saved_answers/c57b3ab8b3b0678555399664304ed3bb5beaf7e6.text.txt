To solve this question, we need to understand the classification of subatomic particles based on the Standard Model of particle physics.

### Analysis of Particle Definitions:
1.  **Hadrons:** These are composite particles made of quarks held together by the strong nuclear force. They are divided into two main categories:
    *   **Baryons:** Composed of three quarks ($qqq$).
    *   **Mesons:** Composed of one quark and one antiquark ($q\bar{q}$).
2.  **Leptons:** These are fundamental particles that do not participate in the strong nuclear force. Examples include electrons, muons, and neutrinos.

### Evaluating the Options:
*   **A. Pion:** A pion is a meson. While it is a **hadron** (because it is made of quarks), it is not a baryon (because it is not made of three quarks).
*   **B. Neutron:** A neutron is composed of three quarks ($udd$). Therefore, it is a **hadron** (it experiences the strong force) and it is specifically a **baryon**. This fits the criteria.
*   **C. Tau neutrino:** This is a fundamental particle belonging to the lepton family. It is neither a hadron nor a baryon.
*   **D. Muon:** This is also a fundamental particle belonging to the lepton family. It is neither a hadron nor a baryon.

### Conclusion:
The neutron is the only particle listed that is classified as both a hadron and a baryon.

**Correct Option: B**
