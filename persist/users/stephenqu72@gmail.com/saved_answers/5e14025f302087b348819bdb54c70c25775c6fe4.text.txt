As an HSC Physics teacher, let's break down this question on stellar spectra, which is a core concept in the "Astrophysics" elective.

### Answer
The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
To arrive at this answer, we need to consider what information is encoded in the light emitted by a star and its resulting absorption spectrum:

1.  **Surface Temperature:** By analyzing the continuous spectrum of a star (Wien’s Law) or the presence/absence of specific absorption lines (which depend on ionization levels), we can determine the surface temperature.
2.  **Elemental Composition:** Each element leaves a unique "fingerprint" of absorption lines in a spectrum. By comparing the observed absorption lines to laboratory-known spectra, we can identify which elements are present in the star's atmosphere.
3.  **Radial Speed:** The Doppler effect causes the spectral lines to shift towards the blue (blueshift) or red (redshift) end of the spectrum depending on whether the star is moving toward or away from the observer. Measuring this shift allows us to calculate the star's radial velocity.

**Why the others are incorrect:**
*   **A:** While temperature is correct, stellar "size" (radius) is generally inferred from the luminosity and temperature using the Stefan-Boltzmann Law ($L = A\sigma T^4$), not directly from the spectrum alone.
*   **B:** Spectroscopy reveals the composition of the **photosphere** (surface atmosphere), not the core. The core's environment is too dense and opaque for the light we observe to originate from there.
*   **D:** While rotational velocity *can* be inferred from spectral line broadening, "size" is not directly inferred, and "motion through space" (proper motion) requires observing the star's position change over many years relative to background stars, rather than using spectroscopy.
