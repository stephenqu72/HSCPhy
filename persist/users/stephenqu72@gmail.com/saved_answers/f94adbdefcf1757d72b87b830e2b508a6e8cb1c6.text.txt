To solve this problem, we use the principles of orbital mechanics and gravitational potential energy.

### Part (a)
**Objective:** Show that the radius of the geostationary orbit is $4.2 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$.

**Method:**
For a satellite in circular orbit, the centripetal force is provided by the gravitational force:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2} \implies v^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$$
Since $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, we substitute to get Kepler’s Third Law:
$$\frac{4\pi^2 r^2}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{r} \implies r^3 = \frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}$$

**Calculation:**
*   $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2 \text{ kg}^{-2}$
*   $M = 6.0 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$
*   $T = 24 \text{ hours} = 24 \times 3600 = 86400 \text{ s}$

$$r^3 = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(6.0 \times 10^{24})(86400)^2}{4\pi^2}$$
$$r^3 \approx 7.536 \times 10^{22}$$
$$r = \sqrt[3]{7.536 \times 10^{22}} \approx 4.22 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$$
Rounding to two significant figures, $r = 4.2 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$.

### Part (b)
**Objective:** Calculate the increase in gravitational potential energy ($\Delta U$).

**Method:**
The gravitational potential energy is given by $U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$. The increase in energy is:
$$\Delta U = U_{\text{final}} - U_{\text{initial}} = \left( -\frac{GMm}{r_f} \right) - \left( -\frac{GMm}{r_i} \right) = GMm \left( \frac{1}{r_i} - \frac{1}{r_f} \right)$$
Where $r_i$ is the Earth's radius ($6.4 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$) and $r_f$ is the orbital radius ($4.2 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$).

**Calculation:**
*   $GMm = (6.67 \times 10^{-11})(6.0 \times 10^{24})(650) \approx 2.6013 \times 10^{17}$

$$\Delta U = 2.6013 \times 10^{17} \left( \frac{1}{6.4 \times 10^6} - \frac{1}{4.2 \times 10^7} \right)$$
$$\Delta U = 2.6013 \times 10^{17} (1.5625 \times 10^{-7} - 0.2381 \times 10^{-7})$$
$$\Delta U = 2.6013 \times 10^{17} (1.3244 \times 10^{-7}) \approx 3.44 \times 10^{10} \text{ J}$$

The increase in potential energy is approximately $3.4 \times 10^{10} \text{ J}$.
