The correct answer is **(D)**.

### Explanation

In high-energy particle physics, collisions between subatomic particles moving at relativistic speeds (very close to the speed of light, $c$) are designed to utilize the principle of mass-energy equivalence, defined by Einstein's famous equation:

$$E = mc^2$$

When two protons are accelerated to $0.9999c$ and collided, their total kinetic energy is immense. According to special relativity, the relativistic mass (or total energy) of the system is extremely high. Upon collision, the kinetic energy of the incoming protons is converted into mass, allowing for the creation of new, more massive particles (such as Higgs bosons, top quarks, or other exotic matter) that could not be formed at lower energy levels.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A)** Particle masses at relativistic speeds are well-defined by the Lorentz factor $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$. Physicists do not need to collide them to "measure" this; it is a standard calculation.
*   **(B)** While protons do repel due to the electromagnetic force, this is a barrier to overcome, not the scientific *purpose* of high-energy collision experiments. 
*   **(C)** Conservation of momentum is a fundamental law of physics confirmed by extensive evidence. Experiments at particle accelerators (like the Large Hadron Collider) take the conservation of momentum as a foundational principle to identify new particles, rather than testing if the law itself holds true.
