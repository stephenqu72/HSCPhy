To solve these problems, we use the principles of circular motion and dynamics.

### (a) Determine the period of the car around the track.
The period $T$ is the time taken for one full revolution. It is related to the speed $v$ and radius $r$ by the circumference divided by the speed:
$$T = \frac{2\pi r}{v}$$
Given $r = 40\text{ m}$ and $v = 5\text{ m s}^{-1}$:
$$T = \frac{2 \pi \times 40}{5} = 16\pi \approx 50.27\text{ s}$$

### (b) Determine the angular frequency of the car.
The angular frequency $\omega$ is defined as the rate of change of the angular position, given by:
$$\omega = \frac{v}{r}$$
Using $v = 5\text{ m s}^{-1}$ and $r = 40\text{ m}$:
$$\omega = \frac{5}{40} = 0.125\text{ rad s}^{-1}$$

### (c) Determine the new speed $v'$ without the assistance of friction.
When a car travels on a banked track without friction, the centripetal force is provided entirely by the horizontal component of the normal force $N$.
1. The vertical component balances weight: $N \cos \theta = mg$
2. The horizontal component provides centripetal force: $N \sin \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$

Dividing the second equation by the first:
$$\frac{N \sin \theta}{N \cos \theta} = \frac{mv^2 / r}{mg}$$
$$\tan \theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$
Rearranging for $v$:
$$v = \sqrt{rg \tan \theta}$$
Given $r = 40\text{ m}$, $g = 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$, and $\theta = 12^\circ$:
$$v = \sqrt{40 \times 9.8 \times \tan(12^\circ)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{392 \times 0.21255} \approx \sqrt{83.32} \approx 9.13\text{ m s}^{-1}$$

***

### How to solve this:
*   **For (a) and (b):** Remember that for circular motion, kinematic relationships are universal. You simply need to recall the definitions of period and angular velocity relative to linear velocity.
*   **For (c):** This is a classic "banked track" derivation. Always start by drawing a force diagram. Since there is no friction, the normal force is the only force with a horizontal component. Setting up the ratio of components ($\sin/\cos = \tan$) is the standard HSC physics shortcut to eliminate the mass and the normal force, allowing you to solve for velocity directly.
