### Answer
The triple alpha process is a set of nuclear fusion reactions by which three helium-4 nuclei (alpha particles) are transformed into one carbon-12 nucleus. 

The key aspects are:
1. Two alpha particles ($^4\text{He}$) fuse to form an unstable isotope, beryllium-8 ($^8\text{Be}$): 
   $$^4\text{He} + ^4\text{He} \rightarrow ^8\text{Be}$$
2. A third alpha particle quickly fuses with the unstable $^8\text{Be}$ before it decays, producing a stable carbon-12 nucleus ($^{12}\text{C}$) and releasing energy: 
   $$^8\text{Be} + ^4\text{He} \rightarrow ^{12}\text{C} + \gamma$$

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the nuclear fusion stages of a star's evolution. When a star exhausts the hydrogen in its core, it leaves the main sequence and begins to cool and contract. As the core contracts, the temperature rises significantly (to approximately $10^8 \text{ K}$).

- **Identify the reactant:** The "alpha particle" is synonymous with a helium-4 nucleus ($^4\text{He}$). 
- **Identify the process:** The "triple" alpha process implies that three of these particles are involved.
- **Structural breakdown for 2 marks:** 
    - The first mark is awarded for identifying that three helium nuclei fuse to form one carbon nucleus.
    - The second mark is awarded for explaining the intermediate step: the formation of the unstable beryllium-8 intermediate, which is essential to the process because $^8\text{Be}$ is highly unstable and must capture the third alpha particle extremely rapidly to result in carbon-12.

When answering HSC Physics questions about stellar nucleosynthesis, always look to mention the specific reactants and the resulting product, as well as the intermediate state if the process involves one.
