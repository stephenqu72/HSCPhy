To justify the need for the speed sign, we must calculate the **ideal speed** for a vehicle traveling around the banked curve without relying on friction. If a vehicle exceeds this speed, it will require friction to stay on the road; if the road is "slippery," that friction is unavailable, leading to skidding.

### Calculation
For a banked curve with no reliance on friction, the horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$

Where:
- $\theta = 15^\circ$ (banking angle)
- $r = 50\text{ m}$ (radius of the curve)
- $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$ (acceleration due to gravity)

Rearranging for velocity ($v$):
$$v = \sqrt{rg \tan(\theta)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{50 \times 9.8 \times \tan(15^\circ)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{490 \times 0.2679}$$
$$v \approx \sqrt{131.29} \approx 11.46\text{ m/s}$$

Convert this to $\text{km/h}$:
$$11.46\text{ m/s} \times 3.6 = 41.25\text{ km/h}$$

### Justification
The "ideal" speed at which a vehicle can negotiate the curve without any reliance on friction is approximately $41\text{ km/h}$. Since the road is identified as "slippery," the coefficient of friction is likely very low. If a vehicle travels significantly faster than $41\text{ km/h}$, it will slide off the road because there is insufficient friction to provide the additional centripetal force required for higher speeds. Therefore, a speed limit of $40\text{ km/h}$ is necessary to ensure safety by keeping the required centripetal force within the limit provided by the banking of the road.
