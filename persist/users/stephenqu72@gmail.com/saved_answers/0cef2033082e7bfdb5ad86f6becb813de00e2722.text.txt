### Explanation
To solve this question, you must understand the historical and scientific context of the Michelson-Morley experiment within the HSC Physics "Advanced Mechanics" or "Electromagnetism" modules.

1.  **The Context:** At the end of the 19th century, physicists believed light required a medium to travel through, similar to how sound waves require air. They called this hypothetical medium the "luminiferous aether." Because the Earth orbits the Sun, scientists hypothesized that the Earth should be moving through this "aether wind."
2.  **The Experiment:** Michelson and Morley designed an extremely sensitive interferometer to measure the speed of light in different directions. If the aether existed, the speed of light would appear different depending on whether it was moving with or against the "aether wind."
3.  **The Result:** The experiment resulted in a **null result**. No difference in the speed of light was detected regardless of the orientation of the apparatus or the time of year.
4.  **Why (B) is the best choice:**
    *   (A) and (C) are incorrect because the null result did not prove the state of the Earth's motion relative to a medium; it proved the medium was not detectable.
    *   (D) is a very strong statement. While the null result eventually led to the abandonment of the aether theory and the development of Einstein’s Theory of Special Relativity, the *immediate* experimental finding was that the effect expected from the aether could not be detected/confirmed. In scientific terms, an experiment shows that a phenomenon "could not be confirmed" (or was not detected), rather than immediately declaring a non-existence, which is a broader theoretical conclusion.

Therefore, (B) is the most accurate description of the experimental outcome within the conventions of scientific inquiry.
