To solve this problem, we must apply Einstein’s photoelectric equation:

$$KE_{max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda} - \Phi$$

Where:
- $KE_{max}$ is the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted electrons.
- $h$ is Planck's constant.
- $c$ is the speed of light.
- $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the incident light.
- $\Phi$ is the work function of the metal (the minimum energy required to remove an electron).

### Analysis
1.  **Understanding the graph:** The x-axis represents the wavelength ($\lambda$). The point where each curve hits the x-axis ($KE = 0$) represents the threshold wavelength ($\lambda_0$) for that metal. At this point, the photon energy exactly equals the work function ($\frac{hc}{\lambda_0} = \Phi$).
2.  **Comparing X and Y:** Looking at the graph, the curve for metal $X$ intersects the x-axis at a smaller wavelength than the curve for metal $Y$. 
    *   Since $\Phi = \frac{hc}{\lambda_0}$, a smaller threshold wavelength means a **larger work function**. Therefore, $\Phi_X > \Phi_Y$.
3.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **A:** Incorrect. For a fixed wavelength, the curve for $Y$ is higher than $X$, meaning $Y$ provides more kinetic energy.
    *   **B:** **Correct.** Since metal $Y$ has a larger threshold wavelength ($\lambda_{0Y} > \lambda_{0X}$), it must have a smaller work function ($\Phi = \frac{hc}{\lambda_0}$).
    *   **C:** Incorrect. Since $\lambda_{0X} < \lambda_{0Y}$ and $f_0 = \frac{c}{\lambda_0}$, metal $X$ has a higher threshold frequency.
    *   **D:** Incorrect. A lower work function (metal $Y$) implies that the electrons are held less tightly by the surface compared to $X$.

### Answer
The correct option is **B**.
