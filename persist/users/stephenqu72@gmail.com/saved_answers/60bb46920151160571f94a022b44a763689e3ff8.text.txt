### Answer
The correct answer is **A. Constant phase difference between the waves**.

---

### Explanation
To observe a stable interference pattern (where bright and dark fringes remain in fixed positions), the two interfering waves must be **coherent**. 

1.  **Coherence:** Two sources are coherent if they maintain a **constant phase difference** over time. If the phase difference varies rapidly and randomly (as it does with two independent light bulbs, for example), the interference pattern will shift so quickly that the human eye or a detector will only see an average, uniform intensity rather than a stable pattern of fringes.
2.  **Why A is the most "essential" condition:** While having the same frequency (Option C) is a requirement for a constant phase difference, having a constant phase difference is the broader, defining requirement for coherence. Without a constant phase relationship, interference cannot be sustained.
3.  **Why the others are secondary:**
    *   **B and D (Amplitude/Intensity):** If the amplitudes or intensities are not equal, interference still occurs; however, the destructive interference will not result in total darkness (zero intensity). The fringes will simply be less distinct (lower contrast), but they will still exist.
    *   **C (Frequency):** While monochromatic light (equal frequency) is typically used to create interference, the fundamental physical requirement is the stability of the phase relationship. The concept of "coherent sources" is formally defined by the maintenance of a constant phase difference.

**Summary for your exam technique:** In HSC Physics, whenever you are asked for the "essential" condition for interference, look for **coherence** or **constant phase difference**. Options that improve the *quality* of the pattern (like equal amplitude) are secondary to the *existence* of the pattern itself.
