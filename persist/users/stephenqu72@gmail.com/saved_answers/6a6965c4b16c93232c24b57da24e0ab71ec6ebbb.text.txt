To solve this problem, we use the principle of conservation of energy. When a charge $q$ is accelerated through a potential difference $V$, the work done by the electric field on the charge is equal to the change in its kinetic energy.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Identify the relationship:**
    The work done on a charge accelerated through a potential difference is given by:
    $$W = qV$$
    Since this work manifests as an increase in kinetic energy ($\Delta EK$), we have:
    $$qV = \Delta EK$$

2.  **Calculate the change in kinetic energy ($\Delta EK$):**
    The kinetic energy increases from $2.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ J}$ to $6.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ J}$.
    $$\Delta EK = EK_{final} - EK_{initial}$$
    $$\Delta EK = 6.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ J} - 2.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ J} = 4.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ J}$$

3.  **Rearrange the formula to solve for the charge ($q$):**
    $$q = \frac{\Delta EK}{V}$$

4.  **Substitute the known values:**
    Given $V = 2000\text{ V}$ and $\Delta EK = 4.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ J}$:
    $$q = \frac{4.0 \times 10^{-5}\text{ J}}{2000\text{ V}}$$
    $$q = \frac{4.0 \times 10^{-5}}{2.0 \times 10^{3}} = 2.0 \times 10^{-8}\text{ C}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, the correct answer is **(B)**.
