### Answer

Max Planck accounted for the black body radiation curve by introducing the revolutionary hypothesis of **quantization**. Classical physics at the time (based on the Rayleigh-Jeans Law) predicted that as the wavelength of emitted radiation decreased, the intensity would increase towards infinity—a discrepancy known as the "ultraviolet catastrophe."

Planck solved this by postulating that the atoms in the black body do not emit energy continuously. Instead, he proposed that energy is emitted or absorbed in discrete packets called **quanta**, given by the equation:
$$E = nhf$$
where $n$ is an integer, $h$ is Planck’s constant, and $f$ is the frequency of the radiation. This effectively constrained the energy available at high frequencies (short wavelengths), causing the theoretical curve to drop back down towards zero, matching the experimental results.

This was initially not an "accepted" explanation because it contradicted the prevailing **classical wave theory** of electromagnetism, which held that energy was a continuous quantity. Planck himself initially viewed this quantization as a purely mathematical "act of desperation" rather than a physical reality, as there was no classical mechanism to explain why energy could only exist in discrete steps.
