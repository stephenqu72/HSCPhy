To answer this question, we must look at the nuclear fusion processes occurring in stars on the main sequence.

### Answer
The correct answer is **D. High mass main sequence**.

### Explanation
In stars, hydrogen fusion into helium occurs primarily through two processes: the proton-proton (p-p) chain and the Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen (CNO) cycle.

1.  **Proton-Proton Chain:** This process is the dominant energy source for stars with temperatures below approximately $17$ million Kelvin, such as our Sun or other low-mass main sequence stars. It relies on direct proton-proton collisions.
2.  **CNO Cycle:** This process acts as a catalytic cycle where carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen nuclei facilitate the fusion of hydrogen into helium. The CNO cycle is highly temperature-dependent (its rate increases approximately as $T^{17}$). 
3.  **Determining the Star Type:** High-mass main sequence stars have much denser cores and higher internal temperatures due to the gravitational pressure exerted by their mass. Once the core temperature exceeds approximately $17$ million Kelvin, the CNO cycle becomes significantly more efficient than the p-p chain and takes over as the primary mechanism for hydrogen burning.

Red giants are generally post-main-sequence stars that have exhausted hydrogen in their cores and are burning hydrogen in a shell around a helium core; therefore, they are not the primary classification for stars where the CNO cycle is the "predominant" *main sequence* reaction.
