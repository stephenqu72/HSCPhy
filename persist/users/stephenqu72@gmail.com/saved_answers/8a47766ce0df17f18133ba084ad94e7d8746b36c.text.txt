### Answer

**Definition of a Black Body:**
A black body is an idealized physical object that absorbs all incident electromagnetic radiation, regardless of frequency or angle of incidence. Because it reflects or transmits no light, it appears perfectly black at room temperature. It also serves as a perfect emitter of radiation when heated, with its emission spectrum depending solely on its temperature.

**Contribution to a Changed Model of Light:**
The experimental curves for a black body at various temperatures (solid lines) showed a peak in intensity and a drop-off at short wavelengths. However, classical physics—specifically the Rayleigh-Jeans law—predicted that intensity should increase infinitely as wavelength decreases (the "ultraviolet catastrophe," shown by the dashed line). 

This contradiction forced a shift in the model of light:
1.  **Quantization of Energy:** Max Planck resolved this by proposing that electromagnetic energy is not emitted continuously, but in discrete packets called *quanta*.
2.  **Planck's Law:** The energy of these quanta is proportional to frequency ($E = hf$). This successfully modeled the experimental curves because high-frequency (short wavelength) emissions were suppressed, as they required energy packets larger than what was statistically available at those temperatures. This transition from continuous wave theory to quantum theory laid the foundation for modern physics.
