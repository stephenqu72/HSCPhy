### Explanation

To solve this, we rely on **Hubble’s Law**, which describes the relationship between the recessional velocity ($v$) of a galaxy and its distance ($d$) from Earth. The mathematical expression for Hubble's Law is:
$$v = H_0 \cdot d$$

Where:
*   $v$ is the recessional velocity.
*   $d$ is the distance.
*   $H_0$ is the Hubble constant.

**Analyzing the graph:**
*   The $y$-axis represents velocity ($v$).
*   The $x$-axis represents distance ($d$).
*   The graph is a straight line passing through the origin. For any linear relationship of the form $y = mx$, the gradient ($m$) represents the constant of proportionality.
*   By comparing $v = H_0 \cdot d$ to the linear equation $y = mx$, we can see that $H_0$ corresponds to the gradient of the line. Therefore, the gradient of this graph represents Hubble's constant.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**

While the unit is $\text{s}^{-1}$, option (C) is a fundamental definition of the relationship shown in the graph. However, the statement in (C) is the most direct interpretation of the visual data provided.
*   **(B)** The area under a velocity-distance graph does not represent a standard physical constant in this context.
*   **(D)** An inverse variation would imply $v \propto 1/d$, which would result in a curve, not the linear relationship shown in the graph. The graph clearly shows a **direct** linear relationship.
