### Answer
Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation and Kepler’s Third Law are fundamental to understanding satellite motion because they establish the relationship between mass, distance, and orbital period.

**1. Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation:**
Newton's law states that $F = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$. For a satellite in a circular orbit, this gravitational force provides the centripetal force required to keep the satellite in motion ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$). By equating these:
$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
This allows us to calculate the orbital velocity ($v$) of a satellite at a specific distance ($r$) from the Earth's center: $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$. This is critical for engineers launching satellites, as they must achieve precise velocities to reach specific altitudes.

**2. Kepler’s Third Law:**
Kepler’s Third Law states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius ($r$), expressed as $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$. 
This law is vital for understanding the relationship between a satellite’s period and its distance from Earth. It explains why geostationary satellites must orbit at a specific altitude: since their period must match Earth's rotation ($24$ hours), there is only one possible radius ($r$) that satisfies the equation.

**Synthesis:**
Together, these laws allow for the design of complex orbital systems. Newton provides the mechanical "how" (the force balance), while Kepler provides the empirical "predictability" of timing and distance. Without these laws, we could not accurately predict satellite lifespans, avoid collisions in orbit, or determine the required launch energy.
