To solve this problem, we must apply the laws governing the intensity of polarized light.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **First Polarizer:** When unpolarised light of intensity $I_0$ passes through the first ideal polarising sheet, its intensity is reduced by half. The intensity of the light emerging from the first sheet ($I_1$) is:
    $$I_1 = \frac{1}{2}I_0$$

2.  **Second Polarizer:** When this now-polarized light passes through the second sheet, we use Malus's Law, which states $I_2 = I_1 \cos^2(\theta)$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the transmission axes of the two sheets. Substituting $I_1$:
    $$I_2 = \left( \frac{1}{2}I_0 \right) \cos^2(\theta)$$

3.  **Solving for $\theta$:** The question states that the transmitted intensity $I_2$ is one-third of the initial intensity $I_0$ ($I_2 = \frac{1}{3}I_0$). We set up the equation:
    $$\frac{1}{3}I_0 = \frac{1}{2}I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$$
    
    Divide both sides by $I_0$:
    $$\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{2} \cos^2(\theta)$$
    
    Multiply by 2:
    $$\cos^2(\theta) = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.6667$$
    
    Take the square root:
    $$\cos(\theta) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} \approx 0.8165$$
    
    Calculate the inverse cosine:
    $$\theta = \arccos(0.8165) \approx 35.26^\circ$$

Rounding to the nearest degree, we get $35^\circ$.
