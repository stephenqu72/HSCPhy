### Answer
The correct answer is **C. 12 mg**.

### Explanation

To solve this problem, we need to determine how much of the original radioactive Technetium-99m ($^{99m}\text{Tc}$) has decayed into the stable isotope Technetium-99 ($^{99}\text{Tc}$) after two half-lives.

**1. Determine the number of half-lives:**
*   The total time elapsed is $12$ hours.
*   The half-life of Technetium-99m is $6$ hours.
*   Number of half-lives ($n$) = $\frac{12 \text{ hours}}{6 \text{ hours}} = 2$ half-lives.

**2. Calculate the remaining amount of the parent isotope ($^{99m}\text{Tc}$):**
*   After the first half-life ($6$ hours): $16 \text{ mg} \times 0.5 = 8 \text{ mg}$ remains.
*   After the second half-life ($12$ hours): $8 \text{ mg} \times 0.5 = 4 \text{ mg}$ remains.
*   So, after $12$ hours, there is $4 \text{ mg}$ of the radioactive isotope left.

**3. Calculate the amount of the product isotope ($^{99}\text{Tc}$):**
*   The law of conservation of mass dictates that the amount of the product formed must equal the amount of the parent isotope that decayed.
*   $\text{Amount decayed} = \text{Initial amount} - \text{Remaining amount}$
*   $\text{Amount decayed} = 16 \text{ mg} - 4 \text{ mg} = 12 \text{ mg}$

Therefore, $12 \text{ mg}$ of Technetium-99 has been produced.
