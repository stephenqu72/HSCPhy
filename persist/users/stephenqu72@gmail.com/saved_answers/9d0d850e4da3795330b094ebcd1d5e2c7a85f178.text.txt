### Explanation
To understand why **D** is the best choice, we must look at the experimental formula used to calculate the wavelength ($\lambda$) of light using a diffraction grating:
$$d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$$
Where $d$ is the grating spacing, $\theta$ is the angle of diffraction, and $n$ is the order of the fringe. From the geometry of the setup provided in the diagram, $\tan(\theta) = \frac{x}{L}$, where $x$ is the distance measured by the students and $L$ is the distance from the grating to the pin ($1\text{ m}$).

The accuracy of an experimental measurement is often limited by the **relative uncertainty** of the readings. 
- When measuring the first order ($n=1$), the distance $x$ is small. A small absolute error in positioning the pin $P$ results in a high percentage error.
- By moving to the second order ($n=2$), the fringe appears at a larger angle, which means the distance $x$ will be significantly larger. Because the absolute uncertainty in your measurement (the ruler's smallest division) remains the same, having a larger measured value for $x$ reduces the percentage (relative) uncertainty of your measurement, thereby improving the overall accuracy.
