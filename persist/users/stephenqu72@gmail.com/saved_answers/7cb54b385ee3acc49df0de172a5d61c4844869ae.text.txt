To solve this problem, we must analyze the physics of circular orbital motion.

### 1. The Physics Principle
For a satellite of mass $m$ to orbit a central body of mass $M$ at a radius $r$, the gravitational force must provide the necessary centripetal force:
$$F_g = F_c$$
$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

By simplifying this equation for orbital velocity $v$, we get:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

### 2. Analyzing the Relationship
*   **Relationship between $v$ and $r$:** The formula $v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}$ shows an inverse square root relationship. Mathematically, this corresponds to a curve that starts high and decreases as $r$ increases. This eliminates graphs (B), (C), and (D), as they show linear or increasing relationships.
*   **Comparing Earth and Moon:** The mass of Earth ($M_E \approx 5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$) is significantly greater than the mass of the Moon ($M_M = 7.35 \times 10^{22}\text{ kg}$). Since $v = \sqrt{GM/r}$, for any given radius $r$, the velocity of a satellite orbiting the Earth will be greater than the velocity of a satellite orbiting the Moon.
    *   $v_{\text{Earth}} = \sqrt{\frac{GM_{\text{Earth}}}{r}}$
    *   $v_{\text{Moon}} = \sqrt{\frac{GM_{\text{Moon}}}{r}}$

Because $M_{\text{Earth}} > M_{\text{Moon}}$, the curve for Earth must lie *above* the curve for the Moon.

### 3. Conclusion
Graph (A) correctly depicts the inverse relationship ($v \propto r^{-1/2}$) and correctly shows the Earth's curve positioned above the Moon's curve due to the larger mass of the Earth.
