To answer this question, we must apply the motor effect formula for magnetic force on a current-carrying conductor:
$$F = BIL\sin(\theta)$$
where $F$ is the magnetic force, $B$ is the magnetic field strength, $I$ is the current, $L$ is the length of the wire, and $\theta$ is the angle between the wire and the magnetic field.

### Analysis of Forces

**1. Before rotation:**
*   **Segment $XY$:** The current flows vertically downward, and the magnetic field is horizontal (from North to South). They are perpendicular ($\theta = 90^\circ$), so $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$. The force is at its maximum: $F = BIL$.
*   **Segment $WX$:** The current flows horizontally, parallel to the magnetic field lines. The angle $\theta = 0^\circ$, so $\sin(0^\circ) = 0$. Therefore, the force on $WX$ is $0$.

**2. After $90^\circ$ rotation:**
The loop rotates around the axis $PQ$. After rotating $90^\circ$, the plane of the loop $WXYZ$ is now parallel to the magnetic field lines.
*   **Segment $XY$:** This segment is now oriented parallel to the magnetic field lines. The angle $\theta$ becomes $0^\circ$, so the force on $XY$ becomes $0$.
*   **Segment $WX$:** This segment is now perpendicular to the magnetic field lines. The angle $\theta$ becomes $90^\circ$, so the force on $WX$ increases to its maximum value, $F = BIL$.

### Comparison Summary
*   **Initially:** There is a maximum force on segment $XY$ and zero force on segment $WX$.
*   **After rotation:** The force on $XY$ drops to zero, while the force on $WX$ increases to its maximum value.
