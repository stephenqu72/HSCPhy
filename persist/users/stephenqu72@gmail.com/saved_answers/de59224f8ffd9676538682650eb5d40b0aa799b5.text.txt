### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the limitations of the classical wave theory of light and how Einstein’s photon model resolved them:

1.  **Classical vs. Einsteinian Model:** Classical physics suggested that light intensity should determine the energy of emitted electrons. However, experiments showed that the frequency of light, not the intensity, was the determining factor for emission.
2.  **Analysis of Option A:** Einstein proposed that light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons, where the energy of a photon is given by $E = hf$. Since $f = c/\lambda$, a lower wavelength corresponds to a higher frequency and thus higher energy. There is a specific "threshold frequency" ($f_0$) or "threshold wavelength" ($\lambda_0$) required to overcome the work function ($\Phi$) of the metal. If the light's wavelength is above this threshold (meaning the frequency is too low), no photoelectrons are ejected, regardless of the intensity. This is exactly what Option A describes.
3.  **Why the other options are incorrect:**
    *   **B:** According to Einstein's equation ($E_k = hf - \Phi$), the maximum kinetic energy depends on the frequency ($f$) of the incident light, not the intensity. Increasing intensity only increases the *number* of photoelectrons emitted per second, not their individual kinetic energy.
    *   **C:** The work function ($\Phi$) is a property unique to each specific metal. Therefore, the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons is highly dependent on the type of metal used.
    *   **D:** Photoelectric emission is near-instantaneous (it does not require "exposure time" to accumulate energy), contradicting the wave theory prediction that a lower intensity would eventually eject an electron if given enough time.

By understanding that $E = hf$ explains the threshold behavior of the photoelectric effect, we identify **A** as the only observation consistent with the quantum model.
