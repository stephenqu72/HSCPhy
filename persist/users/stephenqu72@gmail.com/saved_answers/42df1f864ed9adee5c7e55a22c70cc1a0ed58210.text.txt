### Part a: Calculation of Photon Energy
To find the energy of a photon, we use the formula:
$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$
Where:
*   $h$ (Planck's constant) = $6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
*   $c$ (speed of light) = $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
*   $\lambda$ (wavelength) = $436.6 \text{ nm} = 436.6 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}$

**Calculation:**
$$E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)}{436.6 \times 10^{-9}}$$
$$E \approx 4.55 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

To convert this to electron-volts (eV), divide by $1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/eV}$:
$$E_{\text{(eV)}} = \frac{4.552 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 2.84 \text{ eV}$$

### Part c: Possible Photons Produced
If the atom absorbs a $6.7 \text{ eV}$ photon, it is excited to the **$n=3$** level. When it returns to the ground state ($n=1$), it can follow different paths, emitting photons equal to the energy gaps between levels.

**Possible transitions:**
1.  **Directly from $n=3$ to $n=1$:** $6.70 \text{ eV} - 0 \text{ eV} = \mathbf{6.70 \text{ eV}}$
2.  **From $n=3$ to $n=2$:** $6.70 \text{ eV} - 4.89 \text{ eV} = \mathbf{1.81 \text{ eV}}$
3.  **From $n=2$ to $n=1$:** $4.89 \text{ eV} - 0 \text{ eV} = \mathbf{4.89 \text{ eV}}$

The possible photons produced have energies of **$6.70 \text{ eV}$**, **$4.89 \text{ eV}$**, and **$1.81 \text{ eV}$**.

### How to solve these problems
1.  **Energy of a photon:** Always remember the relationship $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$. If you need eV, either convert at the end by dividing by $1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ or use $hc \approx 1240 \text{ eV}\cdot\text{nm}$ for a quick shortcut.
2.  **Energy level diagrams:** The energy of a photon emitted is exactly equal to the difference between two energy states ($\Delta E = E_{\text{initial}} - E_{\text{final}}$). Always subtract the levels to see if they match your calculated photon energy.
3.  **Excitation paths:** When an atom is excited to a specific level, identify all intermediate levels below it. The atom can cascade down through any combination of those levels until it reaches the ground state ($n=1$).
