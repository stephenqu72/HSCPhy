To solve this problem, we need to understand the relationship between the recession velocity of a galaxy and its distance from Earth in the context of different expansion models.

### Answer
The correct graph is **C**.

### Explanation
1.  **Hubble's Law (The Linear Model):** Edwin Hubble originally observed that the velocity ($v$) of a galaxy is directly proportional to its distance ($d$) from Earth, expressed as $v = H_0d$, where $H_0$ is the Hubble constant. This relationship is represented by a straight line passing through the origin (Graph A).
2.  **The Accelerating Universe:** The discovery in the late 1990s involved measuring Type Ia supernovae in very distant galaxies. Astronomers found that these galaxies were further away than their redshift (velocity) would suggest if the expansion were constant or slowing down due to gravity.
3.  **Interpreting the Graph:** 
    *   In an **accelerating** universe, for very large distances, the velocity increases more rapidly than predicted by a linear relationship. 
    *   If you look at the axes, the x-axis is **Distance** and the y-axis is **Velocity**. 
    *   **Graph C** shows an "upward curving" slope (the gradient of the line increases as distance increases). This indicates that at greater distances, galaxies have a higher velocity than a linear relationship would suggest, which is the signature of an accelerating expansion.
    *   Graph D shows a "downward curving" slope, which would represent a decelerating universe where the expansion rate slows down over time.

Therefore, the upward-curving line in **Graph C** is the only one consistent with the observation that the expansion of the universe is accelerating.
