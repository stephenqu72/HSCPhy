### Answer
The braking system relies on **eddy currents**. As the magnets move past the aluminium fin, the changing magnetic flux induces eddy currents in the conductor (Faraday's Law). These currents create their own magnetic fields that oppose the motion of the magnets (Lenz's Law), resulting in a braking force that produces negative acceleration.

**Similarities:**
*   Both curves exhibit the same general shape: a rapid increase in magnitude of negative acceleration, reaching a peak, followed by a gradual decay back to zero as the car loses velocity.
*   The time taken to reach peak acceleration (approximately $0.75\text{--}0.8\text{ s}$) and the time to return to zero acceleration ($4\text{ s}$) are identical for both initial speeds.

**Differences:**
*   The peak magnitude of acceleration is greater for the car with the higher initial velocity ($u = 12\text{ m s}^{-1}$ reaches $\approx -8.6\text{ m s}^{-2}$, whereas $u = 10\text{ m s}^{-1}$ reaches $\approx -6.6\text{ m s}^{-2}$).
*   Because $F = ma$ and the induced EMF (and thus current) is proportional to the rate of change of flux ($v \cdot B$), a higher initial velocity induces larger eddy currents, leading to a stronger opposing magnetic force and higher deceleration.
