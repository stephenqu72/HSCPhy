### Solution

**Part a) Calculation**

To calculate the wavelength ($\lambda$), we use the Rydberg equation provided:
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right)$$

Given:
- Rydberg constant, $R \approx 1.097 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$
- Initial energy level, $n_i = 5$
- Final energy level, $n_f = 3$

**Step 1: Substitute the values into the equation**
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \frac{1}{3^2} - \frac{1}{5^2} \right)$$
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \left( \frac{1}{9} - \frac{1}{25} \right)$$

**Step 2: Solve the fraction**
$$\frac{1}{9} - \frac{1}{25} = 0.1111 - 0.04 = 0.0711$$
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times 0.07111$$
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 780,077.7 \text{ m}^{-1}$$

**Step 3: Solve for $\lambda$**
$$\lambda = \frac{1}{780,077.7} \approx 1.282 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}$$
$$\lambda \approx 1282 \text{ nm}$$

### How to solve this
1. **Identify the variables:** Always clearly state $n_i$ (initial shell) and $n_f$ (final shell). Remember that $n_i$ is always the higher number in an emission transition.
2. **Apply the constant:** Ensure you use the standard Rydberg constant ($1.097 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$) unless otherwise provided in your data sheet.
3. **Algebraic caution:** A very common mistake is forgetting to take the reciprocal ($1/x$) at the end. After calculating $\frac{1}{\lambda}$, you must divide 1 by your result to get the final wavelength.
4. **Diagramming:** When drawing Bohr model transitions, ensure the arrow points **inward** (towards the nucleus) for emission and **outward** for absorption.
