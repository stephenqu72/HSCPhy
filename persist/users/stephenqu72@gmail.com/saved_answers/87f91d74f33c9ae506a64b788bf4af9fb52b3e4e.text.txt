### Answer and Explanation

The speed of the mass is limited by the interaction between the motor’s output power, the tension in the rope, and the opposing forces acting on the mass. 

**1. Mechanical Constraints (Newton's Laws)**
The net force on the mass is given by Newton’s Second Law: 
$$\sum F = T - F_{f} = ma$$
Where $T$ is tension and $F_f$ is friction. The maximum speed is reached when the system stops accelerating ($a=0$), meaning the tension provided by the motor equals the frictional force: $T = F_{f} = \mu mg$. If the motor cannot provide tension greater than this, the mass cannot move.

**2. Electrical Constraints (The Motor)**
The motor’s ability to provide tension relates to its power output. Since power is defined as $P = Fv$ (where $F$ is the tension $T$ and $v$ is velocity), we have:
$$v = \frac{P}{T}$$
As the required tension $T$ increases (due to higher friction or mass), the speed $v$ must decrease for a fixed power input $P$. Furthermore, as an electric motor spins, it generates a **back EMF** ($\epsilon$). According to the voltage equation:
$$V_{applied} = I R + \epsilon$$
The back EMF is proportional to angular velocity ($\epsilon = k \omega$). As the motor speeds up, $\epsilon$ increases, which reduces the armature current $I$ and consequently reduces the torque $\tau$ produced by the motor ($\tau = k I$). Since $T = \frac{\tau}{r}$, a reduction in torque directly reduces the tension available to pull the mass.

**3. Summary of Limiting Factors**
*   **Frictional Force ($F_f = \mu mg$):** Increases the tension required just to maintain constant velocity.
*   **Motor Power ($P$):** Limits the maximum work done per unit time. 
*   **Back EMF ($\epsilon$):** As speed $v$ increases, $\epsilon$ increases, reducing current $I$ and torque $\tau$. When the motor torque drops to the point where $\tau/r = F_f$, the mass reaches its terminal velocity.

### How to solve this type of question:
1.  **Deconstruct the system:** Break it into the "load" (the mass) and the "source" (the motor).
2.  **Use Physics models:** Always cite the relevant equations. For mechanics, use $F=ma$ and $F_f = \mu N$. For motors, always invoke the relationship between torque, current, and back EMF.
3.  **Link the domains:** The "bridge" between the mechanics and the electricity is the tension ($T$) and the power equation ($P=Tv$). 
4.  **Connect to limiting behavior:** Explicitly explain that "terminal speed" occurs when the driving force equals the resistive force or when the back EMF limits the motor's torque to match the load.
