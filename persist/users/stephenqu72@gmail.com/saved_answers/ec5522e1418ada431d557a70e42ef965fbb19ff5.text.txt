### Answer
The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we must evaluate the historical experiments performed on cathode rays (electrons) during the late 1800s:

*   **(A) True:** Experiments showed that cathode rays are deflected by magnetic fields. This is consistent with the Lorentz force law, $F = qvB\sin(\theta)$, proving that cathode rays consist of charged particles.
*   **(B) True:** By placing an opaque object in the path of the cathode rays, scientists observed a sharp "shadow" on the end of the tube, demonstrating that they travel in straight lines.
*   **(C) NOT True:** This is the correct choice because it is a subtle but important distinction. J.J. Thomson’s experiments in 1897 allowed him to calculate the **charge-to-mass ratio** ($q/m$) of the electron. He could not determine the mass of the electron alone without knowing the value of the elementary charge ($q$). It wasn't until Robert Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment in 1909 that the charge of the electron was determined, which then allowed scientists to calculate the actual mass of the electron ($m$).
*   **(D) True:** By placing a small paddle wheel in the path of the cathode rays, the rays caused the wheel to rotate. This demonstrated that the cathode rays possessed kinetic energy and momentum, proving they were composed of particles with mass rather than just electromagnetic waves.

### How to solve this type of question
1.  **Read the question carefully:** Note that it asks for the statement that is **not true**.
2.  **Recall the key experiments:** Associate J.J. Thomson, the Maltesecross, the paddle wheel, and the magnetic/electric deflection experiments with their specific findings.
3.  **Evaluate the "Charge-to-Mass" trap:** In Physics HSC questions, always distinguish between a "ratio" ($q/m$) and an absolute value ($m$ or $q$). Thomson identified the particle, but he did not measure the mass in isolation.
