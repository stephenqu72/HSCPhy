### Answer
Einstein’s thought experiment regarding a person moving at the speed of light ($c$) facing a mirror created a dilemma that challenged the core principles of classical physics. The two possible outcomes are:

1.  **The passenger sees their reflection:** If the passenger sees their reflection, it implies that light travels from the person to the mirror and back to the person's eyes at the speed $c$ relative to the passenger. This is consistent with the passenger’s own frame of reference (where they are at rest).
2.  **The passenger does not see their reflection:** If an observer outside the train (stationary relative to the track) considers the light beam, they would see the mirror moving away from the light at speed $c$. According to classical Galilean relativity, the light would never catch the mirror. Therefore, no light returns, and the passenger should see nothing.

**The Problem:**
This created a conflict between the **Principle of Relativity** and **Maxwell's Equations of Electromagnetism**. If the passenger sees nothing, it violates the Principle of Relativity (which states that the laws of physics should be the same in all inertial frames). If the passenger sees their reflection, it violates the classical laws of velocity addition, as the light would have to travel at a different speed relative to the outside observer than it does relative to the passenger, which contradicts Maxwell’s finding that $c$ is constant. Einstein resolved this by proposing that $c$ is constant for all observers, leading to the development of special relativity.
