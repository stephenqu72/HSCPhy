### Question 6
**Answer:** C

**Explanation:**
To solve this, we must distinguish between different types of experimental errors:
*   **Random errors** are unpredictable fluctuations that affect measurements in both directions (sometimes higher, sometimes lower). 
*   **Systematic errors** are consistent, repeatable errors that occur throughout an experiment. In this case, the second stopwatch caused the recorded period to be consistently $1 \text{ second}$ longer. Because the error is predictable, persistent, and disappears when the instrument is changed back, it is a clear example of a systematic error (specifically, a calibration error in the timing device). 
*   Options B and D are incorrect because the consistency of the shift ($1 \text{ second}$) points to the instrument rather than a variable human reaction time or a physical change in the string.

### Question 7
**Answer:** C

**Explanation:**
This apparatus is a standard "conical pendulum" or "centripetal force" experiment. 
*   The mass hanging at the bottom is held in equilibrium by the tension in the string.
*   This same tension provides the centripetal force required to keep the stopper moving in a horizontal circle.
*   By adjusting the speed, the tension in the string balances the gravitational force acting on the hanging masses (assuming a steady state where the masses remain at a fixed height). Therefore, the magnitude of the gravitational force on the hanging mass, $F_g = Mg$, is equal to the centripetal force, $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$, acting on the rotating stopper.
