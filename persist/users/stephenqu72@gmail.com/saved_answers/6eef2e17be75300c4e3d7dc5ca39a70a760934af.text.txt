### Answer
If quarks were discovered to be composite particles, the Standard Model of Matter would be fundamentally challenged, as it currently defines quarks as "elementary" or "fundamental" particles—meaning they have no internal structure and are not composed of smaller constituents.

The discovery would necessitate the following:
1.  **Reclassification of "Fundamental":** The definition of what constitutes a fundamental particle would shift. Quarks would no longer be seen as the base units of matter; instead, the constituents of the quarks would become the new candidates for fundamental particles.
2.  **Expansion/Revision of the Standard Model:** Physicists would need to develop a new theoretical framework (an "extension" of the Standard Model) to explain the forces and interactions holding these "sub-quark" particles together. This is similar to how quarks were originally proposed to explain the structure of hadrons.
3.  **New Physical Laws:** The interactions between these new sub-particles might require new gauge bosons or force carriers, potentially introducing physics beyond the Standard Model.
4.  **Shift in Scientific Paradigm:** This would mirror historical shifts in physics (like moving from the atom to the nucleus, and the nucleus to protons/neutrons, and then to quarks). It would suggest that our current "bottom layer" of reality is simply another layer of complexity, prompting further investigation into whether these new particles themselves are fundamental.

*   **Use Scientific Terminology:** Use terms like "elementary particles," "internal structure," "constituents," and "Standard Model extension."
