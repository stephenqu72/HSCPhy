To solve this problem, we must compare the forces acting on the electron in a gravitational field versus an electric field.

### 1. Identify the Forces
*   **Gravitational Field:** The force on an electron is $F_g = mg$, where $m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$ and $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Since the gravity vector points downwards and an electron has mass, it experiences a downward force.
*   **Electric Field:** The force on a charge in an electric field is $F_e = qE$. Since the electron has a negative charge ($-e = -1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$), the force acts in the *opposite* direction to the electric field vector. Given the arrows in the diagram point downwards, the force on the electron is **upwards**.

### 2. Quantitative Comparison
**Gravitational Force:**
$$F_g = (9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}) \times (9.8 \text{ m/s}^2) \approx 8.93 \times 10^{-30} \text{ N}$$

**Electric Force:**
$$F_e = qE = (1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (5.5 \times 10^4 \text{ V/m}) \approx 8.81 \times 10^{-15} \text{ N}$$

**Ratio:**
$$\frac{F_e}{F_g} = \frac{8.81 \times 10^{-15}}{8.93 \times 10^{-30}} \approx 9.87 \times 10^{14}$$

### 3. Qualitative Comparison
*   **Direction:** In the gravitational field, the electron is deflected **downwards** towards the ground. In the electric field, because the electron is negatively charged, the electric force is directed **upwards** (opposing the field lines).
*   **Magnitude:** The electric force is approximately $10^{15}$ times stronger than the gravitational force. For an electron, gravity is negligible compared to electrostatic forces.
*   **Motion:** In both cases, the horizontal velocity remains constant (ignoring air resistance), and the vertical acceleration produces a parabolic trajectory. However, the electron in the electric field will accelerate rapidly upwards, while the electron in the gravitational field will accelerate very slowly downwards.
