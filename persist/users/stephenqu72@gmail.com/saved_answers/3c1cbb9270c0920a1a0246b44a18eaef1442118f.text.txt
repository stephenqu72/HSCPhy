### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) The atom is mostly empty space.**

---

### Explanation
To solve this, we must recall the context of the Geiger-Marsden experiment (Rutherford's gold foil experiment) within the history of the atomic model:

1.  **The Context:** Before this experiment, the "Plum Pudding" model (proposed by J.J. Thomson) was the accepted theory. It suggested that atoms were spheres of positive charge with negatively charged electrons embedded within them like plums in a pudding.
2.  **The Experiment:** Geiger and Marsden fired high-speed alpha particles (positively charged helium nuclei) at a very thin piece of gold foil.
3.  **The Observations:**
    *   The vast majority of alpha particles passed straight through the foil without any deflection. This led Rutherford to conclude that the atom is **mostly empty space**.
    *   A very small percentage of alpha particles were deflected at large angles, and some even bounced directly backward. This indicated that there must be a very small, dense, and positively charged region at the center of the atom, which we now call the **nucleus**.
4.  **Evaluating the distractors:**
    *   (B) The discovery of neutrons occurred much later, attributed to James Chadwick in 1932.
    *   (C) The concept of electrons in discrete energy levels was introduced by Niels Bohr to fix the stability issues of Rutherford’s planetary model.
    *   (D) The existence of positive and negative charges was already known through the work of J.J. Thomson; the gold foil experiment refined the *distribution* of these charges.

Therefore, the fundamental change to the atomic model resulting from this specific experiment was the realization that the atom is mostly empty space surrounding a tiny, dense core.
