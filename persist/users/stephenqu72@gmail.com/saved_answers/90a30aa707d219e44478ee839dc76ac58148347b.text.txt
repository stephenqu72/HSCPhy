### Answer
To achieve full marks for a 9-mark response, you must demonstrate a deep understanding of wave phenomena and provide specific examples of how the classical wave model allows for quantitative predictions.

**1. Evidence Supporting the Wave Model**
The classical wave model is supported by phenomena that demonstrate superposition and interference, which are unique to waves:
*   **Interference (Young’s Double Slit Experiment):** When coherent light passes through two closely spaced slits, it creates a pattern of alternating bright and dark fringes on a screen. This is due to constructive and destructive interference, where light waves overlap and superimpose.
*   **Diffraction:** Light waves bend around corners or through narrow apertures. The degree of spreading depends on the ratio of the wavelength to the size of the aperture ($\frac{\lambda}{w}$), which is consistent with the wave model.
*   **Polarization:** The fact that light can be polarized (e.g., using polaroid filters) confirms that light is a transverse wave. Longitudinal waves cannot be polarized.

**2. Predictive Power of the Model**
The classical wave model allows for precise mathematical predictions:
*   **Double Slit Interference:** The position of the bright fringes can be predicted using the formula:
    $$d \sin \theta = n\lambda$$
    where $d$ is the slit separation, $\theta$ is the angle of the fringe, $n$ is an integer (order), and $\lambda$ is the wavelength.
*   **Diffraction Limits:** The angular resolution of optical instruments can be predicted using the Rayleigh criterion:
    $$\sin \theta \approx 1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}$$
    where $D$ is the diameter of the aperture. This predicts how diffraction limits the clarity of images.
*   **Maxwell’s Equations:** James Clerk Maxwell successfully predicted that light is an electromagnetic wave travelling at a speed $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$. This linked electricity, magnetism, and optics, providing a robust theoretical framework for predicting light propagation.
