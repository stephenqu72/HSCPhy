To determine the mass of Mars, we use the formula for gravitational field strength $g$ at a distance $r$ from the centre of a planet:

$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

Where:
- $G$ is the universal gravitational constant, approximately $6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$
- $M$ is the mass of the planet
- $r$ is the distance from the centre

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Extract data from the graph:**
    Pick a clear coordinate point from the graph to calculate $M$. Looking at the graph, at $r = 4 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$, the gravitational field strength is $g = 2.5 \, \text{m s}^{-2}$.
    *(Note: You can verify this by checking another point, such as $r = 3 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$ where $g \approx 4.4 \, \text{m s}^{-2}$)*.

2.  **Rearrange the formula to solve for $M$:**
    $$M = \frac{g \cdot r^2}{G}$$

3.  **Substitute the values:**
    $$M = \frac{2.5 \times (4 \times 10^6)^2}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}}$$
    $$M = \frac{2.5 \times (16 \times 10^{12})}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}}$$
    $$M = \frac{40 \times 10^{12}}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}}$$
    $$M \approx 5.997 \times 10^{23} \, \text{kg}$$

4.  **Compare with the options:**
    The calculated value is approximately $6 \times 10^{23} \, \text{kg}$, which corresponds to option **C**.
