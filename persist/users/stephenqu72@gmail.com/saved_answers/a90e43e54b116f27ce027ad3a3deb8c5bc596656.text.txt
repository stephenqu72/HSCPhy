### (a) Procedure to collect data
To obtain an accurate value for $g$, you need to collect data for various lengths ($L$) and their corresponding periods ($T$).

1.  **Set up the apparatus:** Attach a string of length $L$ to a fixed support (a retort stand) and attach a pendulum bob to the other end. Use a metre ruler to measure the length $L$ from the pivot point $P$ to the centre of mass of the bob.
2.  **Release and Timing:** Displace the pendulum to a small angle (less than $10^\circ$) to ensure simple harmonic motion conditions are met. Release the bob and use a stopwatch to measure the time taken for 10 or 20 complete oscillations.
3.  **Calculate Period:** Divide the total time by the number of oscillations to find the period $T$ for that specific length. Repeating the count for multiple oscillations reduces human reaction time error.
4.  **Repeat:** Vary the length $L$ of the pendulum (e.g., in increments of $0.1\text{ m}$) and repeat steps 1–3 for at least 5 different lengths to provide enough data points for a reliable line of best fit.

### (b) Graphical analysis
To find $g$ graphically, we start with the given formula:
$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
*(Note: The formula provided in the image implies $T \propto \sqrt{L/g}$; as an HSC student, you must include the $2\pi$ constant for the standard period formula of a simple pendulum).*

Squaring both sides gives:
$$T^2 = \left( \frac{4\pi^2}{g} \right) L$$

This is in the form of a linear equation $y = mx + c$:
*   **$y$-axis:** Plot $T^2$ ($\text{s}^2$)
*   **$x$-axis:** Plot $L$ ($\text{m}$)
*   **Gradient ($m$):** The slope of the line of best fit will be equal to $\frac{4\pi^2}{g}$.

Therefore, you can calculate the experimental value for gravity by rearranging:
$$g = \frac{4\pi^2}{\text{gradient}}$$
