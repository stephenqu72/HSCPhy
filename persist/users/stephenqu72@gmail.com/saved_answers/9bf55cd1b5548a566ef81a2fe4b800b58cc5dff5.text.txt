### (b) Estimating Displacement at Minimum Velocity
In projectile motion, horizontal velocity ($v_x$) is constant (ignoring air resistance), so the total velocity is at a minimum when the vertical component of velocity ($v_y$) is **zero**.
*   **Physics Principle:** When $v_y = 0$, the projectile is at its **maximum height**.
*   **Estimation:** Looking at your plotted points, the peak height occurs between $t = 2s$ ($58m$) and $t = 3s$ ($55m$).
*   If you draw a smooth curve, the peak of your graph should align with a horizontal displacement of approximately **$25-30$ meters**. Looking at the symmetry of the data, the vertex is closest to the point $(30, 58)$.

### (c) Explaining the Parabola
The path of a projectile is parabolic because of the independence of horizontal and vertical motion:
1.  **Horizontal motion:** The horizontal velocity $v_x$ is constant because there is no horizontal acceleration ($a_x = 0$). Thus, $x = v_x t$.
2.  **Vertical motion:** There is a constant vertical acceleration due to gravity ($a_y = -g$), resulting in a displacement equation $y = v_{0y}t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$.
3.  **Combination:** If you rearrange the horizontal equation for time ($t = \frac{x}{v_x}$) and substitute it into the vertical equation, you get an equation of the form $y = Ax - Bx^2$, which is the mathematical definition of a downward-opening parabola.
