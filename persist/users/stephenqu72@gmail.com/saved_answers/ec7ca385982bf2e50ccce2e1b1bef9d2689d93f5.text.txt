To solve this problem, we apply the principles of circular motion and electromagnetism.

### (a) Derivation of the expression for velocity
When a charged particle moves through a magnetic field perpendicular to its velocity, it experiences a magnetic force that acts as a centripetal force, causing the particle to follow a circular path.

1. Set the magnetic force ($F_B$) equal to the centripetal force ($F_c$):
   $$F_B = F_c$$
2. Substitute the formulas $F_B = qvB$ and $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$:
   $$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
3. Rearrange to solve for velocity ($v$):
   $$qB = \frac{mv}{r}$$
   $$v = \frac{qBr}{m}$$

---

### (b) Calculation of the velocity
First, identify the known constants and variables:
- Charge of a proton, $q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}$
- Mass of a proton, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg}$
- Magnetic field strength, $B = 25.0 \, \text{mT} = 0.0250 \, \text{T}$
- Radius, $r = 0.280 \, \text{m}$

Using the derived formula:
$$v = \frac{(1.602 \times 10^{-19}) \times 0.0250 \times 0.280}{1.673 \times 10^{-27}}$$
$$v \approx 6.70 \times 10^5 \, \text{m/s}$$

---

### (c) Two limitations of applying special relativity
1. **Low Velocity:** Special relativity effects (such as time dilation or relativistic mass increase) become significant only as an object approaches the speed of light ($c$). At $6.70 \times 10^5 \, \text{m/s}$, the proton is traveling at approximately $0.2\%$ of the speed of light, making relativistic corrections negligible in this experimental context.
2. **Classical Mechanics Sufficiency:** Because the velocity is non-relativistic ($v \ll c$), the classical expression for momentum ($p = mv$) and the Newtonian analysis of circular motion remain accurate enough for this experimental setup, rendering the complexities of relativistic mechanics unnecessary.
