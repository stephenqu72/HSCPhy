To answer this question, we must look at the mathematical relationship between the Hubble constant ($H_0$) and the age of the universe ($t$).

### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) is older than our universe.**

### Explanation
1.  **The Relationship:** The Hubble constant $H_0$ represents the current rate of expansion of the universe. A simple estimation for the age of the universe (known as the Hubble time) is given by the reciprocal of the Hubble constant:
    $$t \approx \frac{1}{H_0}$$

2.  **Inverse Proportionality:** Because the age of the universe $t$ is inversely proportional to the Hubble constant $H_0$, a smaller value for $H_0$ results in a larger value for the calculated age of the universe.

3.  **Physical Interpretation:** If the expansion rate ($H_0$) is slower (smaller), it would have taken a longer amount of time for the universe to reach its current size from an initial singular point (the Big Bang). Therefore, a universe with a smaller Hubble constant must be older than one with a larger Hubble constant, assuming other cosmological parameters remain constant.

4.  **Eliminating other options:**
    *   (A) The Hubble constant relates to the expansion rate, not directly to the initial density required for star formation.
    *   (B) A smaller $H_0$ implies a slower expansion rate, which does not necessarily imply the matter is more "compacted."
    *   (C) The Hubble constant is a fundamental observation associated with Big Bang cosmology; changing its value does not negate the theory of the Big Bang itself.
