### Answer
The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
To understand this problem, we must use Einstein’s photoelectric equation, which relates the maximum kinetic energy ($K_{max}$) of emitted photoelectrons to the frequency of incident radiation ($f$):

$$K_{max} = hf - \Phi$$

Where:
*   $h$ is Planck's constant.
*   $\Phi$ is the work function of the metal (the minimum energy required to remove an electron).

This equation is in the form of a linear graph $y = mx + c$:
*   The **slope ($m$)** is Planck's constant ($h$), which remains the same for both lines $X$ and $Y$ because the gradient is identical.
*   The **$x$-intercept** represents the threshold frequency ($f_0$), where $hf_0 = \Phi$.
*   The **$y$-intercept** is $-\Phi$.

**Analysis:**
1.  **Line $Y$ has a higher $x$-intercept (threshold frequency):** Since $f_0 = \Phi / h$ and $h$ is constant, a higher threshold frequency directly implies a larger work function ($\Phi$).
2.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **A:** Increasing the frequency of radiation would move the data point along the existing line $X$, not create a new line $Y$.
    *   **B:** A metal with a **greater work function** requires more energy just to eject an electron. This shifts the threshold frequency to the right (higher frequency) and shifts the line downward, matching the behavior of line $Y$.
    *   **C:** Changing the intensity of the radiation does not change the maximum kinetic energy of individual electrons, so it would not shift the line at all.
    *   **D:** Decreasing the maximum energy is a consequence of the change, not the cause or the "change to the experiment" (the physical setup) that produces the result.

Therefore, changing the target metal to one with a higher work function is the correct physical modification.
