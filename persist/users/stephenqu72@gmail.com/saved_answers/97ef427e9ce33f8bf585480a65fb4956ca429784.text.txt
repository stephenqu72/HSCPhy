To solve this problem, we use Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation and the principles of circular motion.

### Constants needed:
*   Mass of satellite ($m$): $100\text{ kg}$
*   Mass of Earth ($M$): $5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$ (standard HSC reference value)
*   Radius of Earth ($R_E$): $6.371 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
*   Altitude ($h$): $1000\text{ km} = 1 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
*   Gravitational constant ($G$): $6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ N m}^2\text{ kg}^{-2}$
*   Orbital radius ($r = R_E + h$): $7.371 \times 10^6\text{ m}$

---

### Part a) Calculate the gravitational force
We use the formula: $$F = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

Substitute the values:
$$F = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times 100}{(7.371 \times 10^6)^2}$$
$$F = \frac{3.982 \times 10^{16}}{5.433 \times 10^{13}}$$
$$F \approx 733\text{ N}$$

**Explanation:** The gravitational force provides the centripetal force required to keep the satellite in orbit. Ensure you always use the total distance from the **centre** of the Earth ($r$), not just the altitude ($h$).

---

### Part b) Calculate the time taken for one revolution (Period $T$)
Since the gravitational force is the centripetal force:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Using $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, we rearrange to find $T$:
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}}$$

Substitute the values:
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (7.371 \times 10^6)^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24})}}$$
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{1.256 \times 10^{22}}{3.982 \times 10^{14}}}$$
$$T = \sqrt{3.155 \times 10^7} \approx 5617\text{ seconds}$$
Converting to minutes: $5617 / 60 \approx 93.6\text{ minutes}$.

**Explanation:** This is derived from equating the gravitational force formula to the centripetal force formula ($F_c = mr\omega^2$ or $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$). This is effectively Kepler's Third Law.
