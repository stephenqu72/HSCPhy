To achieve full marks on this 4-mark HSC Physics question, you must demonstrate a clear understanding of the physical difference between inertial and non-inertial frames. An inertial frame is one where Newton’s laws hold true (a frame moving with constant velocity), while a non-inertial frame is one that is accelerating (or rotating), resulting in "fictitious forces" being observed.

### Suggested Answer

**Apparatus:** A passenger vehicle (or a rotating platform/merry-go-round), a mass suspended on a string (a plumb bob), and a spirit level.

**Method:** 
1. Place the apparatus in an inertial frame of reference (a vehicle moving at constant velocity on a straight, level road). Observe the plumb bob hanging vertically.
2. Place the apparatus in a non-inertial frame of reference (the vehicle accelerating or turning). Observe the plumb bob.

**Results:**
- In the inertial frame, the plumb bob remains vertical as the only forces acting on it are gravity and tension, which sum to zero acceleration in the horizontal direction.
- In the non-inertial frame (e.g., during acceleration), the plumb bob swings backward. An observer in this frame perceives a "fictitious force" pushing the mass, even though no external force is acting in that direction.

**Conclusion:** 
Newton's first law holds in the inertial frame, where the object maintains its state of motion. In the non-inertial frame, the laws of physics appear violated because the object accelerates without a physical force being applied, proving that the frame itself is accelerating.
