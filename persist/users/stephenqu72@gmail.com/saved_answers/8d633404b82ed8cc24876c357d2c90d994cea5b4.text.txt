### Part (b): Determining $g$ and Assessing Accuracy
**1. Derivation:**
For a conical pendulum, the forces acting on the ball are gravity ($mg$ downwards) and tension ($T$). The vertical component of tension balances gravity:
$$T \cos(\theta) = mg$$
The horizontal component provides the centripetal force:
$$T \sin(\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Dividing the two equations:
$$\frac{T \sin(\theta)}{T \cos(\theta)} = \frac{mv^2/r}{mg}$$
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$
Rearranging to match the graph axes ($y = v^2$ and $x = r \tan(\theta)$):
$$v^2 = g(r \tan(\theta))$$
This is in the form $y = mx$, where the gradient ($m$) is equal to $g$.

**2. Calculating the gradient:**
Pick two points far apart on your line of best fit, $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$. 
$$\text{Gradient} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$
Using the data: $\frac{90.73 - 15.75}{9.23 - 1.59} \approx \frac{74.98}{7.64} \approx 9.81 \, \text{m/s}^2$.

**3. Assessing Accuracy:**
The standard value for acceleration due to gravity is approximately $9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2$.
