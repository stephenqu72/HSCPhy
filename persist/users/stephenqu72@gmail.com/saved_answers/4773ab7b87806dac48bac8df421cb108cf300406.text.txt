### Answer
**Newton’s Corpuscular Model:**
Newton proposed that light consists of a stream of tiny, fast-moving particles called "corpuscles." This model successfully explained:
*   **Rectilinear propagation:** Particles travel in straight lines until they hit an obstacle.
*   **Reflection:** Particles bounce off surfaces like billiard balls, obeying the law of reflection ($\theta_i = \theta_r$).
*   **Refraction:** Newton suggested light travelled faster in denser media due to an attractive force, which correctly explained the bending toward the normal.

**Huygens’ Wave Model:**
Huygens proposed that light is a longitudinal wave propagating through an all-encompassing medium called the "luminiferous aether." This model explained:
*   **Reflection and Refraction:** These were explained via wavefronts. Unlike Newton, Huygens correctly predicted that light slows down in a denser medium.
*   **Partial reflection/refraction:** Newton struggled to explain why some light reflects and some refracts at an interface, whereas the wave model easily accounted for this.

**Experimental Evidence and Analysis:**
*   **Newton's Support:** At the time, the observation that light travels in straight lines and the inability to observe diffraction (due to the small wavelength of light) gave his particle model dominance.
*   **The Conflict:** The models competed until the early 19th century. Newton’s model was eventually superseded because it predicted that light speed increases in dense media, which was later proven false by Foucault. Furthermore, the wave model provided a superior explanation for phenomena such as double refraction in calcite crystals and eventually, interference and diffraction patterns (Young’s Double Slit Experiment), which particles could not account for.

    *   To get full marks, you must discuss the *specific* phenomena explained by each (reflection/refraction) and identify the turning point—the failure of the corpuscular model to correctly predict light speed in dense media compared to the wave model.
4.  **Key Terminology:** Ensure you use terms like "rectilinear propagation," "luminiferous aether," "longitudinal waves," and "diffraction."
