### Answer

The debate between the nature of light was a pivotal period in the history of physics, characterized by two competing models.

**1. Newton's Corpuscular Model:**
Isaac Newton proposed the **corpuscular theory**, suggesting that light consists of streams of tiny, massless particles called "corpuscles" emitted from a source. 
*   **Strengths:** This model successfully explained rectilinear propagation (shadows), reflection, and the refraction of light (Newton argued particles accelerated upon entering a denser medium).
*   **Weaknesses:** It struggled to explain simultaneous reflection and refraction, and Newton had to contrive complex "fits of easy reflection and transmission" to account for thin-film interference.

**2. Huygens' Wave Model:**
Christiaan Huygens proposed that light was a longitudinal wave propagating through a pervasive, invisible medium called the "luminiferous aether."
*   **Strengths:** Huygens' principle provided a geometric explanation for reflection and refraction (based on the change in wave speed).
*   **Weaknesses:** At the time, waves were known to bend around corners (diffraction), whereas light was observed to travel in straight lines. Because light showed no obvious diffraction, and the wave model could not explain polarization, Newton’s corpuscular theory remained dominant for over a century due to his immense scientific reputation.

**3. Experimental Evidence for the Wave Model:**
The wave model was eventually validated through key experiments that could not be explained by particles:
*   **Young’s Double Slit Experiment (1801):** Thomas Young demonstrated interference patterns. When light passed through two narrow slits, the overlapping wavefronts created constructive and destructive interference (bright and dark fringes). This confirmed the wave nature of light, as particles would simply create two bright patches.
*   **Foucault’s Speed of Light Experiment (1850):** Leon Foucault measured the speed of light in water and found it to be slower than in air. This contradicted Newton’s prediction (that particles should speed up in denser media) and provided definitive support for the wave theory.
*   **Maxwell’s Equations (1860s):** James Clerk Maxwell mathematically unified electricity and magnetism, identifying light as an electromagnetic wave, which provided the theoretical framework that superseded the need for a mechanical aether.
