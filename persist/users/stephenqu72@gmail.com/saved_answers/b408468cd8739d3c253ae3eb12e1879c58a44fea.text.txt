To solve this problem, we must understand the definition of **work** and the nature of the forces acting on the car.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) 0 J**.

### Explanation
Work done ($W$) is defined by the formula:
$$W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(\theta)$$
where $F$ is the magnitude of the force, $d$ is the magnitude of the displacement, and $\theta$ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector.

1.  **Identify the force:** For a car moving in a horizontal circular path at a constant speed, the force providing the centripetal acceleration is the static friction between the tires and the track. This frictional force $F_f$ always acts **radially inward** toward the center of the circle.
2.  **Identify the displacement:** The velocity vector of the car is always **tangential** to the circular path. Over any infinitesimal interval of time, the displacement vector $d$ is also tangential to the path.
3.  **Determine the angle:** Since the force (radially inward) and the displacement (tangential) are always perpendicular to each other, the angle $\theta$ is always $90^\circ$.
4.  **Calculate the work:**
    $$\cos(90^\circ) = 0$$
    Therefore, the work done by the centripetal frictional force at any given moment is:
    $$W = F_f \cdot d \cdot 0 = 0 \text{ J}$$
5.  **Conclusion:** Since the work done at every point along the circular path is zero, the total work done over one complete revolution is the sum (or integral) of these zeros, which is $0 \text{ J}$.

*Note: Even though there is friction involved, because the speed is constant and the displacement is perpendicular to the centripetal force, no work is done by the centripetal force itself.*
