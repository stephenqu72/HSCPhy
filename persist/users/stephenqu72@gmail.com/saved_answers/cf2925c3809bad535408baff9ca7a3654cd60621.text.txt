### Answer
Yes, the spaceship is in an inertial frame of reference. An inertial frame of reference is defined as a frame that is not accelerating; because the spaceship is travelling at a **constant speed** in a **constant direction** (implied by "constant speed" in the context of special relativity problems unless otherwise specified), its velocity is constant. According to Newton’s First Law, an object with constant velocity has zero net force acting upon it, confirming it is an inertial frame.

### Explanation
To solve this, you need to apply the definition of an inertial frame from the HSC Physics syllabus:

1.  **Recall the definition:** An inertial frame of reference is a coordinate system that is not undergoing acceleration. This means the object is either stationary or moving at a constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line).
2.  **Analyze the prompt:** The question states the spaceship is travelling at a "constant speed." In the context of physics problems regarding inertial frames, this implies constant velocity.
3.  **Link to Newton's Laws:** Since the spaceship has a constant velocity, its acceleration is zero ($a = 0$). By Newton's First Law, if there is no net external force acting on the object to change its state of motion, it remains in an inertial frame. 
4.  **Justification:** You must explicitly mention that the lack of acceleration confirms the frame is inertial. If the ship were turning (changing direction), it would be accelerating (centripetal acceleration), and thus would *not* be an inertial frame, even if the speed remained constant. Therefore, the "constant speed" condition satisfies the requirement for an inertial frame of reference.
