### Answer
The correct answer is **B. Cathode rays possess momentum.**

### Explanation
This apparatus is a **Maltese Cross tube**, a classic piece of historical equipment used to study the properties of cathode rays. Let’s evaluate each option based on the observations made with this device:

*   **C. Cathode rays travel in straight lines:** This is the primary observation. The Maltese cross acts as an obstacle, and because it casts a sharp, well-defined shadow on the end of the tube, it demonstrates that cathode rays travel in straight lines (rectilinear propagation).
*   **D. Cathode rays cannot pass through metals:** Because the metal cross creates a shadow, we can conclude that the rays are blocked by the metal. This confirms that they cannot pass through it.
*   **A. Cathode rays possess energy:** When cathode rays strike the glass wall of the tube, they cause fluorescence (a glow). This indicates that the rays transfer energy to the glass atoms, causing them to emit light.
*   **B. Cathode rays possess momentum:** While cathode rays *do* possess momentum (as demonstrated by J.J. Thomson’s later experiments involving paddle wheels in cathode ray tubes), **this specific apparatus** does not demonstrate it. The Maltese cross tube only shows the shadow and the fluorescence; it does not show mechanical motion or force exerted on an object that would be required to infer momentum.

Since the question asks which statement **CANNOT** be inferred from this specific apparatus, B is the correct choice.
