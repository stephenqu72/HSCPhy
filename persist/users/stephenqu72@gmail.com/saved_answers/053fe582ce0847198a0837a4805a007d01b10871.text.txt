The correct diagram is **(B)**.

### Explanation
1.  **Initial State:** The spacecraft is in a stable circular orbit of radius $R$ with a specific orbital velocity $v$. In this state, the gravitational force provides exactly the centripetal force required to maintain the circular path:
    $$\frac{GMm}{R^2} = \frac{mv^2}{R}$$
2.  **The Impulse:** When the engines fire at point $P$ to increase the speed, the spacecraft's kinetic energy increases. Since the gravitational potential energy remains the same at point $P$, the total mechanical energy of the satellite increases. 
3.  **Orbital Mechanics:** Because the velocity at point $P$ is now greater than the required circular orbital velocity for radius $R$, the spacecraft can no longer maintain a circular orbit at that radius. Instead, it enters an elliptical orbit. 
4.  **Geometry of the new orbit:** Since the speed increase occurs at point $P$, $P$ becomes the **perigee** (the point closest to the Earth) of the new elliptical orbit. Because the spacecraft is moving faster than is required for a circular orbit, it will "climb" away from Earth, reaching a higher altitude at the opposite side of the orbit (the **apogee**). 
5.  **Analyzing the options:**
    *   Diagram (A) suggests a smaller circular orbit, which would require a *decrease* in speed.
    *   Diagram (B) shows an elliptical orbit where point $P$ is the closest point to the Earth. This correctly matches the physics of adding energy at a specific point in a circular orbit.
    *   Diagram (C) shows an orbit that is still circular but larger, which would require a sustained acceleration rather than a short burst.
    *   Diagram (D) shows the orbit shifting entirely away from point $P$, which violates the conservation of energy and angular momentum.

Therefore, the spacecraft enters an elliptical orbit where point $P$ is the perigee, as shown in **(B)**.
