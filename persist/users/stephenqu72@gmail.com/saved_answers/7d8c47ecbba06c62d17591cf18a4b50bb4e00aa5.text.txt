To solve this problem, we must apply the physics of circular motion. When a car safely negotiates a corner, the centripetal force required to keep it in the circular path is provided by the static friction between the tires and the road.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the governing equation:** 
    The centripetal force ($F_c$) required for a car to move in a circle of radius $r$ at velocity $v$ is given by:
    $$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    For the car to safely negotiate the corner, this force is provided by the maximum static friction, $F_f = \mu mg$. Thus:
    $$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \mu mg$$
    By simplifying, we see that the maximum safe velocity $v$ is proportional to the square root of the radius:
    $$v^2 \propto r \implies v \propto \sqrt{r}$$

2.  **Set up the ratio:**
    Let $v_1$ be the initial velocity $v$ at radius $r_1 = r$.
    Let $v_2$ be the new velocity at radius $r_2 = \frac{r}{2}$.
    Using the proportional relationship $v \propto \sqrt{r}$, we can write:
    $$\frac{v_2}{v_1} = \sqrt{\frac{r_2}{r_1}}$$

3.  **Substitute the known values:**
    $$\frac{v_2}{v} = \sqrt{\frac{r/2}{r}}$$
    $$\frac{v_2}{v} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}}$$
    $$\frac{v_2}{v} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

4.  **Solve for $v_2$:**
    $$v_2 = \frac{v}{\sqrt{2}}$$

Comparing this to the given options, the correct answer is **(D)**.

### Answer:
