### Part (a): Outline a thought experiment for time dilation
**Suggested Answer:**
The most classic thought experiment is the **Light Clock**. Consider a light clock consisting of two mirrors facing each other with a photon bouncing between them. 
1. For an observer moving with the clock, the photon travels a vertical distance $2L$ at speed $c$. 
2. For a stationary observer watching the clock move at velocity $v$, the photon follows a diagonal path. 
3. Because the speed of light $c$ is constant for all observers, the light must travel a greater distance over the diagonal path in the same interval. Consequently, the stationary observer concludes that the "tick" of the moving clock takes longer than the "tick" of their own stationary clock, thus demonstrating time dilation.

**How to solve it:**
*   **Key concept:** You must explain the *mechanism* (why time dilates).
*   **Focus:** Emphasize the second postulate of Special Relativity: the constancy of the speed of light ($c$).
*   **Structure:** Define the setup, describe the two frames of reference (moving vs. stationary), and state the logical conclusion based on $distance = speed \times time$.

### Part (b): Outline experimental evidence for time dilation
**Suggested Answer:**
The most widely cited evidence is the **Hafele-Keating experiment (1971)** or the observation of **muon decay**. Using muons is a clearer explanation:
1. Muons are subatomic particles created in the upper atmosphere with a very short half-life. 
2. Classically, given their speed, they should decay long before reaching the Earth's surface. 
3. However, detectors on the ground observe a significant number of muons arriving. 
4. This validates time dilation: because the muons are moving at relativistic speeds relative to Earth, their "internal clocks" run slower, meaning they survive long enough to travel the distance to the surface, despite their short half-life in their own reference frame.

**How to solve it:**
*   **Key concept:** Provide concrete evidence that translates theoretical prediction into real-world observation.
*   **Structure:** Identify the experiment, explain what happens in the "stationary" frame (ground), explain what happens in the "moving" frame (the particle), and conclude by linking the results to the theory of relativity.
