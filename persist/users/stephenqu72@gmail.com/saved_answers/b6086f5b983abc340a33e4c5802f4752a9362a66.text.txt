### Answer

Scientific progress occurs through a cycle where new experimental evidence challenges existing models, necessitating refinements that better align with physical principles.

**1. Thomson’s Plum Pudding Model:**
J.J. Thomson, following his discovery of the electron using cathode ray tubes, proposed the "Plum Pudding" model. He observed that electrons were negatively charged particles and assumed they were embedded in a sphere of uniform positive charge. This model was based on the principle of electrostatic neutrality and the assumption that the positive charge occupied the entire volume of the atom.

**2. Rutherford’s Alpha-Scattering Experiment:**
Rutherford’s team (Geiger and Marsden) tested this model by firing alpha particles at a thin gold foil. According to Thomson’s model, the alpha particles should have passed through with minimal deflection. However, experimental observations showed that while most passed through, some were deflected at large angles, and a small fraction reflected directly backward.

**3. The Shift in Model:**
Rutherford concluded that Thomson’s model could not account for such significant electrostatic repulsion. He applied the physical principle of Coulombic interaction, reasoning that the positive charge could not be spread out. He proposed the **nuclear model**: a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center, with electrons orbiting in the vast empty space surrounding it. This model reconciled the observations of high-angle scattering with the physics of electrostatic force.

**Conclusion:**
This transition illustrates that scientific models are not static truths but dynamic representations refined by experimental anomalies. When observations contradict current theories, scientists apply fundamental laws (like electrostatics) to formulate new, more accurate models, demonstrating that scientific knowledge is tentative and iterative.
