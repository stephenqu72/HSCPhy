To answer this question, we must look at the definition of a **fundamental particle** within the context of the Standard Model of particle physics.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
1.  **Define a Fundamental Particle:** In the Standard Model, a fundamental particle (or elementary particle) is a particle that has no known substructure; it is not composed of any smaller particles. Examples include quarks, leptons (like electrons), and gauge bosons.
2.  **Analyze the Proton:** A proton is classified as a **hadron**, specifically a baryon. Experiments, such as deep inelastic scattering (firing high-energy electrons at protons), have shown that protons are composed of smaller constituent particles called **quarks** (specifically two "up" quarks and one "down" quark, held together by gluons).
3.  **Evaluate the Options:**
    *   (A) Protons are extremely stable particles; they are not observed to decay spontaneously into other particles in standard models.
    *   (B) This is incorrect. Several fundamental particles, such as electrons and quarks, carry electric charge.
    *   **(C) This is correct.** Because protons have a substructure (they are composed of quarks), they can be "broken apart" (or more accurately, the quarks can be probed and interaction energy can be converted into new particles) when subjected to extremely high-energy collisions in particle accelerators. This experimental evidence confirms that they are composite, not fundamental.
    *   (D) This is incorrect. Protons are fermions (baryons), not bosons.
