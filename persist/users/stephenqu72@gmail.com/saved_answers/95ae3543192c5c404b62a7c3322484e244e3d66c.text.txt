To solve this question, we must contrast the wave-based classical model of light with the particle-based quantum model (Einstein’s photon theory) regarding the photoelectric effect.

### Answer

**(a) Classical Predictions:**
1. The maximum kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons should increase with the intensity (flux) of the incident light.
2. Photoelectric emission should occur for any frequency of light, provided the light is bright enough (intense enough) to transfer sufficient energy to electrons over time.

**(b) Experimental Observations:**
1. The maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons is independent of the light flux (as seen in graph (a), where the stopping voltage $V_0$ is the same for all intensities).
2. Photoelectric emission only occurs if the light frequency is above a specific threshold frequency ($f_0$). Below this frequency, no electrons are emitted, regardless of how intense the light is.

**(c) Quantum Explanations:**
1. According to Einstein, light consists of discrete quanta (photons) with energy $E = hf$. Since one photon interacts with one electron, the kinetic energy $K_{max} = hf - W$ depends only on the frequency $f$ and the work function $W$, not on the number of photons (flux).
2. A single photon must possess energy $E > W$ to overcome the binding energy of the electron. If $hf < W$, the electron cannot escape, regardless of the number of photons hitting the surface simultaneously.
