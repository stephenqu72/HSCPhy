To solve this problem, we apply Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$.

### Explanation
1.  **Identify the particles:** An electron ($e^-$) and a positron ($e^+$) have the same rest mass, denoted as $m_e$.
2.  **Recall the rest mass energy:** The rest mass of an electron is approximately $0.511 \text{ MeV/c}^2$. Therefore, the energy equivalent of its mass is $0.511 \text{ MeV}$.
3.  **Annihilation process:** When an electron and a positron annihilate, their combined mass is converted entirely into energy (typically as gamma-ray photons).
4.  **Calculate total energy:**
    *   Mass of electron = $0.511 \text{ MeV/c}^2$
    *   Mass of positron = $0.511 \text{ MeV/c}^2$
    *   Total mass involved = $0.511 + 0.511 = 1.022 \text{ MeV/c}^2$ (or approximately $1.023 \text{ MeV/c}^2$ depending on the precision of the mass constant used in your data sheet).
5.  **Conclusion:** Using $E = mc^2$, the energy released is:
    $$E = (1.022 \dots) \text{ MeV}$$
