### Model Answer

The precision required for GPS satellite orbits relies on the application of celestial mechanics, specifically Kepler’s Laws and the principles of orbital energy.

**1. Predicting Orbits using Kepler’s Laws:**
Kepler’s Laws provide the mathematical framework for orbital prediction. Kepler’s Third Law, $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$, shows that for a satellite orbiting a central body (Earth), the period $T$ is uniquely determined by the orbital radius $r$. By measuring the time it takes for a GPS satellite to complete one orbit, ground stations can determine its precise altitude and orbital path. Furthermore, Kepler’s First Law defines the orbit as an ellipse, which helps engineers predict the satellite's varying speed and distance throughout its cycle.

**2. Orbital Energy and Maintenance:**
The total energy $E$ of a satellite in a circular orbit is given by $E = K + U = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$.
*   **Energy and Altitude:** This equation shows that total energy is negative and inversely proportional to radius. To move to a higher orbit, a satellite must gain energy (increase its total energy $E$).
*   **The Problem of Drag:** In low orbits, atmospheric drag removes kinetic energy from the satellite. As $E$ decreases, $r$ must decrease to satisfy the energy conservation equation, causing the satellite to "decay" into a lower orbit.
*   **Station Keeping:** To maintain a precise orbit, satellites use onboard thrusters to perform "station-keeping" maneuvers. By adjusting their velocity, they change their kinetic energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$), which consequently adjusts the total orbital energy and radius, correcting any drift caused by external perturbations like solar radiation pressure or gravitational pull from the Moon and Sun.

**Conclusion:** 
By monitoring orbital periods (Kepler’s laws) and managing the satellite's mechanical energy through thrusters, engineers can counteract perturbations. This ensures that the time-of-flight measurements, critical for GPS trilateration, remain accurate.

### How to solve this:
1.  **Deconstruct the prompt:** It asks for two distinct things: how to *predict* (Kepler's Laws) and how to *maintain* (Total Energy). Structure your essay to address these separately for clarity.
2.  **Use Physics Definitions:** Explicitly state the relevant formulas ($E = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$ and $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \text{const}$). HSC markers look for accurate mathematical relationships.
3.  **Link to the Context:** Always bring it back to GPS. Mentioning that GPS relies on precise time-of-flight measurements explains *why* this precision matters, which elevates your response to the "discuss" level.
4.  **Logical Flow:** Ensure you explain the "why." Why does drag lead to a lower orbit? Because it removes energy, and a smaller $r$ is required to maintain the balance of the orbital energy equation.
