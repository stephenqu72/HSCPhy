To solve these physics problems, we must relate circular motion dynamics to the provided parameters.

### Part (a): Derivation
To show that $r = \frac{T^2 F_g}{4\pi^2 m_s}$, we start with the centripetal force equation for a satellite in a circular orbit:
1. The gravitational force provides the necessary centripetal force:
   $$F_g = \frac{m_s v^2}{r}$$
2. The velocity $v$ for a circular orbit is the circumference divided by the orbital period:
   $$v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$$
3. Substitute the expression for $v$ into the force equation:
   $$F_g = \frac{m_s}{r} \left( \frac{2\pi r}{T} \right)^2$$
   $$F_g = \frac{m_s}{r} \left( \frac{4\pi^2 r^2}{T^2} \right)$$
   $$F_g = \frac{4\pi^2 m_s r}{T^2}$$
4. Rearrange the formula to solve for $r$:
   $$r = \frac{F_g T^2}{4\pi^2 m_s}$$

### Part (b): Calculation of Orbital Radius
Given: $F_g = 80\text{ N}$, $m_s = 50\text{ kg}$, $T = 2.16 \times 10^4\text{ s}$.
Using the formula derived in part (a):
$$r = \frac{80 \times (2.16 \times 10^4)^2}{4 \times \pi^2 \times 50}$$
$$r = \frac{80 \times 466,560,000}{1,973.92}$$
$$r \approx 18,914,818\text{ m} \approx 1.89 \times 10^7\text{ m}$$

### Part (c): Kinetic Energy
Kinetic energy is defined as $E_k = \frac{1}{2} m_s v^2$.
1. First, find the velocity $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$:
   $$v = \frac{2 \times \pi \times 18,914,818}{21,600} \approx 5,503\text{ m/s}$$
2. Calculate $E_k$:
   $$E_k = \frac{1}{2} \times 50 \times (5,503)^2$$
   $$E_k = 25 \times 30,283,009 \approx 7.57 \times 10^8\text{ J}$$
