### Answer
The detection of muons at the Earth's surface serves as experimental evidence for **time dilation**, a key consequence of Einstein’s theory of Special Relativity.

1.  **The Classical Expectation:** Muons have an extremely short half-life (approximately $2.2 \times 10^{-6}$ s) when measured in their own rest frame. Travelling at relativistic speeds (close to the speed of light, $c$), classical physics predicts that these particles should decay long before they can travel the distance from the upper atmosphere to the Earth’s surface.
2.  **The Relativistic Reality:** Despite their short lifespan, a significant number of muons are detected at ground level.
3.  **Explanation via Special Relativity:** From the perspective of an observer on Earth, the muons are travelling at speeds approaching $c$. Due to their high velocity, the muons experience time dilation, meaning their "internal clock" runs slower relative to an Earth-based observer. As a result, the muons’ mean lifetime is extended by the Lorentz factor, $\gamma$, where $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$. This increased lifetime allows them to travel the necessary distance to reach the Earth's surface before decaying.
4.  **Conclusion:** The fact that we detect these particles at the surface—which would be impossible under classical Newtonian physics—confirms that time is not absolute and validates the phenomenon of time dilation as predicted by Special Relativity.
