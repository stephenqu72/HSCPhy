### Answer
This experiment is the **Geiger-Marsden experiment** (often referred to as the **Rutherford Gold Foil Experiment**).

**Background:**
At the time, the prevailing model of the atom was J.J. Thomson’s "Plum Pudding" model, which suggested that the atom was a sphere of diffuse positive charge with negatively charged electrons embedded throughout. Scientists believed alpha particles, being relatively heavy and fast, would pass through such a "soft" atom with only minor deflections.

**Results:**
Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden, under the direction of Ernest Rutherford, bombarded a thin sheet of gold foil with alpha particles. While most particles passed straight through, they observed that:
*   Most alpha particles were undeflected.
*   A small fraction of alpha particles were deflected at large angles.
*   A very small number (about 1 in 8,000) were deflected backwards, returning towards the source.

**Proposed Model:**
Rutherford concluded that the Plum Pudding model was incorrect. He proposed a new model (the **Nuclear Model**) based on these findings:
1.  **Dense Nucleus:** The atom contains a tiny, extremely dense, positively charged center called the nucleus, which contains almost all of the atom's mass.
2.  **Empty Space:** Because most alpha particles passed through, the majority of the atom must be empty space.
3.  **Electrostatic Repulsion:** The large-angle deflections were caused by the intense electrostatic repulsion between the positive alpha particles and the positive central nucleus.
