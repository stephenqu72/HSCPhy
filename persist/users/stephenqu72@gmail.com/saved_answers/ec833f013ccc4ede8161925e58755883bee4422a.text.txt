This is an 8-mark "Evaluate" question, which requires you to make a judgment based on criteria and provide evidence to support that judgment. To score full marks, you must address both parts of the prompt: Einstein's contribution and the experimental evidence for the constancy of $c$.

### Sample Answer

Albert Einstein’s contribution to special relativity (1905) was revolutionary because he elevated the principle of relativity to a fundamental postulate, while simultaneously declaring the speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) to be an absolute constant for all inertial observers. Prior to Einstein, the physics community struggled with the "luminiferous ether" hypothesis and the implications of Maxwell’s equations, which suggested $c$ was constant. Einstein’s genius was his willingness to discard the ether and accept the consequences—time dilation, length contraction, and the relativity of simultaneity—rather than trying to force light to obey Galilean transformations.

The experimental evidence confirming this postulate is robust:

1.  **Michelson-Morley Experiment (1887):** Though conducted before 1905, this experiment provided the null result that failed to detect the "ether wind." It demonstrated that the speed of light did not vary regardless of the Earth's motion through space, providing the empirical foundation upon which Einstein built his theory.
2.  **Kennedy-Thorndike Experiment (1932):** This refined the Michelson-Morley setup by comparing the speed of light in different inertial frames over time as the Earth moved in its orbit. It confirmed that the round-trip speed of light is isotropic and invariant, reinforcing Einstein's postulate.
3.  **Ives-Stilwell Experiment (1938):** This provided direct evidence of time dilation. By observing the Doppler shift in light emitted from fast-moving ions, researchers confirmed the relativistic time dilation predicted by Einstein’s equations, which relies entirely on the constancy of $c$.
4.  **Modern Particle Accelerators:** In cyclotrons and synchrotrons, subatomic particles like protons are accelerated to speeds very close to $c$. Experiments consistently show that these particles do not exceed $c$ even as more energy is added, and their increased lifetimes (due to time dilation) perfectly match the predictions of special relativity, validating the absolute nature of the speed limit $c$.

In conclusion, Einstein’s contribution transformed physics by shifting the paradigm from absolute space and time to a universe where the speed of light is the invariant foundation. The evidence, from early interferometry to modern particle physics, consistently upholds this postulate, making it one of the most rigorously tested principles in science.

### How to solve this question
1.  **Break it down:** The question has two distinct parts. Spend roughly 4 marks on Einstein’s theoretical leap and 4 marks on the evidence.
2.  **Evaluate:** Use the word "evaluate." You are not just listing facts; you are judging the *validity* and *impact* of his work.
3.  **Keywords:** Use terms like "luminiferous ether," "inertial frames," "Galilean transformations," and "null result." 
4.  **Structure:** 
    *   **Introduction:** State Einstein's radical break from Newtonian mechanics.
    *   **Body Paragraphs:** Detail specific experiments (Michelson-Morley, Ives-Stilwell). Do not just name them; explain *what* they proved regarding $c$.
    *   **Conclusion:** Summarize how the theory and evidence align to form the current scientific consensus.
