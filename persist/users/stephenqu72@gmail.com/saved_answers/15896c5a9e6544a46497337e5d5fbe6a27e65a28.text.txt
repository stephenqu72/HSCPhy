### Answer to Question 5
The correct answer is **A**.

**Explanation:**
This question tests your understanding of **Faraday’s Law of Induction** and **Lenz’s Law**.
*   **Before:** The magnetic field is constant and uniform. Faraday’s Law states that an electromotive force (EMF) is only induced when there is a *change* in magnetic flux ($\Delta\Phi_B$). Since the field is constant and the coil is stationary, the flux is constant, so no current is induced. The globe remains **Off**.
*   **During:** As the magnetic field is switched off, the magnetic flux through the coil decreases rapidly. According to Faraday’s Law, this change in flux induces an EMF in the coil, causing a current to flow through the circuit. The globe turns **On**.
*   **After:** Once the magnetic field is completely switched off, the magnetic flux is zero and no longer changing ($\Delta\Phi_B = 0$). With no change in flux, the induced EMF drops to zero. The current stops flowing, and the globe turns **Off**.

**Explanation:**
To calculate the magnitude of the magnetic flux ($\Phi_B$) through a single loop of the coil, we use the formula:
$$\Phi_B = B \cdot A \cdot \cos(\theta)$$
Given:
*   Magnetic field strength ($B$) = $0.2\text{ T}$
*   Radius ($r$) = $5\text{ cm} = 0.05\text{ m}$
*   The field is perpendicular to the area, meaning the angle between the field and the normal to the coil is $0^\circ$, so $\cos(0^\circ) = 1$.

First, calculate the area ($A$) of the circular coil:
$$A = \pi \cdot r^2 = \pi \cdot (0.05)^2 \approx 3.14159 \cdot 0.0025 \approx 0.00785\text{ m}^2$$

Now, calculate the flux for a single loop:
$$\Phi_B = 0.2 \cdot 0.00785 \approx 0.00157\text{ Wb}$$

Rounding to two significant figures, we get $0.0016\text{ Wb}$.
