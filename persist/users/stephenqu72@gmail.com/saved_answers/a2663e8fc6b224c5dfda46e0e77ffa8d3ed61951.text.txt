To determine the total energy of the satellite, we must consider both its gravitational potential energy and its kinetic energy.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the orbital radius:** The satellite orbits at an altitude $A$ above the Earth's surface. Since the Earth's radius is $R$, the total distance from the center of the Earth to the satellite is $r = R + A$.
2.  **Gravitational Potential Energy ($E_p$):** The potential energy of an object in a gravitational field is given by:
    $$E_p = -\frac{GMm}{r} = -\frac{GMm}{R+A}$$
3.  **Kinetic Energy ($E_k$):** For a stable circular orbit, the centripetal force is provided by gravity:
    $$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2} \implies mv^2 = \frac{GMm}{r}$$
    Since kinetic energy is defined as $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, we substitute the expression for $mv^2$:
    $$E_k = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{GMm}{r} \right) = \frac{GMm}{2r}$$
4.  **Total Energy ($E_{total}$):** The total energy is the sum of kinetic and potential energy:
    $$E_{total} = E_k + E_p = \frac{GMm}{2r} + \left( -\frac{GMm}{r} \right)$$
    $$E_{total} = \frac{GMm}{2r} - \frac{2GMm}{2r} = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$
5.  **Final Expression:** Substituting $r = R + A$ back into the result:
    $$E_{total} = -\frac{GMm}{2(R+A)}$$

Comparing this to the given options, it matches **(A)**.

### Answer:
The correct expression is **(A)**.
