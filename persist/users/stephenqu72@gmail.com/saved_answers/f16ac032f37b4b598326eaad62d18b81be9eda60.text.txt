### Solution

#### Part a) Free Body Diagram
To draw the forces on the mass $m$:
1.  **Gravity ($mg$):** A vertical vector pointing downwards.
2.  **Tension ($T$):** A vector pointing along the string toward the attachment point.
3.  **Net Force ($F_{net}$):** The sum of these two vectors is the centripetal force ($F_c$), which must point horizontally toward the center of the carousel. Draw this as a horizontal dotted arrow.

*Diagram construction:* The vector $T$ must be long enough such that its vertical component balances $mg$ ($T \cos \theta = mg$) and its horizontal component provides the centripetal force ($T \sin \theta = m\omega^2 R$).

#### Part b) Qualitative and Quantitative Explanation

**Qualitative:**
If the radius of rotation $R$ is halved to $r = \frac{1}{2}R$, the required centripetal force to maintain the same angular velocity $\omega$ decreases. Since $F_c = m\omega^2 r$, halving $r$ at a constant $\omega$ means the mass requires less horizontal force to maintain circular motion. Consequently, the angle $\theta$ will **decrease** (the string will hang more vertically).

**Quantitative:**
We equate the vertical and horizontal components of the forces:
1. Vertical: $T \cos \theta = mg$
2. Horizontal: $T \sin \theta = m\omega^2 r$

Dividing the two equations gives:
$$\tan \theta = \frac{m\omega^2 r}{mg} = \frac{\omega^2 r}{g}$$

Given that the angle $\theta$ is small, we use the small-angle approximation $\tan \theta \approx \theta$ (in radians):
$$\theta \approx \frac{\omega^2 r}{g}$$

Let the original angle be $\theta_1 = \frac{\omega^2 R}{g}$ and the new angle be $\theta_2 = \frac{\omega^2 (\frac{1}{2}R)}{g}$.
Comparing the two:
$$\theta_2 = \frac{1}{2} \theta_1$$

Thus, the angle $\theta$ decreases by exactly half.

### How to solve this type of problem
1.  **Identify the forces:** In circular motion problems, always isolate the object and identify the forces. Usually, it is gravity and tension.
2.  **Set up the equations:** Resolve forces into vertical and horizontal components. The vertical components must sum to zero (no vertical acceleration), and the horizontal components must sum to the centripetal force $m\omega^2 r$.
3.  **Use Trigonometry:** Divide the horizontal component by the vertical component to eliminate tension ($T$), allowing you to solve for $\theta$ using $\tan \theta$.
4.  **Apply approximations:** When the question states the angle is "small," use $\sin \theta \approx \tan \theta \approx \theta$. This significantly simplifies the relationship between the radius and the angle.
