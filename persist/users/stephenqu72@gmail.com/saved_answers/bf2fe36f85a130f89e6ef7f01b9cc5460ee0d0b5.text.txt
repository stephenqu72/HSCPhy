To solve this question, we need to understand the relationship between the Hubble constant ($H_0$) and the age of the universe.

### Explanation
The Hubble constant $H_0$ describes the rate at which the universe is expanding. A fundamental approximation for the age of the universe ($t$) derived from the Big Bang model is the inverse of the Hubble constant:

$$t \approx \frac{1}{H_0}$$

This relationship shows that the age of the universe is inversely proportional to the Hubble constant. 

*   If the Hubble constant $H_0$ is **smaller**, then the value of $\frac{1}{H_0}$ becomes **larger**.
*   Therefore, a smaller Hubble constant implies that the expansion has been occurring for a longer period of time, meaning the universe is older.

Let's evaluate the options:
*   (A) Density is related to the critical density and the Omega parameter, not directly determined by the value of $H_0$ alone in this context.
*   (B) A smaller $H_0$ suggests a slower expansion rate, but it does not inherently mean the universe is more "compacted."
*   (C) The expansion observed (Hubble's Law) is a core piece of evidence for the Big Bang; a change in the constant does not negate the Big Bang origin.
*   (D) As shown by the relationship $t \propto \frac{1}{H_0}$, a smaller $H_0$ corresponds to a greater age.

**Answer:** (D)
