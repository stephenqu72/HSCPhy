### Teacher's Solution and Explanation

#### Part (a): Calculating the charge of the oil droplet
To keep the droplet stationary, the upward electric force must equal the downward gravitational force:
$$F_E = F_g \implies qE = mg$$
Since $E = \frac{V}{d}$, we substitute to get:
$$q\left(\frac{V}{d}\right) = mg \implies q = \frac{mgd}{V}$$

**Given values:**
*   $m = 5.0 \times 10^{-15} \text{ kg}$
*   $g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$
*   $d = 20.0 \text{ mm} = 0.020 \text{ m}$
*   $V = 2041.7 \text{ V}$

**Calculation:**
$$q = \frac{(5.0 \times 10^{-15})(9.8)(0.020)}{2041.7} \approx 4.8 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$$

#### Part (b): Determining the elementary charge
To find the charge of a single electron, we look for the **Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)** of all the measured charges. Each measurement represents an integer multiple of the elementary charge ($q = n \times e$).

*   Values: $19.2, 12.8, 9.6, 6.4, 38.4, 16.0, 28.8$ (all $\times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$)
*   Observing the numbers, they are all multiples of $3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.
*   However, Millikan's experiment is intended to find the elementary charge $e \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$. 
*   Since $3.2 = 2 \times 1.6$, the data suggests the underlying quantum of charge is $1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.

**Justification:** The elementary charge is the smallest unit of charge that acts as a building block for all observed charges. Since all values provided are multiples of $3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$, and $3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} = 2 \times (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C})$, we conclude the fundamental value is $1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.
