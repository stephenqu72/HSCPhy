To solve this problem, we apply Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, which is given by the formula:

$$E = mc^2$$

Where:
*   $E$ is the energy released.
*   $m$ is the total mass being converted into energy.
*   $c$ is the speed of light, approximately $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the particles:** The question describes the annihilation of an **electron** and a **positron**. 
2.  **Determine the mass:** An electron has a mass of approximately $9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$. A positron is the antiparticle of an electron and has the exact same mass ($9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$).
3.  **Calculate the total mass:** Since both particles are destroyed, the total mass ($m$) involved in the annihilation is the sum of the mass of the electron and the positron:
    $$m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} + 9.1 \times 10^{-31} = 2 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$$
4.  **Apply the formula:** Substitute the total mass into Einstein’s equation $E = mc^2$:
    $$E = (2 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)^2 \text{ J}$$
