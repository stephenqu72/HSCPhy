To solve this question, we must understand the chronological development of atomic theory as covered in the HSC Physics curriculum (specifically the "From the Universe to the Atom" or "Ideas and Evidence" modules).

### Answer
The correct answer is **b) Rutherford > Bohr > De Broglie**.

### Explanation
To determine the correct order, we look at the historical timeline of these breakthroughs:

1.  **Rutherford Model (1911):** Following his gold foil experiment, Ernest Rutherford proposed the nuclear model of the atom, where a small, dense, positively charged nucleus is surrounded by electrons. This challenged the earlier "plum pudding" model.
2.  **Bohr Model (1913):** Niels Bohr refined Rutherford's model to explain why electrons did not spiral into the nucleus. He proposed that electrons exist in stable, quantized energy levels (or shells) and only emit radiation when jumping between these levels.
3.  **De Broglie Hypothesis (1924):** Louis de Broglie introduced the concept of wave-particle duality. He suggested that if light can act like a particle, matter (like electrons) can act like a wave. This provided the theoretical basis for why electrons occupy specific orbits, leading to the development of the quantum mechanical (Schrödinger) model where electrons exist in "standing waves" around the nucleus.

By comparing these dates:
*   $1911$ (Rutherford) comes before $1913$ (Bohr).
*   $1913$ (Bohr) comes before $1924$ (De Broglie).

Thus, the chronological order is Rutherford, then Bohr, then De Broglie. Option (b) is the only one that reflects this sequence.
