To solve this problem, we use the radioactive decay formula:
$$N(t) = N_0 \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{\frac{t}{T_{1/2}}}$$

Where:
- $N(t) = 1\text{ ng}$ (the final mass)
- $N_0 = 15\text{ ng}$ (the initial mass)
- $T_{1/2} = 22\text{ s}$ (the half-life)
- $t$ is the time elapsed.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Set up the equation:**
We want to find $t$ such that $1 = 15 \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{\frac{t}{22}}$.

**2. Isolate the exponential term:**
Divide both sides by 15:
$$\frac{1}{15} = \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{\frac{t}{22}}$$

**3. Take the logarithm of both sides:**
Using the property $\ln(a^b) = b \ln(a)$:
$$\ln\left(\frac{1}{15}\right) = \frac{t}{22} \ln\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)$$

**4. Solve for $t$:**
$$t = 22 \times \frac{\ln(1/15)}{\ln(1/2)}$$

Since $\ln(1/15) \approx -2.708$ and $\ln(0.5) \approx -0.693$:
$$t = 22 \times \frac{-2.708}{-0.693}$$
$$t \approx 22 \times 3.907$$
$$t \approx 85.95\text{ s}$$

**5. Conclusion:**
The time required for the sample to fall below 1 ng is approximately $86\text{ s}$. Looking at the provided options, (D) $90\text{ s}$ is the only value greater than our calculated time, meaning the mass will have certainly fallen below 1 ng by that point.
