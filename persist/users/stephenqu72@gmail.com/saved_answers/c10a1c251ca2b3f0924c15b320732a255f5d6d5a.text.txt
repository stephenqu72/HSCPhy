### Answer
The correct statement is **A. The stars have different rotational velocities.**

### Explanation
In astrophysics, the width of spectral lines in a star's spectrum is influenced by several factors, including pressure broadening and the **Doppler effect** caused by rotation. 

1.  **Rotational Broadening:** If a star is rotating rapidly, one side of the star will be rotating towards the observer (blueshift) and the opposite side will be rotating away from the observer (redshift). This causes the spectral lines to be "smeared" out or broadened compared to a star that is rotating slowly or not at all. This is the most common reason for observing differences in line widths between two otherwise "similar" stars.
2.  **Why other options are incorrect:**
    *   **B (Translational velocity):** A difference in translational velocity (the star moving towards or away from Earth as a whole) causes a uniform **Doppler shift** (a shift in the entire position of the spectral lines along the wavelength axis), not a broadening of the lines themselves.
    *   **C (Density):** While pressure broadening is related to density, if the stars are "similar" (implying similar spectral type/evolutionary stage), the primary variable leading to a notable difference in line width observed in a standard stellar comparison is typically rotation.
    *   **D (Elements):** Different elements do produce different line patterns, but the "width" of a specific spectral line of an element is a physical property determined by the star's environment (pressure, temperature) and dynamics (rotation/turbulence), not by the identity of the element itself.
