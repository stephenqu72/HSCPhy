To solve this problem, we need to calculate the frequency of the photon emitted when an electron in a hydrogen atom transitions from the $n=3$ energy level to the $n=2$ energy level.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

**1. Identify the energy formula for a hydrogen atom:**
The energy of an electron in the $n$-th energy level of a hydrogen atom is given by:
$$E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}$$

**2. Calculate the energy of the initial and final states:**
*   Initial state ($n_i = 3$): 
    $$E_3 = -\frac{13.6}{3^2} = -\frac{13.6}{9} \approx -1.51 \text{ eV}$$
*   Final state ($n_f = 2$): 
    $$E_2 = -\frac{13.6}{2^2} = -\frac{13.6}{4} = -3.40 \text{ eV}$$

**3. Calculate the energy difference ($\Delta E$):**
The energy of the emitted photon is equal to the difference between these two levels:
$$\Delta E = E_3 - E_2 = -1.51 \text{ eV} - (-3.40 \text{ eV}) = 1.89 \text{ eV}$$

**4. Convert energy to Joules:**
Since $1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$:
$$\Delta E = 1.89 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \approx 3.028 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

**5. Calculate the frequency ($f$):**
Using the relationship $\Delta E = hf$, where $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$):
$$f = \frac{\Delta E}{h} = \frac{3.028 \times 10^{-19}}{6.626 \times 10^{-34}} \approx 4.57 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, it matches option C.

***

### Answer:
**C. $4.57 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$**
