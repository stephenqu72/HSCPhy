### Answer
The correct option is **D. All the choices result in the same acceleration.**

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we use the principles of electrostatics and Newton's Second Law:

1.  **Uniform Electric Field:** Two parallel metal plates connected to a voltage source create a uniform electric field ($E$) between them. The strength of this field is given by $E = \frac{V}{d}$, where $V$ is the potential difference and $d$ is the separation distance. Because the field is uniform, the force exerted on a charged particle is constant at any point between the plates, regardless of the particle's position (whether it is at point P or Q).
2.  **Force on the Particle:** The force ($F$) acting on an electron in an electric field is given by:
    $$F = qE$$
    where $q$ is the charge of the electron. Since $q$, $V$, and $d$ are all constant, the magnitude of the electrostatic force $F$ is constant throughout the space between the plates.
3.  **Acceleration:** According to Newton's Second Law, $F = ma$, the acceleration ($a$) of the electron is:
    $$a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{qE}{m} = \frac{qV}{md}$$
    Because $q$, $V$, $m$, and $d$ are all constant, the **acceleration is independent of the electron's position and its velocity.** 

**Why the direction of motion doesn't matter:**
The electric force acts horizontally (perpendicular to the plates). Whether the electron is moving downwards or to the left, the magnitude of the electric force remains the same. Since we are looking for the magnitude of acceleration, the initial velocity vector does not change the magnitude of the force exerted by the electric field. Thus, the acceleration remains identical in all scenarios presented.
