To solve this problem, we use the standard physical constants for Earth:
*   Gravitational constant: $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$
*   Mass of Earth: $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \, \text{kg}$
*   Radius of Earth: $R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$

### a. Calculate the altitude of the satellite
The gravitational field strength is given by $g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$.
Solving for $r$ (the distance from the center of the Earth):
$$r = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{g}} = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}{0.22}} \approx 1.345 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}$$
Altitude ($h$) is $r - R_E$:
$$h = 1.345 \times 10^7 - 6.37 \times 10^6 = 7.08 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$$

### b. Calculate the period of the satellite
For a circular orbit, the centripetal force is provided by gravity: $\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$, leading to $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$.
The period $T$ is given by $T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM}}$.
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{(1.345 \times 10^7)^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}} \approx 20,135 \, \text{s}$$

### c. Estimate the increase in gravitational potential energy
For a small change in height ($h$ is large, but $\Delta h = 1000 \, \text{m}$ is small), we can approximate the change in potential energy per unit mass ($\Delta U/m$) using the local gravitational field strength:
$$\Delta U \approx m \cdot g \cdot \Delta h$$
Given $g = 0.22 \, \text{N kg}^{-1}$ and $\Delta h = 1000 \, \text{m}$:
$$\frac{\Delta U}{m} = 0.22 \times 1000 = 220 \, \text{J kg}^{-1}$$
