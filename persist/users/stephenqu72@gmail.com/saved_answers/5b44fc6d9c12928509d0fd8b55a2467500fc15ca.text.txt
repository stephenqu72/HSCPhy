The correct answer is **A**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must understand the environment in which the International Space Station (ISS) orbits.

1.  **Analyze the Environment:** Even at an altitude of approximately $400\text{ km}$, the vacuum of space is not perfect. There are still residual traces of Earth's atmosphere (specifically thin layers of gas/air molecules).
2.  **Identify the Force:** As the ISS travels at orbital speeds (approximately $7.66\text{ km/s}$), it constantly collides with these sparse air molecules. 
3.  **Apply Physics Principles:** These collisions result in a drag force acting on the ISS. When the ISS hits an air molecule, it exerts a force on the molecule, and according to Newton's Third Law, the molecule exerts an equal and opposite force on the ISS.
4.  **Conclude the Effect:** This interaction transfers momentum from the ISS to the air molecules. This loss of kinetic energy (and mechanical energy) causes the orbital radius of the ISS to decay over time. As the orbital radius decreases, the altitude of the ISS above the Earth's surface decreases.
5.  **Evaluate other options:**
    *   **B** is incorrect because while the moon exerts a gravitational pull, it does not cause a consistent, unidirectional decay in altitude of this nature.
    *   **C** is incorrect because the altitude decrease is an unwanted consequence of atmospheric drag, not a strategic maneuver to facilitate docking.
    *   **D** is incorrect because while the mass does change, changing mass does not inherently cause a decrease in orbital altitude; the primary driver for altitude loss in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) is atmospheric drag.
