To determine which electron "jump" involves the emission of the greatest amount of energy, we must look at the energy difference between the initial energy level ($n_i$) and the final energy level ($n_f$).

### Analysis
The energy of a photon emitted during an electron transition is given by the difference in energy levels:
$$\Delta E = E_{n_i} - E_{n_f}$$
According to the Bohr model, the energy levels get closer together as $n$ increases. Therefore, the largest energy transitions occur when an electron drops to the lowest possible energy state ($n=1$) from the highest possible starting point.

Let's evaluate the options provided:
*   **A: Level 4 to 1 ($R$):** This involves dropping to the ground state. The energy gap between $n=1$ and $n=2$ is the largest gap in the entire atom. Any transition ending at $n=1$ will result in a much higher energy photon than transitions ending at higher shells (like 2, 3, or 4).
*   **B: Level 6 to 2 ($S$):** This ends at the $n=2$ shell. The energy released is significantly less than a jump ending at $n=1$.
*   **C: Level 7 to 3 ($T$):** This ends at the $n=3$ shell. The energy released is smaller than transitions ending at $n=1$ or $n=2$.
*   **D: Level $\infty$ to 4 ($U$):** This ends at the $n=4$ shell. Even though the starting level is at infinity, the energy levels at $n=4$ and above are very close together, making this transition lower in energy than a jump to $n=1$.

### Conclusion
The transition from level 4 to level 1 involves the largest energy drop because it lands on the lowest energy state ($n=1$), where the spacing between levels is most significant.

**Correct Answer: A**

### How to solve this
1.  **Understand the Relationship:** Recall that energy emitted is proportional to the difference in energy levels ($\Delta E = hf$).
2.  **Identify the Trend:** Recognize that energy levels are not equally spaced. The gap between $n=1$ and $n=2$ is the largest. As $n$ increases, the gaps shrink towards zero at $n = \infty$.
3.  **Compare the Options:** Compare the final destination of the electron jumps. A jump that ends at $n=1$ will always release more energy than a jump that ends at $n=2, 3, 4, \dots$ regardless of the starting level (provided the starting level is reasonably low). Since option A ends at $n=1$ and all other options end at higher levels, option A must represent the greatest energy emission.
