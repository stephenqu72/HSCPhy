### Explanation
To determine the correct answer, we must understand the historical and scientific context of the Michelson-Morley experiment:

1.  **Purpose of the Experiment:** The experiment was designed to detect the existence of the "luminiferous aether," which was hypothesized to be the medium through which light waves traveled. Classical mechanics (Galilean relativity) predicted that if light traveled through an aether, an observer moving through that aether would measure different speeds of light depending on their direction.
2.  **Validity:** The experiment is considered a "valid" scientific experiment because it was highly precise and theoretically sound based on the physics of the time. It utilized an interferometer to measure the interference patterns of light beams traveling in different directions relative to the Earth's supposed motion through the aether.
3.  **Why Option A is correct:** The Michelson-Morley experiment is fundamental to the **principle of relativity**. By failing to detect any "aether wind," it demonstrated that the speed of light is invariant (constant) for all observers, regardless of their motion. This null result provided the experimental basis for Albert Einstein's Special Theory of Relativity, which postulates that the laws of physics and the speed of light are constant in all inertial frames of reference.
4.  **Why the others are incorrect:** 
    *   **B** is a distracter; while they used properties of light (interference), the experiment's "validity" in history stems from its test of a fundamental principle, not just "taking into account" properties of light.
    *   **C and D** claim the experiment was "invalid." In the scientific method, a "null result" (where the expected effect is not observed) is still a valid experimental result. The experiment successfully demonstrated that the aether theory was flawed; therefore, the experiment itself was perfectly valid.
