To achieve full marks (9 marks) on an HSC Physics essay question of this nature, you must demonstrate a deep understanding of the connection between theoretical physics and experimental design. "Discuss" requires you to examine the relationship between the scientific principles (the "why") and the experiments (the "how").

### How to solve this question:
1. **Structure:** Use a chronological approach to show the evolution of the atomic model.
2. **Link Principles to Experiments:** For each experiment, explicitly state the scientific principle that made it possible.
3. **Analyze Impact:** Explain how the results of these experiments forced a revision of the atomic model.

---

### Answer

The development of the atomic model was driven by the application of existing scientific principles to new experimental designs.

**1. J.J. Thomson and the Cathode Ray Tube (CRT)**
Thomson’s experiment was made possible by the principle that charged particles experience a force in an electric and magnetic field (the Lorentz force, $F = qE + qvB$). By applying known electric and magnetic fields to cathode rays, Thomson could measure the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$). This established that cathode rays were subatomic particles (electrons), leading to the "Plum Pudding" model.

**2. Rutherford and the Gold Foil Experiment**
Rutherford utilized the principles of electrostatics and classical mechanics. He fired alpha particles (positively charged, heavy nuclei) at a gold foil. The principle of Coulombic repulsion ($F = k \frac{q_1q_2}{r^2}$) suggested that if the atom were a uniform sphere, alpha particles should pass through with minimal deflection. The observed wide-angle scattering forced the conclusion that the atom contained a small, dense, positively charged nucleus.

**3. Bohr and the Hydrogen Spectrum**
Bohr’s model was made possible by integrating Planck’s quantum hypothesis ($E = hf$) and the Balmer series. Bohr proposed that electrons occupy discrete, stationary energy levels. He used the principle of conservation of energy to explain that light is emitted when an electron "jumps" between orbits, with the energy of the emitted photon equal to the difference in energy between the levels ($E_{photon} = \Delta E$). This provided a theoretical basis for the stability of the atom and the observed spectral lines.

**4. Chadwick and the Neutron**
The discovery of the neutron relied on the principle of the conservation of momentum and energy in nuclear collisions. When Rutherford proposed the existence of a neutral particle to account for the missing mass in the nucleus, Chadwick bombarded beryllium with alpha particles. By applying the conservation laws to the resulting radiation—which could knock protons out of paraffin wax—he proved the radiation consisted of neutral particles with mass similar to a proton.

**Conclusion**
These experiments illustrate that scientific progress relies on using established laws (forces, energy, conservation) to probe the unknown. Each discovery revealed a limitation in the current model, necessitating a new paradigm and advancing our understanding of the subatomic world.
