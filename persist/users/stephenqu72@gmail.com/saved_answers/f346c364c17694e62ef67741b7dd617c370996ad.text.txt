### Explanation

In astrophysics, the spectral classification of a star (the Harvard spectral classification system: O, B, A, F, G, K, M) is primarily determined by the **presence and relative strengths (depths)** of specific absorption lines in its spectrum. These lines correspond to the ionization states and excitation levels of elements present in the star's atmosphere, which are directly dependent on the star's surface temperature.

Here is why the other options are incorrect:
*   **A:** The longest wavelength absorption line is just one specific feature and does not provide enough information to categorize the star's entire temperature profile.
*   **B:** While the wavelength of peak intensity ($\lambda_{max}$) from the black body curve can determine the surface temperature via Wien's Law ($\lambda_{max}T = \text{constant}$), the spectral classification itself is specifically defined by the **absorption lines** (Fraunhofer lines), not the continuous black body curve.
*   **D:** The *widths* of absorption lines are primarily used to determine luminosity classes (e.g., distinguishing between a main-sequence dwarf and a giant star), as line broadening is influenced by pressure and density in the stellar atmosphere, rather than the primary temperature-based spectral class.

By observing the relative depths of absorption lines (e.g., the strength of Balmer lines of hydrogen compared to lines of ionized metals like calcium), astronomers can accurately assign a star to its specific spectral class.
