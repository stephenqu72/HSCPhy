To find the best value for the mass of a single coin, we must look for the **greatest common divisor** (GCD) of the mass differences provided in the table. 

This experiment is a direct analogy to Millikan's Oil Drop experiment, where Millikan determined the charge of a single electron by finding the greatest common factor among the total charges of various oil droplets.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Understand the data:** Each difference in mass represents the mass of a specific number of coins (since the boxes are identical). Therefore, each difference must be an integer multiple of the mass of a single coin ($m$).
2.  **Analyze the differences:** The differences given are:
    *   $7.5\text{ g}$
    *   $5.0\text{ g}$
    *   $12.5\text{ g}$
    *   $20.0\text{ g}$
3.  **Find the Greatest Common Divisor:** We need to find the largest value that divides all these numbers evenly:
    *   $7.5 = 3 \times 2.5$
    *   $5.0 = 2 \times 2.5$
    *   $12.5 = 5 \times 2.5$
    *   $20.0 = 8 \times 2.5$
4.  **Conclusion:** Since $2.5\text{ g}$ is the largest factor that produces an integer number of coins for every recorded difference, it is the most likely mass of a single coin.

Comparing this to the options provided:
A. $1.5\text{ g}$ (Does not divide $5.0$ or $12.5$ into integers)
