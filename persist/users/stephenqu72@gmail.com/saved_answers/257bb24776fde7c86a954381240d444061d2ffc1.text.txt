### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** The net force is zero nowhere on the path shown.
**Explanation:** For the net force on an object to be zero, it must be in inertial motion (constant velocity). Throughout the entire trajectory—whether the satellite is orbiting the planet, performing a flyby, or being influenced by the moon—it is within the gravitational fields of the planet and/or the moon. Gravity is an external force acting on the satellite at all points, causing centripetal acceleration or changing the satellite's speed and direction. Therefore, the net force is never zero.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
*   **Usefulness:** The model is useful because it provides a simplified visual representation of orbital mechanics, demonstrating how gravitational fields (from both the planet and the moon) influence the trajectory of a satellite. It helps illustrate concepts like gravity-assist maneuvers or the transition between different gravitational "spheres of influence."
*   **Limitations:** The model is limited because it is not to scale and lacks dimensionality in terms of mass and distance. It is a 2D simplification of a 3D problem; it ignores orbital inclinations, the non-spherical nature of gravitational fields (perturbations), the specific orbital velocities required for these transitions, and the influence of other celestial bodies. Additionally, "not to scale" drawings often fail to represent the vast distances involved, making the gravitational interactions appear much simpler than they are in reality.

### How to approach this as an HSC student:
1.  **Analyze the Physics (Part a):** Always look for conditions of equilibrium ($F_{net} = 0$). In space, unless you are in a "Lagrangian point" where gravitational forces cancel out (and even then, only in specific frames), gravity is always pulling on the object. Do not be fooled by a "dashed line"—the object is constantly accelerating.
2.  **Evaluate Models (Part b):** In HSC Physics, questions about "models" always look for two sides:
    *   **The Utility:** Why do we simplify? (It makes complex systems easier to visualize, isolates variables, and helps predict basic outcomes).
    *   **The Limitations:** Why is it not reality? (It ignores complexities like $G$ varying with distance, $M$ of bodies, 3D orbits, orbital decay, or atmospheric drag).
