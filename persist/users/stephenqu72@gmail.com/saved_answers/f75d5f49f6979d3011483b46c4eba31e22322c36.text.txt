### Explanation
To solve this, we must distinguish between the scientific definitions of **accuracy** and **reliability**:

1.  **Reliability** refers to the consistency of your results. An experiment is reliable if repeating it yields the same (or very similar) results. By "taking many readings of light intensity for each angle of rotation," the students can calculate a mean value and identify any anomalous results, thereby increasing the reliability of the data.
2.  **Accuracy** refers to how close the experimental results are to the true or theoretical value (in this case, Malus' Law: $I = I_0 \cos^2 \theta$). By taking multiple measurements at each angle, the students can calculate an average, which reduces the impact of random errors (like minor fluctuations in the light source or human error in reading the meter). This minimizes the discrepancy between the measured intensity and the theoretical value predicted by Malus' law, thus improving accuracy.
