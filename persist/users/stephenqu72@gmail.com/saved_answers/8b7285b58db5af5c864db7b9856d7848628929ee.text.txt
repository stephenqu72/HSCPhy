To determine which planet orbits a different star, we must use **Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which states that for planets orbiting the same central body (star), the ratio of the square of the orbital period ($T$) to the cube of the orbital radius ($r$) is constant:

$$\frac{T^2}{r^3} = k$$

Where $k$ is a constant dependent only on the mass of the central star. If a planet orbits a different star, it will have a different value for $k$.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

We calculate the ratio $\frac{T^2}{r^3}$ for each planet. To make the numbers manageable, we will use the units provided in the table (days for $T$ and $10^6\text{ km}$ for $r$):

1. **Planet P:**
   $$\frac{88^2}{58^3} = \frac{7744}{195112} \approx 3.97 \times 10^{-2}$$

2. **Planet Q:**
   $$\frac{687^2}{228^3} = \frac{471969}{11852352} \approx 3.98 \times 10^{-2}$$

3. **Planet R:**
   $$\frac{59800^2}{4495^3} = \frac{3576040000}{90848600000} \approx 3.94 \times 10^{-2}$$

*(Note: Planets P, Q, and R have ratios that are approximately equal, indicating they likely orbit the same star—in our solar system, these correspond to Mercury, Mars, and Neptune.)*

4. **Planet S:**
   $$\frac{80560^2}{5906^3} = \frac{6489913600}{206126600000} \approx 3.15 \times 10^{-2}$$

### Conclusion:
The ratios for planets P, Q, and R are consistent with one another, suggesting they orbit the same central star. Planet S has a significantly different value for the ratio $k$, which implies it orbits a star with a different mass.
