To solve these problems, we use the standard equations for radioactive decay.

### Part (a): Calculate the decay constant ($\lambda$)

**The Theory:** The decay constant is related to the half-life ($t_{1/2}$) by the formula:
$$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$$

**The Calculation:**
Given $t_{1/2} = 8$ days:
$$\lambda = \frac{0.6931}{8 \text{ days}} \approx 0.08664 \text{ days}^{-1}$$

Rounding to two significant figures (consistent with the provided data):
**$\lambda \approx 0.087 \text{ days}^{-1}$**

---

### Part (b): Determine the number of nuclei remaining

**The Theory:** We use the radioactive decay law:
$$N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$
Alternatively, since we know the half-life, we can use the simpler form:
$$N(t) = N_0 \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{\frac{t}{t_{1/2}}}$$

**The Calculation:**
Given:
- Initial nuclei $N_0 = 1.2 \times 10^{13}$
- Time elapsed $t = 76$ days
- Half-life $t_{1/2} = 8$ days

Number of half-lives passed $n = \frac{76}{8} = 9.5$

Substitute into the formula:
$$N = 1.2 \times 10^{13} \times (0.5)^{9.5}$$
$$N = 1.2 \times 10^{13} \times 0.0013889$$
$$N \approx 1.6667 \times 10^{10}$$

Rounding to two significant figures:
**$N \approx 1.7 \times 10^{10}$ nuclei**

---

### How to solve it:
1. **Identify the variables:** For part (a), the only variable needed is the half-life. For part (b), recognize that you need the initial amount, the time elapsed, and the half-life.
2. **Select the correct formula:** Remember that decay constant calculations always involve the natural log of 2 ($\ln 2$). For remaining nuclei, the power-of-two method ($N = N_0 \times 0.5^n$) is often faster and less prone to calculator errors than the exponential function $e^{-\lambda t}$.
3. **Check units:** Ensure your units for time ($t$) and half-life ($t_{1/2}$) match so they cancel out correctly in the exponent.
