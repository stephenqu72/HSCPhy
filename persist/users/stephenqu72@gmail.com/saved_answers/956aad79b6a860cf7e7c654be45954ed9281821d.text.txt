To excel in HSC Physics, you must connect the specific mechanics of the Bohr model to the macroscopic observation of stellar spectra. Here is the breakdown for Question 33.

### (a) Explanation and Strategy
**Strategy:** To gain all 3 marks, you must link electron energy transitions to the emission/absorption of photons and then explain how this acts as a "fingerprint" for elements.

**Answer:**
According to Bohr’s model, electrons exist in discrete, quantized energy levels. When an electron transitions from a higher energy level ($n_{high}$) to a lower energy level ($n_{low}$), it emits a photon with energy equal to the difference between the levels: $E_{photon} = E_{high} - E_{low} = hf$. Conversely, an electron can absorb a photon of that exact frequency to jump to a higher level. 

In a star, the hot core produces a continuous spectrum, but the cooler gas in the stellar atmosphere absorbs specific frequencies corresponding to the electronic transitions of the elements present. This creates dark absorption lines. Since each element has a unique set of energy levels, the pattern of absorption lines acts as a unique chemical "fingerprint," allowing astronomers to identify the composition of the star.

### (b) Explanation and Strategy
**Strategy:** This is a "limitation" question. You must identify that Bohr’s model was limited to a single quantum number ($n$) and that the Quantum Mechanical model (Schrödinger/Dirac) introduced additional quantum numbers to explain fine structure.

**Answer:**
Bohr’s model could not explain the Zeeman effect (the splitting of spectral lines in the presence of a magnetic field) or the hyperfine structure because it only considered the principal quantum number, $n$. 

These observations were explained by the development of the full Quantum Mechanical model, which introduced additional quantum numbers:
1. **The orbital quantum number ($l$) and magnetic quantum number ($m_l$):** Explain how energy levels split in the presence of a magnetic field (Zeeman effect).
2. **Spin quantum number ($m_s$):** Accounts for the intrinsic angular momentum (spin) of the electron.
3. **Nuclear spin:** The interaction between the spin of the electron and the magnetic moment of the nucleus leads to the even finer splitting known as the hyperfine structure.

These refinements show that energy levels are more complex than simple rings, allowing for the precise prediction of these spectral splits.
