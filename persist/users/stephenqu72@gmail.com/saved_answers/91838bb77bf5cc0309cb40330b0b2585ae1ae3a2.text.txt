### Part (a)
**Answer:**
The spectrum shown is an **absorption spectrum**. It is formed when the continuous spectrum of light produced by the hot, dense core of the star passes through the cooler, thinner gases in the star's outer atmosphere (the photosphere/chromosphere). The atoms in these cooler gases absorb specific wavelengths of light that correspond to the energy differences between their electron shells. This results in dark lines at specific wavelengths against the continuous background.

**How to solve:**
*   Identify the type of spectrum: Recognizing the "rainbow" background with dark lines is crucial. 
*   Explain the mechanism: Mention the two-step process: (1) Continuous light from the interior, (2) Selective absorption by cooler gas atoms in the atmosphere.

### Part (b)
**Answer:**
Analysis of a star's spectrum provides vital astrophysical information:
1.  **Chemical Composition:** Each element has a unique "fingerprint" of absorption lines. By comparing the position of lines (like line II) to known laboratory standards, we can identify elements present in the star’s atmosphere.
2.  **Radial Velocity/Doppler Effect:** By comparing the observed wavelength ($\lambda_{obs}$) to the laboratory wavelength ($\lambda_{rest}$), we can determine the star's motion. 
    *   *Example:* Line II is found at $660\text{ nm}$ on Earth. Looking at the scale, the line in the star's spectrum is shifted to the right of $660\text{ nm}$ (towards the red end). This indicates a **redshift**, meaning the star is moving away from Earth.
3.  **Temperature:** The overall shape and peak of the continuous spectrum (Wien’s Law) allow us to estimate the surface temperature of the star.

**How to solve:**
*   You must link theory to evidence. When asked for an example, always point to the Doppler shift, as the question provides a specific reference value ($660\text{ nm}$) to allow for a calculation-based observation.
*   Ensure you explicitly state that a shift toward longer wavelengths (red) indicates recession (moving away).
