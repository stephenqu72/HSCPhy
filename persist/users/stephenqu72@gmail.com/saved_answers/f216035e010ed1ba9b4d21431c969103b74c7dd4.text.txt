To determine the correct answer, we need to understand how the physical characteristics of a star affect the light it emits.

### Answer
The correct answer is **D. Width of spectral lines**.

### Explanation
In stellar astrophysics, the width of spectral lines (often called "line broadening") is a crucial diagnostic tool. While the position of a spectral line (its wavelength) tells us about the star's radial velocity (via Doppler shift) and chemical composition, the **width** of that line is affected by several factors, including:

1.  **Pressure Broadening (Stark Effect):** This is the key to determining density. In the atmosphere of a star, if the gas density is high, the atoms are packed more closely together. These atoms frequently collide or interact with the electric fields of nearby ions and electrons. These interactions perturb the energy levels of the atoms, causing the spectral lines to smear out or widen.
2.  **Luminosity Class:** Because pressure is directly related to the density of the stellar atmosphere, measuring the width of spectral lines allows astronomers to distinguish between stars of different luminosity classes. For example, a supergiant star has a very low-density atmosphere compared to a main-sequence star, resulting in much sharper (narrower) spectral lines for the same chemical elements.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A. Doppler shift:** This is used to determine the radial velocity of a star, not its density.
*   **B. Peak wavelength:** This is used to determine the surface temperature of a star according to Wien’s Law ($\lambda_{max} = b / T$).
*   **C. Absorption spectrum:** While this identifies the *composition* of the stellar atmosphere, it is the *specific shape/width* of those spectral lines within the spectrum, rather than the existence of the absorption spectrum itself, that indicates density.
