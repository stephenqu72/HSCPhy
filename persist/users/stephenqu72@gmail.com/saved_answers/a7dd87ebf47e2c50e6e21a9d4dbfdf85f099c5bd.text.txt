### Answer
Einstein’s approach successfully addressed the limitations of the classical wave model by introducing the photon, a discrete packet of energy ($E = hf$). The classical wave model failed in three key areas, which Einstein’s model resolved:

1.  **Frequency Dependence:** The graph shows a threshold frequency ($f_0$) below which no electrons are emitted, regardless of intensity. Classical theory predicted that any frequency of light would eventually eject electrons if given enough time to "build up" energy. Einstein proposed that a single photon must have enough energy ($hf > \Phi$) to overcome the work function ($\Phi$) instantaneously.
2.  **Kinetic Energy:** The graph shows $KE_{max}$ is directly proportional to frequency. Classical theory predicted that $KE_{max}$ should depend on the intensity (amplitude) of the light wave. Einstein’s equation, $KE_{max} = hf - \Phi$, shows that $KE_{max}$ depends solely on the energy of the incident photon (frequency) and the material's work function.
3.  **Instantaneous Emission:** Classical theory could not explain the lack of a time lag between illumination and emission at low intensities. Einstein’s model explains this because the interaction is a one-to-one collision between a photon and an electron; if the photon has sufficient energy, the electron is ejected immediately.
