### Answer

**Contribution of James Clerk Maxwell:**
James Clerk Maxwell unified the existing laws of electricity and magnetism into a single set of four mathematical equations known as "Maxwell’s Equations." His critical insight was the addition of the "displacement current" term to Ampère’s Law, which accounted for changing electric fields. 

Mathematically, he demonstrated that oscillating electric and magnetic fields would propagate through space as a self-sustaining transverse wave. By calculating the speed of these waves using the constants of free space—the permittivity of free space ($\epsilon_0$) and the permeability of free space ($\mu_0$)—he found:
$$v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$$
The numerical value of this speed was approximately $3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$, which matched the experimentally known speed of light. Maxwell concluded that light itself is an electromagnetic wave and that it is part of a much broader electromagnetic spectrum.

**Experimental Evidence (Heinrich Hertz):**
The experimental confirmation of Maxwell's theory was provided by Heinrich Hertz in 1887, shortly after Maxwell’s death. Hertz used an induction coil to create high-voltage sparks between two metal spheres, which acted as a transmitter. 

He detected the resulting electromagnetic waves at a distance using a loop of wire with a small gap (a receiver). When the waves hit the receiver, they induced an oscillating electromotive force, causing a spark to jump the gap. Hertz further demonstrated that these invisible waves exhibited properties characteristic of light, including:
1. **Reflection:** Using metal plates to bounce the waves.
2. **Refraction:** Using a large pitch prism to bend the waves.
3. **Interference and Diffraction:** Showing wave-like behavior.
4. **Polarization:** Demonstrating that the waves could be blocked by a grid of parallel wires.
These results confirmed that electromagnetic waves exist and travel at the speed of light, validating Maxwell’s theoretical predictions.
