To solve this problem, we first need to identify the parameters provided and determine the resistance of the transmission line.

### Given Data:
*   Power generated ($P_{gen}$) = $150 \text{ MW} = 150 \times 10^6 \text{ W}$
*   Distance ($d$) = $20 \text{ km}$
*   Resistance per unit length = $0.01\ \Omega/\text{km}$
*   Voltage drop ($\Delta V$) = $50 \text{ V}$

### Step 1: Calculate the total resistance ($R$)
The total resistance of the transmission wires is the resistance per km multiplied by the distance:
$$R = 0.01\ \Omega/\text{km} \times 20 \text{ km} = 0.2\ \Omega$$

---

### (a) Calculate the power loss
To calculate power loss ($P_{loss}$), we use the formula $P_{loss} = \frac{(\Delta V)^2}{R}$.
$$P_{loss} = \frac{50^2}{0.2}$$
$$P_{loss} = \frac{2500}{0.2} = 12,500 \text{ W}$$
Converting to kilowatts:
$$P_{loss} = 12.5 \text{ kW}$$

---

### (b) Calculate the voltage transmitted to the town
First, we need to find the transmission current ($I$) using the voltage drop formula $\Delta V = IR$:
$$50 = I \times 0.2$$
$$I = \frac{50}{0.2} = 250 \text{ A}$$

Now, we use the power formula $P_{gen} = V_{gen} \times I$ to find the initial transmission voltage ($V_{gen}$):
$$150 \times 10^6 = V_{gen} \times 250$$
$$V_{gen} = \frac{150,000,000}{250} = 600,000 \text{ V}$$

The voltage transmitted to the town ($V_{town}$) is the initial voltage minus the voltage drop:
$$V_{town} = V_{gen} - \Delta V$$
$$V_{town} = 600,000 - 50 = 599,950 \text{ V}$$

*(Note: In an HSC context, this is often expressed as $599.95 \text{ kV}$)*

---

### Explanation of the approach:
1.  **Total Resistance:** We must determine the total resistance of the physical wire before calculating electrical properties.
2.  **Power Loss:** Using $\Delta V$ and $R$, we apply the power dissipation formula. Alternatively, you could find the current first using $I = \frac{\Delta V}{R}$ and then use $P = I^2R$. Both methods yield the same result.
3.  **Transmitted Voltage:** By finding the current flowing through the line, we can deduce the original source voltage. Subtracting the "lost" voltage ($\Delta V$) from the source voltage gives the effective voltage received at the destination.
