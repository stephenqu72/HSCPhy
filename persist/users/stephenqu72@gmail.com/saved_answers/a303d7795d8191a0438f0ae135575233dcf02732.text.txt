To solve this problem, we use the principle of **Conservation of Mechanical Energy**.

### a. Show that $k = 392 \text{ N m}^{-1}$

The total gravitational potential energy lost by the ball is converted into elastic potential energy stored in the spring. 
- Mass $m = 2.0 \text{ kg}$
- Height fallen $h = 2.0 \text{ m} + 0.50 \text{ m} = 2.5 \text{ m}$
- Compression of spring $x = 0.50 \text{ m}$

The energy balance is:
$$mgh = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$
$$2.0 \times 9.8 \times 2.5 = \frac{1}{2} \times k \times (0.50)^2$$
$$49 = 0.125 \times k$$
$$k = \frac{49}{0.125} = 392 \text{ N m}^{-1}$$

---

### b. Acceleration at maximum speed

Maximum speed occurs when the net force on the ball is zero (i.e., the ball is in equilibrium momentarily).
- At maximum speed, the upward spring force $F_s$ equals the downward gravitational force $mg$.
- Since $\sum F = ma$ and $\sum F = 0$, the **acceleration must be $0 \text{ m s}^{-2}$**.

---

### c. Compression at maximum speed

At maximum speed, the forces are balanced:
$$F_s = mg$$
$$kx = mg$$
$$392 \times x = 2.0 \times 9.8$$
$$392 \times x = 19.6$$
$$x = \frac{19.6}{392} = 0.05 \text{ m}$$

The spring is compressed by **$0.05 \text{ m}$** when the ball reaches its maximum speed.

***

### Explanation of the approach:
1. **For (a):** We equate the total change in gravitational potential energy ($PE_g = mgh$) to the work done on the spring ($PE_s = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$). Note that the ball falls a total distance of $2.5 \text{ m}$ from its release point to its lowest point.
2. **For (b):** Maximum speed occurs at the equilibrium point. Because acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, and at maximum velocity the slope of the velocity-time graph is zero, the acceleration is zero.
3. **For (c):** We apply Hooke’s Law ($F = kx$) where the force exerted by the spring balances the weight of the mass ($mg$). Solving for $x$ gives the compression required for equilibrium.
