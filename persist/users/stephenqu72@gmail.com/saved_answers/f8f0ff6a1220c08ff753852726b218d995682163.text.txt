To answer this question, you need to demonstrate your understanding of the **wave-particle duality** of light, specifically through the lens of quantum mechanics.

### Answer
The interference pattern produced by individual photons demonstrates that even single particles exhibit wave-like behavior. Each photon acts as a wave that passes through both slits simultaneously, interfering with itself to create a probability distribution. While each photon arrives as a discrete point (particle), the accumulation of many such events over time builds up the classic interference pattern of bright and dark fringes. This illustrates that the intensity of the light pattern corresponds to the probability of finding a photon at a particular location.

### How to solve this
When approaching a question about the double-slit experiment with single photons, keep these two key concepts in mind for your marking criteria:

1.  **Wave-Particle Duality:** Mention that light behaves as a wave (allowing for interference) and as a particle (detected as discrete points). The wave function describes the probability of the photon's path, but the photon itself is a quantum object.
2.  **Probability Interpretation:** Explain that the "bright" fringes represent areas where there is a high probability of finding a photon, while "dark" fringes represent areas where the probability is zero due to destructive interference. Crucially, emphasize that this pattern only emerges after a large number of photons have been detected; a single photon provides no pattern on its own.

**Teacher's Tip:** In an HSC exam, always bridge the gap between the "wave" nature (superposition) and the "particle" nature (the discrete detection on the screen). Using the term "probability wave" or "probability distribution" is a high-level way to describe how the interference pattern is formed.
