### Teacher's Guide to Answering this Question

To answer this effectively, you must link the concept of matter waves to Bohr’s model of the atom. Here is how you should structure your response:

#### Part (a) - 3 marks
**Answer:**
Louis de Broglie proposed the hypothesis of **wave-particle duality**, stating that all moving matter possesses wave-like properties, with a wavelength $\lambda$ related to momentum $p$ by the equation $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$, where $h$ is Planck’s constant. 

Regarding the structure of the atom, de Broglie provided a theoretical justification for Bohr’s postulate that electrons in atoms are restricted to stable, quantized orbits. He proposed that an electron in an orbit acts as a **standing wave**. For an orbit to be stable, the circumference of the orbit must be an integer multiple of the electron's wavelength:
$$n\lambda = 2\pi r$$
Substituting his wavelength formula, this leads to the condition for angular momentum quantization: $mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi}$. This explained why only specific energy levels are permitted in an atom.

**How to solve:**
1. State the core hypothesis (matter waves).
2. Show the mathematical relationship (de Broglie equation).
3. Connect it to Bohr's model (the standing wave explanation for quantized orbits).

#### Part (b) - 2 marks
**Answer:**
The two primary pieces of evidence supporting de Broglie’s proposals are:
1. **Davisson-Germer Experiment:** They observed the **diffraction** of electrons when scattered off a nickel crystal. Since diffraction is a property unique to waves, this provided direct empirical evidence that electrons behave as waves.
2. **G.P. Thomson’s Experiment:** Thomson demonstrated that a beam of electrons passing through a thin gold foil produced an interference pattern on a screen, which is characteristic of wave-like behaviour, analogous to X-ray diffraction.

**How to solve:**
Identify two specific experimental proofs. It is important to mention the term "diffraction" or "interference," as these are the phenomena that prove the wave-like nature of matter.
