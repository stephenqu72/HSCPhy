### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) sharper, narrower spectral lines**.

### Explanation
To solve this, you need to apply the concepts of stellar spectroscopy, specifically the phenomenon of **pressure broadening**.

1.  **The Physics:** Spectral lines have a natural width, but they are often broadened by the physical conditions of the star's atmosphere. The primary mechanism affecting the width of these lines in this context is **collisional (pressure) broadening**.
2.  **Pressure Broadening:** When atoms in a gas are in close proximity (high density), they collide frequently. These frequent interactions disturb the energy levels of the electrons, causing the spectral lines to broaden.
3.  **The Relationship:** 
    *   **High-density/High-pressure atmospheres:** Cause significant collisions, leading to **broader** spectral lines. This is characteristic of Main Sequence stars (dwarfs).
    *   **Low-density/Low-pressure atmospheres:** Result in fewer collisions between atoms. Because the particles are less constrained and disturbed by neighbors, the spectral lines remain closer to their natural, narrow width. This is characteristic of giant and supergiant stars.
4.  **Why others are incorrect:** 
    *   (B) is the opposite of the correct answer; broadening occurs at high densities.
    *   (C) and (D) refer to the **Doppler Effect** (redshift/blueshift), which indicates the star's motion relative to the observer. While stellar atmospheres can exhibit these shifts, the question asks specifically about the effect of "low-density," which relates to the *width* of the lines, not their position along the spectrum.
