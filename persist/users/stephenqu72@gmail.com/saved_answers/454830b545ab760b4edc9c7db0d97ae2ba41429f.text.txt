### Answer
The correct statement is **(A)**.

### Explanation
To understand the energy of an orbiting satellite, we must consider the definition of mechanical energy. For any object in a system, the total mechanical energy ($E_{total}$) is defined as the sum of its kinetic energy ($K$) and its potential energy ($U$).

For a satellite of mass $m$ orbiting a planet of mass $M$ at a distance $r$ from the center:

1.  **Gravitational Potential Energy ($U$):** By convention, this is defined as:
    $$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$
    (Note that this value is negative because the satellite is in a bound state within the gravitational field).

2.  **Orbital Kinetic Energy ($K$):** Since the gravitational force provides the centripetal force ($F_g = F_c$), we have:
    $$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    Multiplying both sides by $\frac{r}{2}$, we find that kinetic energy is:
    $$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{GMm}{2r}$$

3.  **Total Mechanical Energy ($E_{total}$):**
    $$E_{total} = K + U$$
    Substituting the expressions above:
    $$E_{total} = \frac{GMm}{2r} + \left(-\frac{GMm}{r}\right)$$
    $$E_{total} = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$

Therefore, the total energy is the sum of the kinetic energy and the potential energy. While the numerical value of the total energy is exactly half of the potential energy (or the negative of the kinetic energy), statement (A) is the fundamental definition of total mechanical energy that applies universally.
