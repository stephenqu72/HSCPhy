To solve this physics problem, we rely on Kepler's Third Law, which relates the orbital period $T$ and the orbital radius $R$ of a satellite to the mass $M$ of the central body:
$$\frac{T^2}{R^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$$

### a. Plotting the Graph
*   **Axes:** Set the horizontal axis for $R^3$ ($10^{24} \text{ m}^3$) and the vertical axis for $T^2$ ($10^{10} \text{ s}^2$).
*   **Scale:** The $R^3$ values range from $6.40$ to $146$. A suitable scale would be $0$ to $160$ in steps of $20$. The $T^2$ values range from $0.66$ to $15.2$. A suitable scale would be $0$ to $16$ in steps of $2$.
*   **Plotting:** Mark the coordinates $(6.4, 0.66), (13.5, 1.39), (25.7, 2.66), (53.6, 5.57),$ and $(146, 15.2)$.
*   **Line of Best Fit:** Draw a straight line passing as close as possible to all points, ideally passing through the origin $(0,0)$.

### b. Calculating the Gradient
To find the gradient ($m$), pick two points far apart on your **line of best fit** (not necessarily data points). Let's use $(20, 2.0)$ and $(140, 14.5)$ as an approximation based on the data trends:
$$m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{14.5 - 2.0}{140 - 20} \times \frac{10^{10}}{10^{24}} = \frac{12.5}{120} \times 10^{-14} \approx 1.04 \times 10^{-13} \text{ s}^2/\text{m}^3$$

### c. Determining the Mass of Saturn
From Kepler's Third Law:
$$\frac{T^2}{R^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$$
The gradient of our graph represents $\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$. Therefore:
$$\text{Gradient} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} \implies M = \frac{4\pi^2}{G \times \text{Gradient}}$$
Using $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2$:
$$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (1.04 \times 10^{-13})}$$
$$M \approx \frac{39.478}{6.937 \times 10^{-24}} \approx 5.69 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$$
*(Note: The actual mass of Saturn is approximately $5.68 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$, confirming the calculation is correct.)*
