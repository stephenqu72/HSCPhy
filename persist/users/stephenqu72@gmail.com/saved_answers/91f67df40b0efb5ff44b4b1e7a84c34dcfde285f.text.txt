To solve this problem, we must analyze the vertical motion of the jumper as a projectile.

### **Part (a): Calculate the maximum height**
To find the maximum height, we first need to determine the time it took for the student to reach the peak of their jump. Looking at the stroboscopic image, there are 8 images in total. The student leaves the ground at image 1 and lands at image 8. The jump is symmetrical, meaning the peak occurs at the midpoint of the flight time.

1. **Total flight time ($t_{total}$):** There are 7 intervals between 8 images. With $0.1\text{ s}$ per interval, $t_{total} = 7 \times 0.1 = 0.7\text{ s}$.
2. **Time to peak ($t_{up}$):** Since the take-off and landing points are roughly at the same vertical level, $t_{up} = \frac{0.7}{2} = 0.35\text{ s}$.
3. **Vertical displacement ($y$):** Using the kinematic equation $y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2$, where $u_y$ is the initial vertical velocity and $a = -9.8\text{ m/s}^2$. At the peak, the vertical velocity $v_y = 0$.
   Using $v_y = u_y + at \Rightarrow 0 = u_y + (-9.8)(0.35) \Rightarrow u_y = 3.43\text{ m/s}$.
4. **Calculate $y_{max}$:**
   $$y_{max} = \frac{1}{2} |g| t_{up}^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 9.8 \times (0.35)^2 \approx 0.60\text{ m}$$

### **Part (b): What was the magnitude of his velocity when he left the ground?**
The magnitude of the initial velocity ($v$) is the vector sum of the horizontal component ($v_x$) and the vertical component ($u_y$).

1. **Horizontal component ($v_x$):** The total horizontal distance is $2.2\text{ m}$ over a total time of $0.7\text{ s}$. Assuming horizontal velocity is constant:
   $$v_x = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{2.2}{0.7} \approx 3.14\text{ m/s}$$
2. **Initial vertical component ($u_y$):** Calculated in part (a) as $3.43\text{ m/s}$.
3. **Resultant velocity ($v$):**
   $$v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + u_y^2}$$
   $$v = \sqrt{(3.14)^2 + (3.43)^2} = \sqrt{9.86 + 11.76} = \sqrt{21.62} \approx 4.65\text{ m/s}$$

The student left the ground with a velocity magnitude of approximately **$4.65\text{ m/s}$**.
