To solve this problem, we will use the principles of orbital mechanics.

### Given Data:
- Mass of Venus ($M$) = $4.867 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$
- Mass of satellite ($m$) = $3445\text{ kg}$
- Orbital radius ($r$) = $6.346 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
- Gravitational constant ($G$) = $6.674 \times 10^{-11}\text{ m}^3\text{ kg}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-2}$

---

### Part (a): Determine the orbital period ($T$)

For a satellite in a circular orbit, the centripetal force is provided by gravity:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GmM}{r^2} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Since $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, we derive Kepler's Third Law:
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}}$$

**Calculation:**
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (6.346 \times 10^6)^3}{6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times 4.867 \times 10^{24}}}$$
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{1.010 \times 10^{22}}{3.248 \times 10^{14}}} \approx \sqrt{3.109 \times 10^7} \approx 5576\text{ seconds}$$
Converting to minutes: $5576 / 60 \approx 92.9\text{ minutes}$.

---

### Part (b): Additional work needed to escape

The "additional work" required is the energy difference between the state of escape (total energy = $0$) and the current orbital energy ($E_{total}$).

1. **Orbital Energy ($E_{orb}$):**
   $$E_{orb} = -\frac{GmM}{2r} = -\frac{6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times 3445 \times 4.867 \times 10^{24}}{2 \times 6.346 \times 10^6}$$
   $$E_{orb} = -8.824 \times 10^{10}\text{ J}$$

2. **Work needed ($W$):**
   The satellite needs to reach an energy of $0\text{ J}$. 
   $$W = E_{final} - E_{initial} = 0 - (-8.824 \times 10^{10}\text{ J})$$
   $$W = 8.824 \times 10^{10}\text{ J}$$

---

### Explanation for Students:
*   **Part (a):** Always start with the force balance equation ($\Sigma F_c = F_g$). This is the foundation of all orbital motion problems. Ensure your units are in SI (meters and seconds) before calculating.
*   **Part (b):** Remember that a satellite in orbit already possesses kinetic and potential energy. Its total energy is negative (a "bound" state). To escape, you must add enough energy to bring its total energy to zero. Using the formula $E = -\frac{GmM}{2r}$ for circular orbits is the most efficient way to find the initial energy.
