The observation of wider-than-usual absorption bands (spectral line broadening) implies that the star has a **lower surface gravity**, which is characteristic of a **giant or supergiant star**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to connect stellar spectroscopy with the physical structure of a star:

1.  **Pressure Broadening (Stellar Atmospheres):** In stellar spectroscopy, the width of absorption lines is influenced by the density and pressure of the gas in the star's atmosphere. In high-gravity stars (like main-sequence stars), the atmospheric gas is highly compressed, causing frequent collisions between atoms. These collisions disturb the energy levels of the atoms, leading to broader absorption lines.
2.  **Low Gravity in Giants:** Conversely, giant and supergiant stars have very large radii and low surface gravity. This results in an atmosphere with much lower pressure and density. Consequently, there are fewer atomic collisions, leading to narrower spectral lines.
3.  **The Context of the Question:** The question states the lines are "wider than usual" for a given temperature. If we assume the comparison is against a main-sequence star, wider lines usually indicate higher pressure. However, in HSC Physics, this specific phenomenon is often used to distinguish luminosity classes. If the lines are **broader**, the star has **higher surface gravity** (Main Sequence/Dwarf). If the lines are **sharper/narrower**, the star has **lower surface gravity** (Giant/Supergiant).
