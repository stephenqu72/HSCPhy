To solve this problem, we rely on the principles of electromagnetism, specifically the work done by electric fields on charged particles and the force exerted by magnetic fields on moving charges.

### Part a: Calculate the increase in kinetic energy
The work done on a charged particle moving through a potential difference $V$ is given by $W = qV$. Since the work done is equal to the change in kinetic energy ($\Delta EK$):
$$\Delta EK = qV$$
Given $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$ and $V = 1500\ \text{V}$:
$$\Delta EK = (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) \times 1500 = 2.4 \times 10^{-16}\ \text{J}$$

### Part b: Show the ion's speed
We use the kinetic energy formula $EK = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Since the initial speed is negligible, the kinetic energy gained is equal to the final kinetic energy.
$$2.4 \times 10^{-16} = \frac{1}{2} \times (4.80 \times 10^{-27}) \times v^2$$
$$v^2 = \frac{2.4 \times 10^{-16} \times 2}{4.80 \times 10^{-27}}$$
$$v^2 = 1.0 \times 10^{11}$$
$$v = \sqrt{1.0 \times 10^{11}} \approx 3.16 \times 10^5\ \text{m s}^{-1}$$

### Part c: Calculate the diameter $D$
Inside the magnetic field, the magnetic force provides the centripetal force:
$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Solving for the radius $r$:
$$r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$
Substitute the known values ($m = 4.80 \times 10^{-27}\ \text{kg}$, $v = 3.16 \times 10^5\ \text{m s}^{-1}$, $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$, $B = 0.10\ \text{T}$):
$$r = \frac{(4.80 \times 10^{-27}) \times (3.16 \times 10^5)}{(1.6 \times 10^{-19}) \times 0.10}$$
$$r = \frac{1.5168 \times 10^{-21}}{1.6 \times 10^{-20}} = 0.0948\ \text{m}$$
The diameter $D$ is $2r$:
$$D = 2 \times 0.0948 = 0.1896\ \text{m} \approx 0.19\ \text{m}$$
