### Answer
The correct option is **B**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must associate specific experiments with the phenomena of Einstein's Theory of Special Relativity:

*   **Time Dilation:** This is the phenomenon where time is measured to pass more slowly for an object in motion relative to a stationary observer. A classic experimental verification is the observation of **muons**. Muons are unstable subatomic particles created in the upper atmosphere. Because they decay very rapidly, they should not be able to reach the Earth's surface under classical physics. However, because they travel at speeds close to the speed of light ($c$), time dilation extends their "proper lifetime," allowing them to travel far enough to be detected at ground level. This matches **Option B**.

*   **Evaluating other options:**
    *   **A:** The change in mass (relativistic mass) of electrons in particle accelerators is evidence for **relativistic momentum/mass-energy equivalence**, not time dilation.
    *   **C:** Thought experiments (Gedankenexperiments) with light and mirrors on a train are *theoretical arguments* used to derive time dilation, not "experimental evidence."
    *   **D:** The use of atomic clocks on planes (such as the Hafele-Keating experiment) is a direct experimental verification of **time dilation**, not length contraction.

Therefore, Option B is the only statement that correctly pairs a feature of special relativity with its valid experimental evidence.
