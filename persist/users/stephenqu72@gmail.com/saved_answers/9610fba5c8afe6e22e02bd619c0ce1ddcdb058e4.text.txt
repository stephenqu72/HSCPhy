To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of the **Doppler Effect** as it relates to light in astrophysics.

### Analysis of the Spectral Lines
1.  **Laboratory Reference:** The first diagram shows the "rest" positions of spectral lines for the element.
2.  **Star P:** The spectral lines are shifted toward the **blue** (shorter wavelength) end of the spectrum compared to the laboratory reference. This indicates a **blueshift**, meaning Star P is moving *toward* the observer.
3.  **Star Q:** The spectral lines are shifted toward the **red** (longer wavelength) end of the spectrum compared to the laboratory reference. This indicates a **redshift**, meaning Star Q is moving *away* from the observer.

### Evaluating the Options
*   **Redshift and Distance:** According to Hubble’s Law, light from more distant galaxies exhibits a greater redshift. Since Star Q is redshifted (moving away) and Star P is blueshifted (moving toward us), Star Q is at a cosmological distance such that it is receding, whereas Star P is a nearby object (likely within our own galaxy or a local group) moving toward us. Therefore, **Star Q is further away from Earth** than Star P.
*   **Rotation/Width of Lines:** In stellar spectroscopy, the width of the spectral lines is affected by the star's rotation. Faster rotation causes "Doppler broadening," where the spectral lines appear wider or more blurred. Looking at the diagrams, the lines for **Star P are significantly wider/thicker** than the lines for Star Q. This indicates that Star P is rotating faster. 

### Conclusion
*   Star Q is further away (due to the redshift characteristic of distant galaxies).
*   Star P is rotating faster (due to the broader spectral lines).
