To determine the correct option, we need to analyze the forces acting on the passenger at the bottom of the Ferris wheel and calculate the weight ($mg$).

### 1. Calculate the weight ($mg$)
The weight of the passenger is given by:
$$W = mg$$
Using the given mass $m = 91.84\text{ kg}$ and the standard acceleration due to gravity $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$:
$$W = 91.84 \times 9.8 \approx 899.992\text{ N} \approx 900\text{ N}$$
This allows us to identify that the gravitational force ($mg$) must be approximately $900\text{ N}$.

### 2. Analyze the forces at the bottom of the circle
At the lowest point of a vertical circular path, two forces act on the passenger:
*   The **Normal reaction force ($N$)** acting upwards from the seat.
*   The **Gravitational force ($mg$)** acting downwards.

For the passenger to move in a circle, there must be a **net centripetal force** directed towards the center (upwards):
$$\sum F = F_c = N - mg$$
Rearranging this to solve for the reaction force:
$$N = F_c + mg$$
Since $N = F_c + mg$ and $F_c$ (centripetal force) must be positive for circular motion, the reaction force $N$ must be **greater than** the weight ($mg$).

### 3. Evaluate the options
*   $mg$ must be $\approx 900\text{ N}$. This leaves options (C) and (D).
*   $N$ must be greater than $mg$ because the seat must support the weight *plus* provide the centripetal force.
*   In option (C), $N = 1000\text{ N}$ and $mg = 900\text{ N}$. Since $1000 > 900$, this satisfies the condition.
*   In option (D), $N = 900\text{ N}$ and $mg = 900\text{ N}$, which would imply zero net force and no circular motion.

Therefore, **(C)** is the correct choice.
