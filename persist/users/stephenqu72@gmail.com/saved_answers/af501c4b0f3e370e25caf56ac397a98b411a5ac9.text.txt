### (a) Explanation of Circular Motion at Constant Speed

To achieve full marks here, you must address two points: the circular path and the constant speed.

1.  **Circular Path:** When a charged particle moves through a uniform magnetic field, it experiences a magnetic force given by $F = qvB\sin(\theta)$. Since the velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field ($\theta = 90^\circ$), the force acts at $90^\circ$ to the direction of motion at all times. This force acts as a **centripetal force**, constantly pulling the proton toward the center of a circular path.
2.  **Constant Speed:** Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the direction of motion (velocity), it performs **no work** on the proton ($W = Fd\cos(90^\circ) = 0$). Since the work done is zero, there is no change in kinetic energy, and therefore, the speed of the proton remains constant.

---

### (b) Calculation of the Radius

To calculate the radius ($r$), we equate the magnetic force to the centripetal force required for circular motion:

$$F_B = F_c$$
$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Rearranging to solve for $r$:
$$r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$

**Given values:**
*   Mass of a proton ($m$) $\approx 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}$
*   Charge of a proton ($q$) $\approx 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$
*   Velocity ($v$) = $3.3 \times 10^6\text{ m s}^{-1}$
*   Magnetic field strength ($B$) = $0.80\text{ T}$

**Calculation:**
$$r = \frac{(1.67 \times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}) \times (3.3 \times 10^6\text{ m s}^{-1})}{(1.60 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}) \times (0.80\text{ T})}$$
$$r = \frac{5.511 \times 10^{-21}}{1.28 \times 10^{-19}}$$
$$r \approx 0.043\text{ m}$$

The radius of the circular path is approximately **$0.043\text{ m}$** (or $4.3\text{ cm}$).
