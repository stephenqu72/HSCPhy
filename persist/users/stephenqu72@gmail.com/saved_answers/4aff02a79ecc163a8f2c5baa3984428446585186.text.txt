To solve this problem, we use Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion, which states that the square of the orbital period $T$ is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius $r$:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

### Step 1: Convert units
First, convert the orbital period of Planet X ($T_X$) from days to seconds:
$T_X = 326 \text{ days} \times 24 \times 3600 \approx 2.81664 \times 10^7 \text{ s}$
$T_Y = 516 \text{ days} \times 24 \times 3600 \approx 4.45824 \times 10^7 \text{ s}$

### Step 2: Determine the orbital radius of Y ($r_Y$)
Since both planets orbit the same star, we can use the ratio form of Kepler’s Third Law:
$$\frac{r_X^3}{T_X^2} = \frac{r_Y^3}{T_Y^2} \implies r_Y = r_X \times \left(\frac{T_Y}{T_X}\right)^{2/3}$$
Substituting the values:
$r_Y = (1.9 \times 10^{11}) \times \left(\frac{516}{326}\right)^{2/3}$
$r_Y \approx (1.9 \times 10^{11}) \times (1.5828)^{2/3} \approx (1.9 \times 10^{11}) \times 1.355$
$r_Y \approx 2.57 \times 10^{11} \text{ m}$

### Step 3: Determine the mass of the star ($M$)
Using the formula $\frac{r_X^3}{T_X^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$, we solve for $M$:
$$M = \frac{4\pi^2 r_X^3}{G T_X^2}$$
Using $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$:
$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (1.9 \times 10^{11})^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (2.81664 \times 10^7)^2}$
$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times 6.859 \times 10^{33}}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 7.933 \times 10^{14}}$
$M \approx 5.12 \times 10^{29} \text{ kg}$

**Summary of Answers:**
- Orbital radius of Y: $\approx 2.57 \times 10^{11} \text{ m}$
- Mass of the star: $\approx 5.12 \times 10^{29} \text{ kg}$
