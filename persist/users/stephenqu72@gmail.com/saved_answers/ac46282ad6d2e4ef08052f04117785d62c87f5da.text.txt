To solve this problem, we need to use the formula for gravitational field strength $g$ at a distance $r$ from the center of a planet:

$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

### Step-by-step Solution:

1.  **Define the field at Earth's surface:**
    At the surface, the distance from the center is the radius of the Earth, $R$. Let the gravitational field strength at the surface be $g_s$:
    $$g_s = \frac{GM}{R^2}$$

2.  **Define the field at the orbital distance:**
    The satellite is at an altitude $h$ above the surface, so its distance from the center of the Earth is $r = R + h$. We are told the field strength here ($g_{orbit}$) is half of the value at the surface:
    $$g_{orbit} = \frac{1}{2} g_s = \frac{GM}{(R + h)^2}$$

3.  **Set up the equation:**
    Substitute the expression for $g_s$ into the equation for $g_{orbit}$:
    $$\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{GM}{R^2} \right) = \frac{GM}{(R + h)^2}$$

4.  **Solve for $h$:**
    Cancel out $GM$ from both sides:
    $$\frac{1}{2R^2} = \frac{1}{(R + h)^2}$$
    Invert both sides:
    $$2R^2 = (R + h)^2$$
    Take the square root of both sides:
    $$\sqrt{2}R = R + h$$
    Subtract $R$ from both sides to isolate the altitude $h$:
    $$h = \sqrt{2}R - R$$
