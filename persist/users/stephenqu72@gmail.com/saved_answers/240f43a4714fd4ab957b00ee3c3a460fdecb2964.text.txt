To solve these problems, we use the standard physical constants for Earth:
- Mass of Earth ($M$) $\approx 5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg
- Radius of Earth ($R_e$) $\approx 6.37 \times 10^{6}$ m
- Gravitational constant ($G$) $\approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11}$ N m$^2$ kg$^{-2}$

### (a) Calculate the orbital speed of the station
**Solution:**
The orbital radius $r$ is the sum of Earth's radius and the altitude:
$r = 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m} + 0.40 \times 10^6 \text{ m} = 6.77 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$

The formula for orbital speed is derived from equating gravitational force to centripetal force:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{G M m}{r^2} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}{6.77 \times 10^6}}$$
$$v \approx 7670 \text{ m/s (or 7.67 km/s)}$$

### (b) Energy changes involved in moving to a higher orbit
**Solution:**
When the station moves to a higher orbit ($r$ increases):
1. **Gravitational Potential Energy ($U = -GMm/r$):** As $r$ increases, $U$ becomes less negative, meaning it **increases**.
2. **Kinetic Energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$):** Since $v = \sqrt{GM/r}$, as $r$ increases, $v$ decreases. Therefore, kinetic energy **decreases**.
3. **Total Mechanical Energy ($E = U + K = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$):** As $r$ increases, the magnitude of the negative total energy decreases, meaning the total energy of the system **increases**. 
*Summary:* Moving to a higher orbit requires an input of work (energy) from the spacecraft's engines, which results in a net gain in total mechanical energy.

### (c) Why satellites in higher orbits require less speed
**Solution:**
Satellites maintain orbit because the gravitational force provides the necessary centripetal force:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
Simplifying for $v^2$:
$$v^2 = \frac{GM}{r} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Because the gravitational force ($F_g = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$) follows the inverse-square law, it weakens significantly as the orbital radius $r$ increases. Since less gravitational force is acting on the satellite at a greater distance, the required centripetal force to maintain a circular path is smaller. Consequently, a lower orbital velocity is sufficient to stay in equilibrium.
