#### Part (b): Assessment of Validity
**Conclusion:** The teacher's suggestion that the range is *inversely* proportional to the cosine of the angle is **invalid**.

**Justification:**
1.  **Mathematical Analysis:** The standard formula for the range ($R$) of a projectile on level ground is:
    $$R = \frac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g} = \frac{2v^2 \sin\theta \cos\theta}{g}$$
    where $\theta$ is the angle of elevation.
2.  **Graphical Evidence:** If $R \propto \frac{1}{\cos\theta}$ (inverse proportionality), the graph of $R$ vs $\cos\theta$ would be a hyperbola ($y = \frac{k}{x}$). Your plotted data shows the range *increasing* as the cosine value *decreases*. An inverse relationship would imply that as the cosine value increases (towards $1.0$, i.e., $0^\circ$), the range should approach $0$. However, looking at the physics, as $\theta$ approaches $0^\circ$, $\cos\theta$ approaches $1$, and the range actually approaches $0$. While this matches the "direction" of inverse proportionality, the relationship is actually a product of sine and cosine.
3.  **Physics Reasoning:** The range is proportional to the product $\sin\theta \cos\theta$. As $\theta$ increases from $0^\circ$ to $45^\circ$, $\cos\theta$ decreases from $1$ to $0.707$, but the range $R$ increases from $0$ to a maximum. Therefore, the range is not inversely proportional to the cosine of the angle; it is a trigonometric function dependent on the angle itself.
