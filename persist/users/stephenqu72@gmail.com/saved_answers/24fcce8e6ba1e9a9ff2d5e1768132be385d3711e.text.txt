### Answer
The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
To determine the correct answer, we must examine the specific purpose and outcome of J.J. Thomson’s 1897 experiment.

1.  **Understanding the Goal:** Thomson’s experiment involved firing cathode rays through crossed electric and magnetic fields. By balancing the electric force ($F_E = qE$) and the magnetic force ($F_B = qvB$), he was able to determine the velocity of the particles. He then used the magnetic deflection alone ($F_B = \frac{mv^2}{r}$) to calculate the charge-to-mass ratio ($\frac{q}{m}$).
2.  **Evaluating Option (A):** Thomson did not calculate the absolute mass ($m$) of the electron in this experiment; he only found the ratio $\frac{q}{m}$. Millikan’s oil drop experiment later allowed for the determination of the mass of the electron once the charge was known.
3.  **Evaluating Option (B):** Similar to (A), Thomson did not measure the charge ($q$) of the electron. This was famously done later by Robert Millikan.
4.  **Evaluating Option (C):** By finding a finite, consistent value for the charge-to-mass ratio that was much larger than that of a hydrogen ion, Thomson provided compelling evidence that cathode rays were composed of subatomic particles with a very small mass—much smaller than any known atom. This proved that cathode rays had mass and were corpuscular in nature.
5.  **Evaluating Option (D):** Heinrich Hertz had performed experiments that suggested cathode rays did not deflect in an electric field, leading him to believe they were a form of electromagnetic radiation (waves). Thomson’s experiment, by successfully deflecting the rays, actually **refuted** Hertz’s claims, rather than supporting them.

Therefore, the experiment is best characterized by the evidence it provided for the existence of subatomic particles with mass.
