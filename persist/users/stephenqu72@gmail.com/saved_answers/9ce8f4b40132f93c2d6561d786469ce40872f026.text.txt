### Answer

**(a) Relate Einstein’s equivalence of mass and energy to explain how the Sun produces this energy.**

The Sun produces energy primarily through the process of nuclear fusion, where hydrogen nuclei (protons) fuse to form helium nuclei in the core. Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle is expressed by the equation:
$$E = \Delta mc^2$$
During this fusion process, the mass of the resulting helium nucleus is slightly less than the combined mass of the four hydrogen nuclei that formed it. This difference in mass is known as the "mass defect" ($\Delta m$). This "missing" mass is not destroyed but is converted directly into a massive amount of energy ($E$), where $c$ is the speed of light ($3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$). Because $c^2$ is an extremely large number, even a tiny amount of mass defect results in the enormous energy output observed from the Sun.

**(b) Explain how the spectra of our Sun would differ from a much larger, distant star.**

The spectra would differ primarily in two ways:
1.  **Absorption Lines (Composition):** The pattern of absorption lines (Fraunhofer lines) would differ because the two stars likely have different chemical compositions, temperatures, and surface gravities. These factors affect the ionization states of the atoms in the stellar atmosphere.
2.  **Width of Spectral Lines:** A much larger star (likely a giant or supergiant) will have a lower surface gravity than the Sun. This leads to lower atmospheric pressure, resulting in less "pressure broadening" of spectral lines. Therefore, the Sun will exhibit broader absorption lines compared to the narrower, sharper lines of a larger, lower-density star. (Note: If the "larger" star is a main-sequence star of a different spectral class, its peak wavelength would also shift according to Wien’s Law based on its temperature).

### How to solve it
*   **For part (a):** Identify the core physics concept (nuclear fusion). Always explicitly state the formula $E = \Delta mc^2$ and define the variables. Crucially, explain the "mass defect"—this is the key to demonstrating that energy is not coming from nowhere, but from the conversion of matter.
*   **For part (b):** Think about what creates spectral features. Absorption lines are unique "fingerprints" of chemical elements. When comparing stars, you must discuss:
    *   **Composition:** Different elements produce different lines.
    *   **Pressure broadening:** This is a classic HSC physics point—larger stars have lower density/pressure, leading to thinner spectral lines compared to smaller, denser stars like our Sun.
