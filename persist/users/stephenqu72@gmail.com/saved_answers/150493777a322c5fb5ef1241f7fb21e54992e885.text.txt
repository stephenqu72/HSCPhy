The correct answer is **A**.

### Explanation

To solve this question, you must understand the limitations of the Rutherford model and how Bohr’s model addressed them:

1.  **The Limitation of Rutherford's Model:** Rutherford proposed that electrons orbit a nucleus like planets around the sun. According to classical electromagnetic theory, an accelerating charged particle (like an electron in orbit) should continuously lose energy as electromagnetic radiation. This would cause the electron to spiral into the nucleus, meaning atoms should be inherently unstable and produce a continuous spectrum of light.
2.  **Bohr’s Postulates:** Niels Bohr proposed that electrons exist in specific, stationary energy levels where they do *not* radiate energy. He stated that electrons can only transition between these discrete energy levels by emitting or absorbing a photon with energy equal to the difference between the levels ($E = \Delta E = hf$). 
3.  **The Evidence:** Bohr used the observation of **discrete emission spectra** (the spectral lines of elements like hydrogen) to support his model. Because the light emitted was not a continuous rainbow but specific, distinct wavelengths, it proved that the internal energy of the atom was "quantized" (restricted to specific values).

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **B:** This refers to pair annihilation, which is a concept in particle physics not related to Bohr's model of the atom.
*   **C:** This describes the **Geiger-Marsden experiment**, which provided the evidence for *Rutherford's* model of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus, not Bohr’s improvement.
*   **D:** This describes the **Davisson-Germer experiment**, which provided evidence for the wave-particle duality of electrons (de Broglie's hypothesis), which came after Bohr.
