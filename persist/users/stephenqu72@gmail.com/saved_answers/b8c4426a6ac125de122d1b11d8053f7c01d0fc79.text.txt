To answer this question effectively, we must apply the physics of circular motion on a banked track.

### The Physics Explanation

For a cyclist moving in a circle on a banked track, the net centripetal force ($F_c$) required to keep the bike turning is provided by the horizontal component of the normal force ($N$). The formula for a perfectly banked track is:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$
where:
- $\theta$ is the angle of the track.
- $v$ is the velocity.
- $r$ is the radius of the turn.
- $g$ is gravity.

**1. Why the first position (vertical) is necessary:**
When the cyclist enters the track at a low speed, the required centripetal force is small. If the cyclist were to lean perpendicular to the track, the horizontal component of the normal force would be too large for the required centripetal force at that low speed. To prevent the bike from sliding down the track, the rider maintains a vertical posture. In this case, friction between the tires and the track surface acts to prevent slipping, balancing the system.

**2. Why the second position (perpendicular) is necessary:**
As the cyclist increases their speed ($v$), the required centripetal force ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$) increases significantly. By leaning the bike so that the rider's body and bike are perpendicular to the banked track, the cyclist aligns the normal force vector ($N$) such that its horizontal component provides exactly the required centripetal force. At this specific "design speed" for that angle, the rider does not need to rely on friction to maintain their path, preventing the tires from scrubbing or sliding.

### Summary for the Examiner
*   **Low speed:** Centripetal force requirements are minimal. The rider stays vertical to keep the center of mass balanced and uses static friction to stay on the track.
*   **High speed:** As speed increases, the required centripetal force increases. Leaning the body perpendicular to the track increases the horizontal component of the normal force, allowing the track's banking to provide the necessary centripetal force for the turn without relying on lateral friction.
