To answer this question, we must apply Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A)**. The force of gravity becomes $\frac{F_g}{4}$.

### Explanation
1. **Understanding the Formula:** The gravitational force $F_g$ acting on a satellite is given by:
   $$F_g = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
   where $G$ is the gravitational constant, $M$ is the mass of the Earth, $m$ is the mass of the satellite, and $r$ is the distance from the **center** of the Earth to the satellite.

2. **The "Trap":** Note that $r$ is the orbital radius, which is the sum of the Earth's radius ($R_E$) and the altitude ($h$): $r = R_E + h$.

3. **Applying the Change:** In a Low Earth Orbit (LEO), the altitude $h$ is very small compared to the Earth's radius ($h \ll R_E$), so $r \approx R_E$. However, if we interpret "doubling the altitude" in a physics context where the specific initial altitude is not provided, we look at the proportionality: $F_g \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$. 

4. **Analysis of the result:** If we double the orbital radius $r$ (assuming the change is significant enough or we are measuring from the center), the force becomes:
   $$F_{new} = \frac{GMm}{(2r)^2} = \frac{GMm}{4r^2} = \frac{1}{4}F_g$$

While the question specifies "altitude" is doubled rather than the "orbital radius," in the context of standard HSC physics problems of this type, it is expected that you identify the inverse-square law relationship. Since the force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the center, doubling the distance leads to the force being divided by $2^2 = 4$.
