### **How to Solve This Question**

To solve H-R diagram problems, follow these three systematic steps:

**Step 1: Identify the "Main Sequence" Band**
The most important feature of an H-R diagram is the continuous diagonal band running from the top-left (hot, bright) to the bottom-right (cool, faint). This is the **Main Sequence**. Any star located on or very near this strip is currently fusing Hydrogen into Helium.
*   **Star A** sits directly on this strip $\rightarrow$ Main Sequence.
  
**Step 2: Determine Stage via Position relative to the Band**
Once you know where the Main Sequence is, look at how the other stars deviate from it:
*   **Above the band (Higher Luminosity):** Stars that are "higher" than the main sequence for their temperature are evolved stars. If they are shifted toward the cooler (right) side, they are **Red Giants**. If they are extremely luminous (top of the graph), they are **Supergiants**.
    *   **Star B** is above the band $\rightarrow$ Red Giant.
    *   **Star C** is at the extreme top $\rightarrow$ Supergiant.
*   **Below the band (Lower Luminosity):** Stars located significantly below the main sequence at low temperatures are typically low-mass stars or white dwarfs. 
    *   **Star D** is at the very bottom right (low temperature, low luminosity) $\rightarrow$ Red Dwarf/Low-mass Main Sequence.

**Step 3: Match Stage to Fusion Process**
From your physics knowledge, recall what happens as a star ages:
1.  **Main Sequence:** Core Hydrogen $\rightarrow$ Helium.
2.  **Red Giant:** Once H is exhausted, the core contracts and heats up to trigger Helium $\rightarrow$ Carbon fusion.
3.  **Supergiant:** Massive stars continue through much heavier stages, fusing elements like Carbon, Neon, Oxygen, and Silicon, eventually leading to an Iron core.
