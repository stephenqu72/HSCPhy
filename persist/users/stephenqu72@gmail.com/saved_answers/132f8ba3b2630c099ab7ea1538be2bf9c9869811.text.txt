To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of Einstein's Special Theory of Relativity as observed from a stationary frame (Earth) relative to an object (the spaceship) moving at relativistic speeds (a significant fraction of $c$).

### Explanation:

1.  **Mass of the Ship (Relativistic Mass):** According to relativistic mechanics, as an object's velocity $v$ approaches the speed of light $c$, its measured mass $m$ increases according to the Lorentz factor $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$. The relativistic mass is given by $m = \gamma m_0$. Thus, an observer on Earth would perceive the mass of the spaceship to **increase**.

2.  **Time inside the Ship (Time Dilation):** Special Relativity dictates that time intervals for an object moving relative to an observer appear dilated (stretched). Therefore, clocks inside the fast-moving spaceship will appear to tick more slowly compared to clocks on Earth. In other words, time inside the ship **slows** down from the perspective of an Earth observer.

3.  **Length of the Ship (Length Contraction):** Length contraction occurs in the direction of motion for an object moving at relativistic speeds. The observed length $L$ is given by $L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$, where $L_0$ is the proper length. Consequently, an observer on Earth would perceive the length of the ship to **decrease** (contract) in the direction of travel.

**Conclusion:**
Comparing these relativistic effects to the options provided:
*   Mass: Increases
*   Time: Slows
*   Length: Decreases
