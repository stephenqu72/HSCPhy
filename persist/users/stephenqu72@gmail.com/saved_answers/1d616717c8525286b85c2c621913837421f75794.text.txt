To solve this problem, we will use the principles of the transit method for exoplanet detection and orbital mechanics.

### 1. Calculations

**A. Orbital Period ($T$):**
The orbital period is the time between successive transits. Looking at the graph, the first dip (transit) occurs at $t = 5$ hours and the second dip occurs at $t = 15$ hours.
$$T = 15\text{ h} - 5\text{ h} = 10\text{ hours} = 36,000\text{ s}$$

**B. Orbital Radius ($r$):**
We assume a circular orbit. Using Kepler's Third Law:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$
*Note: Since the star's mass $M \approx 9.9 \times 10^{30}\text{ kg}$ is provided, we use it for the central body.*
$$r^3 = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (9.9 \times 10^{30}) \times (36000)^2}{4\pi^2}$$
$$r^3 \approx 2.16 \times 10^{28} \implies r \approx 2.78 \times 10^9\text{ m}$$

**C. Mass of the Exoplanet ($m_p$):**
The star moves because both bodies orbit a common barycenter. The relationship is $Mv = m_p v_p$, or $M \cdot r = m_p \cdot r_p$. We need the velocity of the planet ($v_p$):
$$v_p = \frac{2\pi r}{T} = \frac{2 \cdot \pi \cdot 2.78 \times 10^9}{36000} \approx 4.85 \times 10^5\text{ m/s}$$
Using the conservation of momentum:
$$m_p = \frac{M \cdot v_{star}}{v_p} = \frac{(9.9 \times 10^{30}) \times 150}{4.85 \times 10^5} \approx 3.06 \times 10^{27}\text{ kg}$$

### 2. Explanation

*   **Cause of movement:** The star moves because the planet exerts a gravitational force on the star, causing both to orbit a common center of mass (barycenter). Since the star is much more massive, its orbit is much smaller, resulting in a slower speed.
*   **Deducing motion:** The star's motion is detected via the **Doppler Effect** (Radial Velocity method). As the star orbits the barycenter, it moves towards and away from Earth. This causes a periodic Doppler shift in the star's spectral lines (blueshift when approaching, redshift when receding).
