### Answer

**(a)**
The energy of an electron in a hydrogen atom is given by $E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2} \text{ eV}$.
For $n=3$: $E_3 = -\frac{13.6}{3^2} = -1.511 \text{ eV}$
For $n=2$: $E_2 = -\frac{13.6}{2^2} = -3.4 \text{ eV}$
The energy emitted is $\Delta E = E_3 - E_2 = -1.511 - (-3.4) = 1.889 \text{ eV}$.
To convert to Joules: $1.889 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} = 3.026 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$.
Using $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$:
$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{E} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{3.026 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 6.57 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} \text{ (or } 657 \text{ nm)}$$

**(b)**
In the Bohr model, electrons orbit the nucleus in stable, discrete energy levels (stationary states) where they do not radiate energy. When an electron transitions from a higher energy level to a lower one, it must lose energy. According to the law of conservation of energy, this energy cannot be destroyed; it is emitted as a photon of light with energy exactly equal to the difference between the two levels ($E_{photon} = E_{initial} - E_{final}$). Conversely, to move to a higher level, the electron must absorb a photon of the exact required energy.
