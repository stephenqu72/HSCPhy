To solve this problem, we must analyze the vertical displacement of the particle under two different force conditions. In both scenarios, the horizontal velocity $v_x$ and the time of flight $t$ are identical because the horizontal distance $d$ is the same.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

**1. Scenario 1: Gravity only**
Under the influence of gravity ($a = g$), the vertical displacement $\Delta y_1$ is given by the kinematic equation $\Delta y = \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$$\Delta y_1 = 10\text{ cm} = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

**2. Scenario 2: Electric field and Gravity**
In the second scenario, the particle strikes the upper surface. Let $E$ be the magnitude of the electric field and $F_e = qE$ be the electric force. The net vertical acceleration $a_{net}$ acts upwards to move the particle $10\text{ cm}$ in the opposite direction.
The net force is $F_{net} = qE - mg = ma_{net}$, so $a_{net} = \frac{qE}{m} - g$.
The displacement $\Delta y_2$ is:
$$\Delta y_2 = 10\text{ cm} = \frac{1}{2} a_{net} t^2$$
$$\Delta y_2 = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{qE}{m} - g \right) t^2$$

**3. Equating the two scenarios**
Since $\Delta y_1 = \Delta y_2 = 10\text{ cm}$, we have:
$$\frac{1}{2}gt^2 = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{qE}{m} - g \right) t^2$$
Dividing both sides by $\frac{1}{2}t^2$:
$$g = \frac{qE}{m} - g$$
$$2g = \frac{qE}{m}$$
Solving for $E$:
$$E = \frac{2mg}{q}$$
