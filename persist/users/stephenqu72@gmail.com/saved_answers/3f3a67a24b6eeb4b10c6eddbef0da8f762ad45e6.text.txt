### Explanation
To arrive at this answer, we must distinguish between the Bohr model and the Schrödinger (quantum mechanical) model of the atom:

1.  **The Bohr Model:** Niels Bohr proposed that electrons travel in fixed, circular orbits at specific distances from the nucleus, similar to planets orbiting a sun. In this model, you could theoretically define the position and momentum of an electron with certainty.
2.  **Schrödinger’s Advancement:** Erwin Schrödinger moved away from the concept of "orbits" to "orbitals." Using wave equations, he described the electron as having wave-like properties. According to the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle (which is foundational to this model), it is impossible to simultaneously know both the precise position and the momentum of an electron.
3.  **Why (A) is correct:** Schrödinger’s model replaced the idea of defined paths (orbits) with a "probability cloud" or an electron density map. This mathematical framework explicitly removed the classical certainty regarding the exact position of an electron.
4.  **Why the others are incorrect:**
    *   **(B)** is the opposite of the truth; the model introduced statistical probability, not certainty.
    *   **(C)** is partially true in that he treated electrons *like* waves, but the fundamental change to atomic theory was the shift from certainty to probability. 
    *   **(D)** is incorrect because modern physics accepts wave-particle duality; Schrödinger did not prove electrons are "not particles," but rather that they exhibit wave-like behavior in their motion around the nucleus.

Therefore, the defining contribution of Schrödinger to atomic theory in the context of HSC Physics is the move toward a probabilistic understanding, effectively removing the certainty of "orbits."
