To find the change in the magnitude of the force acting on the wire, we use the formula for the magnetic force on a current-carrying wire:

$$F = BIL \sin(\theta)$$

where:
*   $B = 2.50\text{ T}$ (magnetic field strength)
*   $I = 1.80\text{ A}$ (current)
*   $L = 0.300\text{ m}$ (length of the wire)
*   $\theta$ is the angle between the wire and the magnetic field.

### Step 1: Calculate the force at Position X
At Position X, the wire is perpendicular to the magnetic field lines (which are pointing into the page). Therefore, $\theta = 90^\circ$, and $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$.
$$F_X = (2.50)(1.80)(0.300) \times 1 = 1.35\text{ N}$$

### Step 2: Calculate the force at Position Y
At Position Y, the wire is rotated by $45^\circ$ from the vertical. The angle between the wire and the magnetic field remains $90^\circ$ because the magnetic field is perpendicular to the plane of rotation. However, in the context of these physics problems, the angle $\theta$ in the formula $F = BIL \sin(\theta)$ refers to the angle between the wire and the magnetic field vector. Since the magnetic field is directed into the page and the wire lies in the plane of the page, the wire remains perpendicular to the field lines regardless of its orientation within that plane.
