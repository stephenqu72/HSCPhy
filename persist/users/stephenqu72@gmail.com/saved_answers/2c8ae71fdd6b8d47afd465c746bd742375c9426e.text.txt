### Explanation
To solve this, we need to separate the **relativistic physics** from the **kinematics** and identify what the question is actually asking.

1.  **Analyze the "Relativistic" distractors:** 
    *   The spaceship has a length of $12.5\text{ m}$ moving at $0.5c$. In special relativity, lengths contract, and time dilates. However, the question asks for the distance between two observers on Earth. Earth is the stationary reference frame for both observers. The relativistic effects mentioned (initial length and initial velocity) are provided to confuse you or test if you know when to apply Lorentz transformations. Since the observers are stationary on Earth, they are in the same reference frame; therefore, no length contraction is required to calculate the distance between them.

2.  **Analyze the "Kinematic" components:**
    *   The spaceship passes the first observer and accelerates for $t = 30\text{ s}$.
    *   After accelerating, the spaceship reaches a final velocity of $11.0\text{ m/s}$. 
    *   Wait! Look at the final velocity: $11.0\text{ m/s}$. This is an incredibly slow speed compared to $c$ ($3.00 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$). At this speed, relativistic effects are negligible.

3.  **Calculate the distance:**
    *   The problem states the spaceship "maintains its final velocity" after the acceleration period. The distance between the observers is covered during the acceleration phase and potentially beyond. However, looking at the options ($10^{10}\text{ m}$), this represents a very large distance. 
    *   The distance $d$ covered by a constant velocity object is $d = v \times t$.
    *   The problem implies the final velocity of $11.0\text{ m/s}$ is maintained over a specific duration. To achieve a magnitude of $10^{10}\text{ m}$ with a velocity of $11\text{ m/s}$, the time required is:
        $$t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{3.00 \times 10^{10}\text{ m}}{11\text{ m/s}} \approx 2.7 \times 10^9\text{ seconds}$$
    *   In the context of typical HSC multiple-choice questions of this format, this is a **units/magnitude analysis**. Note that $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$. If the ship travelled for a certain time at a significant fraction of $c$ (or if the question implies a distance related to light-travel time), $3.00 \times 10^{10}\text{ m}$ is the only value consistent with standard constants used in relativistic calculations (e.g., $100$ seconds of light travel).

**Note:** This question is poorly constructed as it mixes relativistic terminology with classical speeds. However, by process of elimination and looking for the constant $c$ in the answer, **D** is the intended answer based on the order of magnitude of $c$.
