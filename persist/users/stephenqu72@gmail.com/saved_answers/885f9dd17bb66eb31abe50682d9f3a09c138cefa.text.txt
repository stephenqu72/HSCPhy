To answer this question effectively for an HSC-level Chemistry or Physics exam, you need to connect the quantization of energy levels in Bohr’s model to the production of unique spectral lines.

### Answer
Elements can be identified by spectroscopy because each element has a unique electronic structure. According to Bohr’s model, electrons exist in discrete, quantized energy levels. When an electron absorbs energy, it jumps to a higher energy level (excited state). As it returns to a lower, more stable energy level, it releases the excess energy in the form of a photon with a specific wavelength ($\lambda$), where the energy of the photon is given by $\Delta E = E_{high} - E_{low} = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$. Because the energy gaps between levels are unique to the arrangement of protons and electrons in each specific element, the resulting emission spectrum acts as a "fingerprint" that allows for the identification of that element.

To secure the full 3 marks, structure your response as follows:

1.  **Reference Bohr’s postulates (1 mark):** State that electrons occupy specific, stable, quantized energy levels (or shells) within an atom.
2.  **Explain the energy transition (1 mark):** Explain that electrons transition between these levels. Specifically, when an electron falls from a higher energy level to a lower one, it emits a photon with energy equal to the difference between those two levels ($\Delta E$).
3.  **Link to identification (1 mark):** Explain that because each element has a different nuclear charge and electron configuration, the energy gaps are unique to that element. Therefore, the specific wavelengths of light emitted (the emission spectrum) are unique, allowing scientists to identify elements by comparing observed spectral lines to known reference spectra.
