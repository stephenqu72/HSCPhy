### Teacher's Solution and Explanation

#### Part (a): Calculation
To calculate the altitude, we use Kepler's Third Law: $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$.

**Step 1: Identify known values**
*   Period of a geostationary orbit, $T = 24 \text{ hours} = 86,400 \text{ s}$
*   Gravitational constant, $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$
*   Mass of Earth, $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$
*   Radius of Earth, $R_E \approx 6.38 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$

**Step 2: Solve for orbital radius ($r$)**
$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}}$$
$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (86400)^2}{4\pi^2}}$$
$$r \approx 4.22 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$$

**Step 3: Calculate altitude ($h$)**
The orbital radius $r$ is measured from the center of the Earth. To find the altitude $h$ above the Earth's surface:
$$h = r - R_E$$
$$h = 4.22 \times 10^7 \text{ m} - 6.38 \times 10^6 \text{ m} \approx 3.58 \times 10^7 \text{ m} \text{ (or } 35,800 \text{ km)}$$

---

#### Part (b): Justification
A satellite in geostationary orbit remains fixed at a constant position relative to a point on the Earth's surface because its orbital period matches the Earth's rotation (24 hours). This is advantageous for communications because:
1.  **Constant Connectivity:** Ground-based satellite dishes do not need to track the satellite; they can be pointed in a fixed direction permanently.
2.  **Wide Coverage:** Being at a high altitude allows a single satellite to "see" and cover approximately one-third of the Earth's surface, facilitating regional or national communication networks without the need for a complex constellation of moving satellites (as required in Low Earth Orbit).
