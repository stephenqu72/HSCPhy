To solve this problem, we must recognise that for an electron to move in a circular path, the magnetic force must provide the necessary centripetal force.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Identify the given values:**
*   Velocity ($v$) = $3.0 \times 10^6\text{ m s}^{-1}$
*   Radius ($r$) = $10\text{ m}$
*   Charge of an electron ($q$) = $1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$
*   Mass of an electron ($m$) = $9.109 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$

**2. Set up the physics relationship:**
The centripetal force required for circular motion is $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$.
The magnetic force acting on a moving charge is $F_B = qvB$ (assuming the velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field, which is standard for a circular path).
Equating the two:
$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

**3. Rearrange for Magnetic Field ($B$):**
$$B = \frac{mv}{qr}$$

**4. Substitute the values and calculate:**
$$B = \frac{(9.109 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}) \times (3.0 \times 10^6\text{ m s}^{-1})}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}) \times (10\text{ m})}$$
$$B = \frac{2.7327 \times 10^{-24}}{1.602 \times 10^{-18}}$$
$$B \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-6}\text{ T}$$

**Final Answer:**
The magnetic field required is approximately $1.7 \times 10^{-6}\text{ T}$.

### Explanation of the Method:
*   **Concepts:** You must equate the magnetic force expression ($qvB$) with the centripetal force expression ($\frac{mv^2}{r}$). This is the fundamental principle for charged particles in magnetic fields.
*   **Constants:** Ensure you recall or look up the standard values for the mass and charge of an electron, as they are essential for this calculation.
