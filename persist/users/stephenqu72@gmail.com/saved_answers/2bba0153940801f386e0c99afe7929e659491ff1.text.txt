To solve this problem, we use Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion, which relates the orbital period $T$ to the orbital radius $r$.

### 1. Identify Constants and Variables
*   **Altitude ($h$):** $4.00 \times 10^3\text{ km} = 4.00 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
*   **Radius of Earth ($R_E$):** $\approx 6.37 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
*   **Orbital Radius ($r$):** $r = R_E + h = 6.37 \times 10^6 + 4.00 \times 10^6 = 1.037 \times 10^7\text{ m}$
*   **Mass of Earth ($M$):** $\approx 5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$
*   **Gravitational Constant ($G$):** $6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ N m}^2\text{ kg}^{-2}$

### 2. The Formula
Kepler's Third Law states:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$
Rearranging to solve for $T$:
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}}$$

### 3. Calculation
Plug the values into the formula:
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (1.037 \times 10^7)^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24})}}$$
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times 1.115 \times 10^{21}}{3.98 \times 10^{14}}}$$
$$T = \sqrt{110757} \approx 10524\text{ seconds}$$

### 4. Convert to Hours
$$T_{\text{hours}} = \frac{10524}{3600} \approx 2.92\text{ hours}$$

**Answer:** The orbital period of the satellite is approximately **$2.92$ hours**.
