To determine the escape velocity of Voyager 1 from Jupiter, we use the principle of conservation of energy. Escape velocity is defined as the minimum speed needed for an object to break free from the gravitational attraction of a massive body, reaching a point of zero gravitational potential energy at an infinite distance with zero kinetic energy.

### 1. Identifying the Formula
The gravitational potential energy $U$ of an object of mass $m$ at a distance $r$ from a mass $M$ is given by:
$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$
To escape, the object's initial kinetic energy must equal the magnitude of its gravitational potential energy:
$$\frac{1}{2}mv_e^2 = \frac{GMm}{r}$$
Solving for escape velocity ($v_e$):
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}$$

### 2. Given Data
*   Gravitational constant $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3\text{ kg}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-2}$
*   Mass of Jupiter $M = 1.898 \times 10^{27} \text{ kg}$
*   Distance from the centre of Jupiter $r = 3.492 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$

*Note: The distance from the Sun is provided as a distractor. The question specifically asks for the escape velocity from Jupiter, so we only use Jupiter's mass and the distance to its centre.*

### 3. Calculation
Substitute the values into the formula:
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (6.674 \times 10^{-11}) \times (1.898 \times 10^{27})}{3.492 \times 10^8}}$$

First, calculate the numerator:
$$2 \times 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times 1.898 \times 10^{27} \approx 2.533 \times 10^{17}$$

Next, divide by $r$:
$$\frac{2.533 \times 10^{17}}{3.492 \times 10^8} \approx 7.2537 \times 10^8$$

Finally, take the square root:
$$v_e = \sqrt{7.2537 \times 10^8} \approx 26,933 \text{ m/s}$$

Rounding to four significant figures (consistent with the provided data):
$$v_e \approx 2.693 \times 10^4 \text{ m/s} \text{ (or } 26.93 \text{ km/s)}$$
