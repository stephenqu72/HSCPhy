### Explanation
To solve this, you must apply the core definitions from Newton's Laws of Motion:

1.  **Inertial Frame of Reference:** This is a frame that is either at rest or moving at a **constant velocity** (zero acceleration). In an inertial frame, Newton’s First Law holds true without the need for "fictitious" forces.
    *   (A) A lift descending at a steady rate has an acceleration of $a = 0$. This is an inertial frame.
    *   (B) A rocket traveling at a constant velocity has an acceleration of $a = 0$. This is an inertial frame.
    *   (D) While the Earth is technically rotating (which involves centripetal acceleration), in most HSC-level contexts, a building is considered an inertial frame because its acceleration is negligible compared to the scenarios involving powered flight or high-speed maneuvers.

2.  **Non-Inertial Frame of Reference:** This is a frame that is **accelerating** (this includes changing speed or changing direction). In these frames, Newton's Laws appear to be violated unless you introduce fictitious (inertial) forces.
    *   (C) A rocket taking off is undergoing rapid acceleration due to engine thrust. Because it is accelerating, it is a non-inertial frame of reference. If you were inside this rocket, you would feel yourself being pushed back into your seat—this sensation is a classic effect of being in a non-inertial frame.
