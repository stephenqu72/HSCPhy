### a. Identify which of Kepler's laws of planetary motion is supported by the data shown in the graph.

**Answer:** Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion.
**Explanation:** Kepler's Third Law states that the square of the orbital period ($T^2$) is directly proportional to the cube of the orbital radius ($r^3$), expressed as $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \text{constant}$. Since the graph displays a straight-line relationship passing through the origin between $r^3$ and $T^2$, it confirms this direct proportionality.

### b. Use the line of best fit provided in the graph to show that the mass of Saturn is $5.7 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$.

**Explanation:**
1. **Derive the formula:** Starting with Kepler's Third Law derived from Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:
   $$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2} \implies r^3 = \left( \frac{GM}{4\pi^2} \right) T^2$$
   This matches the equation of a straight line $y = mx$, where the gradient $m = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$.
2. **Calculate the gradient:** Pick two points on the line. Using $(0, 0)$ and $(15 \times 10^{10}, 15 \times 10^{25})$:
   $$m = \frac{15 \times 10^{25} - 0}{15 \times 10^{10} - 0} = 1.0 \times 10^{15} \text{ m}^3/\text{s}^2$$
3. **Solve for Mass ($M$):**
   $$1.0 \times 10^{15} = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \cdot M}{4\pi^2}$$
   $$M = \frac{1.0 \times 10^{15} \cdot 4\pi^2}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}} \approx 5.9 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$$
*(Note: Using precise values from the graph points may yield results closer to $5.7 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$ depending on reading accuracy).*

### c. Calculate the escape velocity of a $50\,000 \text{ kg}$ rocket on the surface of Saturn if the radius of Saturn is $5.8 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$.

**Explanation:**
The escape velocity is independent of the object's mass. The formula for escape velocity is:
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}$$
Using $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ Nm}^2/\text{kg}^2$, $M = 5.7 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$, and $R = 5.8 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$:
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot (6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \cdot (5.7 \times 10^{26})}{5.8 \times 10^7}}$$
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{7.60 \times 10^{16}}{5.8 \times 10^7}} = \sqrt{1.31 \times 10^9}$$
$$v_e \approx 36\,200 \text{ m/s} \text{ (or } 36.2 \text{ km/s)}$$
