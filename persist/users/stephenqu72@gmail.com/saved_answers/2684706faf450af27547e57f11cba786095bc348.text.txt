### Answer
The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
James Clerk Maxwell unified the theories of electricity and magnetism into a set of four equations (Maxwell's equations). Through these equations, he derived the existence of transverse waves that propagate through space at a specific velocity, $v$. 

The theoretical velocity of these electromagnetic waves was given by the expression:
$$v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$$

Where:
- $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space.
- $\epsilon_0$ is the permittivity of free space.

When Maxwell plugged the experimental values for these constants into his equation, he calculated a speed of approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s. This matched the experimentally determined speed of light (measured by Fizeau and others at the time) with remarkable accuracy. This correlation led Maxwell to propose that light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** While light was already believed to be a wave (due to interference and diffraction evidence), this did not specifically prove it was an *electromagnetic* wave.
*   **B:** Maxwell’s contribution was primarily theoretical/mathematical at this stage; he did not measure the fields of a light ray experimentally.
*   **D:** The double-slit experiment (Young's experiment) had already been conducted to prove the wave nature of light, but Maxwell's specific breakthrough was the mathematical connection via the speed of light.
