### Part (a)
**Answer:**
*   **Composition:** A star's spectrum contains absorption lines at specific wavelengths unique to the elements present in its outer atmosphere. By comparing these spectral lines (the "fingerprint") against known laboratory emission spectra, we can identify which elements (e.g., hydrogen, helium, calcium) are present.
*   **Temperature:** A star acts approximately as a black body. According to Wien’s Displacement Law, the wavelength of peak intensity ($\lambda_{max}$) is inversely proportional to the surface temperature ($T$). By measuring the wavelength at which the star emits the most radiation, we can calculate the temperature using $\lambda_{max} T = b$. Additionally, the relative strength of specific absorption lines acts as a "thermometer," as different ions are only present at specific excitation temperatures.

**Explanation:** To maximize marks here, ensure you explicitly mention the two distinct concepts: "spectral lines/fingerprints" for composition and "Wien’s Law/black body radiation" for temperature.

### Part (b)
**Answer:**
*   **Explanation:** As a star rotates, one side of the star moves toward the observer (blue-shift) and the other side moves away (red-shift). This causes the photons emitted from different parts of the star to be shifted by varying amounts. Consequently, the single sharp emission line at $656\text{ nm}$ will undergo **spectral line broadening**. The line will become wider (thicker) and less intense, centered around the original $656\text{ nm}$ position.
*   **Modification to diagram:** You should draw a wider, slightly more diffuse vertical bar centered exactly at the $656\text{ nm}$ position to represent the broadening effect.

**Explanation:** This is a classic Doppler effect application. Do not confuse this with a simple shift (which would occur if the entire star were moving away from us); because the star is rotating, we see the cumulative effect of the entire stellar disk, resulting in "rotational broadening."
