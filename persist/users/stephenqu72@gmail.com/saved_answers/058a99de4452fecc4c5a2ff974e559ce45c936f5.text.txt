To solve this problem, we use the relationship between the work function ($\Phi$) and the threshold frequency ($f_0$):

$$\Phi = h f_0$$

Where:
*   $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$)
*   $f_0$ is the threshold frequency ($1 \times 10^9 \text{ MHz}$)

### Step 1: Convert units to standard SI units
The threshold frequency is given in MHz. We must convert this to Hertz (Hz):
$$f_0 = 1 \times 10^9 \text{ MHz} = 1 \times 10^9 \times 10^6 \text{ Hz} = 1 \times 10^{15} \text{ Hz}$$

### Step 2: Calculate the energy in Joules
$$\Phi = (6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}) \times (1 \times 10^{15} \text{ Hz})$$
$$\Phi = 6.626 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

### Step 3: Convert Joules to electron-volts (eV)
Since $1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$, we divide the result by $1.602 \times 10^{-19}$:
$$\Phi = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/eV}}$$
$$\Phi \approx 4.136 \text{ eV}$$

Comparing this result to the provided options, the value is approximately $4.1 \text{ eV}$.
