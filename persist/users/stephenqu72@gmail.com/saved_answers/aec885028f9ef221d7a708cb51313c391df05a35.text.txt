### Solution and Explanation

To solve this problem, we use the standard physical constants for Earth:
*   Mass of Earth ($M$) $\approx 5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg
*   Radius of Earth ($R_E$) $\approx 6.37 \times 10^6$ m
*   Gravitational constant ($G$) $\approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11}$ $\text{m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$

#### (a) Identify the nature of that orbit.
The satellite is at an altitude of $300$ km. Orbits between roughly $160$ km and $2,000$ km are classified as **Low Earth Orbit (LEO)**.

#### (b) Calculate the orbital period of the satellite.
First, calculate the orbital radius ($r$), which is the distance from the center of the Earth:
$$r = R_E + \text{altitude} = 6.37 \times 10^6\text{ m} + 0.3 \times 10^6\text{ m} = 6.67 \times 10^6\text{ m}$$

Using Kepler's Third Law, where $T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2r^3}{GM}$:
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2r^3}{GM}}$$
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (6.67 \times 10^6)^3}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 5.97 \times 10^{24}}}$$
$$T \approx \sqrt{\frac{1.176 \times 10^{22}}{3.98 \times 10^{14}}} \approx \sqrt{2.95 \times 10^7} \approx 5434\text{ seconds}$$
Converting to minutes: $\frac{5434}{60} \approx 90.6$ minutes.

#### (c) Describe the properties of the orbit that would make it useful.
Because the satellite is in LEO, it has the following properties:
1.  **Proximity to Earth:** The low altitude allows for high-resolution imaging (ideal for Earth observation, spy satellites, or mapping) because the sensors are closer to the ground.
2.  **Short Orbital Period:** The satellite orbits the Earth roughly every 90 minutes. This allows for frequent "revisit" times over different areas of the globe.
3.  **Lower Launch Costs:** It requires significantly less energy to place a satellite into LEO compared to higher orbits (like Geostationary orbit), making it cost-effective for smaller satellites.
4.  **Signal Latency:** Because the altitude is low, communication signals experience minimal time delay, making it useful for low-latency communications (e.g., Starlink-style internet constellations).
