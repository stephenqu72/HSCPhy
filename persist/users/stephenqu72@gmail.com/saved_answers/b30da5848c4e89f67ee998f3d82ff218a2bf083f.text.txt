To determine the correct answer, we must refer to the diffraction grating equation:

$$d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$$

Where:
- $d$ is the distance between the slits (grating spacing).
- $\theta$ is the angle of diffraction for the $n$-th order maximum.
- $n$ is the order of the maximum.
- $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light.

The "separation of the maxima" on the screen is determined by the angle $\theta$. For small angles (which is often the case in these problems), we can approximate $\sin(\theta) \approx \tan(\theta) \approx \frac{y}{L}$, where $y$ is the linear distance from the central maximum and $L$ is the distance to the screen. Substituting this into the diffraction equation gives:

$$y \approx \frac{n \lambda L}{d}$$

### Analysis of the options:
*   **(A) Using light with a longer wavelength:** If $\lambda$ increases, $y$ increases. The separation increases. Incorrect.
*   **(B) Using a grating with a greater slit separation:** If $d$ increases, $y$ decreases because $d$ is in the denominator. This makes the maxima appear closer together. **Correct.**
*   **(C) Not using monochromatic light:** This would cause the maxima to smear into spectra because different wavelengths diffract at different angles, not specifically decreasing the separation of the set pattern. Incorrect.
*   **(D) Increasing the distance between the screen and grating:** If $L$ increases, $y$ increases. The separation increases. Incorrect.

**Conclusion:** 
To decrease the separation of the maxima, we need to increase the value of $d$ (the slit separation).

The correct answer is **(B)**.
