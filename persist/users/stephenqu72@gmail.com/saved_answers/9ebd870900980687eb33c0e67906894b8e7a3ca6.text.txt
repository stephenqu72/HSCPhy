### Solutions

**Part (a)**
The force exerted by the car on the road is its weight ($F_g$). We use the formula $F_g = mg$, where $g \approx 9.8 \text{ ms}^{-2}$.
$$18,250 = m \times 9.8$$
$$m = \frac{18,250}{9.8} \approx 1862.24 \text{ kg}$$
Rounding to a sensible number of significant figures (based on the input), the mass is **$1862 \text{ kg}$**.

**Part (b)**
For an object moving in a circle at a constant speed, the centripetal acceleration is given by $a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$.
Given $v = 20 \text{ ms}^{-1}$ and $r = 25.0 \text{ m}$:
$$a_c = \frac{20^2}{25.0} = \frac{400}{25.0} = 16 \text{ ms}^{-2}$$
The acceleration is **$16 \text{ ms}^{-2}$** toward the center of the curve.

**Part (c)**
To find the net force (centripetal force) on the driver, we use Newton’s Second Law, $F_{net} = ma$. We use the mass of the driver ($68 \text{ kg}$) and the acceleration calculated in part (b).
$$F_{net} = 68 \times 16$$
$$F_{net} = 1088 \text{ N}$$
The net force on the driver is **$1088 \text{ N}$**.

### Explanation of How to Solve

1.  **For part (a):** Recognize that a stationary object on a flat surface exerts a force on the ground equal to its weight. Use the standard gravitational field strength of $g = 9.8 \text{ ms}^{-2}$. Rearrange the formula $W = mg$ to solve for $m$.
2.  **For part (b):** Identify that the car is performing circular motion. Even though the speed is constant, the *direction* is changing, which results in centripetal acceleration. Use the standard circular motion formula $a = \frac{v^2}{r}$.
3.  **For part (c):** Apply Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$). Since the driver is inside the car moving in a circle, they must also be accelerating at the same rate as the car. Use the mass provided for the driver specifically and the acceleration value found in the previous step.
