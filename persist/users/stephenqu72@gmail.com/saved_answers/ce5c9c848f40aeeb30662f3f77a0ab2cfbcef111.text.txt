#### Part (a)
**Answer:**
The diffraction pattern observed in the transmission electron microscope demonstrates the wave-like nature of electrons. In classical physics, electrons were strictly considered as point-like particles with a defined mass and trajectory. According to classical mechanics, particles should not produce interference or diffraction patterns; these phenomena are characteristic of waves. The experimental evidence shown here confirms de Broglie’s hypothesis that matter exhibits wave properties, with a wavelength given by $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$. This directly contradicts the classical view of matter, as electrons must undergo diffraction and constructive interference upon interacting with the crystal lattice, proving that classical physics is insufficient to describe the quantum behavior of matter.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
Erwin Schrödinger contributed to this model by proposing a wave-mechanical approach to the atom. Rather than treating electrons as discrete particles orbiting in fixed paths (as in the Bohr model), Schrödinger treated electrons as three-dimensional standing waves described by a wave function, $\psi$. He formulated the Schrödinger equation, which provides the probability density of finding an electron in a particular region of space. This shifted the understanding of the atom from defined orbits to "orbitals"—regions where an electron is most likely to be found—resulting in the "electron cloud" model presented in the image.
