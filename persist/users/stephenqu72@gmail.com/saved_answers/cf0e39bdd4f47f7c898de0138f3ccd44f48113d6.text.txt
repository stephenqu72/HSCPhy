### Answer and Explanation

**Answer:** (B) Quarks, electrons and neutrinos

**Explanation:**
To solve this question, we must understand the definition of a "fundamental particle" in the context of the Standard Model. A fundamental particle is one that has no known internal structure—it is not composed of smaller parts.

1.  **Analyze the terminology:**
    *   **Quarks:** These are fundamental particles that make up composite particles like hadrons.
    *   **Leptons:** These include the electron, the neutrino, the muon, and the tau. They are fundamental particles.
    *   **Hadrons (Baryons and Mesons):** These are **not** fundamental. They are composite particles made up of quarks held together by the strong nuclear force.
    *   **Fermions:** This is a broad category that includes *all* quarks and *all* leptons (like electrons and neutrinos). While all fundamental matter particles are fermions, "fermion" itself is a classification of spin, not a specific list of fundamental particles.

2.  **Evaluate the options:**
    *   **(A)** Incorrect because "fermions" includes composite particles like baryons, which are not fundamental.
    *   **(B) Correct.** Quarks, electrons, and neutrinos are all elementary (fundamental) particles within the Standard Model.
    *   **(C)** Incorrect because hadrons are composite particles, not fundamental.
    *   **(D)** Incorrect because hadrons and baryons are composite particles made of quarks.

In the HSC Physics curriculum, it is essential to distinguish between the building blocks of matter (quarks and leptons, such as electrons and neutrinos) and the composite particles they form (hadrons).
