To answer this question effectively, you must identify two key experiments that provided quantitative data verifying the predictions of Special Relativity regarding time dilation.

### Answer

**1. The Muon Experiment (Rossi and Hall, 1941):**
Muons are unstable subatomic particles created in the upper atmosphere with a very short mean lifetime ($\approx 2.2 \mu s$). According to classical physics, they should decay before reaching the Earth's surface. However, experiments measured a significantly higher number of muons reaching detectors at sea level than predicted. Because muons travel at relativistic speeds (close to $c$), their internal clocks run slower relative to an Earth-based observer. This time dilation allows them to travel a much greater distance before decaying, providing quantitative confirmation of the factor $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$.

**2. The Hafele-Keating Experiment (1971):**
In this experiment, scientists placed high-precision atomic clocks on commercial airliners and flew them around the world in both eastward and westward directions. When compared to stationary atomic clocks at the US Naval Observatory, the moving clocks showed a discrepancy in elapsed time that matched the predictions of both Special Relativity (time dilation due to velocity) and General Relativity (gravitational time dilation). The observed differences in nanoseconds provided precise, quantitative empirical support for time dilation.
