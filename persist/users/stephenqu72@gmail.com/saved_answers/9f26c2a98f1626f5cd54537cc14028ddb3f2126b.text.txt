### Explanation

To solve this, we must look at the historical contribution of James Clerk Maxwell in the context of the HSC Physics syllabus ("Electromagnetism" module):

1.  **Analysis of (A):** Maxwell’s most significant theoretical contribution was the synthesis of existing experimental laws (Gauss’s laws, Ampère’s law, and Faraday’s law) into a unified set of four partial differential equations, now known as **Maxwell's Equations**. By manipulating these equations, he derived the wave equation and calculated the theoretical velocity of electromagnetic waves. He discovered that this velocity was approximately equal to the measured speed of light, leading him to the conclusion that light itself is an electromagnetic wave. This unification is the foundational pillar of modern electromagnetism.
2.  **Analysis of (B):** This was an experimental contribution made later by **Heinrich Hertz**, who used an oscillating spark gap to detect the electromagnetic waves predicted by Maxwell.
3.  **Analysis of (C):** While Maxwell's theory predicts that electric and magnetic fields in an electromagnetic wave are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation, this is a property of the wave described by his equations, not an "observation" he made himself. 
4.  **Analysis of (D):** The speed of light being a constant (specifically, independent of the motion of the source or observer) is a postulate of **Albert Einstein's Theory of Special Relativity**, which corrected Newtonian physics to align with Maxwell's equations. While Maxwell predicted that electromagnetic waves travel at a specific speed, the "constant" nature of the speed of light in all inertial frames was Einstein's breakthrough.

Therefore, the unification of electricity and magnetism is the primary theoretical contribution that fundamentally changed our understanding of the nature of light.
