### Given Data:
- Radius ($r$) = $25.6\text{ m}$
- Mass ($m$) = $885\text{ kg}$
- Frictional Force ($F_f$) = $9420\text{ N}$

---

### Part (a)
**Calculation:**
For a car on a flat track, the centripetal force is provided entirely by friction.
$$F_f = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$9420 = \frac{885 \times v^2}{25.6}$$
$$v^2 = \frac{9420 \times 25.6}{885} \approx 272.4$$
$$v \approx 16.5\text{ m/s}$$

**Explanation:** Rearrange the formula for centripetal force to solve for $v$. Since the friction is the *only* force keeping the car on the curve, setting it equal to $mv^2/r$ allows us to find the maximum velocity before the tires lose grip.

---

### Part (b)
**Explanation:** 
In wet weather, water acts as a lubricant, significantly reducing the coefficient of friction ($\mu$) between the tyres and the road. Since the maximum centripetal force is defined by $F_f = \mu N = \mu mg$, a decrease in $\mu$ leads to a decrease in the maximum allowable centripetal force. Because $F_c \propto v^2$, the maximum speed $v$ must decrease to ensure the car does not skid outward off the path of the curve.

---

### Part (c)
**Calculation:** 
For a banked turn (ignoring friction for the "ideal" speed to isolate the effect of banking), the centripetal force is provided by the horizontal component of the normal force ($N \sin \theta$):
$$\tan \theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$
$$v = \sqrt{rg \tan \theta}$$
$$v = \sqrt{25.6 \times 9.8 \times \tan(8.0^\circ)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{25.6 \times 9.8 \times 0.1405} \approx \sqrt{35.25} \approx 5.94\text{ m/s}$$

*Note:* In an HSC context, when comparing to a flat road with friction, the banking provides an *additional* component of force toward the center. The total force available is $F_{net} = F_{friction} + N_{horizontal}$. By banking at $8.0^\circ$, the normal force provides a component of $mg \tan \theta$ toward the center, allowing the car to maintain its path at higher speeds even if friction remains constant. The combined max speed becomes $v = \sqrt{gr \frac{\mu + \tan \theta}{1 - \mu \tan \theta}}$.
