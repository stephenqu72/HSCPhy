To evaluate this experimental design for a 9-mark HSC question, we must look at **reliability, validity, and accuracy**.

### Evaluation of the Design

1.  **Validity (Is it testing the right variable?):** The design is flawed because the student is placing the rod "at one end of the solenoid." The magnetic field $B$ is only uniform *inside* the solenoid. Near the ends (fringing fields), the field is non-uniform and weaker. This compromises the validity as the force $F = IlB\sin\theta$ requires a constant $B$ across the length $l$.
2.  **Reliability (Are the results consistent?):** The student intends to "take one set of readings." In scientific investigations, reliability is improved through **repetition**. One set of readings is insufficient to identify outliers or ensure consistency.
3.  **Accuracy (Calculations and Sensitivity):** 
    *   The magnetic field $B$ inside a solenoid is $B = \mu_0 n I$. 
    *   $n = \frac{750}{0.14} \approx 5357 \text{ turns/m}$.
    *   $B = (4\pi \times 10^{-7}) \times 5357 \times 5.0 \approx 0.0336 \text{ T}$.
    *   The force $F = IlB$. For $V = 12\text{ V}$, $I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{12}{6} = 2.0\text{ A}$.
    *   Assuming $l = 0.060\text{ m}$: $F = 2.0 \times 0.060 \times 0.0336 \approx 0.0040\text{ N}$.
    *   Converting to mass on scales: $m = \frac{F}{g} = \frac{0.0040}{9.8} \approx 0.00041\text{ kg} = 0.41\text{ g}$.
    *   The scales have a precision of $1.00 \times 10^{-5}\text{ kg}$ ($0.01\text{ g}$). The scale is sufficiently sensitive to detect the change, as $0.01\text{ g}$ is well within the predicted change of $\approx 0.4\text{ g}$.

*   **Identify Flaws:**
    *   **Positioning:** State that the rod must be placed in the *centre* of the solenoid to ensure a uniform magnetic field ($B$). 
    *   **Methodology:** Mention the lack of repeat trials (need to calculate averages).
    *   **Control Variables:** Identify that the temperature of the copper rod might increase, changing resistance—a constant current should ideally be maintained or monitored.
*   **The Calculation:** Show the calculation for $B$ and the expected change in mass to prove that the equipment chosen *is* sensitive enough to measure the effect, which is a key scientific assessment skill.
