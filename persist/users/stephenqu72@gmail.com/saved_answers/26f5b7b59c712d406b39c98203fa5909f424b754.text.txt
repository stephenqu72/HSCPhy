### Answer
The correct answer is **B. transverse wave model for light.**

---

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental physical requirements for polarisation to occur:

1.  **Defining Polarisation:** Polarisation is the process of restricting the oscillations of a wave to a single plane.
2.  **Transverse vs. Longitudinal Waves:**
    *   **Longitudinal waves** (like sound waves) have oscillations that occur parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Because they vibrate along the axis of travel, there is no "side-to-side" or "up-and-down" orientation to filter out; therefore, longitudinal waves cannot be polarised.
    *   **Transverse waves** have oscillations that occur perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction of energy transfer. Because these oscillations exist in multiple planes perpendicular to the direction of motion, they can be filtered so that only waves vibrating in a specific plane pass through (e.g., using a polarising filter).
3.  **Application to Light:** Since experiments (such as passing light through a polaroid filter) show that light can be polarised, light must oscillate perpendicularly to its direction of travel. This is the defining characteristic of a transverse wave.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** A standing wave is a result of interference between two waves traveling in opposite directions; it is not a fundamental category of wave motion that defines polarisation.
*   **C:** Light does not require a medium to travel (it travels through a vacuum), so it is not a mechanical wave.
*   **D:** As established above, longitudinal waves cannot be polarised.
