### Part (a) Explanation
To earn the 2 marks here, you must reference **path difference** and **constructive interference**.

**Answer:**
A bright fringe is formed at point P because the light waves originating from slits $S_1$ and $S_2$ undergo **constructive interference**. This occurs because the path difference between the two rays reaching point P is an integer multiple of the wavelength ($n\lambda$), where $n = 0, 1, 2, ...$. Consequently, the waves arrive in phase, crest-to-crest and trough-to-trough, resulting in a resultant wave with increased amplitude (intensity).

---

### Part (b) Calculation
To calculate the wavelength, we use the Young’s double-slit formula for bright fringes:
$$\Delta x = \frac{n\lambda L}{d}$$
Where:
*   $\Delta x$ = distance from the central fringe ($1.26 \times 10^{-2}\text{ m}$)
*   $d$ = slit separation ($4.0 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}$)
*   $L$ = distance to screen ($2.00\text{ m}$)
*   $n$ = fringe order

**Step 1: Determine the fringe order ($n$)**
Looking at Figure 11b, point C is the central fringe ($n=0$). Counting the bright fringes starting from C:
- C is $n=0$
- The next bright fringe is $n=1$
- The next is $n=2$
- The next is $n=3$
- The next is $n=4$
- The next is $n=5$
- P is the $6^{\text{th}}$ bright fringe from the center, so **$n=6$**.

**Step 2: Calculate $\lambda$**
Rearrange the formula: $\lambda = \frac{d \cdot \Delta x}{n \cdot L}$

$$\lambda = \frac{(4.0 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}) \times (1.26 \times 10^{-2}\text{ m})}{6 \times 2.00\text{ m}}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{5.04 \times 10^{-6}}{12}$$
$$\lambda = 4.2 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$$

Converting to nanometers (often requested in HSC exams):
**$\lambda = 420\text{ nm}$**
