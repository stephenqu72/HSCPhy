To find the minimum wavelength of photons required to overcome the work function, we use the relationship between photon energy and wavelength derived from the photoelectric effect.

### 1. Identify the given values
*   Work function ($\Phi$): $4.5\text{ eV}$
*   Planck’s constant ($h$): $6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
*   Speed of light ($c$): $3.00 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$
*   Conversion factor: $1\text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$

### 2. Convert units
First, convert the work function from electronvolts ($\text{eV}$) to Joules ($\text{J}$):
$$\Phi = 4.5 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19} = 7.209 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$$

### 3. Apply the formula
The energy of a photon is given by $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$. Since the work function is the minimum energy required to eject an electron, we set $E = \Phi$ and rearrange for $\lambda$:
$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{\Phi}$$

Substitute the values:
$$\lambda = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)}{7.209 \times 10^{-19}}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{7.209 \times 10^{-19}}$$
$$\lambda \approx 2.757 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$$

Rounding this to the most appropriate option provided, we get $2.76 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$.

**Correct Option: (D)**

### Explanation
To solve this, we relate the work function to the threshold wavelength. The work function is the minimum energy required to liberate an electron from a metal surface. A photon with energy exactly equal to the work function will have the longest wavelength (threshold wavelength) capable of causing photoemission; any wavelength shorter than this will also provide sufficient energy. Using the fundamental constant equation $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$, we calculate the required wavelength by ensuring the units are consistent (converting $\text{eV}$ to Joules is essential).
