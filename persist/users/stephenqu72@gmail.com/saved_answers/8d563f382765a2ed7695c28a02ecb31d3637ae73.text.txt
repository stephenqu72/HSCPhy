To solve this problem, we need to determine the period ($T$) of the wave from the oscilloscope trace and then use the relationship between frequency ($f$) and period.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Understand the Axes:** The horizontal axis represents time ($t$) in milliseconds (ms).
2.  **Determine the Period ($T$):** The period is the time taken for one full cycle. Looking at the grid, we can count the number of cycles between the $0$ and $10$ ms marks.
    *   Starting at $t = 0$, there are $4.5$ cycles that occur by the time we reach $10$ ms.
    *   Alternatively, let's look at one specific cycle. From the first peak to the next peak:
        *   The first peak is at $0.5$ ms.
        *   The second peak is at $2.75$ ms.
        *   $T = 2.75\text{ ms} - 0.5\text{ ms} = 2.25\text{ ms}$.
    *   Let's check the $10$ ms mark: $10\text{ ms} / 2.25\text{ ms} \approx 4.44$ cycles. Given the grid spacing, each major block is $2.5$ ms (since $10$ ms spans $4$ major blocks). Therefore, each small division is $0.25$ ms.
    *   The period $T$ is $2.25\text{ ms}$, which is $0.00225$ seconds.

3.  **Calculate the Frequency ($f$):**
    The formula relating frequency and period is:
    $$f = \frac{1}{T}$$
    Substituting our value for $T$:
    $$f = \frac{1}{0.00225\text{ s}}$$
    $$f \approx 444.44\text{ Hz}$$

Rounding to the nearest appropriate value provided in the options, we get $440$ Hz.
