### Answer
The refinement of the atomic model was driven by observations that contradicted existing theories:

*   **Rutherford:** Through the **Geiger-Marsden (gold foil) experiment**, Rutherford observed that while most alpha particles passed straight through a gold foil, a small percentage were deflected at large angles. This led to the rejection of the "plum pudding" model and the proposal of the **nuclear model**, where a small, dense, positively charged nucleus exists at the center of the atom, surrounded by empty space.
*   **Bohr:** Rutherford’s model failed to explain why electrons did not lose energy and spiral into the nucleus. Bohr analyzed **atomic emission spectra** (specifically for Hydrogen) and observed discrete spectral lines rather than a continuous spectrum. He proposed that electrons exist in **quantized energy levels (stationary states)** and only emit or absorb light (photons) when transitioning between these fixed levels.
*   **de Broglie:** Bohr’s model could not explain *why* energy levels were quantized. De Broglie proposed the theory of **wave-particle duality**, suggesting that particles like electrons possess a wavelength given by $\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$. This implies that electrons move in standing waves around the nucleus. This theoretical refinement provided the physical basis for Bohr’s quantization, explaining that orbits are stable only when the circumference is an integer multiple of the electron's wavelength, eventually leading to the quantum mechanical (Schrödinger) model.
