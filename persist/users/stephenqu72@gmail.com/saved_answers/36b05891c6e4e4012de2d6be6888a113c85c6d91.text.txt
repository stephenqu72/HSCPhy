### Answer

The relationship between the supplied emf ($V$), back emf ($\varepsilon$), and the net emf ($V_{net}$) in a motor is governed by the principle of conservation of energy. As a motor spins, it acts as both a motor and a generator, creating a "back emf" that opposes the supplied voltage. The net emf driving the current through the internal resistance ($r$) of the motor coil is given by:

$$V_{net} = V - \varepsilon = Ir$$

1. **At start-up:** The speed of the motor is zero, so the back emf ($\varepsilon$) is zero. Consequently, the net emf is equal to the full supply voltage ($V_{net} = V$). This results in a high "inrush current" ($I = V/r$).
2. **As it accelerates:** As the motor gains speed, the magnetic flux through the coils changes more rapidly, causing the back emf to increase. As $\varepsilon$ increases, the net emf ($V - \varepsilon$) decreases, which in turn reduces the current flowing through the motor.
3. **At constant speed:** The motor reaches a steady state where the back emf is just enough to keep the net emf at a value that provides the current necessary to maintain constant rotation against load and friction.

*(In this graph, $V$ is a horizontal constant line, $\varepsilon$ starts at $0$ and curves upwards towards $V$, and $V_{net}$ starts at $V$ and drops towards $0$ as the motor reaches operating speed.)*
