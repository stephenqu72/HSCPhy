The correct answer is **C: to produce coherent light at $S_1$ and $S_2$.**

### Explanation
To solve this, we must understand the fundamental requirements for observing a stable interference pattern in Young’s Double Slit Experiment:

1.  **Coherence:** Two light sources are coherent if they maintain a constant phase relationship over time. If the light source were to strike the double slits directly, the wavefronts arriving at $S_1$ and $S_2$ might not be in phase, or they might change phase randomly.
2.  **The Role of the Single Slit:** By placing a single narrow slit $S$ in front of the monochromatic light source, we act as a "point source" (via diffraction). The light waves emanating from $S$ act as the primary wavefront. When this single wavefront reaches the two slits $S_1$ and $S_2$ simultaneously, $S_1$ and $S_2$ act as secondary sources. 
3.  **Why it works:** Because both $S_1$ and $S_2$ originate from the *same* wavefront from the single slit, any phase variations in the original source affect both $S_1$ and $S_2$ identically. This ensures that the light waves arriving at $S_1$ and $S_2$ are in phase with each other, fulfilling the condition for coherence required to produce a clear, sustained interference pattern on the screen.
