To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental structure and purpose of a Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram in astrophysics.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) apparent brightness**.

### Explanation
An H-R diagram is a scatter plot that shows the relationship between the **intrinsic** physical properties of stars. Let's break down why each option fits or doesn't fit:

*   **(A) Surface Temperature:** This is plotted on the x-axis (often decreasing from left to right). It is an intrinsic property determined by the star's peak emission wavelength (Wien's Law).
*   **(B) Apparent Brightness (Correct Option):** Apparent brightness (or apparent magnitude) depends on two factors: how much light the star actually emits (luminosity) and **how far away the star is from Earth**. Because the H-R diagram describes the intrinsic properties of stars regardless of their distance from us, it does not represent apparent brightness. A very dim, nearby star could have the same apparent brightness as a very luminous, distant star, so apparent brightness cannot be used to classify stars on an H-R plot.
*   **(C) Spectral Class:** This is closely related to surface temperature and is often plotted alongside or on the x-axis (e.g., O, B, A, F, G, K, M).
*   **(D) Luminosity:** This is plotted on the y-axis. It represents the total energy radiated by the star per unit time, which is an intrinsic property.

**Summary for HSC students:** Always remember that the H-R diagram maps *intrinsic* properties ($L$ and $T$) to evolutionary stages. Apparent brightness is an *extrinsic* property that depends on the observer's location, making it unsuitable for a universal classification diagram.
