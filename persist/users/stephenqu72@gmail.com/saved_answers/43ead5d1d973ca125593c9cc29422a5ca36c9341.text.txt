To determine the ratio of the velocities of two satellites, we use the formula for orbital velocity:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

where $G$ is the gravitational constant, $M$ is the mass of the Earth, and $r$ is the orbital radius (distance from the center of the Earth).

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Determine the radii for both satellites:**
The orbital radius $r$ is the sum of the Earth's radius ($R_E \approx 6371 \text{ km}$) and the altitude ($h$).
*   For the low Earth orbit (LEO) satellite: $r_1 = 6371 + 300 = 6671 \text{ km}$
*   For the geostationary (GEO) satellite: $r_2 = 6371 + 35700 = 42071 \text{ km}$

**2. Set up the ratio:**
We want to find the ratio $v_1 : v_2$:
$$\frac{v_1}{v_2} = \frac{\sqrt{\frac{GM}{r_1}}}{\sqrt{\frac{GM}{r_2}}} = \sqrt{\frac{r_2}{r_1}}$$

**3. Substitute the values and calculate:**
$$\frac{v_1}{v_2} = \sqrt{\frac{42071}{6671}}$$
$$\frac{v_1}{v_2} = \sqrt{6.3065...} \approx 2.511$$

**4. Express as a simplified ratio:**
Since $2.511 \approx \frac{25}{10} = \frac{5}{2}$, the simplified ratio of the velocity of the LEO satellite to the geostationary satellite is approximately **$2.5:1$** or **$5:2$**.

*(Note: Depending on the precision of the Earth's radius used, values may vary slightly, but $2.5:1$ is the standard expectation for this HSC-level calculation.)*
