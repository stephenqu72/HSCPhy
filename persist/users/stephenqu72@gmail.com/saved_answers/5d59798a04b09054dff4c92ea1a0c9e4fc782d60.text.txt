To answer this question effectively for an HSC Physics exam, you need to identify the specific physical inefficiencies inherent in real-world transformer operation and link each to a corresponding engineering design strategy.

### Answer
In practical transformers, energy is lost primarily as heat, meaning the output power is always less than the input power ($P_{out} < P_{in}$). The main shortcomings and their corresponding improvements are:

1.  **Eddy Currents:** The changing magnetic flux induces circular currents in the iron core, which generate heat (Joule heating).
    *   **Strategy:** The core is **laminated** (made of thin, insulated sheets) to increase resistance and break the path of eddy currents.
2.  **Resistance in Windings:** The copper wires used for the coils have inherent resistance, leading to energy loss via the Joule effect ($P = I^2R$).
    *   **Strategy:** Use thicker copper wire to reduce resistance or choose materials with high conductivity.
3.  **Flux Leakage:** Not all magnetic flux produced by the primary coil reaches the secondary coil due to magnetic field lines escaping into the air.
    *   **Strategy:** Wind the primary and secondary coils tightly together or use a closed soft-iron core with high magnetic permeability to "guide" the flux.
4.  **Hysteresis Losses:** Reversing the magnetic polarity of the core 50 times per second (for 50 Hz AC) requires energy to overcome the internal magnetic friction of the core material.
    *   **Strategy:** Construct the core from "soft" iron (e.g., silicon steel), which is easily magnetized and demagnetized.
