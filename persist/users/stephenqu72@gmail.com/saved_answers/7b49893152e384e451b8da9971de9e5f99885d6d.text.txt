### Answer
Maxwell’s electromagnetic theory is compatible with the production of radio waves because it establishes that an accelerating electric charge creates a changing electric field. According to Maxwell’s equations, a changing electric field induces a changing magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field, in turn, induces a changing electric field. 

In a transmitting antenna, an alternating current causes electrons to accelerate and decelerate (oscillate). This oscillation creates a time-varying electric field and a time-varying magnetic field that are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. These self-propagating, mutually inducing fields form an electromagnetic wave that radiates outward from the antenna at the speed of light, $c$.

To approach this HSC-level physics question, you must demonstrate a conceptual understanding of Maxwell's contribution to electromagnetism. Follow these steps:

1.  **Identify the link between current and charge:** Start by noting that an alternating current in an antenna involves the oscillation (acceleration) of electrons.
2.  **State Maxwell's principle:** Connect the acceleration of charges to Maxwell’s prediction that accelerated charges generate time-varying electric and magnetic fields.
3.  **Explain the self-propagation mechanism:** Clearly articulate how, according to Maxwell, these fields are linked: a changing electric field ($dE/dt$) generates a magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field ($dB/dt$) generates an electric field.
4.  **Synthesize:** Conclude that this continuous cycle of mutual induction allows the electromagnetic wave to exist and travel through space, confirming that the physical movement of charges in an antenna is exactly what is required to initiate the process Maxwell described theoretically.

Key terms to include for full marks: "accelerating charges," "time-varying electric/magnetic fields," "mutual induction," and "self-propagation."
