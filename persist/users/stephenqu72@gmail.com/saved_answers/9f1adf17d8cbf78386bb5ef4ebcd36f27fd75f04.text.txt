### Answer
The correct answer is **(D)**.

### Explanation
The Geiger-Marsden experiment involved firing high-energy alpha particles ($He^{2+}$ nuclei) at a very thin sheet of gold foil. Based on the "plum pudding" model of the atom (which was the accepted theory at the time), physicists expected that the positive charge and mass of the atom were spread out, meaning alpha particles should pass through with only very minor deflections.

However, the experiment produced three key observations that led to the development of the nuclear model of the atom:
1.  **Most alpha particles passed straight through the foil undeflected.** This provided evidence that the atom is mostly empty space.
2.  **A small fraction of alpha particles were deflected at various angles.** This indicated the presence of a concentrated, positively charged nucleus that repelled the positively charged alpha particles.
3.  **A very small number (about 1 in 8,000) were reflected back at angles greater than $90^{\circ}$.** This suggested that the nucleus contains almost all of the atom's mass and is extremely dense.

Since the question asks for the **most** correct description of an observation made, we look for the most frequent outcome. The vast majority of the alpha particles travelled through the gold foil without encountering significant resistance, proving the majority of the atomic volume is empty space.

*   (A) is incorrect because alpha particles are highly penetrating and were not absorbed by the thin foil.
*   (B) is incorrect because this only happened to a tiny fraction of the particles.
*   (C) is incorrect because large deflections were extremely rare, not the "most" common observation.
