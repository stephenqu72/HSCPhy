### Answer
The experimental results demonstrate that the emission of photoelectrons is dependent on the **frequency** of the incident light, not its **intensity**. 

1.  **Classical Wave Theory Failure:** According to classical wave theory, light is a continuous wave. It predicts that if the intensity (amplitude) of the light is increased, the energy delivered to the metal surface would increase regardless of frequency. Eventually, enough energy should be absorbed by electrons to overcome the work function ($\Phi$) of the metal. Julie’s hypothesis is based on this classical misunderstanding.
2.  **Observation:** The experiment shows that even with high-intensity light, if the frequency is below the threshold frequency ($f_0$), no emission occurs. This confirms that the energy of individual photons is insufficient to overcome the work function.
3.  **Quantum Nature of Light:** The observation provides evidence that light behaves as discrete "packets" of energy called photons. The energy of a photon is given by $E = hf$. 
4.  **Evidence:** 
    *   One-to-one interaction: A single electron absorbs exactly one photon. If $hf < \Phi$, the electron does not gain enough energy to escape, no matter how many photons (intensity) hit the surface per second.
    *   Threshold frequency: Emission only occurs when $f > f_0$, where $hf_0 = \Phi$.
    *   Intensity impact: Increasing intensity only increases the *number* of photons per second (the rate of emission), but cannot force emission if the individual photon energy is too low.
