To solve this problem, we must analyze how the kinetic energy ($K$) of a projectile changes over time.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Understand Kinetic Energy:** Kinetic energy is defined as $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Since mass ($m$) is constant, $K$ is proportional to the square of the magnitude of the velocity ($v^2$).
2.  **Analyze Velocity Components:** The velocity of a projectile at any time $t$ is the vector sum of its horizontal component ($v_x$) and vertical component ($v_y$):
    *   $v_x = v_0 \cos(\theta)$ (constant, as there is no horizontal acceleration).
    *   $v_y = v_0 \sin(\theta) - gt$ (changes linearly with time due to gravity).
3.  **Combine into Velocity Squared:** The square of the magnitude of velocity is $v^2 = v_x^2 + v_y^2$. Substituting the components:
    $$v^2 = (v_0 \cos\theta)^2 + (v_0 \sin\theta - gt)^2$$
    Expanding this, we get:
    $$v^2 = v_0^2 \cos^2\theta + v_0^2 \sin^2\theta - 2v_0 \sin\theta gt + g^2t^2$$
    Since $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$:
    $$v^2 = v_0^2 - 2v_0 \sin\theta gt + g^2t^2$$
4.  **Interpret the Mathematical Function:** This equation is a quadratic in terms of $t$ (of the form $at^2 + bt + c$). A quadratic function with a positive $t^2$ coefficient represents a parabola that **opens upward**.
5.  **Physical Reasoning:**
    *   At the start, the ball has both horizontal and vertical velocity.
    *   As the ball rises, the vertical component $v_y$ decreases, reaching zero at the peak, so $K$ reaches its **minimum** value (but is not zero, because $v_x$ is still present).
    *   After the peak, the vertical speed increases again, causing the total kinetic energy to increase.
    *   Therefore, the graph must start at a high value, decrease to a minimum, and increase again to the same value it started with.

Graph **D** is the only one that shows a parabolic curve with a non-zero minimum, which correctly represents the variation of kinetic energy for a projectile.

**Correct Answer: D**
