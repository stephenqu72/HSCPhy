### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the fundamental difference between random and systematic errors in experimental physics:

1.  **Random Errors:** These occur due to unpredictable fluctuations in an experiment (e.g., small variations in human reaction time or slight changes in environmental conditions). Because these fluctuations are equally likely to be positive or negative, they cluster around the "true" value. By taking multiple measurements and calculating the **mean (average)**, these errors tend to cancel each other out, thereby reducing their impact on the final result.

2.  **Systematic Errors:** These occur due to a consistent, predictable flaw in the experimental setup or equipment (e.g., a "zero error" on a balance or a calibration drift). Because the error is biased in one direction (it is always too high or too low by the same amount), repeating the measurement will simply replicate the same error every single time. Consequently, calculating the mean will **not** reduce or eliminate a systematic error.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **B and C:** These are incorrect because repeating readings does not address the bias inherent in systematic errors; it only improves precision, not accuracy, regarding systematic issues.
*   **D:** This is incorrect because random errors are specifically mitigated by the process of averaging repeated trials.
