### Explanation:

To understand why (A) is the correct answer, we must look at the nuclear physics governing Beta-minus ($\beta^-$) decay.

1.  **The Process:** In $\beta^-$ decay, a neutron within the nucleus is converted into a proton. This process releases an electron (the beta particle) and an electron antineutrino. The nuclear equation is represented as:
    $${}^{1}_{0}n \rightarrow {}^{1}_{1}p + {}^{0}_{-1}e + \bar{\nu}_{e}$$

2.  **Why it happens:** Nuclei have a specific "ratio" of neutrons to protons that keeps them stable. If a nucleus has an excess of neutrons compared to protons, it becomes unstable. By converting a neutron into a proton, the nucleus moves closer to a more stable neutron-to-proton ratio.

3.  **Evaluating the distractors:**
    *   **(B) "When a nucleus is unstable":** While technically true that all radioactive decay occurs because a nucleus is unstable, this is too broad. Alpha decay and Beta-plus decay also occur when a nucleus is unstable. We look for the most specific condition provided.
    *   **(C) "Extreme heat and gravitational pressure":** These are conditions related to stellar nucleosynthesis or fusion, not the standard radioactive decay of unstable isotopes.
    *   **(D) "When a nucleus has too many protons":** This condition typically leads to **Beta-plus ($\beta^+$) decay** or electron capture, where a proton is converted into a neutron, not Beta-minus decay.

Therefore, because $\beta^-$ decay is the mechanism by which a nucleus reduces its neutron count to achieve stability, **(A)** is the best answer.
