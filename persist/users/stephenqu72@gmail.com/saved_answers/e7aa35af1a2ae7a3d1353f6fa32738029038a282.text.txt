To find the magnitude of the gravitational force acting on the satellite, we must recognize that the gravitational force is the **centripetal force** required to keep the satellite in its circular orbit.

### 1. Identify the given variables
*   Mass of the satellite ($m$): $3.50 \times 10^3\text{ kg}$
*   Orbital radius ($r$): $9.17 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
*   Orbital period ($T$): $1.50\text{ hours} = 1.50 \times 3600\text{ s} = 5400\text{ s}$

### 2. Determine the formula
The centripetal force ($F_c$) required for circular motion is given by:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Since the velocity ($v$) of an object in a circular orbit is the circumference divided by the period ($v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$), we can substitute this into the force equation:
$$F = m \left( \frac{2\pi r}{T} \right)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{r}$$
$$F = \frac{4\pi^2 mr}{T^2}$$

### 3. Calculation
Substitute the values into the derived formula:
$$F = \frac{4 \cdot \pi^2 \cdot (3.50 \times 10^3) \cdot (9.17 \times 10^6)}{(5400)^2}$$

Calculate the numerator:
$$4 \cdot \pi^2 \cdot 3500 \cdot 9,170,000 \approx 1.267 \times 10^{12}$$

Calculate the denominator:
$$(5400)^2 = 29,160,000 = 2.916 \times 10^7$$

Divide the results:
$$F = \frac{1.267 \times 10^{12}}{2.916 \times 10^7} \approx 43,450\text{ N}$$
Rounding to three significant figures, we get $4.34 \times 10^4\text{ N}$.
