To calculate the mass of the pulsar, we equate the centripetal force required to keep the planet in orbit with the gravitational force exerted by the pulsar.

### Given Data:
*   Orbital radius ($r$) = $6.9 \times 10^{10}\text{ m}$
*   Period ($T$) = $8.47 \times 10^6\text{ s}$
*   Gravitational constant ($G$) $\approx 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\text{ m}^3\text{ kg}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-2}$

### Method:
1. The centripetal force is provided by gravity:
   $$F_c = F_g \implies \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
2. Simplify to solve for $M$ (mass of the pulsar):
   $$M = \frac{v^2r}{G}$$
3. Substitute the orbital velocity formula $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$:
   $$M = \frac{(\frac{2\pi r}{T})^2 r}{G} = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GT^2}$$

### Calculation:
Substitute the values into the equation:
$$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (6.9 \times 10^{10})^3}{6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times (8.47 \times 10^6)^2}$$

Calculate the numerator:
$$4 \times \pi^2 \times 3.285 \times 10^{32} \approx 1.296 \times 10^{34}$$

Calculate the denominator:
$$6.674 \times 10^{-11} \times 7.174 \times 10^{13} \approx 4.788 \times 10^3$$

Final result:
$$M \approx \frac{1.296 \times 10^{34}}{4.788 \times 10^3} \approx 2.7 \times 10^{30}\text{ kg}$$

The mass of the pulsar is approximately $2.7 \times 10^{30}\text{ kg}$.
