### Answer
The history of light demonstrates that scientific models are not absolute truths, but rather frameworks that are validated or falsified through experimental evidence.

1.  **The Classical Wave Model:** Early 19th-century experiments, specifically Young’s Double Slit experiment, provided clear evidence of interference and diffraction. This invalidated the corpuscular (particle) model held by Newton, leading to the dominance of the wave model, further supported by Maxwell’s equations which described light as an electromagnetic wave.
2.  **The Crisis of the Model:** The classical wave model failed to explain the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe" (blackbody radiation) and the photoelectric effect. In the latter, classical physics predicted that light intensity (not frequency) should eject electrons, but experimental observations showed that ejection was dependent on the frequency of incident light.
3.  **Refinement/Paradigm Shift:** To account for these anomalies, Albert Einstein proposed the photon model, suggesting light behaves as quantized packets of energy. This revived the particle-like nature of light, which seemed to contradict the established wave model.
4.  **Validation through Synthesis:** Rather than discarding one model entirely, scientists reconciled them by adopting the theory of wave-particle duality. The validation of this new, more complex model was achieved through de Broglie’s hypothesis (matter waves) and later experimental confirmation through electron diffraction (Davisson-Germer experiment).

**Conclusion:** Scientists test models by making predictions based on theory and subjecting them to rigorous experimental testing. When a model cannot explain new, high-quality data (anomalies), it must be modified or replaced by a broader theory that explains both the old and new evidence.
