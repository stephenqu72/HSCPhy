### Answer

**(a) Compare the times of flight of the two projectiles.**

The time of flight $T$ for a projectile launched from level ground is given by the formula:
$$T = \frac{2u \sin \theta}{g}$$
- For Projectile A ($\theta_A = 20^\circ$): $T_A = \frac{2u \sin 20^\circ}{g}$
- For Projectile B ($\theta_B = 70^\circ$): $T_B = \frac{2u \sin 70^\circ}{g}$

Since $\sin 70^\circ > \sin 20^\circ$ (or noting that $\sin 70^\circ = \cos 20^\circ$), it follows that $T_B > T_A$. Projectile B will remain in the air for a longer duration than Projectile A because it has a greater initial vertical velocity component.

**(b) Show that the ranges of both projectiles are equal.**

The horizontal range $R$ of a projectile is given by the formula:
$$R = \frac{u^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$$
- For Projectile A: $R_A = \frac{u^2 \sin(2 \times 20^\circ)}{g} = \frac{u^2 \sin 40^\circ}{g}$
- For Projectile B: $R_B = \frac{u^2 \sin(2 \times 70^\circ)}{g} = \frac{u^2 \sin 140^\circ}{g}$

Using the trigonometric identity $\sin(180^\circ - x) = \sin x$, we can see that:
$$\sin 140^\circ = \sin(180^\circ - 40^\circ) = \sin 40^\circ$$
Therefore:
$$R_B = \frac{u^2 \sin 40^\circ}{g} = R_A$$
Both projectiles have the same range.

### How to solve it

1.  **Understand Projectile Formulas:** To excel in HSC Physics, memorize the standard kinematic equations for projectiles on level ground. The vertical component of initial velocity $u_y = u \sin \theta$ determines time of flight, while the combination of horizontal and vertical components determines range.
2.  **Part (a) Strategy:** Recognize that time of flight is purely dependent on the initial vertical velocity. Since $\sin \theta$ increases as $\theta$ increases (from $0^\circ$ to $90^\circ$), a higher launch angle results in a longer time of flight.
3.  **Part (b) Strategy:** Use the range formula $R = \frac{u^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$. The key insight is identifying complementary angles ($20^\circ + 70^\circ = 90^\circ$). When two angles are complementary, their double angles ($2\theta_1$ and $2\theta_2$) will sum to $180^\circ$. Because $\sin(x) = \sin(180^\circ - x)$, any two projectiles launched at the same speed with complementary angles will always have the same range.
