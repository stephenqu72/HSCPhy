### Explanation
To determine which statement is incorrect, let's analyze each option based on HSC Physics principles:

*   **A: Correct.** Binding energy ($BE$) is defined by Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, given by the formula:
    $$BE = \Delta m c^2$$
    where $\Delta m$ is the mass defect and $c$ is the speed of light. This statement is accurate.
*   **B: Incorrect.** The stability of a nucleus is not determined by its total binding energy, but by its **binding energy per nucleon**. The most stable nuclei (such as Iron-56) have the **highest** binding energy per nucleon, not the smallest. Comparing total binding energy is misleading because larger nuclei naturally have more nucleons and thus higher total binding energy, even if they are less stable.
*   **C: Correct.** Binding energy is defined as the work required to disassemble a nucleus into its individual protons and neutrons (nucleons). This effectively represents the "potential energy well" of the nucleus.
*   **D: Correct.** Due to the law of conservation of energy, the energy required to pull a nucleus apart (C) is exactly equal to the energy released when those same nucleons come together to form the nucleus.

Since statement B claims that the most stable nuclei have the *smallest* binding energy (when in reality they have the *highest* binding energy per nucleon), it is the incorrect statement.
