To determine which star is the hottest based on its absorption spectrum, we need to apply the principles of stellar classification, specifically the relationship between stellar surface temperature and the presence of spectral lines (the Harvard spectral classification system).

### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) Star B**.

### Explanation
In HSC Physics, we identify the temperature of a star by observing the specific absorption lines present in its spectrum:

1.  **Ionization Energy:** Hotter stars have higher energy atmospheres, which leads to the ionization of atoms. Conversely, cooler stars have neutral atoms or molecules that can survive in their atmospheres.
2.  **Spectral Indicators:** 
    *   **Hot stars** (Spectral types O and B) are characterized by lines of ionized helium, neutral helium, and sometimes highly ionized metals.
    *   **Medium-temperature stars** (Spectral types A and F) are dominated by hydrogen Balmer lines.
    *   **Cooler stars** (Spectral types G, K, and M) show lines of neutral metals (like Iron and Calcium) and even molecules (like Titanium Oxide).
3.  **Analyzing the Data:**
    *   **Star A and Star C:** These show prominent **Hydrogen** lines. Stars with strong hydrogen lines (Type A) are relatively hot, but they are not the hottest.
    *   **Star D:** This shows lines of **Iron and Calcium**. Neutral metal lines are characteristic of cooler stars (like our Sun, which is a G-type star).
    *   **Star B:** This spectrum contains lines for **Helium, Magnesium, Oxygen, and Iron**. Notably, the presence of **Helium** is the key indicator here. Helium lines only appear in the spectra of very hot stars (O and B types) because it requires high temperatures to excite helium atoms to energy levels where they can produce visible absorption lines. 

Because Star B exhibits helium lines, it possesses the highest surface temperature among the choices provided.
