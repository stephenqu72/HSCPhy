**Part b. Explain why there was no current.**

The issue here relates to the spectral sensitivity of the photodetector. Photodetectors (such as photodiodes) have a specific "spectral response" range. They are designed to convert photons into electrons only if the incident photon energy ($E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$) exceeds the bandgap energy of the semiconductor material in the detector.

1.  **Energy difference:** A red laser has a longer wavelength ($\approx 650\text{ nm}$) than a blue laser ($\approx 450\text{ nm}$). Consequently, individual photons from a red laser have significantly less energy than those from a blue laser.
2.  **Threshold:** The photodetector used in the first part was likely optimised for blue light. The semiconductor material in the detector has a bandgap energy that is higher than the energy provided by the red laser photons. 
3.  **Conclusion:** Because the red laser photons do not possess enough energy to excite electrons across the bandgap of the detector, no electron-hole pairs are created, and no photocurrent is generated.
