To solve this problem, we use Kepler's Third Law, which states that for orbiting bodies around a central mass $M$:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$
where $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$.

### (a) Determine the mass of the planet Calculon
First, calculate the gradient of the line of best fit. Using points $(2.3, 1.9)$ and $(9.6, 7.8)$ from the graph (units are $\times 10^8 \text{ s}^2$ and $\times 10^{20} \text{ m}^3$):
$$\text{Gradient} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{(7.8 - 1.9) \times 10^{20}}{(9.6 - 2.3) \times 10^8} = \frac{5.9 \times 10^{20}}{7.3 \times 10^8} \approx 8.08 \times 10^{11} \text{ m}^3/\text{s}^2$$
Using the formula $M = \frac{4\pi^2}{G} \times \text{gradient}$:
$$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}} \times 8.08 \times 10^{11} \approx 4.78 \times 10^{23} \text{ kg}$$

### (b) Determine the orbital velocity of satellite B
For satellite B, the graph shows $T^2 \approx 3.4 \times 10^8 \text{ s}^2$ and $r^3 \approx 2.8 \times 10^{20} \text{ m}^3$.
Thus, $T = \sqrt{3.4 \times 10^8} \approx 18,439 \text{ s}$ and $r = \sqrt[3]{2.8 \times 10^{20}} \approx 6.54 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$.
Orbital velocity $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$:
$$v = \frac{2 \times \pi \times 6.54 \times 10^6}{18,439} \approx 2,229 \text{ m/s}$$

### (c) If Satellite C is in a geostationary orbit
For satellite C, the graph shows $T^2 \approx 6.1 \times 10^8 \text{ s}^2$.
A geostationary orbit means the orbital period of the satellite equals the rotational period of the planet.
$$T = \sqrt{6.1 \times 10^8} \approx 24,698 \text{ s}$$
Converting to hours:
$$T \approx \frac{24,698}{3600} \approx 6.86 \text{ hours}$$
The day on the planet is approximately $6.86$ hours long.
