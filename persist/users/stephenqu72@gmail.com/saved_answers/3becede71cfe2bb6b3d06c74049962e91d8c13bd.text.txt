To solve this problem, we use **Malus's Law**, which states that the intensity of polarized light emerging from a polarizer is given by:
$$I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$$
where $I_0$ is the incident intensity and $\theta$ is the angle between the light's polarization direction and the transmission axis of the polarizer.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Polarizer A ($0^\circ$):**
The incident light has an intensity of $64\text{ W/m}^2$. Assuming the light is initially **unpolarized** (standard assumption for such physics problems unless specified otherwise), the first polarizer reduces the intensity by half:
$$I_A = \frac{1}{2} I_{\text{incident}} = \frac{64}{2} = 32\text{ W/m}^2$$

**2. Polarizer B ($30^\circ$ relative to A):**
The light emerging from A is vertically polarized ($0^\circ$). The axis of B is at $30^\circ$ to the vertical.
Using Malus's Law:
$$I_B = I_A \cos^2(30^\circ)$$
Since $\cos(30^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$, then $\cos^2(30^\circ) = \frac{3}{4} = 0.75$:
$$I_B = 32 \times 0.75 = 24\text{ W/m}^2$$

**3. Polarizer C ($90^\circ$ relative to the vertical):**
The light emerging from B is at $30^\circ$ to the vertical. The axis of C is at $90^\circ$ to the vertical. The angle between the light from B and the axis of C is $\theta = 90^\circ - 30^\circ = 60^\circ$.
Using Malus's Law again:
$$I_C = I_B \cos^2(60^\circ)$$
Since $\cos(60^\circ) = 0.5$, then $\cos^2(60^\circ) = 0.25$:
$$I_C = 24 \times 0.25 = 6\text{ W/m}^2$$

**Final Answer:**
The intensity of the light emerging from the last polarizer, C, is **$6\text{ W/m}^2$**.
