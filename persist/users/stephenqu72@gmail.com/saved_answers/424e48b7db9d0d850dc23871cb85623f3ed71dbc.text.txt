### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) having power stations located much closer to the consumers.**

---

### Explanation
To understand why this is the correct answer, we must compare the transmission of Alternating Current (AC) and Direct Current (DC).

1.  **The Role of Transformers:** The primary advantage of AC electricity is the ease with which its voltage can be stepped up or stepped down using transformers. In power transmission, electricity is stepped up to very high voltages (e.g., $400\text{ kV}$) to minimize energy loss due to heating in the wires. The power loss in transmission lines is given by the formula:
    $$P_{\text{loss}} = I^2R$$
    By increasing the voltage ($V$), we can decrease the current ($I$) for a given power output ($P = VI$), which significantly reduces $P_{\text{loss}}$.

2.  **The Limitation of DC:** Transformers operate on the principle of electromagnetic induction, which requires a changing magnetic field—a property inherent to AC. DC does not change direction and therefore cannot be easily stepped up or down using a simple transformer. 

3.  **Transmission Efficiency:** If we were forced to use DC for transmission without the ability to easily change voltages, we would have to transmit electricity at the same voltage that is used in the home (or close to it). Because of the high current required to transmit significant power at low voltage, the resistive energy losses ($I^2R$) would be massive over long distances. 

4.  **The Consequence:** To avoid these unacceptable energy losses, power stations would need to be located very close to the residential and commercial areas they serve. This is effectively what occurred during the early days of electricity, such as the "War of the Currents" between Edison (DC) and Tesla/Westinghouse (AC).

**Why other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A):** Household appliances are designed for specific voltages regardless of whether the source is AC or DC.
*   **(C):** DC transmission would actually require *more* local, smaller power stations to cover the same area that one large, remote AC plant could cover.
*   **(D):** Light bulbs and other appliances can function on both AC and DC; DC does not inherently make an appliance "less efficient."
