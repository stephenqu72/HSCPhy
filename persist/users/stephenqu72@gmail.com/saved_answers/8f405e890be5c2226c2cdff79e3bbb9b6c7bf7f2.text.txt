### Answer

In Newtonian physics, momentum is defined as $p = mv$. According to this relationship, momentum is a linear function of velocity. If a constant force (thrust) is applied to an object, the acceleration should remain constant, and the object's speed should increase indefinitely.

However, Einstein’s theory of Special Relativity states that as an object approaches the speed of light ($c$), its relativistic momentum becomes:
$$p = \gamma mv = \frac{mv}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$
where $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor. 

As the velocity $v$ approaches $c$, the denominator $\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$ approaches zero. This causes the value of $\gamma$ (and consequently the momentum $p$) to approach infinity. Therefore, as the spacecraft approaches $c$, an increasingly large amount of energy (momentum) is required to produce even a tiny increment in speed. This explains the asymptotic curve shown in the graph, where speed is limited by $c$ regardless of the increase in momentum provided by the thrusters.
