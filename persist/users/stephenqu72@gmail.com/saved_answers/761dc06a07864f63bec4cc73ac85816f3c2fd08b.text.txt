To answer this question, we must look at the principles of electromagnetic induction and how they interact with the motor driving the system.

### The Physics Explanation

1.  **Why the speed of rotation decreases:** As the aluminium disc rotates through the static magnetic field of the horseshoe magnet, the free electrons in the metal move through the field. This results in a change in magnetic flux linkage through the sections of the disc moving into and out of the magnetic field. According to Faraday’s Law, this induces an electromotive force (EMF), which drives circulating currents known as **eddy currents** within the disc.
2.  **Applying Lenz's Law:** Lenz’s Law states that the direction of the induced current is such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it. The interaction between these eddy currents and the external magnetic field creates a magnetic braking force (Lorentz force) that opposes the direction of the disc's rotation. This increased resistance to motion causes the motor to slow down.
3.  **Why the current in the ammeter increases:** An electric motor works by drawing current to produce a torque that overcomes external loads and internal friction. When the magnetic braking force acts on the disc, it exerts a "load" on the motor. To maintain rotation against this additional resistive torque, the motor requires more electrical power. In a DC motor, the "Back EMF" ($\varepsilon$) is proportional to the speed of rotation ($\omega$), expressed as $\varepsilon \propto \omega$. As the rotation speed decreases, the back EMF generated by the motor decreases. Given the circuit equation $V = I R + \varepsilon$ (where $V$ is battery voltage and $R$ is internal resistance), a decrease in $\varepsilon$ results in an increase in the current $I$ drawn from the battery.
