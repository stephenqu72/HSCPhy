### Part (a)
**Solution:**
We use the Work-Energy Theorem: $W = \Delta KE$. The work done by the force $F$ over the distance $d$ equals the kinetic energy gained by the ball.
$W = F \times d = 431\text{ N} \times 0.70\text{ m} = 301.7\text{ J}$.
The final kinetic energy is $KE_f = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 4.0\text{ kg} \times (12\text{ m/s})^2 = 288\text{ J}$.
Wait, look at the values! The energy provided by the work ($301.7\text{ J}$) is greater than the kinetic energy at release ($288\text{ J}$). This implies work was done against other factors (like internal muscle energy or drag), but the problem asks for the angle. Since the force and velocity are vectors, we look at the work done in the direction of motion. However, there is no information about the launch angle provided by the work alone. **Re-evaluating the prompt**: The question asks "at what angle did she throw the ball". In physics problems of this type, we assume the force vector acts in the direction of the launch. If the energy input and output are calculated, we find the work is not fully converted to kinetic energy of the ball, or there is an ambiguity. Given this is a standard exam question, if the parameters provided allow for calculations, we assume the ball's final velocity components $v_x$ and $v_y$ are influenced by the launch geometry. 
*Note:* Given the standard nature of these problems, often a piece of information might be inferred. If no further data is given, we assume the work provides the energy for the launch speed $12\text{ m/s}$.

### Part (b)
**Solution:**
We use kinematic equations with $h_0 = 2\text{ m}$, $v = 12\text{ m/s}$, and $\theta = 45^\circ$.
Initial components:
$v_x = 12 \cos(45^\circ) \approx 8.485\text{ m/s}$
$v_y = 12 \sin(45^\circ) \approx 8.485\text{ m/s}$

Find time of flight $t$ using $y = y_0 + v_y t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$:
$0 = 2 + 8.485t - 4.9t^2$
Using the quadratic formula for $4.9t^2 - 8.485t - 2 = 0$:
$t = \frac{8.485 + \sqrt{(-8.485)^2 - 4(4.9)(-2)}}{9.8} \approx \frac{8.485 + 10.85}{9.8} \approx 1.97\text{ s}$

Range $R = v_x \times t = 8.485 \times 1.97 \approx 16.7\text{ m}$.

### Part (c)
**Solution:**
The optimal angle of $45^\circ$ for a projectile only applies when the launch height is equal to the landing height ($h_0 = 0$). In this case, the shot put is released from a height of $2\text{ m}$. Because the ball spends extra time falling from the release height to the ground, the optimal angle for maximum range is actually **less than $45^\circ$**. A lower angle results in a higher horizontal velocity component, which allows the projectile to travel further during the additional time it takes to fall the final $2\text{ meters}$.
