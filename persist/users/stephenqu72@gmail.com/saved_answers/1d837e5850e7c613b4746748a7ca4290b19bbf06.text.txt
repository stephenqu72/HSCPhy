To solve this question, we must understand the definition of a **fundamental particle** within the Standard Model of particle physics. A fundamental particle is an elementary particle that is not composed of any smaller subunits (i.e., it has no internal structure).

### Analysis of the options:

*   **(A) Higgs boson, baryon, photon, neutron:** Incorrect. While the Higgs boson and photon are fundamental, a **baryon** is a composite particle (made of three quarks) and a **neutron** is also a composite particle (a type of baryon).
*   **(B) Up quark, electron neutrino, proton, tau:** Incorrect. While the up quark, electron neutrino, and tau are fundamental, a **proton** is a composite particle (a baryon made of two up quarks and one down quark).
*   **(C) Gluon, tau, z boson, strange quark, muon:** **Correct.** Every particle listed here is an elementary particle:
    *   **Gluon:** Gauge boson (mediates the strong nuclear force).
    *   **Tau:** Lepton (a generation of the electron).
    *   **Z boson:** Gauge boson (mediates the weak nuclear force).
    *   **Strange quark:** Quark (matter particle).
    *   **Muon:** Lepton (a generation of the electron).
*   **(D) Proton, neutron, electron, electron neutrino:** Incorrect. As mentioned above, both the **proton** and **neutron** are composite particles.

### Explanation:
In the Standard Model, particles are categorized as either **elementary** (fundamental) or **composite**. Fundamental particles include quarks, leptons, and gauge bosons (plus the Higgs boson). Composite particles, such as hadrons (baryons and mesons), are made up of quarks held together by the strong nuclear force. When you encounter a question like this, look for "hadrons," "protons," "neutrons," or "nuclei," as these are immediate indicators that the option contains non-fundamental particles.

The correct answer is **(C)**.
