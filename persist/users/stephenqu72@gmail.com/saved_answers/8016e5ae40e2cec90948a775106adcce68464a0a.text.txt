To determine if the car can maintain its height on the banked track, we must calculate the required centripetal force and compare the forces acting on the car in the plane of the incline.

### 1. Identify the given values
*   Mass $m = 1800\text{ kg}$
*   Velocity $v = 20\text{ m/s}$
*   Radius $r = 28\text{ m}$
*   Angle $\theta = 10^\circ$
*   Coefficient of friction $\mu = 1.4$
*   Gravity $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$

### 2. Calculate the required centripetal force ($F_c$)
The car requires a horizontal centripetal force to maintain its circular path:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{1800 \times 20^2}{28} \approx 25,714.3\text{ N}$$

### 3. Analyze the forces acting perpendicular to the incline
The forces acting on the car are gravity ($mg$), the normal force ($N$), and static friction ($f$). For the car to maintain its height, the net force parallel to the incline must be zero. 

The horizontal centripetal force is provided by the horizontal components of the normal force and friction. We decompose $F_c$ into the plane of the incline:
The component of the centripetal force acting *down* the slope is $F_c \cos(10^\circ)$.
The component of gravity acting *down* the slope is $mg \sin(10^\circ)$.

To maintain height, the frictional force ($f$) must balance these:
$$f = F_c \cos(10^\circ) - mg \sin(10^\circ)$$
$$f = (25,714.3 \times \cos 10^\circ) - (1800 \times 9.8 \times \sin 10^\circ)$$
$$f \approx 25,324 - 3,063 \approx 22,261\text{ N}$$

### 4. Check against maximum available friction
The maximum static friction is $f_{max} = \mu N$. 
The normal force $N$ must balance the vertical components of gravity and centripetal force (or specifically, the force perpendicular to the surface):
$$N = mg \cos(10^\circ) + F_c \sin(10^\circ)$$
$$N = (1800 \times 9.8 \times \cos 10^\circ) + (25,714.3 \times \sin 10^\circ)$$
$$N \approx 17,370 + 4,466 = 21,836\text{ N}$$
$$f_{max} = 1.4 \times 21,836 \approx 30,570\text{ N}$$

### Conclusion
Since the required frictional force ($22,261\text{ N}$) is less than the maximum available frictional force ($30,570\text{ N}$), the car **can** maintain its current height on the track.
