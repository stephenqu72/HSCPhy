### Answer and Explanation

#### Part a: Calculate the magnitude of the net force
To find the net force (centripetal force) acting on the car, we use the formula:
$$F_{net} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

**Given values:**
*   Mass ($m$) = $800\text{ kg}$
*   Velocity ($v$) = $40\text{ m s}^{-1}$
*   Radius ($r$) = $80.0\text{ m}$

**Calculation:**
$$F_{net} = \frac{800 \times (40)^2}{80.0}$$
$$F_{net} = \frac{800 \times 1600}{80.0}$$
$$F_{net} = \frac{1,280,000}{80.0} = 16,000\text{ N}$$

**Answer:** $16,000\text{ N}$

---

#### Part b: Direction of the net force
The net force required for circular motion is the **centripetal force**. This force is always directed toward the **center of the circular path**. 
*   **On Figure 8b:** You should draw an arrow pointing from the car perpendicularly inward toward the center of the curve (the center of the radius $r$).

---

#### Part c: Explanation
1.  **Why a net force is needed:** According to Newton's First Law, an object in motion will continue to move in a straight line at a constant speed unless acted upon by a net external force. Because the car is changing its direction (it is accelerating toward the center of the curve), a centripetal force must act on the car to change its velocity vector.
2.  **What exerts the force:** The horizontal net force is provided by the **static friction** between the car's tires and the road surface. This friction acts inward toward the center of the corner.
