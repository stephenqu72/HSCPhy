### Answer

The statement is **valid**. Lenz’s law, which states that an induced electromotive force (EMF) always gives rise to a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it, is a direct application of the Law of Conservation of Energy.

If Lenz’s law were incorrect—that is, if the induced current created a magnetic field that *aided* or reinforced the change in flux—the resulting current would create a stronger magnetic field, inducing an even greater current. This would lead to an exponential increase in current and energy without any external work input, violating the Law of Conservation of Energy, which dictates that energy cannot be created from nothing.

**Example: A magnet approaching a solenoid**
When the North pole of a magnet is pushed toward a coil:
1. According to Lenz's law, the coil induces a current that creates a North pole at the end nearest the magnet to *repel* it.
2. To move the magnet toward the coil, work must be done against this repulsive force.
3. This mechanical work done by the person pushing the magnet is precisely the energy converted into electrical energy in the coil.
4. If the induced pole were a South pole (attraction), the magnet would accelerate toward the coil without any work done, creating kinetic and electrical energy out of "thin air," which is physically impossible.
