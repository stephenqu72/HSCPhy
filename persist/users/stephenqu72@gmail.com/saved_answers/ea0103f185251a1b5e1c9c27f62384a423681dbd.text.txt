### (a) Definition of Geostationary
A geostationary satellite orbits directly above the Earth's equator, has an orbital period equal to the Earth's sidereal rotation period ($23.93$ hours or $\approx 86\,164$ seconds), and maintains a fixed position relative to a point on the Earth's surface.

---

### (b) Calculation of Orbital Radius
To solve this, we use the formula for orbital period derived from equating gravitational force to centripetal force:
$$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2r^3}{GM}$$
Rearranging for $r$:
$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}}$$

**Constants:**
- $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$
- $M (\text{Earth}) = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \, \text{kg}$
- $T \approx 86\,164 \, \text{s}$

**Calculation:**
1. Calculate $T^2$: $(86\,164)^2 \approx 7.424 \times 10^9$
2. $r^3 = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (7.424 \times 10^9)}{4\pi^2}$
3. $r^3 \approx 7.498 \times 10^{22}$
4. $r \approx 4.22 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}$ (or $42\,200 \, \text{km}$)

---

### (c) Kepler’s Second Law and Conservation of Energy
Kepler’s Second Law states that a line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. 

**Relationship to Conservation of Energy:**
As a planet moves in an elliptical orbit, its distance from the Sun changes. 
- When the planet is closer to the Sun (perihelion), its **gravitational potential energy** is lower (more negative), and it moves faster, resulting in higher **kinetic energy**.
- When the planet is further from the Sun (aphelion), its **gravitational potential energy** is higher (less negative), and it moves slower, resulting in lower **kinetic energy**.
- Because the total mechanical energy ($E = U + K$) must remain constant, the exchange between kinetic and potential energy ensures that the planet obeys the conservation of energy while satisfying the area-sweeping rate required by Kepler’s Second Law.
