### Part a: Calculation

**Step 1: Calculate the time measured by an Earth observer ($t$)**
The distance $L = 8.61$ light-years and the velocity $v = 0.800c$. Since $v = \frac{d}{t}$, we can find the time elapsed on Earth clocks:
$$t = \frac{L}{v} = \frac{8.61 \text{ light-years}}{0.800c} = 10.7625 \text{ years}$$

**Step 2: Calculate the time for the astronaut ($t_0$)**
We use the time dilation formula $t = \gamma t_0$, where $\gamma = 1.67$.
$$t_0 = \frac{t}{\gamma}$$
$$t_0 = \frac{10.7625}{1.67} \approx 6.4446 \text{ years}$$

**Rounding:** Given the data, we should provide the answer to three significant figures: **6.44 years**.

***

### Part b: Reasoning

**Answer:** Yes, the time measured by the astronaut is a **proper time**.

**Explanation:** In Special Relativity, proper time is defined as the time interval between two events measured by an observer for whom the two events occur at the **same spatial location**. 
*   The two events here are the "start of the trip" (leaving Earth) and the "end of the trip" (arriving at Sirius).
*   For the astronaut, both the starting point and the ending point of the trip occur at the same location (the spaceship). Since the astronaut is stationary relative to their own clock throughout the journey, they measure the proper time interval.
