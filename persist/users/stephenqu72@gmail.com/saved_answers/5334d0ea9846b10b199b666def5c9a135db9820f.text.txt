To solve this problem, we must link the energy provided by the electric field to the circular motion caused by the magnetic field.

### Part a: Calculation of the radius ($R$)

**1. Determine the velocity ($v$) of the proton:**
The proton is accelerated by a potential difference $V = 10,000 \text{ V}$. The work done by the electric field equals the kinetic energy gained by the proton:
$$qV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
Solving for $v$:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2qV}{m}}$$
Using the constants for a proton:
- Charge $q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$
- Mass $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times (1.602 \times 10^{-19}) \times 10000}{1.673 \times 10^{-27}}} \approx 1.383 \times 10^6 \text{ m/s}$$

**2. Determine the radius ($R$) of the path:**
When the proton enters the magnetic field, the magnetic force acts as the centripetal force:
$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{R}$$
Rearranging for $R$:
$$R = \frac{mv}{qB}$$
Substitute the known values ($B = 0.1 \text{ T}$):
$$R = \frac{(1.673 \times 10^{-27}) \times (1.383 \times 10^6)}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19}) \times 0.1}$$
$$R \approx 0.144 \text{ m} \text{ (or } 14.4 \text{ cm)}$$

---

### Part b: Effect of replacing the proton with an electron

If an electron enters the magnetic field at the same velocity:
1. **Direction:** Because the electron is negatively charged, the direction of the magnetic force (given by the right-hand rule) will be reversed. Instead of curving "up" as the proton does, the electron will curve "downward."
2. **Radius:** The radius is governed by $R = \frac{mv}{qB}$. While the charge $q$ is the same magnitude, the mass of an electron ($9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$) is significantly smaller than the mass of a proton. Since $R \propto m$, the radius of the electron's path will be much smaller than the proton's path.
