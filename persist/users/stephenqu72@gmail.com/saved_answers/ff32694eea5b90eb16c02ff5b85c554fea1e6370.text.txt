To solve this problem, we apply **Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which states that the square of the orbital period $T$ is directly proportional to the cube of the average distance $r$ from the centre of the central body:

$$T^2 \propto r^3 \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{T^2}{r^3} = \text{constant}$$

### Step-by-step Solution:

1.  **Set up the ratio:** Since both satellites orbit the same central body (Earth), we can equate their ratios:
    $$\frac{T_P^2}{r_P^3} = \frac{T_Q^2}{r_Q^3}$$

2.  **Substitute the given values:**
    *   For satellite $P$: distance $= r$, period $= T_P$
    *   For satellite $Q$: distance $= 6r$, period $= T_Q$

    $$\frac{T_P^2}{r^3} = \frac{T_Q^2}{(6r)^3}$$

3.  **Simplify the equation:**
    $$\frac{T_P^2}{T_Q^2} = \frac{r^3}{(6r)^3}$$
    $$\frac{T_P^2}{T_Q^2} = \frac{r^3}{216r^3}$$
    $$\frac{T_P^2}{T_Q^2} = \frac{1}{216}$$

4.  **Find the ratio of the periods ($T_P$ to $T_Q$):**
    Take the square root of both sides:
    $$\frac{T_P}{T_Q} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{216}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{216}}$$
    Since $\sqrt{216} = \sqrt{36 \times 6} = 6\sqrt{6} \approx 14.7$, we look at the provided options.
