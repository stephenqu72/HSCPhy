### Answer
The correct answer is **C. $35\,800\text{ km}$**.

---

### Explanation
To solve this problem, you need to understand the concept of a **Geostationary Orbit (GEO)**.

1.  **Understand the requirement:** A "communication satellite" is most effective for global or regional television, weather, and telecommunications coverage if it remains at a fixed position relative to a point on the Earth's surface. This allows ground-based satellite dishes to remain pointed in a constant direction without needing to track the satellite as it moves across the sky.
2.  **Define Geostationary Orbit:** For a satellite to appear stationary, its orbital period must match the Earth's rotational period (which is one sidereal day, approximately $23\text{ hours, } 56\text{ minutes, and } 4\text{ seconds}$).
3.  **The Physics:** We can derive the orbital radius $r$ required for this period using Kepler's Third Law:
    $$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2r^3}{GM}$$
    Where:
    *   $T$ is the orbital period ($\approx 86\,164\text{ seconds}$)
    *   $G$ is the gravitational constant
    *   $M$ is the mass of the Earth
4.  **Calculation:** Solving for $r$ (the distance from the **center** of the Earth) gives a value of approximately $42\,164\text{ km}$.
5.  **Altitude vs. Orbital Radius:** The question asks for the "orbital altitude," which is the distance from the **surface** of the Earth. To find this, we subtract the Earth's mean radius ($R_E \approx 6\,378\text{ km}$) from the orbital radius $r$:
    $$\text{Altitude} = r - R_E$$
    $$\text{Altitude} = 42\,164\text{ km} - 6\,378\text{ km} \approx 35\,786\text{ km}$$
6.  **Conclusion:** Rounding to the nearest value provided in the options, we get $35\,800\text{ km}$.
