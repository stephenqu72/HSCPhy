### Experimental Design: Contrasting Horizontal and Banked Circular Motion

**Aim:** To compare the centripetal forces and stability requirements for an object moving in a horizontal circular path on a flat surface versus a banked track.

**Apparatus:** A rotating platform (or turntable), a model car/object, a force sensor (attached to the car), a banking wedge (adjustable angle), and a stopwatch or motion sensor.

**Methodology:**
1.  **Horizontal Bend Setup:** Place the object on a flat, horizontal rotating platform. Gradually increase the angular velocity $\omega$ until the object slips. Record the maximum angular velocity $\omega_{max}$ using the motion sensor. The centripetal force is provided solely by friction $F_f = \mu N = \mu mg$.
2.  **Banked Track Setup:** Place the object on a banking wedge at a specific angle $\theta$ on the platform. Increase the angular velocity until the object begins to slip up or down the bank.
3.  **Data Collection:** Measure the radius $r$ of the path, the angle of the bank $\theta$, the mass of the object $m$, and the velocity $v$ (or angular velocity $\omega$).

**Key Variables:**
*   **Independent Variable:** The angle of the banking ($\theta$) and the surface type (flat vs. banked).
*   **Dependent Variable:** The maximum velocity $v$ at which the object maintains uniform circular motion without slipping.
*   **Controlled Variables:** Radius of the path ($r$), mass of the object ($m$), and the coefficient of static friction ($\mu$) between the object and the surface.

**Observations:**
*   **Quantitative:** Record velocity $v$ using a motion sensor and calculate the required centripetal force using $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$. Compare this to the theoretical values derived from $F_{net} = m \cdot g \tan(\theta)$ for banked tracks.
*   **Qualitative:** Observe the "slip point" where the object starts to drift outward. Note the smoothness of the motion.

### How to solve this as an HSC student:
1.  **Physics Principles:** You must mention that on a flat track, friction provides the centripetal force ($F_c = F_f$). On a banked track, the horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force ($N \sin \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$).
2.  **Validity:** To make the experiment valid, ensure the radius remains constant for both trials. Use a spirit level to ensure the platform is perfectly horizontal.
3.  **Reliability:** Repeat each trial three times at different velocities and calculate the mean to reduce random error.
4.  **Structure:** Use headings like "Aim," "Variables," "Method," and "Discussion" to ensure your answer is clear to the marker.
5.  **Marking Criteria:** 9 marks imply depth. Don't just list equipment; explain *why* the physics differs (i.e., the advantage of banking is that it reduces reliance on friction, allowing higher speeds).
