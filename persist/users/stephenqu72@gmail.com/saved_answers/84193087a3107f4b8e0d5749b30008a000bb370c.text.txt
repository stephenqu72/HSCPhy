### Answer
The work done on an object undergoing uniform circular motion is zero because the centripetal force acting on the object is always directed toward the centre of the circle, while the instantaneous displacement (velocity vector) of the object is always directed tangentially to the circle. Since the force vector and the displacement vector are perpendicular ($90^\circ$) to each other at every point in the motion, and the work done is defined by the dot product $W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{s} = Fs \cos(\theta)$, the value of $\cos(90^\circ)$ is $0$, resulting in zero total work.

### Explanation of how to solve this
When approaching HSC Physics theory questions, follow the "link and justify" method:

1.  **State the definition of work:** Recall the formula $W = Fs \cos(\theta)$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the force and the displacement.
2.  **Identify the directional vectors:** In circular motion, identify that the centripetal force is a radial vector (pointing to the center) and the displacement vector (or instantaneous velocity) is a tangential vector.
3.  **Apply the geometry:** State that the radius of a circle is always perpendicular to the tangent at any given point.
4.  **Mathematical conclusion:** Because the angle $\theta$ is always $90^\circ$, and $\cos(90^\circ) = 0$, the mathematical product must be zero. 

By explicitly mentioning the relationship between the **radial force** and **tangential velocity**, you secure all marks by demonstrating a deep understanding of the vector physics involved.
