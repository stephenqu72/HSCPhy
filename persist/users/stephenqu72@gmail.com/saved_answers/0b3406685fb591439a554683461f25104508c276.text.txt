To maintain level flight, the aircraft must be in **rotational equilibrium**. This means the net torque ($\tau$) acting on the aircraft about its centre of mass must be zero.

### Explanation and Calculation

**1. Identify the pivot point:**
We use the centre of mass as our pivot point. Any forces acting exactly at the centre of mass produce zero torque.

**2. Resolve the forces:**
*   **Force $F$:** This acts at a distance of $1\text{ m}$ to the left of the centre of mass. It acts upwards, producing a counter-clockwise torque.
    $$\tau_F = F \times d_1 = F \times 1 = F$$
*   **The $1000\text{ N}$ force:** This acts at a distance of $1.2\text{ m}$ to the right of the centre of mass. Only the vertical component of this force creates torque about the horizontal axis of the plane. The angle is $10^\circ$ to the vertical, so the vertical component is:
    $$F_y = 1000 \cos(10^\circ)$$
    This force acts downwards relative to the pivot to create a clockwise torque (trying to rotate the nose down).
    $$\tau_{1000} = 1000 \cos(10^\circ) \times 1.2$$

**3. Apply the Equilibrium Condition:**
For rotational equilibrium, the sum of clockwise torques must equal the sum of counter-clockwise torques:
$$\sum \tau = 0$$
$$\text{Clockwise Torque} = \text{Counter-clockwise Torque}$$
$$1000 \cos(10^\circ) \times 1.2 = F \times 1$$

**4. Solve for $F$:**
$$F = 1200 \times \cos(10^\circ)$$
Using $\cos(10^\circ) \approx 0.9848$:
$$F \approx 1200 \times 0.9848$$
$$F \approx 1181.76\text{ N}$$

Rounding to significant figures consistent with the data (usually 2 or 3), we get approximately **$1182\text{ N}$**.
