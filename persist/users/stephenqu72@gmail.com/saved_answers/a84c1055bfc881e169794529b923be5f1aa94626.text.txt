### Answer

**The Geiger-Marsden Experiment**
In 1909, Geiger and Marsden fired high-energy, positively charged alpha particles at a thin sheet of gold foil in a vacuum. A fluorescent screen was used to detect the paths of these particles as they scattered. They observed that while most alpha particles passed straight through, a small fraction were deflected at large angles, and approximately 1 in 8,000 were reflected directly back toward the source.

**Relating Evidence to the Model**
Rutherford interpreted these results to propose the nuclear model of the atom:
1. **Most pass through:** This indicated that the atom is mostly empty space.
2. **Large deflections:** This suggested the presence of a concentrated, positively charged region (the nucleus) that exerted an electrostatic repulsive force on the alpha particles.
3. **Backscattering:** This confirmed that the nucleus is extremely dense and contains almost all the mass of the atom, as only a highly concentrated mass could reverse the momentum of the incoming alpha particles.

**Evaluation of Rutherford’s Model**
Rutherford’s model was a significant advancement over the "Plum Pudding" model, successfully explaining the existence of a central nucleus. However, it faced two major limitations:
1. **Classical Physics contradiction:** According to Maxwell’s electromagnetism, an accelerating charged electron orbiting a nucleus should continuously lose energy as electromagnetic radiation and spiral into the nucleus, implying that all atoms should be inherently unstable.
2. **Spectral lines:** It could not explain the origin of discrete emission spectra (atomic hydrogen spectra), as the model predicted a continuous range of orbital radii and energies rather than quantized levels. These gaps in understanding were later addressed by the Bohr model.
