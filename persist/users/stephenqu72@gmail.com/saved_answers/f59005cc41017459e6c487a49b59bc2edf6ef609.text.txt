### Part (a) Answer: Maxwell’s Contribution
James Clerk Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism into a single field theory. His key contributions include:
1.  **Mathematical synthesis:** He developed a set of partial differential equations (Maxwell's Equations) that describe how electric and magnetic fields behave and interact.
2.  **Prediction of EM waves:** He mathematically predicted the existence of self-propagating electromagnetic waves that travel through space.
3.  **Speed of light:** He calculated the theoretical speed of these waves using the constants $\epsilon_0$ (permittivity of free space) and $\mu_0$ (permeability of free space) via the formula:
    $$c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0 \mu_0}}$$
4.  **Modern model:** By showing that the calculated speed of these waves matched the known experimental value for the speed of light, he concluded that light itself is an electromagnetic wave, shifting the scientific paradigm away from mechanical models (like the luminiferous aether).

**How to solve:** To get 3 marks, you must link his theoretical work (equations/constants) to the conclusion that light is an electromagnetic wave.

### Part (b) Answer: Historical Attempts and Accuracy
*Candidate Response:*
"Two significant historical attempts were Fizeau (1849) and Michelson (1926). Fizeau used a toothed-wheel apparatus to measure the time taken for a light beam to travel to a distant mirror and back. He obtained a value of $315,000\text{ km s}^{-1}$. Michelson improved upon Foucault’s rotating mirror method, using highly precise optics and a long-baseline vacuum path, obtaining $299,796 \pm 4\text{ km s}^{-1}$. Comparing the two, Michelson’s measurement was significantly more accurate, as evidenced by his much smaller margin of error ($\pm 4\text{ km s}^{-1}$) and a result much closer to the modern accepted value of approximately $299,792\text{ km s}^{-1}$."

**How to solve:** 
1.  **Describe:** Briefly explain the mechanism (e.g., toothed wheel or rotating mirror).
2.  **Compare:** Use the data in the table. Note that "accuracy" refers to how close a measurement is to the true value. Michelson’s measurement is clearly superior because the uncertainty range is narrow and the mean value is extremely close to the modern standard.
