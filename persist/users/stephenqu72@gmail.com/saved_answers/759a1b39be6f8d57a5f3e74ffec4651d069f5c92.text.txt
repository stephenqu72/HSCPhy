### Answer
The correct answer is **C) explaining the spectrum of the visible lines in the hydrogen spectrum.**

---

### How to solve this
To solve this, we need to compare the limitations of the Rutherford model with the advancements provided by the Bohr model:

1.  **Analyze Rutherford's Model:** Rutherford’s model (the "planetary" model) successfully identified that the atom has a small, dense, positively charged nucleus. However, according to classical electromagnetic theory, an electron orbiting a nucleus is accelerating and should lose energy by emitting radiation. This would cause the electron to spiral into the nucleus, meaning Rutherford's atom would be inherently unstable. It also could not explain why elements emit specific, discrete wavelengths of light (emission spectra).
2.  **Analyze Bohr's Model:** Bohr proposed that electrons exist in fixed, stationary energy states (or orbits) where they do not radiate energy. He successfully used the concept of quantized energy levels ($E_n$) to derive the Rydberg formula and explain the specific visible spectral lines of hydrogen (the Balmer series) as transitions between these energy levels.
3.  **Evaluate the distractors:**
    *   **A:** Rutherford is credited with the nucleus (containing protons), not Bohr.
    *   **B:** In Bohr's model, electrons do not radiate energy while in stable orbits; the Rutherford model implied continuous acceleration and energy loss.
    *   **D:** Bohr’s model described orbits, but it was later supplanted by the Quantum Mechanical model (Schrödinger/Heisenberg), which introduced the concept of electron *probability clouds* (orbitals) rather than "firm" locations.

Because Bohr’s primary scientific triumph was resolving the issue of atomic stability and emission spectra, **C** is the definitive answer.
