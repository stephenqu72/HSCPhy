### Answer and Explanation

**Correct Answer: A**

**Explanation:**

To determine the correct answer, we must apply the **Work-Energy Theorem**, which states that the net work done on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy.

1.  **Energy Changes:** As the tennis ball moves from the bottom of the circle to the top, its height ($h$) increases relative to the bottom position. This means the ball's **gravitational potential energy** ($U = mgh$) increases. Furthermore, because gravity acts against the motion, the ball slows down as it rises, meaning its **kinetic energy** ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) decreases. Because the mechanical energy is being exchanged between kinetic and potential, and potentially affected by the external force (the person swinging it), the mechanical energy of the ball is not constant.

2.  **Work is Done:** The work-energy principle states that $W_{net} = \Delta E_k$. Since the velocity of the ball changes as it moves upward (speeding up or slowing down depending on the tension and gravity components), the kinetic energy is changing. For kinetic energy to change, work must be performed on the ball. Even if we consider the system as a whole, the force exerted by the hand/arm (via the string) is performing work to move the ball through the vertical circular path.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **B and D:** "Total work is a constant" is physically misleading in this context. Work is a process, not a state function, and the work done on the ball varies as it moves through different points of the circle.
*   **C and D:** "Its energy remains the same" is incorrect because the ball is changing height, which necessitates a change in gravitational potential energy. 

Therefore, as the ball moves from the bottom to the top, its energy state changes, and the external forces acting upon it perform work.
