To secure full marks (5/5) on this HSC Physics question, you need to articulate how specific observations of electromagnetic radiation provide empirical evidence for the Big Bang.

### Answer
The Big Bang theory is supported by two primary pieces of evidence derived from the observation of electromagnetic radiation:

1.  **Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) Radiation:** Observations by satellites (like COBE, WMAP, and Planck) have detected a uniform, isotropic glow of microwave radiation across the entire sky. This corresponds to a blackbody temperature of approximately $2.7\text{ K}$. The CMB is the "afterglow" of the Big Bang, representing the moment roughly $380,000$ years after the expansion began when the universe cooled sufficiently for protons and electrons to combine into neutral hydrogen atoms (recombination). This rendered the universe transparent, allowing photons to travel freely through space. As the universe expanded, the wavelength of these photons was stretched (redshifted) from the visible/infrared spectrum into the microwave region.
2.  **Hubble's Law and Redshift:** By observing the absorption spectra of distant galaxies, astronomers measure the Doppler shift of light. The observation that almost all distant galaxies exhibit a redshift indicates they are moving away from us. The relationship, expressed as $v = H_0d$, shows that the recession velocity $v$ is proportional to the distance $d$. This provides direct evidence of the expansion of the universe, implying that if we rewind time, the universe would have originated from a singular, dense, hot point.
