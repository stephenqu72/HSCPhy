### Answer

**(a)** The Geiger-Marsden experiment involved firing alpha particles at a thin gold foil. The observations provided key evidence for the nuclear model:
1.  **Most alpha particles passed straight through:** This indicated that the atom is mostly empty space.
2.  **Some alpha particles were deflected at small angles:** This suggested the presence of a central, positively charged core (the nucleus) that repelled the positive alpha particles.
3.  **A very small number were deflected at large angles (or backscattered):** This confirmed that the nucleus is extremely dense and contains almost all of the atom's mass, as only a highly concentrated positive charge could exert a force strong enough to reverse the path of an alpha particle.

**(b)** James Chadwick verified that beryllium rays were neutral particles (neutrons) rather than electromagnetic radiation (gamma rays) by applying the laws of **conservation of momentum and kinetic energy**. He observed the rays striking a target of paraffin wax and ejecting protons. If the rays were gamma rays (massless), the energy required to eject protons would be impossibly high based on $E=mc^2$. By analyzing the collisions between the unknown particles and protons (which have similar mass), he calculated that the incident particles must have a mass approximately equal to that of a proton. Since they were unaffected by magnetic or electric fields, he concluded they were neutral, naming them neutrons.
