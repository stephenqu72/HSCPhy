To answer this 8-mark question effectively, you must adopt a "historical evolution" framework. You are not just listing facts; you are demonstrating how the **limitations** of one model directly prompted the **successes** of the next.

### Suggested Response Structure

**1. Rutherford: The Planetary Model**
*   **Success:** The Geiger-Marsden experiment (gold foil) proved the existence of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus.
*   **Limitation:** According to classical electrodynamics, an accelerating electron orbiting a nucleus should continuously emit electromagnetic radiation, lose energy, and spiral into the nucleus. This predicted that atoms should be unstable, which contradicted the observed stability of matter.

**2. Bohr: Solving Stability with Quantization**
*   **Success:** Building on Rutherford's nucleus, Bohr introduced the idea of "stationary states." He proposed that electrons exist in discrete, quantized orbits where they do not radiate energy. Transitions between these orbits explain the emission spectra of hydrogen (Balmer series).
*   **Limitation:** Bohr’s model was "ad hoc"—he arbitrarily imposed quantization rules without a physical explanation for *why* orbits were quantized. It also failed to explain the intensities of spectral lines or the behavior of atoms with more than one electron.

**3. de Broglie: Explaining the "Why"**
*   **Success:** de Broglie proposed that matter exhibits wave-particle duality ($p = h/\lambda$). He suggested that electrons in an atom behave like standing waves. This provided the physical justification for Bohr’s orbits: only orbits with a circumference equal to an integer multiple of the electron's wavelength are stable, as constructive interference occurs.
*   **Impact:** This shifted the atomic model from "orbits" to "orbitals," providing the foundation for modern quantum mechanics (Schrödinger’s wave equation).
