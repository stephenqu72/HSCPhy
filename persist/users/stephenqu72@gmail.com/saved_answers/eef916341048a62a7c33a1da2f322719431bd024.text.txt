### Answer
As the planet moves from perihelion to aphelion, its velocity decreases. This occurs because the gravitational force from the Sun acts in the opposite direction to the planet's displacement as it moves away, doing negative work on the planet. This results in a conversion of the planet's kinetic energy into gravitational potential energy, causing the planet to slow down.

---

### How to solve this
To answer this question effectively for 2 marks, you need to connect the concepts of orbital mechanics, energy conservation, and work done:

1.  **Identify the energy change:** Use the principle of Conservation of Mechanical Energy. At perihelion, the planet is at its closest point to the Sun, meaning it has minimum gravitational potential energy and maximum kinetic energy (maximum velocity). As it moves to aphelion, it gains height in the Sun's gravitational field, meaning its gravitational potential energy increases. Because total mechanical energy is conserved, this gain in potential energy must come at the expense of kinetic energy. Therefore, the velocity must decrease.
2.  **Use the concept of Work:** Alternatively, you can explain this using forces. As the planet moves from perihelion to aphelion, it is moving against the gravitational pull of the Sun. The angle between the gravitational force vector and the velocity vector is obtuse (greater than $90^\circ$). This means the gravitational force does negative work on the planet, which, according to the Work-Energy Theorem ($W = \Delta KE$), results in a decrease in the planet's kinetic energy and, consequently, its velocity.

**HSC Tip:** In physics, "accounting for changes" requires you to explain the *why* (physics principles) rather than just stating the *what*. Mentioning both energy conservation and the work done by gravity will ensure you secure both marks.
