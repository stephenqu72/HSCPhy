To answer this question, we must analyze the forces acting on a cyclist on a banked track when they are traveling **faster** than the "design speed."

### Analysis of the Physics
1.  **The Forces:** Two main forces act on the cyclist:
    *   **Weight ($mg$):** Acting vertically downwards (represented by the green arrow).
    *   **Normal Force ($N$):** Acting perpendicular to the surface of the track (represented by the red arrow).
2.  **The Requirement:** For circular motion, the net force (resultant of weight and normal force) must provide the necessary **centripetal force** directed horizontally towards the center of the track's curvature.
3.  **Design Speed:** At the design speed, the horizontal component of the normal force alone provides the required centripetal force ($F_c = N \sin\theta$). 
4.  **Exceeding Design Speed:** If the cyclist travels faster than the design speed, the horizontal component of the normal force is insufficient to provide the necessary centripetal force ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$). To compensate, the cyclist requires an additional force acting toward the center of the turn. This is provided by **friction** acting **down the slope** of the track.

### Evaluating the Options
*   The red arrow is the Normal Force.
*   The green arrow is the Weight.
*   The third arrow represents the required extra force (friction) to maintain the turn at the higher speed.
*   Since the cyclist is moving faster than the design speed, they have a tendency to slide *up* the bank. Therefore, static friction must act **down the slope** to keep the cyclist on their intended path.

**Diagram C** correctly shows the friction force vector pointing down the slope of the track.

### Explanation
When a vehicle (or cyclist) exceeds the design speed of a banked curve, the centripetal force required exceeds that provided by the normal force alone. The system relies on static friction to provide the additional centripetal force required to complete the turn. This frictional force acts parallel to the track surface and directed down the slope, as shown in diagram C.
