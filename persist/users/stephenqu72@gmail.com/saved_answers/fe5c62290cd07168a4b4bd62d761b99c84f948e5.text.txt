To solve this problem, we must first analyze the stroboscopic photograph. Counting the images from the first to the last, there are $8$ images. Since there are $8$ images, there are $7$ intervals of $0.1\text{ s}$ each, meaning the total time of flight is $t = 0.7\text{ s}$.

### (a) Calculate the maximum height

**Assumptions:**
1. Air resistance is negligible.
2. The motion is perfectly symmetrical (the launch and landing heights are the same).
3. The acceleration due to gravity is $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$.

**Calculation:**
In projectile motion, the time taken to reach the maximum height is exactly half the total flight time:
$$t_{up} = \frac{0.7}{2} = 0.35\text{ s}$$
Using the vertical motion equation $v_y = u_y + at$, at maximum height $v_y = 0$:
$$0 = u_y - (9.8)(0.35) \implies u_y = 3.43\text{ m/s}$$
Now, use the displacement formula $s_y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2$:
$$h_{max} = (3.43)(0.35) + 0.5(-9.8)(0.35)^2$$
$$h_{max} = 1.2005 - 0.60025 = 0.60\text{ m}$$

### (b) At what velocity did the student launch upwards?

The question asks for the velocity the student launched **upwards**, which refers to the initial vertical component of velocity ($u_y$).

**Calculation:**
As derived in part (a) using the time to reach peak height ($0.35\text{ s}$) and the fact that vertical velocity becomes zero at the peak:
$$v_y = u_y - gt$$
$$0 = u_y - (9.8\text{ m/s}^2)(0.35\text{ s})$$
$$u_y = 3.43\text{ m/s}$$
The student launched with an initial vertical velocity of $3.43\text{ m/s}$.
