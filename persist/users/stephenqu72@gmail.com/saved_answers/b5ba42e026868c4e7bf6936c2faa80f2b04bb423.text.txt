### Part (a)
**Newton’s Corpuscular Theory:**
Newton proposed that light consists of tiny particles called "corpuscles." To explain refraction, he argued that particles of light experience an attractive force perpendicular to the surface of a denser medium. This force increases the velocity of the light particles, causing them to bend towards the normal as they enter the denser medium. Therefore, Newton’s theory incorrectly predicted that light travels faster in denser media (e.g., water) than in air.

**Huygens’ Wave Theory:**
Huygens proposed that light is a wave propagating through an all-encompassing "luminiferous aether." He explained refraction using the wave-front model. As a light wave hits a denser medium at an angle, one part of the wave-front enters the medium first and slows down. Because the rest of the wave-front continues to travel at the original speed for a moment longer, the wave-front pivots and bends towards the normal. Huygens correctly predicted that light travels slower in denser media.

### Part (b)
Young’s double slit experiment involved passing a coherent light source through two closely spaced narrow slits. The light emerging from the slits acted as coherent point sources, which overlapped on a distant screen to produce an interference pattern consisting of alternating bright and dark fringes. 

This provided strong evidence against Newton’s corpuscular theory because:
1. **Interference:** Particles (corpuscles) would simply pass through the slits and create two distinct bright bands on the screen, rather than a pattern of interference.
2. **Wave characteristics:** The presence of dark fringes (where light cancels light) could only be explained by destructive interference, a property unique to waves. Newton’s theory could not explain how two streams of particles could combine to produce "darkness."
