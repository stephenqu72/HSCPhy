### Part (a)
To find the initial velocity ($u$), we use the Pythagorean theorem because the horizontal velocity ($u_x$) and vertical velocity ($u_y$) are perpendicular vectors.

**Calculation:**
$$u = \sqrt{u_x^2 + u_y^2}$$
$$u = \sqrt{10.0^2 + 12.0^2}$$
$$u = \sqrt{100 + 144}$$
$$u = \sqrt{244}$$
$$u \approx 15.62 \, m \, s^{-1}$$

**Explanation:** The magnitude of the initial velocity is the vector sum of its horizontal and vertical components. Since they form a right-angled triangle, we use the Pythagorean theorem to calculate the resultant vector.

---

### Part (b)
In projectile motion, horizontal velocity is constant because there is no air resistance (acceleration $a_x = 0$). The horizontal distance ($s$) covered is simply the product of horizontal velocity and time.

**Calculation:**
$$s = u_x \times t$$
$$s = 10.0 \, m \, s^{-1} \times 2.27 \, s$$
$$s = 22.7 \, m$$

**Explanation:** Because we are ignoring air resistance, the ball maintains a constant horizontal velocity of $10.0 \, m \, s^{-1}$ throughout its entire flight. Regardless of the vertical heights (starting at $1.00 \, m$ and ending at $3.00 \, m$), the horizontal distance is dictated solely by how fast it moves horizontally and how long it remains in the air.
