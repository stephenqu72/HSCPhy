### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** To determine the metal, you must extrapolate the line of best fit through the data points until it intercepts the horizontal axis (the frequency axis). This intercept represents the **threshold frequency** ($f_0$) of the metal. Once identified, you can calculate the work function using the formula $W = hf_0$, where $h$ is Planck's constant. By comparing this calculated value to the table of known work functions, you can identify the metal used.

**Explanation:** In the photoelectric effect, the relationship between kinetic energy ($E_k$) and frequency ($f$) is defined by Einstein's equation: $E_k = hf - W$. When $E_k = 0$, the frequency corresponds to the threshold frequency ($f_0$), which is unique to the material's work function ($W = hf_0$). Graphically, this is the $x$-intercept.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:** The photoelectric current is generally **independent** of the frequency of the incident light, provided the frequency is above the threshold frequency. 
- The **magnitude** of the photoelectric current is determined by the **intensity** (or brightness) of the incident light, which relates to the number of photons striking the surface per second.
- Changing the frequency of light only changes the kinetic energy of the individual ejected photoelectrons; it does not change the number of electrons ejected per second.
- Note: If the frequency is below the threshold frequency, the photoelectric current will be zero, regardless of the intensity.

**Explanation:** This is a classic HSC physics concept. Students often confuse "energy of electrons" (linked to frequency) with "number of electrons" (linked to intensity). Remember:
1. Intensity $\rightarrow$ Number of photons $\rightarrow$ Number of electrons $\rightarrow$ **Current**.
2. Frequency $\rightarrow$ Energy per photon $\rightarrow$ Energy per electron $\rightarrow$ **Kinetic Energy/Stopping Potential**.
