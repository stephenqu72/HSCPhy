### Answer
The correct answer is **D. Protons and neutrons can undergo beta decay.**

---

### Explanation
To determine if a particle is "fundamental" (an elementary particle), we look for evidence that it is composed of smaller sub-components. According to the Standard Model of particle physics, protons and neutrons are classified as **hadrons** (specifically baryons), which are made up of smaller, indivisible particles called **quarks**.

Here is why the other options are incorrect:
*   **A and B:** Mass and charge are intrinsic properties of particles. Protons having a specific mass or neutrons having zero net charge are simply observed characteristics of these particles, but they do not prove that they are composed of smaller internal structures.
*   **C:** This statement is actually factually incorrect. Neutrons do possess a non-zero magnetic moment (magnetic field) because they are composed of charged quarks that result in a net magnetic distribution, even though the total electric charge is zero.

**Why D is the correct answer:**
Beta decay is a process governed by the weak nuclear force, where a neutron transforms into a proton (or vice versa). During beta-minus decay, a neutron decays into a proton, an electron, and an antineutrino ($n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$). 

At the level of quarks, this is represented as a down quark ($d$) changing into an up quark ($u$) by emitting a $W^-$ boson. The fact that these particles can transform into one another and interact via the weak force is strong evidence that they are not fundamental "points," but are composite structures made of quarks interacting via the strong force.
