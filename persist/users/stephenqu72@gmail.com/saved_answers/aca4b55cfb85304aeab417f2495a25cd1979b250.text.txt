### Explanation
To solve this, you need to understand the fundamental difference between transverse and longitudinal waves in the context of oscillation:

1.  **Define Polarisation:** Polarisation refers to the restriction of the orientation of the oscillations of a wave to a single plane.
2.  **Transverse Waves:** In a transverse wave (such as light or electromagnetic waves), the oscillations occur perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation. Because these oscillations can occur in any direction within the plane perpendicular to the direction of travel, they can be "filtered" or constrained to a specific plane. This is what we call polarisation.
3.  **Longitudinal Waves:** In a longitudinal wave (such as sound waves), the particles oscillate parallel to the direction of energy propagation (along the same axis as the travel). Because there is no "side-to-side" oscillation relative to the direction of travel, there is no plane to polarise. Therefore, longitudinal waves cannot be polarised.

Since polarisation requires oscillations to have a direction that can be filtered out (which is only possible if the oscillation is perpendicular to the direction of propagation), it is an exclusive property of **transverse waves**.
