The correct answer is **(C) Meson**.

### Explanation

To solve this, you need to understand the classification of particles in the Standard Model of particle physics:

1.  **Fundamental Particles:** These are particles that are not composed of smaller sub-components. According to the Standard Model, these include **quarks** and **leptons**.
2.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **(A) Electron:** This is a fundamental particle (it is a lepton).
    *   **(B) Muon:** This is also a fundamental particle (it is a lepton, specifically a second-generation cousin of the electron).
    *   **(D) Strange:** This refers to the "strange quark," which is a fundamental particle (a type of quark).
    *   **(C) Meson:** A meson is a **composite particle**. It is made up of a quark and an antiquark held together by the strong nuclear force. Because it is composed of multiple smaller particles, it is **not** a fundamental particle.

By identifying which particles are indivisible (fundamental) versus which are bound states (composite), we can conclude that the meson is the only choice that does not fit the definition of a fundamental particle.
