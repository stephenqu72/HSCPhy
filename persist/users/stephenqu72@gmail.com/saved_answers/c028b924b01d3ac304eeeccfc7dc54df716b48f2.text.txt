### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must understand the fundamental physical principle behind Kepler’s Second Law: the **Conservation of Angular Momentum**.

1.  **Understanding Kepler's Second Law:** Kepler’s Second Law states that a line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. This implies that a planet moves faster when it is closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when it is further away (aphelion).
2.  **The Physics Principle:** Because the gravitational force exerted by the Sun on the planet acts along the radial line (pointing toward the center of the Sun), the torque $\tau$ exerted on the planet is zero ($\tau = r \times F \sin(\theta) = 0$). Since torque is the rate of change of angular momentum ($\tau = \frac{dL}{dt}$), the angular momentum $L$ of the planet must be conserved.
3.  **Energy Considerations:** As a planet moves closer to the central body (the Sun), it moves from a region of higher gravitational potential energy to a region of lower gravitational potential energy. Since the total mechanical energy of the orbit is conserved, the decrease in gravitational potential energy ($U_g = -\frac{GMm}{r}$) must be compensated for by an increase in kinetic energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$).
    *   As $r$ decreases, gravitational potential energy becomes more negative (it decreases/loses value).
    *   To conserve total energy, the kinetic energy must increase, which is why the planet speeds up.
4.  **Why other options are incorrect:**
    *   (A) is incorrect because the planet *gains* kinetic energy as it moves closer.
    *   (B) describes the *geometry* of the orbit (Kepler's First Law), not the underlying physical law of motion.
    *   (C) describes the relationship found in Kepler's Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$), not the second law.

Therefore, the physics underpinning the observation that a planet sweeps equal areas in equal times (which necessitates changing speeds) is the conservation of energy, specifically the planet **losing gravitational potential energy as it moves closer to the central body**, resulting in an equal gain in kinetic energy.
