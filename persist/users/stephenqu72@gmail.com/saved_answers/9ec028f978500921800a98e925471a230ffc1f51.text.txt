### Explanation
To solve this problem, we evaluate each statement based on the physics of the nucleus:

*   **Statement A is correct:** According to Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = \Delta m c^2$. Binding energy is defined as the energy equivalent of the mass defect ($\Delta m$), where $c$ is the speed of light.
*   **Statement B is INCORRECT:** Stability is determined by the **binding energy per nucleon**, not just the total binding energy. Furthermore, the most stable nuclei (like Iron-56) have the *highest* binding energy per nucleon, not the smallest. A small binding energy generally indicates a less stable nucleus.
*   **Statement C is correct:** This is a standard definition of nuclear binding energy. It represents the work required to overcome the strong nuclear force to separate a nucleus into individual, unbound protons and neutrons.
*   **Statement D is correct:** Due to the law of conservation of energy, the energy required to disassemble a nucleus is exactly equal to the energy released when those same components are brought together to form the nucleus.

Because statement B describes the relationship between stability and binding energy incorrectly, it is the correct choice for this question.
