### Answer
The correct answer is **A. 1:1**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we look at the physics of orbital motion. A satellite in a circular orbit is held in place by the gravitational force provided by the Earth, which acts as the centripetal force. 

We can set the gravitational force equal to the centripetal force:
$$F_g = F_c$$
$$\frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v^2}{r}$$

Where:
- $G$ is the gravitational constant
- $M$ is the mass of the Earth
- $m$ is the mass of the satellite
- $r$ is the orbital radius
- $v$ is the orbital speed

Notice that the mass of the satellite ($m$) appears on both sides of the equation and therefore cancels out:
$$\frac{G M}{r} = v^2$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}}$$

As shown by the final formula, the orbital speed ($v$) depends **only** on the mass of the central body ($M$) and the orbital radius ($r$). It is independent of the mass of the satellite ($m$). 

Since both satellites (WS-1 and WS-2) are in the same orbit, their orbital radii ($r$) are identical. Because their speeds do not depend on their masses, both satellites must travel at the same orbital speed regardless of the fact that WS-1 is four times heavier than WS-2. Thus, the ratio of their speeds is $1:1$.
