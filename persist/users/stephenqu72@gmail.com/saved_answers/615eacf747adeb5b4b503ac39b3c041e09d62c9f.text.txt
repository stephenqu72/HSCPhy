To determine the time at which the induced electromotive force (EMF) is at a maximum, we must apply Faraday’s Law of Induction.

### The Physics Principle
Faraday's Law states that the magnitude of the induced EMF ($\varepsilon$) in a coil is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux ($\Phi$) with respect to time ($t$):
$$\varepsilon = -N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$
In terms of a graph of flux versus time, the rate of change of flux is represented by the **gradient (slope)** of the line. Therefore, the induced EMF is at a maximum where the absolute value of the slope of the flux-time graph is steepest.

### Step-by-Step Analysis
1.  **Point A:** The graph is a horizontal line. The gradient is zero, so the induced EMF is zero.
2.  **Point B:** This point lies on the steep, straight-line section of the graph connecting the negative flux region to the positive flux region. This section has a constant, high positive slope. This indicates a high rate of change, meaning the induced EMF is at a high, constant value here.
3.  **Point C:** The graph is a horizontal line at the peak of the flux. The gradient is zero, so the induced EMF is zero.
4.  **Point D:** The graph shows a curve transitioning to a horizontal line. While the slope is changing, it is clearly less steep than the straight-line section near point B.

### Conclusion
The magnitude of the slope is greatest at the straight-line segment passing through point B. Since the induced EMF is directly proportional to the magnitude of the slope, the EMF is at a maximum at point B.

The correct answer is **B**.
