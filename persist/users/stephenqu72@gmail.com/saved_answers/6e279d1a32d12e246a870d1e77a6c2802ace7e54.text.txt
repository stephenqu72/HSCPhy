### Answer
To explain how atoms remain intact, we must address the interplay of three of the four fundamental forces within the Standard Model:

1.  **Electromagnetic Force:** This force acts between the positively charged nucleus and the negatively charged electrons. It is responsible for the electrostatic attraction that keeps electrons bound in their orbitals around the nucleus, defining the overall structure and size of the atom.
2.  **Strong Nuclear Force:** Within the nucleus, the electromagnetic force creates a massive repulsive force between the positively charged protons. The strong nuclear force (mediated by gluons) acts between quarks to bind them into nucleons (protons and neutrons) and, more broadly, acts as the residual strong force to overcome the electrostatic repulsion between protons, keeping the nucleus stable.
3.  **Weak Nuclear Force:** While not responsible for the static "intactness" of stable atoms, the weak nuclear force is responsible for radioactive decay (e.g., beta decay). It allows for the transmutation of neutrons into protons (or vice versa), which influences the stability of the nucleus over time.
4.  **Gravitational Force:** Gravity is negligible at the atomic scale, as the masses of subatomic particles are too small for gravitational attraction to have any significant effect on the structure of the atom compared to the other three forces.
