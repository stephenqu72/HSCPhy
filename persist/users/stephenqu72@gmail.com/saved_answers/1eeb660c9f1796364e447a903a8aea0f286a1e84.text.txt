The correct answer is **B. Emission spectroscopy**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the history of atomic models and how experimental evidence shaped our current understanding of the atom:

1.  **The Context:** Before the Bohr model (which introduced quantized energy levels for electrons), classical physics suggested that an orbiting electron should continuously lose energy as it accelerates, causing it to spiral into the nucleus. This would result in a "continuous" spectrum of light emission.
2.  **Emission Spectroscopy:** By passing electricity through gases (like hydrogen) and analyzing the emitted light using a spectrometer, scientists observed that atoms emit light only at specific, discrete wavelengths (a line spectrum).
3.  **The Link:** This observation was the crucial evidence that led Niels Bohr to propose that electrons move in stable orbits with discrete (quantized) energy levels. When an electron transitions between these levels, it emits a photon with energy $E = \Delta E = hf$, corresponding exactly to the specific lines seen in the emission spectrum.
4.  **Why the others are incorrect:**
    *   **Electron diffraction:** (Option A) provided evidence for the wave nature of electrons (de Broglie's hypothesis), which *explains* why energy levels are quantized, but it was not the *first* technique to provide evidence that energy levels exist.
    *   **Radioactive decay:** (Option C) relates to nuclear physics and the stability of the nucleus, not the electronic energy levels of an atom.
    *   **X-ray diffraction:** (Option D) provided evidence for the crystalline structure of materials and the arrangement of atoms, rather than the internal energy states of electrons within a single atom.

Therefore, the observation of discrete lines in emission spectra was the first piece of experimental evidence that forced a move toward a quantum model of electron energy.
