To solve this problem, we use the radioactive decay formula, which describes the remaining quantity of a substance over time.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the variables:**
    *   Initial number of nuclei, $N_0 = 90$.
    *   Elapsed time, $t = 1.3 \times t_{1/2}$.
    *   We need to find the remaining number of nuclei, $N(t)$.

2.  **Recall the radioactive decay formula:**
    The number of nuclei remaining after time $t$ is given by:
    $$N(t) = N_0 \left( \frac{1}{2} \right)^{\frac{t}{t_{1/2}}}$$

3.  **Substitute the known values into the equation:**
    Since $t = 1.3 \times t_{1/2}$, the exponent becomes:
    $$\frac{t}{t_{1/2}} = \frac{1.3 \times t_{1/2}}{t_{1/2}} = 1.3$$

    Now, plug this into the formula:
    $$N(t) = 90 \times (0.5)^{1.3}$$

4.  **Perform the calculation:**
    Using a calculator for the power:
    $$(0.5)^{1.3} \approx 0.4061$$
    $$N(t) = 90 \times 0.4061 \approx 36.549$$

5.  **Interpret the result:**
    In physics problems involving discrete counts of radioactive nuclei, we generally round to the nearest whole number. The result $36.549$ rounds to **37**.
