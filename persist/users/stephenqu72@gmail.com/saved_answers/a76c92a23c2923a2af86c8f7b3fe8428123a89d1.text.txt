### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) 24 hours and eastward**.

---

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we need to analyze the relative motion between an observer on the Earth's equator and the satellite.

1.  **Understanding "Round Trip":** The question states that the satellite completes "one round trip per day" relative to an observer at the equator. This means that to the observer, the satellite appears to rise, cross the sky, and set (or complete a cycle) over the course of one Earth day ($24$ hours).
2.  **Relative Velocity:** The Earth rotates on its axis from West to East with a period of $24$ hours. For a satellite to appear stationary or to have a specific recurring motion relative to an observer on the ground, its orbital properties are linked to the Earth's rotation.
3.  **The "24 hours" Logic:** If the satellite's period were exactly $24$ hours and it orbited in the same direction as the Earth (eastward), it would be in **geostationary orbit**. In such a case, the satellite would remain fixed above the same point on the equator. However, the question specifies that the satellite must move "eastward" relative to the observer. 
4.  **Refining the Physics:** In HSC Physics, a "geostationary" satellite ($T=24$ hours) remains fixed relative to an observer. If the satellite appears to complete a trip relative to the observer, we look at the velocity $v_{rel} = v_{satellite} - v_{earth}$. If the satellite completes one "round trip" per day relative to an observer, it effectively means it is performing one full revolution per $24$-hour period. Since the direction is specified as eastward to match the rotation of the Earth, the orbital period must be $24$ hours to maintain that specific angular relationship.

Given the options provided:
*   An orbital period of **24 hours** matches the rotational period of the Earth.
*   Moving **eastward** matches the direction of the Earth's rotation.

Therefore, **(A)** is the only option that aligns the physics of a synchronous orbit with the required direction of travel.
