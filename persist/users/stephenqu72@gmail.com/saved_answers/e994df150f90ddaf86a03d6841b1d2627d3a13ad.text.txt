To achieve full marks on this HSC physics question, you must demonstrate a structured understanding of the transition from classical physics to quantum mechanics.

### Answer

The nature of light has evolved from the classical wave model to the modern quantum model, each addressing different physical phenomena.

**1. The Wave Model of Light**
Historically, the wave model gained dominance in the 19th century. **Thomas Young’s double-slit experiment** (1801) provided definitive evidence by demonstrating diffraction and interference patterns, which could not be explained by Newton’s corpuscular theory. Later, **James Clerk Maxwell** (1860s) unified electricity and magnetism, proposing that light is an electromagnetic wave travelling at $c = 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$. This model effectively explains reflection, refraction, diffraction, and interference.

**2. The Quantum Model of Light**
By the turn of the 20th century, the wave model failed to explain the **photoelectric effect** and **black-body radiation**.
*   **Max Planck** proposed that energy is emitted in discrete "quanta," where $E = hf$.
*   **Albert Einstein** extended this in 1905 to explain the photoelectric effect, proposing that light itself consists of particles called photons. Unlike waves, where intensity dictates energy, the quantum model dictates that energy depends on frequency: $E = hf$. A single photon must possess enough energy (the work function, $\Phi$) to eject an electron, regardless of intensity.

**3. Comparison and Contrast**
*   **Contrast:** The wave model treats light as a continuous, delocalised disturbance in the electromagnetic field, whereas the quantum model treats light as discrete, localised packets (photons). The wave model explains interference (where intensity adds); the quantum model explains the photoelectric effect (where discrete energy transfer occurs).
*   **Comparison:** Both models are now synthesised under the principle of **wave-particle duality**. Light exhibits wave-like behaviour during propagation and particle-like behaviour during interaction with matter.
