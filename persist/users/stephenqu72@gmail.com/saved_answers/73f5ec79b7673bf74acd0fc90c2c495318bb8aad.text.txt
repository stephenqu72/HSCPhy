### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) Translational velocity of the star**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must apply the principles of the **Doppler Effect** as it relates to astrophysics:

1.  **Understanding the Doppler Effect:** When a source of waves (such as light from a star) is moving relative to an observer, the observed wavelength of those waves changes. 
    *   If the source is moving **away** from the observer, the light waves are "stretched," shifting them toward the red end of the spectrum (a **redshift**).
    *   If the source is moving **toward** the observer, the light waves are "compressed," shifting them toward the blue end of the spectrum (a **blueshift**).

2.  **Analyzing the Diagram:** The diagram shows a spectral line (a peak representing specific light frequencies emitted by atoms in the star) shifting its position along the horizontal wavelength axis. This change in the observed wavelength compared to a reference (stationary) spectrum is a direct measurement of the radial (translational) velocity of the star relative to Earth.

3.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **(A) Temperature:** Typically determined by the peak wavelength of the continuous blackbody spectrum (Wien's Displacement Law) or the relative intensities of different spectral lines.
    *   **(B) Translational velocity:** Correct. The Doppler shift is directly related to the radial velocity of the star along the line of sight.
    *   **(C) Rotational velocity:** This is typically identified by the *broadening* of spectral lines (Doppler broadening), not a shift of the entire peak.
    *   **(D) Density:** This is usually determined by the pressure broadening of spectral lines.

Therefore, because the entire spectral feature is shifting along the wavelength axis, it indicates the **translational velocity** of the star.
