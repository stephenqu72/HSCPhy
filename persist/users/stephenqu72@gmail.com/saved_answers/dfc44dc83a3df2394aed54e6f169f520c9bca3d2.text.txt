### Answer

**(a)** The force of gravity exerted on the moon by the Earth is **equal in magnitude** and **opposite in direction** to the force of gravity exerted on the Earth by the moon. This is a direct application of Newton’s Third Law of Motion.

**(b)**
*   **Mass:** The mass of the person is constant regardless of location. Therefore, the mass on the moon is $70\text{ kg}$ and the mass on the Earth is $70\text{ kg}$.
*   **Weight:** Weight is calculated using $W = mg$.
    *   Weight on Earth: $W_E = 70\text{ kg} \times 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2} = 686\text{ N}$.
    *   Weight on the moon: $W_M = 70\text{ kg} \times 1.6\text{ m s}^{-2} = 112\text{ N}$.
    *   Comparison: The person weighs significantly less on the moon ($112\text{ N}$) compared to the Earth ($686\text{ N}$).

### Explanation

**Part (a):**
In HSC Physics, it is a common trap to assume that the larger body (Earth) exerts a "stronger" force. However, Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation states that the force is $F = G \frac{M_1 M_2}{r^2}$. Because both $M_1$ and $M_2$ are included in the calculation, the force is mutual. According to Newton’s Third Law, for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction; thus, the Earth pulls on the moon with the exact same force that the moon pulls on the Earth.

**Part (b):**
To solve this, you must distinguish between the two physical concepts:
1.  **Mass** is a scalar quantity representing the amount of matter in an object. It is intrinsic and does not change based on gravitational fields.
2.  **Weight** is the force due to gravity acting on an object, defined by the formula $W = mg$. Since the acceleration due to gravity ($g$) differs between the two locations, the weight will differ accordingly. By substituting the given values for $g$ and the constant mass of $70\text{ kg}$, you can calculate the numerical values and compare them.
