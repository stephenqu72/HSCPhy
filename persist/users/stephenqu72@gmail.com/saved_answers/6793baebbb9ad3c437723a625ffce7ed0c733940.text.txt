To achieve a geostationary orbit, a satellite must satisfy three primary physical requirements:

1.  **Orbital Period:** The satellite must have a period of exactly one sidereal day ($T \approx 86\,164$ seconds) so that it remains fixed relative to a point on the Earth's surface.
2.  **Orbital Plane:** The orbit must lie directly above the Earth's equator.
3.  **Direction:** The satellite must orbit in the same direction as the Earth's rotation (west to east).

### Calculation
To find the required orbital radius ($r$), we equate the gravitational force to the centripetal force:

$$F_g = F_c$$
$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m r \omega^2$$

Since $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$, we use Kepler’s Third Law relationship:
$$r^3 = \frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}$$

Using the Earth's mass $M \approx 5.97 \times 10^{24}$ kg and the universal gravitational constant $G \approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ Nm}^2/\text{kg}^2$:

$$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 5.97 \times 10^{24} \times 86\,164^2}{4\pi^2}}$$
$$r \approx 4.22 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$$

This corresponds to an altitude of approximately $35\,800$ km above the Earth's surface.
