### Answer
The correct answer is **B. $0.87\text{ m s}^{-1}$**.

### Explanation
To solve this projectile motion problem, we must understand how velocity behaves at different points in a trajectory:

1.  **Decompose the initial velocity:** The initial speed $v = 1.35\text{ m s}^{-1}$ is directed at an angle $\theta = 50^\circ$ to the horizontal. We can split this into horizontal ($v_x$) and vertical ($v_y$) components:
    *   $v_x = v \cdot \cos(\theta)$
    *   $v_y = v \cdot \sin(\theta)$

2.  **Analyze the motion at the highest point:** 
    *   In projectile motion, air resistance is neglected. Therefore, there is no horizontal acceleration, meaning the horizontal velocity component ($v_x$) remains constant throughout the entire flight.
    *   At the **highest point** of the trajectory, the vertical component of the velocity ($v_y$) is momentarily zero, as the object stops moving upwards before it begins to descend.

3.  **Calculate the speed:** Since the vertical velocity is zero at the peak, the resultant speed of the frog is simply equal to the horizontal velocity component:
    $$v_{peak} = v_x = v \cdot \cos(\theta)$$
    $$v_{peak} = 1.35 \cdot \cos(50^\circ)$$
    Using a calculator (ensure it is in degree mode):
    $$v_{peak} \approx 1.35 \cdot 0.6428$$
    $$v_{peak} \approx 0.8677\text{ m s}^{-1}$$

Rounding to two decimal places, we get **$0.87\text{ m s}^{-1}$**, which corresponds to option **B**.
