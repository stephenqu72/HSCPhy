### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** The mass of the planet does not affect its orbital speed because the centripetal force required to keep the planet in orbit is provided entirely by the gravitational force of the star. When you equate the centripetal force to the gravitational force:
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
The mass of the planet ($m$) appears on both sides of the equation and therefore cancels out. Rearranging for orbital speed ($v$), we get:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Since $v$ depends only on the gravitational constant ($G$), the mass of the star ($M$), and the orbital radius ($r$), the mass of the planet itself is irrelevant to the orbital speed.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:** To solve this, we use Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion, which states that the ratio of the square of the orbital period ($T$) to the cube of the semi-major axis ($r$) is constant for all bodies orbiting the same central mass:
$$\frac{r_1^3}{T_1^2} = \frac{r_2^3}{T_2^2}$$

**Step-by-step calculation:**
1. Identify the knowns:
   - $r_1 = 1.64$ AU
   - $T_1 = 590$ days
   - $T_2 = 365$ days
   - $r_2 = ?$
2. Set up the equation:
   $$\frac{(1.64)^3}{(590)^2} = \frac{r_2^3}{(365)^2}$$
3. Isolate $r_2^3$:
   $$r_2^3 = \frac{(1.64)^3 \times (365)^2}{(590)^2}$$
   $$r_2^3 = \frac{4.410944 \times 133225}{348100}$$
   $$r_2^3 \approx 1.688$$
4. Solve for $r_2$:
   $$r_2 = \sqrt[3]{1.688} \approx 1.19 \text{ AU}$$

The satellite is approximately **1.19 AU** from Pollux.
