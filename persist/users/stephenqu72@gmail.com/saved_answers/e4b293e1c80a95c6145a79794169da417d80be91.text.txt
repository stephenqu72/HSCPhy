### Explanation
To solve this, we need to compare the orbital characteristics and energy of satellites in Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) and Geostationary Orbit (GEO).

**1. Comparing Orbital Period:**
Kepler’s Third Law states that the square of the orbital period $T$ is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius $r$ ($T^2 \propto r^3$). 
*   A LEO satellite is much closer to Earth than a GEO satellite.
*   Since the orbital radius of a GEO satellite is significantly larger than that of a LEO satellite, the GEO satellite must have a **greater orbital period**. (A GEO satellite takes 24 hours to orbit, while a LEO satellite typically takes about 90 minutes).

**2. Comparing Total Energy:**
The total energy $E_{total}$ of an orbiting satellite is given by the sum of its kinetic and gravitational potential energy. Using the formula provided in the prompt (and standard orbital mechanics), the total energy is:
$$E_{total} = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$
*   Where $G$ is the gravitational constant, $M$ is the mass of the Earth, $m$ is the mass of the satellite, and $r$ is the orbital radius.
*   Because the energy is negative, as the orbital radius $r$ increases, the denominator increases, making the value *less negative* (i.e., closer to zero).
*   A "less negative" number is a larger number mathematically. Therefore, the satellite further away from Earth (the GEO satellite) has a **greater total energy** than the satellite closer to Earth (the LEO satellite).

**Conclusion:**
*   Greater orbital period: **GEO**
*   Greater total energy: **GEO**

This corresponds to row **D**.
