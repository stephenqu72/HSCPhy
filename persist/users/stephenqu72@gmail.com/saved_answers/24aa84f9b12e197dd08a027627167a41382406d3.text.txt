### Solution

#### (a) Calculate the mass of the star
To find the mass of the central star, we use Kepler's Third Law (in its Newtonian form). For an object in a circular orbit:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$
Where:
- $r = 7.0 \times 10^{10} \text{ m}$
- $T = 50 \text{ days} = 50 \times 24 \times 3600 \text{ s} = 4,320,000 \text{ s}$
- $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3 \text{kg}^{-1} \text{s}^{-2}$

Rearranging for $M$:
$$M = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GT^2}$$

Substitute the values:
$$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (7.0 \times 10^{10})^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (4,320,000)^2}$$
$$M \approx \frac{1.36 \times 10^{34}}{1.24 \times 10^3} \approx 1.10 \times 10^{31} \text{ kg}$$

#### (b) Explain why it is not possible to calculate the mass of the planet
The mass of the orbiting planet cancels out in the derivation of the orbital mechanics equations (Kepler’s Third Law). The gravitational force providing the centripetal force is:
$$\frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v^2}{r}$$
As seen here, the mass of the planet ($m$) appears on both sides of the equation and is eliminated. Consequently, the orbital period and radius of a planet depend only on the mass of the central star ($M$) and the distance ($r$), regardless of the planet's own mass.

### How to solve it
1.  **Unit Conversion:** Always ensure time is in SI units (seconds). $50 \text{ days} \times 24 \text{ hours} \times 3600 \text{ seconds}$.
2.  **Formula Selection:** Recognise that for circular orbits, $F_g = F_c$ leads to $GM/r^2 = v^2/r$. By substituting $v = 2\pi r / T$, you derive Kepler’s Third Law, which is the standard tool for calculating stellar mass.
3.  **Conceptual Understanding:** For part (b), remember the equivalence principle or simply the mathematics of orbital motion: the inertia of an object is proportional to its mass, exactly cancelling out the increased gravitational pull it experiences. Therefore, observation of the orbit alone cannot reveal the planet's mass.
