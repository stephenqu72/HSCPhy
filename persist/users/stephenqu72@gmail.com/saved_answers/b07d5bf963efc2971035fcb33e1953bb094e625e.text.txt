### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must use Einstein’s photoelectric equation, which relates the maximum kinetic energy ($E_{k\text{max}}$) of photoelectrons to the frequency ($f$) of incident light:

$$E_{k\text{max}} = hf - \Phi$$

Where:
- $h$ is Planck's constant.
- $f$ is the frequency of incident light.
- $\Phi$ is the work function of the metal (the minimum energy required to remove an electron).

This equation is in the form of a linear equation $y = mx + c$, where:
- The slope ($m$) is equal to Planck's constant ($h$).
- The y-intercept ($c$) is equal to negative work function ($-\Phi$).
- The x-intercept occurs when $E_{k\text{max}} = 0$, which is $f_0 = \frac{\Phi}{h}$ (where $f_0$ is the threshold frequency).

**Analysis of the changes:**
1. **Slope:** Since the experiment uses the same fundamental physics (Planck's constant remains unchanged), the slope of the line must remain the same. This rules out options C and D, where the slopes change.
2. **Work Function ($\Phi$):** The problem states the new metal has a **larger** work function. 
3. **Threshold Frequency ($f_0$):** Since $f_0 = \frac{\Phi}{h}$, an increase in $\Phi$ results in a higher threshold frequency. This means the x-intercept of the graph must shift to the right.

Looking at the options, **A** shows a dashed line that is parallel to the original solid line (same slope) but shifted to the right (larger x-intercept/higher threshold frequency). This perfectly represents a metal with a larger work function.
