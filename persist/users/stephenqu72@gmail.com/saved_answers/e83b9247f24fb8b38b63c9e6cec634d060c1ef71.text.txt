### **Answer**

**a) Outline Young’s double-slit experiment and its implications (3 marks)**
Thomas Young’s experiment involved passing a coherent light source through two narrow, closely spaced slits. The light emerging from these slits acts as two coherent sources, which overlap on a distant screen. This produces a characteristic interference pattern consisting of alternating bright and dark fringes. 
**Implications:** The observation of these fringes proves that light behaves as a wave. Bright fringes represent regions of constructive interference (where waves arrive in phase), and dark fringes represent destructive interference (where waves arrive out of phase). This effectively challenged the then-dominant Newtonian corpuscular (particle) theory of light.

**b) Single-photon double-slit experiment as evidence of dual nature (3 marks)**
When the intensity of light is reduced so that only one photon is emitted at a time, an interference pattern still builds up over time on the detector. This indicates that a single photon displays wave-like properties by passing through both slits simultaneously and interfering with itself. However, the photon is detected as a discrete "hit" at a specific location on the screen, demonstrating particle-like properties. This experiment provides evidence for wave-particle duality: light exhibits both wave-like behavior (to form the pattern) and particle-like behavior (the discrete arrival at the detector).

2.  **Part (b) Strategy:**
    *   **Focus on the paradox:** The key is to explain how a *single* photon can still produce an interference pattern. 
    *   **Address Duality:** You must clearly articulate why this experiment forces the acceptance of both models. If light were *only* a wave, we wouldn't see discrete hits. If it were *only* a particle, it would pass through only one slit and not create an interference pattern. The result necessitates the dual-nature model.
