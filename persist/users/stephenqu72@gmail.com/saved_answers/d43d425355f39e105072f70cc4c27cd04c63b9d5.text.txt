To determine which planet has the lowest escape velocity, we use the formula for escape velocity ($v_e$):

$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}$$

Where:
*   $G$ is the gravitational constant (a constant for all).
*   $M$ is the mass of the planet.
*   $r$ is the radius of the planet.

### Analysis
Since $2$ and $G$ are constants, the escape velocity is directly proportional to $\sqrt{\frac{M}{r}}$. To find the **lowest** escape velocity, we need to identify the planet that provides the **smallest ratio of mass to radius** ($\frac{M}{r}$).

Let's calculate the ratio $M/r$ (in units of $10^{24} \text{ kg} / \text{km}$) for each:

*   **A) Newton:** $\frac{5}{8000} = 0.000625$
*   **B) Einstein:** $\frac{8}{7800} \approx 0.001026$
*   **C) Bohr:** $\frac{5}{9500} \approx 0.000526$
*   **D) Hawkins:** $\frac{8}{10200} \approx 0.000784$

### Conclusion
Comparing the ratios:
*   Einstein has the highest ratio (largest mass, smallest radius), meaning it has the *highest* escape velocity.
*   Bohr has the lowest ratio ($0.000526$), meaning it has the *lowest* escape velocity. 

Intuitively, escape velocity decreases as mass decreases and radius increases. Planet Bohr has one of the smallest masses ($5 \times 10^{24}$) and one of the largest radii ($9500 \text{ km}$), making it the easiest planet to escape from.

The correct answer is **C) Bohr**.
