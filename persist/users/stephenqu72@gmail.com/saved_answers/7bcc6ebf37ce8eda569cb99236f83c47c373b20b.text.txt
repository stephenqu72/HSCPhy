### Answer
At the time of the Geiger-Marsden experiment, the **"Plum Pudding" model** proposed by J.J. Thomson suggested that atoms consisted of a diffuse sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded throughout.

1. **Support for the existing model (Insufficient evidence):** The vast majority of alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil with little or no deflection. This supported the notion that the atom was mostly empty space and that the positive charge was diffuse rather than concentrated, as Thomson’s model predicted that alpha particles would experience only negligible electrostatic forces.
2. **Evidence that the model was insufficient:** A small but significant proportion of alpha particles were deflected at large angles, and a very small fraction were backscattered (deflected by more than $90^{\circ}$). This could not be explained by the Plum Pudding model, as a diffuse cloud of charge would not exert a strong enough electric field to reverse the trajectory of a fast-moving, massive alpha particle. These results suggested that the atom must contain a tiny, dense, and positively charged center (the nucleus) to provide the necessary electrostatic repulsion.
