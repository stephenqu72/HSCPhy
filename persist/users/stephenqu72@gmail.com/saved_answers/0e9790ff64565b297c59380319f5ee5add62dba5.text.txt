### Explanation

To solve this question, we must understand the historical context of the **Geiger-Marsden experiment** (also known as the Rutherford gold foil experiment). 

1.  **The Procedure:** Geiger and Marsden fired a beam of positively charged alpha particles ($\alpha$) at an extremely thin sheet of gold foil.
2.  **The Observations:**
    *   **The vast majority** of alpha particles passed straight through the foil with little to no deflection. This led to the conclusion that the atom consists primarily of **empty space**.
    *   **A very small fraction** of the alpha particles were deflected at large angles, and some even bounced directly backward (rebounded).
3.  **The Conclusion:** Because alpha particles are positively charged and relatively massive, these large-angle deflections indicated the presence of a very small, dense, and positively charged region at the center of the atom, which we now call the **nucleus**.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** This is backwards; most particles passed through, they were not deflected.
*   **B:** This incorrectly claims "most" particles were deflected at a large angle.
*   **D:** This experiment used alpha particles, not beta particles. Furthermore, the description of the deflection results in this option is inaccurate regarding the observed experimental evidence.
