### Question 12: Minimum Speed

**Answer:** (C) $5.9 \text{ ms}^{-1}$

**Explanation:**
To maintain a vertical circular path, the motorbike must have enough speed at the top of the loop so that gravity alone does not cause it to fall. At the "critical" or minimum speed, the normal force exerted by the track on the bike becomes zero at the highest point.

At this point, the centripetal force is provided entirely by the gravitational force ($mg$):
$$F_c = F_g$$
$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = mg$$

The mass $m$ cancels out from both sides, leaving:
$$\frac{v^2}{r} = g$$
$$v^2 = rg$$
$$v = \sqrt{rg}$$

Now, plug in the given values ($r = 3.6 \text{ m}$ and using $g \approx 9.8 \text{ ms}^{-2}$):
$$v = \sqrt{3.6 \times 9.8}$$
$$v = \sqrt{35.28}$$
$$v \approx 5.94 \text{ ms}^{-1}$$

Rounding to two significant figures, we get **$5.9 \text{ ms}^{-1}$**.

### Question 13: Acceleration

**Answer:** (B) constant; changes

**Explanation:**
In uniform circular motion, the object travels at a **constant speed**. The magnitude of the centripetal acceleration is given by the formula:
$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
Since the speed $v$ and the radius $r$ are constant, the **magnitude** of the acceleration is **constant**.

However, the **direction** of the centripetal acceleration is always directed towards the center of the circle. As the motorbike moves along the path, its position changes, and therefore the direction toward the center of the circle continuously changes. Thus, the direction is **not constant**.
