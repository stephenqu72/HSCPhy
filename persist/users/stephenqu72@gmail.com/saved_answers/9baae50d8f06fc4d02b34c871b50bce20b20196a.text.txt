### Answer
Analysing stellar spectra provides a wealth of information about a star's physical properties. By examining the electromagnetic radiation emitted by a star, astrophysicists can determine:

1.  **Surface Temperature:** By plotting the intensity of light against wavelength, we obtain a continuous spectrum. According to **Wien’s Law** ($\lambda_{max} T = \text{constant}$), the wavelength at which the peak intensity occurs is inversely proportional to the star's surface temperature. Additionally, the presence and strength of specific absorption lines (e.g., Hydrogen Balmer lines) indicate the star's spectral class (O, B, A, F, G, K, M).
2.  **Chemical Composition:** As light passes through the cooler outer layers of a star’s atmosphere, specific elements absorb photons at characteristic wavelengths, creating "dark" absorption lines. By comparing the position and intensity of these lines against laboratory reference spectra, we can determine the chemical elements present in the star's photosphere.
3.  **Radial Velocity:** By measuring the **Doppler shift** of known spectral lines, we can determine if a star is moving towards or away from Earth. A shift towards the red end of the spectrum (redshift) indicates motion away, while a shift towards the blue end (blueshift) indicates motion towards us.
4.  **Rotational Velocity:** Rapid rotation causes different parts of the star to move toward and away from the observer simultaneously. This broadens the spectral absorption lines; by measuring the degree of **line broadening**, we can calculate the star's rotational speed.
