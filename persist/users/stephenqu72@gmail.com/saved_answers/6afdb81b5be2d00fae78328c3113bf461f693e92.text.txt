To determine how to improve the accuracy of this experiment, we must look at how to minimize measurement errors, specifically **random error**.

1.  **Understanding the objective:** The experiment aims to calculate $g$ using the period $T$ of a pendulum, given by the formula:
    $$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
    The primary source of random error in this experiment is human reaction time when starting and stopping the stopwatch for a single oscillation.

2.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **A:** While higher precision is generally good, a stopwatch measuring to nanoseconds is overkill. The dominant error here is the **human reaction time**, which is typically around $0.1$ to $0.2$ seconds. A stopwatch with millisecond precision is already more than sufficient to record the period; faster precision does not fix the systematic delay caused by human reaction.
    *   **B:** Conducting the experiment at different locations will change the value of $g$ itself, introducing a variable that ruins the consistency of your data set. This is not an improvement to the experimental method.
    *   **C:** By recording the time for **ten oscillations** and then dividing by ten to find the period of a single oscillation, you effectively distribute the human reaction error across ten cycles. This significantly reduces the *relative* percentage error of the measurement. This is a standard HSC Physics technique for improving the reliability and accuracy of timing measurements.
    *   **D:** According to the provided formula, the mass of the pendulum does not affect the period $T$. Changing the mass will not change the result or improve the accuracy of the measurement.

Therefore, recording multiple oscillations is the most effective way to minimize the impact of human reaction time, thereby improving the accuracy of the experiment.
