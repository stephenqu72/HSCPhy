### Explanation

To solve this question, we must understand the physics of a nuclear fission reactor:

1.  **The Role of the Moderator:** Fission reactions are most efficiently triggered by "thermal" (slow-moving) neutrons. The moderator slows down the fast neutrons produced during fission, ensuring they are at the correct energy level to sustain the chain reaction. If moderator is lost, fewer neutrons are slowed down, which decreases the probability of further fission events. This causes the reaction rate to drop.
2.  **The Role of Control Rods:** Control rods are made of materials that effectively absorb neutrons. By inserting them further into the core, more neutrons are removed from the reaction (reducing the rate). By withdrawing them, fewer neutrons are absorbed, which allows more neutrons to remain available to trigger fission (increasing the rate).
3.  **Compensating for the Loss:** The problem states that the loss of moderator reduces the efficiency of the fission process (fewer neutrons are hitting the fuel at the right speed). To compensate for this decrease in the reaction rate, we need to increase the number of neutrons available to hit the fuel. Withdrawing the control rods reduces the number of neutrons being absorbed, thereby increasing the neutron flux and "compensating" for the loss of the moderating effect.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **B:** Lowering control rods further would absorb *more* neutrons, decreasing the reaction rate further, which would exacerbate the problem.
*   **C and D:** These actions relate to thermal management. While they affect the temperature of the core, they do not directly influence the neutron population density or the fission probability to compensate for the lack of moderation.
