### Answer
The experimental evidence supporting the postulate that **the speed of light in a vacuum is an absolute constant** is provided by the **Michelson-Morley experiment**.

In this experiment, Michelson and Morley attempted to detect the existence of the "luminiferous aether," a medium through which light was thought to travel. By using an interferometer to compare the speed of light in different directions relative to the Earth’s motion through space, they expected to find a difference in the measured speed. However, they consistently found that the speed of light remained the same regardless of the Earth's orientation or velocity, effectively disproving the aether theory and supporting the idea that $c$ is invariant for all observers.

### Explanation
To solve this question, follow these steps:

1.  **Identify the Postulate:** The question asks for evidence for *one* of the two. It is significantly easier and more common in the HSC Physics syllabus to discuss the Michelson-Morley experiment as it directly addresses the constancy of $c$.
2.  **State the Experiment:** Explicitly name the "Michelson-Morley experiment."
3.  **Explain the Logic:**
    *   Explain what they were looking for: They wanted to detect changes in the speed of light caused by the "aether wind" as the Earth moved through space.
    *   Explain the result: They found a "null result"—the speed of light was identical in all directions.
4.  **Connect to Theory:** State clearly that this result demonstrates that the speed of light is independent of the observer's motion, which is the definition of the first postulate.

*Alternative option:* You could also mention the **observation of muon decay**. High-speed muons created in the upper atmosphere reach the Earth's surface in numbers greater than expected by classical physics. This supports the time dilation aspect of special relativity, which is fundamentally derived from the constancy of $c$.
