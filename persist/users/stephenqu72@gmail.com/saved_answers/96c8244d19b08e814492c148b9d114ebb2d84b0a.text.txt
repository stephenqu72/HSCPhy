### Answer
In Newtonian physics, the kinetic energy of an object is given by $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. This formula implies that an object can be accelerated indefinitely to any speed by adding more energy. However, according to Special Relativity, this is incorrect.

As a spacecraft’s velocity $v$ approaches the speed of light $c$, its relativistic mass (or total energy) increases according to the Lorentz factor $\gamma$:
$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

The relativistic total energy of an object is given by $E = \gamma mc^2$. As $v \to c$, the denominator of the Lorentz factor approaches zero, causing $\gamma$ to approach infinity. Consequently, the energy required to further increase the velocity of the spacecraft also approaches infinity. Therefore, the work (thrust) performed on the spacecraft results in an increase in the spacecraft's relativistic mass rather than a significant increase in velocity. This explains why the speed of light $c$ acts as a universal speed limit that cannot be reached or exceeded by any massive object.
