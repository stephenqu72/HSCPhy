### Explanation

To understand the Balmer series, we must look at the Bohr model of the hydrogen atom:

1.  **The Balmer Series:** This is a specific set of spectral lines observed in the visible spectrum of hydrogen. These lines correspond to the emission of photons when an electron transitions from a higher energy level ($n = 3, 4, 5, \dots$) down to the second energy level ($n = 2$).
2.  **Why (A) is incorrect:** The classical model suggested that electrons orbiting a nucleus would continuously radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus. This was the specific problem the Bohr model was designed to solve, as it would make atoms unstable.
3.  **Why (B) is correct:** Bohr proposed that electrons exist in stable, quantized energy levels. When an electron moves from a higher energy level ($E_i$) to a lower energy level ($E_f$), it releases the energy difference as a photon with energy $E = hf = E_i - E_f$. The Balmer series is exactly the set of emissions resulting from these transitions to the $n=2$ state.
4.  **Why (C) is incorrect:** The spectral lines originate from the electron shells (the "electron cloud"), not the nucleus. Nuclear radiation involves phenomena like alpha, beta, and gamma decay.
5.  **Why (D) is incorrect:** The Balmer series is an *emission* spectrum, not a reflection spectrum. It occurs due to the internal energy transitions of the atom, not the scattering or reflection of external light off electrons.
