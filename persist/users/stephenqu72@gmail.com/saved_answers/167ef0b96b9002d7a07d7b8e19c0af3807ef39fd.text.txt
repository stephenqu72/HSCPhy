### Answer
A helium atom ($^4_2\text{He}$) consists of two electrons, two protons, and two neutrons. According to the Standard Model of matter:

*   **Electrons:** These are elementary particles belonging to the lepton family. They are fundamental and possess a charge of $-1e$.
*   **Protons:** Each proton is a composite particle made of three quarks held together by the strong nuclear force (mediated by gluons). Specifically, a proton consists of two up quarks ($+2/3e$ each) and one down quark ($-1/3e$), resulting in a total charge of $+1e$.
*   **Neutrons:** Each neutron is also a composite particle made of three quarks: one up quark and two down quarks, resulting in a total charge of $0$.
*   **Summary:** While electrons are fundamental point-like particles, the nucleons (protons and neutrons) are hadrons composed of quarks.

### How to solve this
To achieve full marks (4 marks) for this HSC-style physics question, you must demonstrate a clear understanding of the difference between fundamental particles and composite particles within the context of the Standard Model:

1.  **Identify the composition of the atom:** State that helium has 2 protons, 2 neutrons, and 2 electrons.
2.  **Classify the electrons:** Identify them as fundamental particles (leptons).
3.  **Classify the nucleons:** Explain that protons and neutrons are hadrons, not fundamental particles.
4.  **Describe the quark structure:** Explicitly mention the quark composition ($uud$ for protons, $udd$ for neutrons).
5.  **Contextualize with forces:** Briefly mention that quarks are held together within the nucleons by the strong nuclear force (gluons).

**Marking guide tip:** Examiners look for the distinction that electrons are fundamental, while protons and neutrons are composite structures made of quarks.
