### Sample Answer

**Historical Methods to Determine $c$:**
Historically, scientists moved from attempting to prove light had a finite speed to measuring it with increasing precision.
1.  **Galileo:** Used lanterns on hilltops. His experiment failed because human reaction time was too slow compared to the immense speed of light.
2.  **Ole Rømer (1676):** Observed the eclipses of Jupiter’s moon, Io. He noted that the timing of eclipses varied as Earth moved closer to or further from Jupiter, providing the first quantitative evidence that light has a finite speed.
3.  **Fizeau (1849):** Used a rotating cogwheel and a distant mirror. By measuring the angular velocity of the wheel needed to block the returning light beam, he calculated $c$ with reasonable accuracy.
4.  **Foucault (1862):** Refined Fizeau’s method by replacing the cogwheel with a rotating mirror, confirming that light travels slower in denser media (like water) than in air.

**Factors Leading to the Contemporary Definition:**
The primary motivation for changing the definition of the metre was the need for a standard that is **universal, invariant, and reproducible** in any laboratory. Previous standards, such as the platinum-iridium bar, were subject to degradation, thermal expansion, and potential destruction. Scientists needed a definition based on fundamental constants of nature rather than physical artifacts.

**Relationship to the Metre and Second:**
In 1983, the General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) redefined the metre based on the speed of light. Since $c$ is now defined as an exact constant ($c = 299,792,458 \text{ m/s}$), the metre is defined as:
*The distance light travels in a vacuum in $1/299,792,458$ of a second.*
The "second" is defined independently based on the hyperfine transition frequency of the Caesium-133 atom. By fixing $c$ and using the atomic standard for the second, the metre becomes a derived unit linked to the time taken for light to cover a specific distance.
