### Part (a) Labels
To label the four main groups on a standard Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram:
1.  **Main Sequence:** The diagonal band stretching from top-left to bottom-right.
2.  **Red Giants:** The group located above the main sequence (the large cluster to the right of the upper main sequence).
3.  **Supergiants:** The group at the very top of the diagram.
4.  **White Dwarfs:** The small cluster located at the bottom left (below the main sequence).

### Part (b) Evolutionary Process
A sun-like star spends most of its life on the **Main Sequence**. As it exhausts its hydrogen fuel, it moves off the main sequence toward the **Red Giant** branch (shifting up and to the right on the diagram). Eventually, it sheds its outer layers and the core is revealed as a **White Dwarf**, shifting its position to the bottom-left of the diagram.
*   **Drawing instruction:** Draw an arrow starting in the middle of the main sequence, moving up and to the right toward the Red Giant region, and then a dashed arrow down toward the bottom-left White Dwarf region.

### Part (c) Energy Production (CNO Cycle)
The CNO (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen) cycle is the primary mode of hydrogen fusion for stars that are significantly more massive and hotter than our Sun. While the Sun uses the proton-proton chain, more massive stars on the **upper main sequence** and **supergiants** rely on the CNO cycle due to their higher core temperatures.
*   **Action:** Circle the top-left portion of the Main Sequence and the Supergiant group.

### How to solve it:
1.  **Recall the H-R Diagram structure:** Always remember the axes: Temperature decreases from left to right (X-axis) and Luminosity increases from bottom to top (Y-axis).
2.  **Memorize evolutionary tracks:** Remember that stars stay on the main sequence for $\approx 90\%$ of their lives. When hydrogen in the core runs out, the star expands and cools (moving right) but becomes more luminous (moving up) to become a giant.
3.  **Differentiate Fusion Cycles:** Understand that fusion processes are temperature-dependent. The Proton-Proton chain is efficient for smaller stars ($T < 1.5 \times 10^7$ K), whereas the CNO cycle becomes dominant at higher core temperatures found in massive, hot stars.
