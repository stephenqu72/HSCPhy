**Sample Response:**
The Hafele-Keating experiment (1971) validated Einstein’s predictions of time dilation by flying atomic clocks around the world in both eastward and westward directions.

*   **Validity:** The experiment directly tested Einstein’s theory by incorporating both special relativity (velocity-based time dilation) and general relativity (gravitational potential-based time dilation). By flying clocks in opposite directions relative to the Earth's rotation, they ensured the variables (speed and altitude) were correctly manipulated to test the specific hypothesis.
*   **Reliability:** Reliability was ensured by using multiple highly precise cesium-beam atomic clocks. Comparing the results of several clocks against each other and against a stationary ground-based reference clock allowed for the identification and exclusion of individual clock errors or anomalies, ensuring the results were consistent and reproducible.
*   **Accuracy:** Accuracy was achieved through the high precision of the atomic clocks, which could measure time differences in the nanosecond range. Furthermore, the experiment accounted for systematic errors, such as variations in gravitational potential ($g$) at different altitudes and the Earth's rotational velocity, by applying rigorous mathematical corrections to the data, ensuring the measured time differences matched the predicted values within the experimental uncertainty.
