### Answer
The correct answer is **A**. The ratio of their orbital speeds is $1:1$.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must consider the physics of uniform circular motion for a satellite orbiting a central body (like Earth).

1.  **Identify the forces:** The gravitational force provides the centripetal force required to keep the satellite in orbit. We can equate the two:
    $$F_g = F_c$$
    $$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

    Where:
    - $G$ is the gravitational constant
    - $M$ is the mass of the Earth
    - $m$ is the mass of the satellite
    - $r$ is the orbital radius
    - $v$ is the orbital speed

2.  **Simplify the equation:** Notice that the mass of the satellite ($m$) appears on both sides of the equation. We can cancel it out:
    $$\frac{GM}{r} = v^2$$
    $$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

3.  **Analyze the variables:** The resulting formula for orbital speed, $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$, shows that the speed of a satellite depends **only** on the mass of the central body ($M$) and the radius of the orbit ($r$). It is entirely independent of the mass of the satellite ($m$).

4.  **Conclusion:** Since both satellites are in the same orbit (meaning $r$ is the same) and orbiting the same planet (meaning $M$ is the same), they must both have the same orbital speed, regardless of their individual masses. Therefore, the ratio of their orbital speeds is $1:1$.
