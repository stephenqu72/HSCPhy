### Front
Why can a cyclist navigate a curve at a higher speed when leaning compared to staying upright?

### Back
- **Key idea:** By leaning, the rider angles the Normal force ($N$), allowing its horizontal component ($N \sin\theta$) to provide the necessary centripetal force instead of relying solely on static friction.
- **Formula:** $\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$
- **When to use it:** To explain the physics of banked turns or circular motion of non-vertical objects (bikes/planes).
- **Common trap:** Assuming the Normal force remains equal to $mg$ when leaning; in reality, $N = \frac{mg}{\cos\theta}$, which is greater than the rider's weight.
