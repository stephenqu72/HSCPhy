### Sample Answer

**1. Applications of Transformers**
*   **Step-up Transformers:** Located at power stations, these increase the output voltage of the generator. By the transformer equation $\frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{N_p}{N_s}$, increasing the secondary voltage ($V_s$) results in a proportional decrease in current ($I_s$) for a constant power ($P=VI$). This is crucial for long-distance transmission to reduce heating losses in wires.
*   **Step-down Transformers:** Located near urban areas or substations, these decrease the high transmission voltage to safer, usable levels (e.g., $240V$) for domestic and industrial consumption, ensuring the safety of appliances and end-users.

**2. Limiting Power Loss in Transmission Wires**
*   Power loss in wires is given by $P_{loss} = I^2R$. 
*   By using step-up transformers to increase voltage, the current ($I$) is significantly reduced. Because power loss is proportional to the **square** of the current, even a small reduction in current leads to a massive reduction in energy wasted as heat. 
*   Wires are typically made of materials with low resistivity (e.g., aluminum or copper) to minimize $R$.

**3. Limiting Power Loss in Transformers**
*   **Eddy Currents:** These are circular currents induced in the iron core. They are minimized by using a **laminated core** (thin layers of metal separated by insulating material), which breaks the path of the currents.
*   **Joule Heating (Resistance):** Thick copper wire is used for coils to minimize resistance ($R$), reducing $I^2R$ losses.
*   **Magnetic Flux Leakage:** The core is designed as a closed-loop (e.g., shell-type core) to ensure maximum flux linkage between primary and secondary coils.
*   **Hysteresis Losses:** Using "soft" iron cores allows the magnetic domains to flip easily with the alternating current, minimizing energy lost as heat during magnetization cycles.
