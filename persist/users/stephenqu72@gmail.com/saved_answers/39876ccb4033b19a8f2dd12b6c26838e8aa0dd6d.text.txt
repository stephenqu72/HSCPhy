To solve this problem, we need to determine the energy of a single photon and then calculate how many of those photons are needed to reach a total energy of $31\text{ eV}$.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Calculate the energy of a single photon ($E_p$):**
The energy of a photon is given by the formula:
$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$
Where:
- $h \approx 4.136 \times 10^{-15}\text{ eV}\cdot\text{s}$ (Planck's constant in eV units)
- $c \approx 3 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$ (speed of light)
- $\lambda = 400\text{ nm} = 400 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}$

Substituting the values:
$$E_p = \frac{(4.136 \times 10^{-15}\text{ eV}\cdot\text{s}) \times (3 \times 10^8\text{ m/s})}{400 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}}$$
$$E_p \approx \frac{1.2408 \times 10^{-6}}{400 \times 10^{-9}}\text{ eV}$$
$$E_p \approx 3.102\text{ eV}$$

(Alternatively, using the shortcut $E(\text{eV}) \approx \frac{1240}{\lambda(\text{nm})}$: $E = \frac{1240}{400} = 3.1\text{ eV}$)

**2. Calculate the number of photons ($n$):**
We want to transfer at least $31\text{ eV}$ of energy. The number of photons required is:
$$n = \frac{\text{Total Energy}}{\text{Energy per photon}}$$
$$n = \frac{31\text{ eV}}{3.1\text{ eV}} = 10$$

Since we need exactly 10 photons to reach $31\text{ eV}$, the minimum number of photons required is 10.
