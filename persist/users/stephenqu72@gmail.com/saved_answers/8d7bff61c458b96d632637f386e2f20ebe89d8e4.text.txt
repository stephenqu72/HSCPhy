The correct answer is **A. To determine a value for the speed of light.**

### Explanation
The experiment shown in the diagram is **Fizeau’s Experiment** (conducted by Hippolyte Fizeau in 1849, though 1848 is often associated with his initial preparations). This was a landmark experiment in the history of physics because it was the first successful attempt to measure the speed of light using terrestrial equipment, rather than astronomical observations.

**How it works:**
1.  **The setup:** Light is passed through a rotating toothed wheel (cog) and directed toward a distant mirror. The mirror reflects the light back toward the original position.
2.  **The mechanism:** If the cog is stationary, the light passes through a gap, reflects off the mirror, and passes back through the same gap to the observer.
3.  **Measuring speed:** As the cog rotates at higher speeds, the teeth move across the gap. If the speed of the cog is increased to a critical point, a tooth will move into the path of the returning light pulse, blocking it from the observer’s view.
4.  **The calculation:** By knowing the distance $d$ to the distant mirror, the number of teeth $n$ on the cog, and the angular frequency of rotation $f$ at which the light is first blocked, Fizeau could calculate the time $t$ it took for light to travel to the mirror and back. Using the fundamental formula for speed, $v = \frac{d}{t}$, he was able to provide a remarkably accurate value for $c$ (the speed of light).

Therefore, the experiment's sole purpose was to measure the speed of light.
