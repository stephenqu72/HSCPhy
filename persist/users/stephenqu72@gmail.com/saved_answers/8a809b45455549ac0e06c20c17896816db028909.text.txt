This is a classic question from the HSC Physics "Ideas to Implementation" or "The Nature of Light" topics. Understanding the history of light models is essential for identifying the paradigm shift from classical mechanics to wave theory.

### Answer

**(a) Explanation of refraction:**
*   **Newton’s Corpuscular Model:** Newton proposed that light consists of tiny particles (corpuscles). He argued that when these particles approach a denser medium, they experience an attractive force perpendicular to the surface. This causes the particles to accelerate, increasing their velocity component towards the normal, which results in the light bending towards the normal. This model incorrectly predicted that light travels faster in denser media.
*   **Huygens’ Wave Model:** Huygens proposed that light travels as a series of wavefronts. When a wavefront hits a boundary at an angle, one side of the wavefront enters the denser medium first and slows down, while the other side continues at the original speed in the rarer medium. This difference in speed across the wavefront causes the wave to pivot and bend (refract) towards the normal. This model correctly predicted that light travels slower in denser media.

**(b) Piece of evidence:**
*   **Evidence:** **Diffraction** (or Interference).
*   **Explanation:** Diffraction is the bending of waves around obstacles or through slits. This is a characteristic property of waves, which Huygens’ model could explain as secondary wavelets interfering with each other. Newton’s corpuscular model could not explain how particles would "bend" around corners or interfere constructively/destructively, as particles would simply travel in straight lines or be blocked by obstacles.

---

### How to solve this
1.  **Understand the Models:** For part (a), you must distinguish between the *particle* nature (Newton) and the *wave* nature (Huygens). 
2.  **Focus on the Mechanism:** For refraction, the key is the change in velocity. Newton incorrectly linked refraction to an increase in speed due to gravity-like forces, while Huygens correctly linked it to a decrease in speed. Mentioning "velocity change" is crucial for full marks.
3.  **Identify Distinguishing Phenomena:** For part (b), look for "wave-only" phenomena. Diffraction, interference, and polarization are the classic examples. Since Newton treated light as independent particles, he struggled with any behavior that required the superposition of waves. 
4.  **Mark Allocation:** 
    *   Part (a) is worth 3 marks: 1 mark for Newton's mechanism, 1 mark for Huygens' mechanism, and 1 mark for addressing the change in medium/refraction. 
    *   Part (b) is worth 2 marks: 1 mark for naming the phenomenon (e.g., diffraction) and 1 mark for explaining why it supports the wave model over the particle model.
