To solve this problem, we use the principles of projectile motion, specifically looking at the vertical component of the motion provided by the velocity-time graph.

### 1. Initial Velocity ($u$)
From the graph, at $t = 0$, the initial vertical velocity component is $v_{y0} = 20 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Given the launch angle $\theta = 40^\circ$:
$$v_{y0} = u \sin(40^\circ)$$
$$20 = u \sin(40^\circ)$$
$$u = \frac{20}{\sin(40^\circ)} \approx \frac{20}{0.6428} \approx 31.11 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

### 2. Firing Height ($h$)
The displacement $h$ in the vertical direction is the area under the velocity-time graph. Since the bullet starts at a height $h$ and ends at $0$, the total displacement $\Delta y = -h$.
The area consists of two triangles:
- Area 1 (upward motion): $A_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times 2.04 \text{ s} \times 20 \text{ m s}^{-1} \approx 20.4 \text{ m}$
- Area 2 (downward motion): The bullet lands at $t \approx 4.58 \text{ s}$. The base is $4.58 - 2.04 = 2.54 \text{ s}$. The velocity at $4.58 \text{ s}$ is $v = 20 - 9.8(4.58) \approx -24.88 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.
- $A_2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 2.54 \times (-24.88) \approx -31.60 \text{ m}$.
- Total displacement $\Delta y = A_1 + A_2 = 20.4 - 31.6 = -11.2 \text{ m}$.
Since $\Delta y = -h$, the height $h \approx 11.2 \text{ m}$.

### 3. Range
The horizontal velocity is constant:
$$v_x = u \cos(40^\circ) = 31.11 \times \cos(40^\circ) \approx 31.11 \times 0.766 \approx 23.83 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$
The total time of flight $t \approx 4.58 \text{ s}$.
$$\text{Range} = v_x \times t = 23.83 \times 4.58 \approx 109.14 \text{ m}$$
