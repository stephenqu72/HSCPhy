### Answer
The correct answer is **(C) Millikan’s oil drop experiment.**

### Explanation
To determine the mass of the electron ($m_e$), physicists relied on the combination of two critical pieces of experimental data:

1.  **The charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$):** J.J. Thomson had previously determined the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron using cathode ray tubes (deflection in electric and magnetic fields). This established the relationship:
    $$\frac{q_e}{m_e} \approx 1.76 \times 10^{11} \text{ C/kg}$$

2.  **The elementary charge ($q_e$):** Millikan’s oil drop experiment was the breakthrough that allowed for the precise measurement of the elementary charge ($q_e$) of an electron. By balancing the gravitational force and the electric force on charged oil droplets, Millikan calculated $q_e \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.

Once $q_e$ was known from Millikan's work, the mass of the electron ($m_e$) could be calculated by substituting the known charge into Thomson's ratio:
$$m_e = \frac{q_e}{(q_e/m_e)}$$

While Thomson provided the ratio, it was the specific determination of the electron's charge provided by **Millikan’s oil drop experiment** that allowed the mass to be isolated and calculated.
