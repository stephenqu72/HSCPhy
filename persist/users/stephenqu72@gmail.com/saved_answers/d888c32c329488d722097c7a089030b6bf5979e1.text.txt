For a 6-mark question, you must provide a comprehensive evaluation of Maxwell’s work, linking his theoretical breakthroughs to their experimental validation and their profound impact on modern physics.

### Answer
James Clerk Maxwell’s contribution to electromagnetic theory is considered one of the most significant intellectual achievements in the history of physics, acting as the foundation for modern electrodynamics and field theory.

1.  **Synthesizing Laws:** Maxwell’s primary contribution was the unification of electricity and magnetism into a single, cohesive framework. By refining Ampere’s Law to include the "displacement current," he accounted for the charging of capacitors and ensured the conservation of charge. He integrated Gauss's Laws, Faraday’s Law of Induction, and Ampere-Maxwell Law into what are now known as **Maxwell’s Equations**.
2.  **Theoretical Prediction:** Through these equations, Maxwell demonstrated mathematically that electric and magnetic fields propagate through space as transverse waves. He calculated the velocity of these waves using the constants $\epsilon_0$ (permittivity of free space) and $\mu_0$ (permeability of free space) via the formula $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\epsilon_0 \mu_0}}$. His resulting value was remarkably close to the measured speed of light, leading him to the revolutionary conclusion that light is an electromagnetic wave.
3.  **Experimental Validation:** While his work was initially purely theoretical, it provided the essential roadmap for later experimentalists. Specifically, Heinrich Hertz’s experiments in the 1880s, which generated and detected radio waves, provided the empirical evidence needed to confirm Maxwell’s theory, effectively ending the debate between particle and wave models of light.
4.  **Impact:** Maxwell’s theory provided the basis for the development of modern telecommunications (radio, television, radar) and predicted the existence of the entire electromagnetic spectrum. By shifting the focus from "action-at-a-distance" to the concept of **fields**, he paved the way for Einstein’s Special Theory of Relativity and the development of quantum field theory.
