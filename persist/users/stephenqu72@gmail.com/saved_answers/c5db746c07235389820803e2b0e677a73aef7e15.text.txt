### Part (a) and (b): Free-Body Diagram and Forces
To draw the free-body diagram for the girl, represent her as a point mass. There are three primary forces acting on her:
1.  **Weight ($mg$):** Acting vertically downwards.
2.  **Friction ($f_s$):** Acting vertically upwards, preventing her from sliding down the wall.
3.  **Normal Force ($N$):** Acting horizontally towards the center of the cylinder (the centripetal force).

**Analysis of forces:**
*   **Balanced forces:** The vertical forces are balanced. Since the girl is not accelerating vertically, the upward frictional force must exactly equal the downward weight: $f_s = mg$.
*   **Unbalanced force:** The horizontal force (Normal force) is unbalanced. This provides the centripetal acceleration ($a_c$) required for circular motion: $N = F_{net} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$.

***

### Part (c): Calculating Linear Speed
To calculate the linear speed ($v$), we use the relationship between the period ($T$), the radius ($r$), and the speed.

**1. Identify given values:**
*   Diameter $= 10\text{ m} \implies$ Radius $r = 5.0\text{ m}$
*   Period $T = 2.50\text{ s}$

**2. Select the formula:**
The speed is the distance traveled in one full circle (circumference) divided by the time taken (period):
$$v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$$

**3. Substitute and calculate:**
$$v = \frac{2 \times \pi \times 5.0}{2.50}$$
$$v = \frac{10\pi}{2.50}$$
$$v = 4\pi \approx 12.57\text{ m/s}$$

**Answer:** The linear speed of the girl is approximately **$12.6\text{ m/s}$** (to 3 significant figures).

### Tips for the HSC:
*   **Diagrams:** Always label your forces clearly in a free-body diagram. Never draw the "centrifugal force"—it is a pseudo-force and does not exist in an inertial frame of reference.
*   **Calculations:** Ensure your radius is half the diameter before substituting into the circular motion formulas.
