### Model Answer

**Investigation: The relationship between Centripetal Force and Radius**

*   **Hypothesis:** The centripetal force ($F_c$) required to keep an object in circular motion is inversely proportional to the radius ($r$) of the circle, provided that mass ($m$) and velocity ($v$) remain constant ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$).
*   **Variables:**
    *   **Independent:** The radius of the circular path ($r$).
    *   **Dependent:** The centripetal force ($F_c$), measured via a tension sensor or hanging masses.
    *   **Controlled:** Mass of the object ($m$) and linear speed ($v$) (kept constant by timing rotations).
*   **Method:**
    1. Attach a rubber stopper of mass $m$ to a string passed through a glass tube.
    2. Attach a set of weights to the bottom of the string to provide a constant centripetal force (tension).
    3. Rotate the stopper in a horizontal circle of radius $r$. 
    4. Adjust the speed of rotation until the system is stable and the radius remains constant.
    5. Measure the time for 10 revolutions to calculate the period ($T$), then determine $v$ using $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$.
    6. Repeat for different radii $r$ while maintaining a constant hanging mass.
*   **Results:** As the radius $r$ increases, the speed $v$ must change to satisfy the relationship. Data would show that for a constant centripetal force, $v^2 \propto r$.
*   **Conclusion:** The experimental data supports the formula $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$. Specifically, as the radius of the circular path increases (at constant mass and force), the velocity of the object must increase proportionally to the square root of the radius.

### How to solve this question
1.  **Structure is key:** With 7 marks, the marker looks for the inclusion of all five headings: Hypothesis, Variables, Method, Results, and Conclusion.
2.  **Define your Variables:** Clearly state the Independent, Dependent, and Controlled variables. If you forget to control variables, you lose marks for reliability.
3.  **The "Physics":** Always relate your investigation back to the theoretical formula. In this case, $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$ is the foundation of your argument.
4.  **Practical Detail:** When describing the method, mention specific equipment (e.g., "glass tube," "stroboscope," or "timer") to show you have actually performed or deeply understood the practical.
5.  **Refine the Conclusion:** Ensure your conclusion directly addresses your initial hypothesis. Did it support or refute it?
