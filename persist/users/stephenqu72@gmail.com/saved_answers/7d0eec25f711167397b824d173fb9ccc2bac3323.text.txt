### Explanation
To solve this, we need to understand the fundamental physics governing a star's position on the **Main Sequence** of the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram.

1.  **Understanding the Axes**: The H-R diagram plots luminosity (related to $M_v$ on the y-axis) against surface temperature (related to the $B-V$ color index on the x-axis).
2.  **The Main Sequence**: Stars on the Main Sequence are all currently fusing hydrogen into helium in their cores.
3.  **The Role of Mass**: The position of a star on the Main Sequence is determined almost entirely by its **initial mass**.
    *   **High-mass stars** (like Vega, located towards the top left) have higher gravitational pressure in their cores. This leads to higher core temperatures, which cause hydrogen fusion to occur at a much faster rate. Consequently, these stars are much hotter (bluer) and more luminous.
    *   **Low-mass stars** (like Proxima Centauri, located towards the bottom right) have lower core pressures and temperatures. They fuse hydrogen much more slowly, resulting in lower surface temperatures (redder) and lower luminosity.
4.  **Evaluating the Options**:
    *   **A is incorrect**: Both are Main Sequence stars, meaning both are currently undergoing hydrogen fusion in their cores.
    *   **B is incorrect**: The location of a star in the galaxy does not determine its fundamental evolutionary state on the H-R diagram.
    *   **C is incorrect**: Stars do not "move up" the main sequence; they remain on the main sequence for most of their lives, and their position is fixed by their mass at the time of formation.
    *   **D is correct**: It is the mass of the star at its formation that dictates its temperature and luminosity while on the main sequence. Because Vega is hotter and brighter, it must have had a significantly higher initial mass than Proxima Centauri.
