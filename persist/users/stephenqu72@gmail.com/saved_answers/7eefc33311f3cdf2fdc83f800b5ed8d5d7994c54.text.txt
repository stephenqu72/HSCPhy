### Explanation

To solve this question, we must understand the formal scientific definition of **experimental uncertainty**:

*   **Why A is correct:** Experimental uncertainty is defined as a parameter associated with the result of a measurement that characterizes the dispersion of the values that could reasonably be attributed to the measurand. Essentially, it is a quantitative estimate that tells us how much "doubt" we should have in a measurement, usually expressed as $x \pm \Delta x$.
*   **Why B is incorrect:** The "degree of confidence" refers to the scientist's subjective belief, whereas uncertainty is a measurable, objective property of the measurement process itself.
*   **Why C is incorrect:** This is the definition of **error** (specifically absolute error), not uncertainty. We rarely know the "true value" of a quantity, which is precisely why we must estimate uncertainty instead.
*   **Why D is incorrect:** While repeated measurements can help reduce random error (improving precision), they cannot eliminate uncertainty. Systematic errors (like calibration issues) will persist regardless of how many times you repeat the measurement.

In HSC physics and chemistry, distinguishing between "error" (the difference from the true value) and "uncertainty" (the range of potential values) is a core skill for the Working Scientifically module.
