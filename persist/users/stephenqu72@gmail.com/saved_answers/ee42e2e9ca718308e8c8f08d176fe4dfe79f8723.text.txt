### Answer
The correct answer is **A. Polarisation**.

### Explanation
To solve this, you need to understand the fundamental difference between longitudinal and transverse waves:

1.  **Transverse waves:** The oscillations of the medium (or the field, in the case of light) occur perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Because these oscillations can occur in any plane perpendicular to the direction of travel, they can be filtered to restrict them to a single plane. This process is called **polarisation**.
2.  **Longitudinal waves:** (e.g., sound waves) The oscillations occur parallel to the direction of travel. Because the oscillation is along the line of propagation, you cannot "filter" them into a single plane in the same way.

**Why the others are incorrect:**
*   **Diffraction (B) and Interference (C):** These are wave behaviours that prove light exhibits wave-like properties (as opposed to being just a stream of particles), but they can occur in both longitudinal waves (like sound) and transverse waves. Therefore, they do not prove that light is specifically *transverse*.
*   **Refraction (D):** This is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed. This occurs for all types of waves and does not provide evidence for a transverse nature.

**HSC Tip:** Always link **polarisation** directly to the **transverse nature of light**. It is the unique behaviour that confirms light waves have fields oscillating perpendicular to the direction of travel.
