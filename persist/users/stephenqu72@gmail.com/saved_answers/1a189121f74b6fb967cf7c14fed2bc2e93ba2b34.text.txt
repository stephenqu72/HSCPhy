To determine the correct answer, we must analyze the limitations of Isaac Newton's corpuscular (particle) theory of light compared to the wave theory proposed by Christiaan Huygens.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) Diffraction**.

### Explanation
In the history of physics, the competition between the particle model (Newton) and the wave model (Huygens) was a major turning point in our understanding of light.

*   **Why Newton could account for the others:**
    *   **Reflection:** Newton proposed that light particles bounced off surfaces like elastic balls, which successfully predicted the law of reflection ($\theta_i = \theta_r$).
    *   **Refraction:** Newton explained this by suggesting that light particles accelerated when entering a denser medium due to attractive forces, which correctly predicted the path of light bending toward the normal.
    *   **White light/Colours:** Newton famously used prisms to show that white light is composed of a spectrum of colours, which he explained by suggesting that different "corpuscles" of light had different sizes or properties that caused them to be refracted at different angles.

*   **Why diffraction was the problem:**
    *   Diffraction is the bending of waves around obstacles or through small apertures. Because Newton viewed light as a stream of discrete particles moving in straight lines, he could not explain why light would "bend" into the geometric shadow of an object.
    *   Huygens' Principle, which states that every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary spherical wavelets, naturally explains diffraction. It wasn't until later experiments (like Thomas Young’s double-slit experiment) that the wave nature of light was definitively proven, highlighting that Newton’s model failed to account for wave-like phenomena such as diffraction and interference.
