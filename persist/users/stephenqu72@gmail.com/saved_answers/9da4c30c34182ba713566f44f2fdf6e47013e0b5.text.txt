### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
*   **Star A:** Being a "very large white star" ($10\times$ the radius of the Sun), it is significantly larger and hotter than the Sun. On an H-R diagram, white stars are on the left (high temperature), and large stars are high up (high luminosity). You should place **A** in the **top-left region** of the diagram (the main sequence or slightly above it if it is evolving).
*   **Star B:** Being an "orange star, similar in size to our Sun," it is cooler than the Sun and falls into the K-type spectral range. You should place **B** on the **lower-right portion of the main sequence**.

#### Part (b)
*If you choose Star B (Main Sequence Star):*
The primary nuclear reaction is the **proton-proton chain**. In this process, four hydrogen nuclei ($^1H$) are fused to create one helium nucleus ($^4He$). This process releases a significant amount of energy in the form of gamma rays and neutrinos, following the mass-energy equivalence principle $E=mc^2$, where a small amount of mass is converted into energy.

*If you choose Star A (High Mass/White Star):*
Because Star A is a large, massive star, the core temperature is significantly higher. Therefore, the dominant reaction is the **CNO cycle** (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle). In this catalytic cycle, carbon acts as a catalyst to fuse four hydrogen nuclei into one helium nucleus. This is far more efficient at the higher temperatures found in the cores of massive stars compared to the proton-proton chain.

### How to solve this question:
1.  **Analyze the H-R Diagram axes:** Note that the x-axis represents temperature (or spectral class) and the y-axis represents luminosity.
2.  **Use Stellar Physics principles:** Remember that for main sequence stars, there is a direct correlation between size, temperature, and luminosity. Larger stars are generally hotter and more luminous.
3.  **Nuclear Fusion Knowledge:** Recall that stars on the main sequence fuse hydrogen into helium. Distinguish between the two methods:
    *   **Proton-proton chain:** Dominates in lower-mass, cooler stars like our Sun (and Star B).
    *   **CNO cycle:** Dominates in higher-mass, hotter stars because the increased temperature overcomes the higher electrostatic repulsion of the carbon nuclei (and Star A).
