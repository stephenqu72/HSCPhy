### Answer
The correct statement is **A. The clock on the ship will run slower.**

### Explanation
This question tests your understanding of **Time Dilation**, a core postulate of Einstein’s Special Theory of Relativity.

1.  **The Concept:** According to Special Relativity, time is not absolute; it is relative to the observer's frame of reference. When an object moves at a significant fraction of the speed of light ($v \approx c$) relative to an observer, time for that moving object passes more slowly compared to the stationary observer's clock.
2.  **Mathematical Basis:** The relationship is governed by the Lorentz factor $\gamma$ (gamma), where the dilated time $\Delta t$ is calculated as:
    $$\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$$
    Where $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$. Because $\gamma$ is always greater than 1 for any velocity $v > 0$, the time interval $\Delta t$ measured by the stationary observer on Earth will be larger than the proper time interval $\Delta t_0$ measured on the spaceship.
3.  **Applying it to the problem:** The observer on Earth is in the stationary frame, and the spaceship is in the moving frame. Therefore, the Earth observer sees the clock on the spaceship "ticking" at a slower rate than his own clock. This phenomenon is known as time dilation. 

*Note: While a spaceship passenger would see the Earth clock moving slower, the question specifically asks for the perspective of the observer on Earth viewing the spaceship.*
