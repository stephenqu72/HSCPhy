To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of projectile motion, specifically the independence of horizontal and vertical motion.

### Explanation

1.  **Horizontal Motion:** Neglecting air resistance, there are no horizontal forces acting on the projectile once it is launched. According to Newton's First Law, an object in motion will maintain a constant velocity if no net external force acts upon it. Therefore, the **horizontal component of velocity ($v_x$) remains constant** throughout the entire flight. This is represented by identical horizontal arrows in all options.
2.  **Vertical Motion:** The projectile is subject to the constant downward acceleration due to gravity ($g$). 
    *   As the projectile rises, its vertical velocity is initially positive (upwards) but decreases as gravity acts against the motion.
    *   At the peak of the trajectory, the vertical velocity is $0$.
    *   As the projectile falls, the vertical velocity becomes increasingly negative (downwards).
    *   Essentially, the vertical component is always being acted upon by a downward force, meaning it is constantly accelerating downwards. This results in the velocity vector changing direction from pointing "up" to pointing "down."

Looking at the options provided for the **Vertical** column:
*   **A and C:** Show the velocity as always pointing downwards. This is incorrect, as a projectile launched from ground level must have an initial upward velocity.
*   **B and D:** Show the vertical velocity changing from pointing up (as it rises) to pointing down (as it falls). 
*   **D** shows a decreasing magnitude while going up, zero at the top, and increasing magnitude while going down, which correctly describes the motion of a projectile. (Note: In standard HSC physics, option D is the correct representation of the vertical velocity vector's change in direction).

### Answer
The correct option is **D**.
