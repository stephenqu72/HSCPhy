Hello! As an HSC Physics teacher, let's break this down by analyzing what the vertical velocity-time graph is telling us.

### Analysis of the Graph
1.  **Initial Velocity:** At $t=0$, the velocity is positive. This means the object is projected **upwards** from its launch point.
2.  **Peak of Motion ($t_m$):** At $t_m$, the vertical velocity is zero ($v=0$). This is the maximum height of the projectile.
3.  **Returning to motion:** After $t_m$, the velocity becomes negative, meaning the object is moving downwards.
4.  **Final State ($t_f$):** At $t_f$, the projectile stops moving. Crucially, look at the magnitude of the velocity at $t_f$ compared to the initial velocity at $t=0$. The vertical velocity at $t_f$ (the downward velocity) is **greater** than the initial vertical velocity (the upward velocity). 

### Evaluating the Options
*   **A (Ball rolled off a table):** This would start with a vertical velocity of $0$ and become increasingly negative. This does not match.
*   **B (Bouncing ball):** A bounce would show an instantaneous change in velocity direction (a jump from negative to positive). The graph shows a single, constant gradient (acceleration due to gravity), meaning there is no bounce.
*   **C (Launched from a cliff to sea level):** If you launch something upwards from a cliff, it will eventually pass the cliff edge and fall below the launch point. Because the final position is lower than the launch point, the magnitude of the final downward velocity will be greater than the initial upward velocity. This perfectly matches the graph where the final velocity value is more negative than the initial velocity was positive.
*   **D (Launched from ground to ground):** For a launch and landing at the same level, the final velocity would have the same magnitude as the initial velocity (just in the opposite direction). The graph shows a larger magnitude at the end, so this is incorrect.

**Correct Answer: C**

### How to solve this type of question
*   **Identify the sign conventions:** In physics problems, "up" is usually positive and "down" is negative. 
*   **Check the area under the curve:** The displacement $s$ is the area under the $v$-$t$ graph. From $0$ to $t_m$, there is positive displacement (rising). From $t_m$ to $t_f$, there is negative displacement (falling). Since the graph shows a larger area on the negative side, the total displacement is negative, meaning the object ends up below its starting position.
*   **Use Symmetry (or lack thereof):** Remember that projectile motion is symmetric in time and velocity *only if* the launch and landing heights are the same. If the landing height is lower than the launch height, the final downward velocity will be higher than the initial upward velocity.
