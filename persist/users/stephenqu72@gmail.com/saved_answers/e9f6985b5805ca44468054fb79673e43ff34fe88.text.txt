### Answer

**(a) Historical and Contemporary Methods**

*   **Historical Method (Fizeau’s Method):** In 1849, Hippolyte Fizeau used a rotating cogwheel and a distant mirror. Light was shone through a gap in the rotating cogwheel, reflected off a mirror $8\text{ km}$ away, and returned to the cogwheel. By adjusting the speed of rotation, he found the speed at which the reflected light was blocked by the next tooth. Knowing the distance ($d$) and the frequency of the cogwheel rotation, he could calculate the speed of light ($c$) using $c = \frac{2d}{t}$, where $t$ is the time taken for the light to travel the round trip.
*   **Contemporary Method (Laser Interferometry):** Modern measurements use lasers and atomic clocks to measure the frequency ($f$) and wavelength ($\lambda$) of light in a vacuum. By using the relationship $c = f\lambda$, the speed of light can be determined with extreme precision. Because the speed of light is now a defined constant, these measurements are used to calibrate instruments and maintain the accuracy of the SI system.

**(b) Definition of Units**

Since 1983, the speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) has been defined as exactly $299\,792\,458\text{ m/s}$. Consequently, the **metre** is defined as the distance light travels in a vacuum during a time interval of $1/299\,792\,458$ of a second. This links the definition of length directly to the constancy of the speed of light and the precision of atomic clocks.
