To calculate the work required to put the rocket into orbit, we must determine the change in total mechanical energy (kinetic + gravitational potential energy) from the surface of the Earth to the final orbit.

### Constants
*   Mass of Earth ($M$): $5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$
*   Radius of Earth ($R_E$): $6.38 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
*   Gravitational Constant ($G$): $6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2$
*   Mass of rocket ($m$): $1.70 \times 10^5\text{ kg}$
*   Altitude ($h$): $35800\text{ km} = 3.58 \times 10^7\text{ m}$
*   Orbital radius ($r$): $R_E + h = 4.218 \times 10^7\text{ m}$

### Step 1: Initial Energy at the Equator ($E_i$)
At the surface, the rocket has gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy due to Earth's rotation ($v_{rot} = \frac{2\pi R_E}{T}$ where $T = 86400\text{ s}$):
$$E_i = -\frac{GMm}{R_E} + \frac{1}{2}mv_{rot}^2$$
Calculating $v_{rot} \approx 464\text{ m/s}$:
$$E_i \approx -1.59 \times 10^{10} + 1.83 \times 10^{10} \text{ (Total potential dominates)} \approx -1.59 \times 10^{10} \text{ J}$$

### Step 2: Final Energy in Geosynchronous Orbit ($E_f$)
In a stable orbit, the kinetic energy is equal to half the absolute value of the potential energy ($K = -\frac{1}{2}U$):
$$E_f = K + U = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$
$$E_f = -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(1.70 \times 10^5)}{2(4.218 \times 10^7)} \approx -8.02 \times 10^9 \text{ J}$$

### Step 3: Work Done ($W$)
The work done is the change in total energy:
$$W = E_f - E_i$$
$$W = (-8.02 \times 10^9) - (-1.59 \times 10^{10}) \approx 7.88 \times 10^9 \text{ J}$$

*(Note: In HSC physics, if the rotational kinetic energy at the surface is considered negligible compared to the gravitational potential, $E_i$ is simply $-\frac{GMm}{R_E}$. Using the total energy difference, the work required is approximately $7.9 \times 10^9\text{ J}$.)*
