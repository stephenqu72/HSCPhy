### Part (d)
The decay equation is $A = A_0 e^{-\lambda t}$. Taking the natural log of both sides gives:
$$\ln(A) = \ln(A_0) - \lambda t$$
This is in the form $y = mx + c$, where the gradient $G = -\lambda$.
Therefore, $\lambda = -G = 0.0072 \text{ minute}^{-1}$.
Using the provided half-life formula:
$$T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(2)}{\lambda} = \frac{0.693}{0.0072} \approx 96.25 \text{ minutes}$$
The half-life is approximately **96 minutes**.

### How to solve this type of question:
1.  **Understand the Physics:** Recognize that radioactive decay follows an exponential model. By linearizing the equation using natural logarithms, you transform an exponential curve into a straight line, which allows for easier calculation of the decay constant ($\lambda$).
2.  **Background Correction:** Always look for the difference between the raw measurement and the reported corrected measurement to find the systematic background error.
3.  **Graphical Analysis:** When calculating the gradient from a line of best fit, use two points that lie **on the line itself**, not necessarily the data points themselves, to ensure high accuracy.
4.  **Connecting the Gradient:** In physics graphing problems, always identify what your axes represent in terms of the underlying formula (here, $y = \ln(R_c)$ and $x = t$, so $slope = -\lambda$).
