To answer this question, we must look at the wave nature of light, specifically the phenomenon of **diffraction**.

### Answer
The bright spot at the centre of the shadow (known as Arago's spot or Poisson's spot) and the surrounding fringes occur because light behaves as a wave. 

1.  **The Bright Centre:** As the laser light passes the edges of the coin, it diffracts. The light waves traveling from all points along the circumference of the coin travel equal distances to the central point on the screen. Because these waves arrive in phase, they undergo **constructive interference**, creating a bright spot.
2.  **The Fringes:** Beyond the shadow, light waves diffracting from different parts of the edges of the coin overlap. Depending on the path difference between these waves, they either arrive in phase (leading to constructive interference, forming **bright fringes**) or out of phase (leading to destructive interference, forming **dark fringes**).

### How to solve this
When approaching HSC Physics questions regarding light, follow these steps:

*   **Identify the physical phenomenon:** Recognizing that this setup involves a small obstacle (the coin) blocking a light source identifies this as **diffraction**.
*   **Link the phenomenon to wave properties:** You must explicitly state that light is exhibiting wave-like behavior. Diffraction is the bending of waves around obstacles.
*   **Explain the interference:** 
    *   For the **centre**, emphasize that the symmetry of the circular coin results in paths of equal length from all edges to the centre, ensuring the waves are in phase.
    *   For the **fringes**, emphasize the concept of path difference. When the path difference is a whole number of wavelengths ($n\lambda$), you get a maximum (bright); when it is an odd multiple of half-wavelengths ($(n + 0.5)\lambda$), you get a minimum (dark).
