To determine the correct graph, we need to analyze the relationship between gravitational potential energy ($U$) and time ($t$) for a projectile.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the governing formula:**
    The gravitational potential energy of an object is given by:
    $$U = mgh$$
    where $m$ is the mass, $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity, and $h$ is the vertical displacement.

2.  **Determine the height over time:**
    For a projectile launched at an angle, the vertical displacement $h$ as a function of time is given by the kinematic equation:
    $$h(t) = v_{0y}t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
    This is a **downward-opening parabola** because of the $-t^2$ term. Since $U$ is directly proportional to $h$ ($U \propto h$), the graph of $U$ vs $t$ must also be a downward-opening parabola.

3.  **Analyze the initial and final conditions:**
    *   **Start ($t=0$):** At the point of release, the ball is at a certain height (we can define this as $h=0$ for simplicity, so $U=0$).
    *   **Peak ($t_{peak}$):** The height increases, reaches a maximum, and then decreases. Thus, $U$ must increase, reach a maximum, and then decrease.
    *   **End ($t_{total}$):** The ball lands at the same height it was thrown, so the potential energy must return to its starting value.

4.  **Evaluate the options:**
    *   Graphs (C) and (D) show $U$ on the negative y-axis, which is physically unconventional for standard $U = mgh$ calculations.
    *   Graph (B) shows a "u-shape" (a concave up parabola), which implies the energy decreases then increases, which is the opposite of the projectile's motion.
    *   **Graph (A)** shows a concave down parabola starting from a specific energy level, rising to a maximum at the apex of the flight, and returning to the initial energy level at the end. This matches the parabolic nature of the height function $h(t)$.

**Conclusion:** Graph (A) is the only one that correctly represents the parabolic trajectory of the ball's potential energy over time.
