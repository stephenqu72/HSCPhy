### Answer
James Clerk Maxwell’s primary contribution to physics was the development of a unified theory of electromagnetism, published in the 1860s. He synthesized existing laws of electricity and magnetism into a set of four equations (Maxwell's equations) that described how electric and magnetic fields propagate through space. 

Key aspects of his contribution include:
*   **Prediction of Electromagnetic Waves:** Maxwell mathematically demonstrated that a changing electric field generates a changing magnetic field, and vice versa. This mutual induction results in a self-propagating electromagnetic wave.
*   **Nature of Light:** By calculating the speed of these waves using the constants of electricity ($\varepsilon_0$) and magnetism ($\mu_0$), he found the value:
    $$c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}}$$
    This value closely matched the known speed of light, leading Maxwell to correctly propose that light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

The experimental evidence that supported his theory was provided by Heinrich Hertz in 1887:
*   **Hertz’s Experiment:** Hertz used an induction coil to create high-voltage sparks between two metal spheres, which generated electromagnetic oscillations. 
*   **Detection:** He used a secondary loop of wire with a small gap to detect the sparks at a distance, proving that energy was transmitted through space without wires.
*   **Verification:** Hertz demonstrated that these invisible waves exhibited properties identical to light, such as reflection, refraction, diffraction, and polarization, and that they traveled at the speed of light. This empirical evidence confirmed Maxwell's prediction that light is part of the electromagnetic spectrum.
