This is a high-mark Physics question that requires a structured response. To secure full marks, you must link the physics of spectroscopy (absorption/emission lines) to the broader scientific advancements in atomic structure, stellar composition/dynamics, and cosmology.

### Answer Guide

Spectroscopy provided the empirical evidence required to transition from classical physics to quantum mechanics and modern astrophysics.

**1. Understanding of Atoms:**
*   **Discrete Energy Levels:** Spectroscopy revealed that light is emitted or absorbed at discrete wavelengths. This provided the experimental foundation for the Bohr model of the atom, which proposed that electrons exist in quantized energy levels.
*   **Photon Transitions:** The relationship $E = hf$ (or $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$) demonstrates that the energy of a photon corresponds exactly to the energy difference between two electronic shells. This confirmed the quantized nature of matter at the subatomic scale.

**2. Understanding of Stars:**
*   **Chemical Composition:** By comparing the dark absorption (Fraunhofer) lines in a stellar spectrum with known emission spectra of elements on Earth, scientists identified the elemental composition of stars (e.g., discovering Helium in the Sun before it was found on Earth).
*   **Stellar Physics:** Spectroscopic analysis allows us to determine stellar temperatures (using Wien’s Law and the peak of the blackbody curve) and stellar classification based on the presence of specific ionisation lines.

**3. Understanding of the Universe:**
*   **Cosmological Redshift:** The Doppler effect applied to spectral lines provided evidence for the expansion of the universe. When the spectral lines of distant galaxies are shifted toward the red end of the spectrum ($z = \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}$), it indicates they are receding.
*   **Hubble’s Law:** By measuring the redshift, astronomers were able to determine the recession velocity of galaxies, leading to the formulation of Hubble’s Law ($v = H_0d$) and the theory of the Big Bang, fundamentally changing our understanding of the origin and evolution of the universe.
