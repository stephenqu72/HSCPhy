### (a) Calculate the maximum height when the angle is set to $45^\circ$
To find the maximum height ($h_{max}$), we use the vertical component of the initial velocity: $v_y = v \sin(\theta)$.
$$v_y = 150 \sin(45^\circ) = 150 \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 106.07\text{ ms}^{-1}$$
Using the formula $v_f^2 = u_y^2 + 2ay$, where $v_f = 0$ at the peak:
$$0 = (106.07)^2 + 2(-9.8)h$$
$$h = \frac{11250}{19.6} \approx 573.98\text{ m}$$
**Answer:** The maximum height is approximately **$574\text{ m}$**.

### (b) Calculate the maximum range when the angle is set to $45^\circ$
The range ($R$) of a projectile on level ground is given by:
$$R = \frac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$$
$$R = \frac{150^2 \times \sin(2 \times 45^\circ)}{9.8} = \frac{22500 \times \sin(90^\circ)}{9.8} = \frac{22500}{9.8} \approx 2295.92\text{ m}$$
**Answer:** The range is approximately **$2296\text{ m}$**.

### (c) Justify whether the mortar will be able to hit a target $100\text{ m}$ away
The range is a function of the angle $\theta$. The minimum range for this mortar occurs at the extreme ends of the firing range ($20^\circ$ or $70^\circ$).
Let's check the range at $\theta = 20^\circ$:
$$R = \frac{150^2 \times \sin(40^\circ)}{9.8} \approx \frac{22500 \times 0.6428}{9.8} \approx 1475\text{ m}$$
Since the *minimum* possible range ($\approx 1475\text{ m}$) is significantly greater than $100\text{ m}$, the mortar cannot hit a target that close. It will overshoot the target.

### (d) What effect, if any, does plating the shells with lead have on the range?
In an ideal projectile motion model (ignoring air resistance), mass has no effect on the range because the acceleration due to gravity acts equally on all masses. However, in a real-world scenario with air resistance, a heavier shell (increased mass) increases the inertia of the object, making it less susceptible to the decelerating effects of air resistance relative to its momentum, which *may* slightly increase the range compared to a lighter shell of the same size and shape. 
**Answer:** In ideal physics, there is no effect. In reality, increasing mass relative to the cross-sectional area (increasing the ballistic coefficient) will reduce the impact of air resistance, potentially slightly increasing the range.
