### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:**
*   **Doppler Shift:** Due to the Doppler effect, the spectral lines of Star X will be **blueshifted** (shifted toward shorter wavelengths) because it is moving toward the Earth. Conversely, the spectral lines of Star Y will be **redshifted** (shifted toward longer wavelengths) because it is moving away from the Earth.
*   **Rotational Broadening:** The spectral lines of Star X will appear **broader** than those of Star Y. This is because Star X has a significantly higher rotational velocity, which causes different parts of the star's surface to move toward or away from the observer at different relative speeds, resulting in a wider range of Doppler shifts for the same absorption line.

**How to solve:**
1.  Apply the Doppler principle: Source moving toward observer = blueshift; source moving away = redshift.
2.  Relate rotation to spectral features: Rapid rotation causes "line broadening" because one side of the star is approaching (blueshift) and the other is receding (redshift), smearing the spectral line.

#### Part (b)

*   **Star Y (Supergiant/High Luminosity Star):** Located in the upper region of the H-R diagram, Star Y is a massive star. It is likely undergoing advanced nuclear fusion, such as the fusion of helium into carbon, or potentially heavier elements if it is a late-stage massive star.
*   **Star X (Main Sequence Star/Red Dwarf):** Located in the lower right region of the H-R diagram, Star X is a low-mass, cool main sequence star. It is likely performing the **proton-proton chain reaction**, which fuses hydrogen nuclei into helium nuclei ($4^1H \rightarrow ^4He + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + \text{energy}$).

**How to solve:**
1.  Analyze the H-R diagram: Identify the evolutionary stage based on position. Star Y is high-luminosity (likely a supergiant), while Star X is low-luminosity and low-temperature (a red dwarf).
2.  Connect to nucleosynthesis: Recall that main sequence stars (like X) primarily fuse hydrogen. Massive/evolved stars (like Y) possess core temperatures high enough to fuse helium and potentially heavier elements.
