To achieve full marks on this HSC Physics question, you need to clearly link the physical motion of the star to the resulting changes observed in its spectral lines.

### Answer
A star's rotation can be identified by the **broadening of its spectral absorption lines** (specifically Doppler broadening). As a star rotates, one side of the star moves toward the observer while the opposite side moves away. 
1. Light from the side rotating towards Earth is blueshifted (wavelengths are compressed).
2. Light from the side rotating away from Earth is redshifted (wavelengths are stretched).
3. The light collected from the entire stellar disk is a combination of these shifted wavelengths, which causes the absorption lines to appear wider (spread out) compared to the lines of a non-rotating star. The greater the rotational velocity, the broader the spectral lines become.

### How to solve this
When approaching "explain" questions in HSC Physics, use a logical "cause-and-effect" structure to ensure you pick up all 3 marks:

*   **Mark 1: Identify the phenomenon.** State clearly that rotation causes spectral line broadening. This is the "what."
*   **Mark 2: Explain the mechanism.** Describe the Doppler effect. Specifically, mention that different parts of the star are moving at different velocities relative to the observer (one side approaching, one side receding).
*   **Mark 3: Link the mechanism to the observation.** Explain that the combination of these shifted wavelengths results in a wider range of wavelengths for a single absorption feature, creating a "blurred" or broader line on the spectrograph.

**Teacher's Tip:** Always use the term **"Doppler broadening"** in your response—it is the specific scientific terminology examiners look for in this topic to demonstrate high-level understanding.
