To determine which of these options supports Newton’s corpuscular (particle) model of light, we must evaluate how Newton’s model explained optical phenomena compared to the competing wave model.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) Snell's Law**.

### Explanation
Newton proposed the **corpuscular model**, which suggested that light consists of tiny particles called "corpuscles." To explain reflection and refraction using this model, Newton argued that:

1.  **Reflection:** Light particles bounce off surfaces like elastic balls, obeying the law of reflection (angle of incidence equals angle of reflection).
2.  **Refraction (Snell's Law):** Newton hypothesized that light particles experience an attractive force perpendicular to the surface of a denser medium, which accelerates the particles upon entering that medium. According to Snell's Law, $n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2 \sin(\theta_2)$. Newton’s model successfully derived this mathematical relationship, predicting that light travels **faster** in a denser medium (like water) than in air.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A) Young’s double slit experiment:** This demonstrated interference and diffraction, which are wave properties. It provided evidence *against* the corpuscular model and strongly supported the wave model of light.
*   **(B) Malus's Law:** This law relates to the polarization of light. Polarization is a property exclusive to transverse waves; it cannot be explained by a simple corpuscular model.
*   **(C) Measurements of the speed of light in water:** When Foucault later measured the speed of light in water, he found it was slower than in air. This **falsified** Newton's prediction (which claimed light should be faster in water) and provided critical evidence for the wave model of light.

Therefore, while Snell's Law is a property of both particles and waves, it is the only one in the list that Newton was able to account for and use as an argument to support his corpuscular model at the time.
