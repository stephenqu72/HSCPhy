To find the work function of the cathode, we use Einstein's photoelectric equation:

$$E = \Phi + K_{max}$$

Where:
*   $E$ is the energy of the incident photon.
*   $\Phi$ is the work function of the metal.
*   $K_{max}$ is the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons.

### Step 1: Calculate the energy of the incident photon ($E$)
The energy of a photon is given by $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$. In physics problems involving electron-volts (eV), it is often easier to use the constant $hc \approx 1240 \text{ eV}\cdot\text{nm}$.

Given $\lambda = 200 \text{ nm}$:
$$E = \frac{1240 \text{ eV}\cdot\text{nm}}{200 \text{ nm}} = 6.2 \text{ eV}$$

### Step 2: Determine the maximum kinetic energy ($K_{max}$)
The stopping voltage ($V_s$) is the potential required to stop the most energetic electrons. Therefore, the maximum kinetic energy is equal to the stopping voltage in electron-volts:
$$K_{max} = eV_s = 4.2 \text{ eV}$$

### Step 3: Calculate the work function ($\Phi$)
Rearranging the photoelectric equation to solve for $\Phi$:
$$\Phi = E - K_{max}$$
$$\Phi = 6.2 \text{ eV} - 4.2 \text{ eV}$$
$$\Phi = 2.0 \text{ eV}$$

Comparing this result to the given options:
A. 1.2
B. 2.0
C. 3.0
D. 3.2

The correct option is **B**.
