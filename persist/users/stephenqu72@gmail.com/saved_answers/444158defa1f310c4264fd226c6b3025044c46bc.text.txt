### Answer and Explanation

This question assesses your understanding of the early universe, specifically the timeline of fundamental forces, the evidence for the Big Bang, and nucleosynthesis.

---

#### Part (a)
**Answer:**
1. Gravity
2. Strong Nuclear Force
3. Weak Nuclear Force and Electromagnetism (Electroweak symmetry breaking)

**Explanation:** To solve this, you must recall the timeline of the "Grand Unification" era. As the universe expanded and cooled, these forces "froze out" or decoupled from one another. Gravity was the first to separate from the unified superforce, followed by the Strong force, leaving the Electroweak force to separate into the Weak and Electromagnetic forces at a later time.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation is the "afterglow" of the Big Bang. The Big Bang theory predicts that as the universe cooled to approximately $3000\text{ K}$ (the era of recombination), neutral atoms formed, allowing photons to travel freely through space for the first time. The CMB represents this light, which has since been redshifted by the expansion of the universe into the microwave region of the spectrum. Its near-perfect blackbody nature and uniformity provide strong evidence that the universe began in a hot, dense state.

**Explanation:** In HSC Physics, "support" questions require you to link an observation to a prediction. Key points to include:
- The Big Bang predicts a hot, dense origin.
- Recombination allowed photons to decouple from matter.
- The CMB is the remnant of this event, cooled and redshifted to its current state.

#### Part (c)
**Answer:**
If we assume a ratio of $14$ protons to $2$ neutrons (maintaining the $7:1$ ratio):
- The $2$ neutrons will combine with $2$ protons to form $1$ Helium-4 nucleus.
- This leaves $12$ protons remaining, which exist as $12$ Hydrogen nuclei.
- Comparing mass: $1$ Helium nucleus has a mass of $4$ units, while $12$ Hydrogen nuclei have a mass of $12$ units.
- The ratio of the mass of Hydrogen to Helium is $12:4$, which simplifies to $3:1$.

**Explanation:** This is a classic calculation/reasoning task.
1. Start by scaling the $7:1$ ratio to a total number of nucleons that allows for easy formation of Helium-4 (which consists of $2$ protons and $2$ neutrons).
2. Calculate how many protons are "locked up" in Helium.
3. Determine how many protons are left over as Hydrogen.
4. Convert particle count to mass ratio, remembering that Helium ($4$ nucleons) is four times heavier than Hydrogen ($1$ nucleon).
