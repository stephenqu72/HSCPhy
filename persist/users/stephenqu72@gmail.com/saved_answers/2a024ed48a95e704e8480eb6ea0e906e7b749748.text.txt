### Explanation
To understand the torque acting on the arm of this pendulum, we look at the formula for torque ($\tau$) about the pivot point $O$:
$$\tau = rF \sin(\theta)$$

In this context:
1.  **$r$** is the length of the rigid arm (constant).
2.  **$F$** is the force of gravity acting on the mass ($mg$), which always points vertically downwards.
3.  **$\theta$** is the angle between the arm and the vertical line of equilibrium.

**Analysis at different positions:**
*   **At position $Y$:** The mass is at the equilibrium point. The arm is hanging vertically, meaning the force of gravity acts directly in line with the pivot $O$. The angle $\theta$ between the force vector and the arm is $0^\circ$. Since $\sin(0^\circ) = 0$, the torque $\tau$ is **zero**.
*   **At positions $X$ and $Z$:** These are the points of maximum displacement. The angle $\theta$ between the arm and the vertical is at its greatest value. Since $\sin(\theta)$ is at its maximum at these extremes, the torque $\tau = mgL \sin(\theta)$ is at its **maximum**. 

Because torque acts to restore the pendulum toward the equilibrium position $Y$, it changes direction as the pendulum swings from side to side; however, the magnitude is undeniably greatest when the displacement is largest (at $X$ and $Z$) and vanishes as it passes through the center ($Y$).
