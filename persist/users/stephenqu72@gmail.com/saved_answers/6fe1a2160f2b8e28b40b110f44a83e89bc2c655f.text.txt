To solve this problem, we use the principles of electromagnetism related to DC motors.

### Part (a): Determine the torque on the coil at the position shown.

**1. Identify given variables:**
*   Side length $s = 3\text{ cm} = 0.03\text{ m}$
*   Area $A = s^2 = (0.03\text{ m})^2 = 9 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^2$
*   Magnetic field strength $B = 0.15\text{ T}$
*   Current $I = 3\text{ A}$

**2. Formula:**
The torque $\tau$ on a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field is given by:
$$\tau = nI AB \sin(\theta)$$
where $\theta$ is the angle between the normal to the coil and the magnetic field.

**3. Analysis:**
In the provided image, the plane of the coil is parallel to the magnetic field lines. Therefore, the **normal** to the coil is perpendicular ($90^\circ$) to the magnetic field.
$$\tau = (1)(3\text{ A})(9 \times 10^{-4}\text{ m}^2)(0.15\text{ T}) \sin(90^\circ)$$
$$\tau = 3 \times 0.0009 \times 0.15 \times 1 = 4.05 \times 10^{-4}\text{ N m}$$

**Answer:** The torque is $4.05 \times 10^{-4}\text{ N m}$.

### Part (b): Draw a torque vs time graph.

**1. Analyze the movement:**
*   The coil rotates at $240\text{ rpm} = 4\text{ revolutions per second}$.
*   The period $T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25\text{ s}$.
*   At the start position shown, the torque is at its maximum ($4.05 \times 10^{-4}\text{ N m}$).
*   As the coil rotates, the angle $\theta$ changes, following a cosine wave: $\tau(t) = \tau_{\text{max}} \cos(\omega t)$.
*   Due to the split-ring commutator in a DC motor, the current direction reverses every half turn, ensuring the torque remains in the same direction for a full revolution.

**2. Graph construction:**
*   **Y-axis:** Label "Torque ($10^{-4}\text{ N m}$)" with the peak at $4.05$.
*   **X-axis:** Label "Time ($s$)" with markers at $0$, $0.0625$, $0.125$, $0.1875$, and $0.25$.
*   **Shape:** The graph should start at $+4.05$, drop to $0$ at $0.0625\text{ s}$, reach $0$ again at $0.125\text{ s}$ (the vertical position), and follow the absolute value of a cosine curve (or a rectified sine wave) since the commutator keeps the torque positive. It will have two identical "humps" within one full $0.25\text{ s}$ revolution.
