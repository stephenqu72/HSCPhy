To determine the relationship between the frequency of revolution $f$ and the radius $r$ for a charged particle moving in a uniform magnetic field, we start by equating the magnetic force to the centripetal force.

### Step-by-Step Explanation

1.  **Magnetic Force:** A particle with charge $q$ moving with velocity $v$ perpendicular to a magnetic field $B$ experiences a magnetic force:
    $$F_B = qvB$$

2.  **Centripetal Force:** This force provides the centripetal acceleration required for circular motion:
    $$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

3.  **Equating the two:**
    $$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    Solving for the radius $r$:
    $$r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$

4.  **Relating Velocity to Frequency:** The velocity of a particle in circular motion is related to its period $T$ and frequency $f$ by $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} = 2\pi rf$.

5.  **Substituting into the radius equation:**
    Substitute $v = 2\pi rf$ into the equation $r = \frac{mv}{qB}$:
    $$r = \frac{m(2\pi rf)}{qB}$$

6.  **Simplifying:**
    Dividing both sides by $r$ (since $r \neq 0$):
    $$1 = \frac{2\pi mf}{qB}$$
    Rearranging to solve for frequency $f$:
    $$f = \frac{qB}{2\pi m}$$

### Conclusion
As shown in the final expression, the frequency $f$ is independent of the radius $r$. The variables $q$, $B$, and $m$ are constants for this scenario. Therefore, regardless of how large or small the radius of the circular path is, the frequency of revolution remains constant.
