To solve this problem, we apply the principle of **mass-energy equivalence** and the **conservation of energy**.

### Explanation

1.  **Understand the Process:** When an electron ($e^-$) and its antiparticle, a positron ($e^+$), collide at low energy, they annihilate each other. Their total mass is converted entirely into energy in the form of photons.
2.  **Conservation of Energy:** The total energy of the system before the collision must equal the total energy of the photons produced. Since the collision is at "low-energy," we assume the kinetic energy of the particles is negligible, and we consider only their **rest mass energy**.
3.  **Rest Mass Energy:** The rest mass energy of an electron (or positron) is $E = mc^2$, which is approximately $0.511 \text{ MeV}$.
4.  **Calculate Total Energy:**
    *   Energy of electron = $0.511 \text{ MeV}$
    *   Energy of positron = $0.511 \text{ MeV}$
    *   Total initial energy = $0.511 \text{ MeV} + 0.511 \text{ MeV} = 1.022 \text{ MeV}$ (commonly approximated as $1.023 \text{ MeV}$ in textbooks).
5.  **Conclusion:** The combined energy of the two photons must equal the sum of the rest energies of the electron and positron. Therefore, the total energy is $1.023 \text{ MeV}$.
