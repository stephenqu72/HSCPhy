### Explanation
To solve this problem, you need to understand the historical development of the wave theory of light and the specific properties that characterize different types of waves.

1.  **The Nature of Polarisation:** Polarisation is a phenomenon where the orientation of oscillations in a wave is restricted to a specific plane. This phenomenon can only occur in **transverse waves**, where the displacement of the medium (or the field, in the case of electromagnetic waves) is perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.
2.  **The Limitation of Longitudinal Waves:** Longitudinal waves (like sound waves) oscillate in the same direction as the wave propagates. Because they lack a "side-to-side" component, they cannot be polarised. 
3.  **Historical Context:** In the early 19th century, scientists like Thomas Young had demonstrated interference (proving light behaved as a wave), but there was a significant debate about whether light was a longitudinal or transverse wave. If light were a longitudinal wave, it could not be polarised. It was not until Augustin-Jean Fresnel and François Arago demonstrated the polarisation of light that it became clear that light must be a **transverse wave**. 

Therefore, the wave theory of light could only fully explain polarisation once it was accepted that light possessed a transverse nature, allowing for the restriction of oscillations to a single plane.
