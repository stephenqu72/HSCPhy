To solve this problem, we first recognize that the provided equation $E = hf = q_e(V_0 + k)$ must be rearranged to fit the form $y = mx + c$ to determine the gradient.

Given $E = hf$ and $c = f\lambda$, we know $f = \frac{c}{\lambda}$. Substituting this into the equation:
$$h\left(\frac{c}{\lambda}\right) = q_e V_0 + q_e k$$
$$V_0 = \left(\frac{hc}{q_e}\right)\left(\frac{1}{\lambda}\right) - k$$

This shows that if we plot $V_0$ (y-axis) against $1/\lambda$ (x-axis), the gradient $m = \frac{hc}{q_e}$.

### (c) Gradient calculation
Using two points on your line of best fit (e.g., $(1.1 \times 10^6, 0.85)$ and $(2.2 \times 10^6, 2.50)$):
$$\text{Gradient } (m) = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{2.50 - 0.85}{(2.2 - 1.1) \times 10^6} \approx 1.50 \times 10^{-6} \text{ V}\cdot\text{m}$$

### (d) Calculate Planck's constant
Using $m = \frac{hc}{q_e}$:
$$h = \frac{m \cdot q_e}{c}$$
$$h = \frac{(1.50 \times 10^{-6}) \times (1.602 \times 10^{-19})}{3.00 \times 10^8} \approx 8.01 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$$
