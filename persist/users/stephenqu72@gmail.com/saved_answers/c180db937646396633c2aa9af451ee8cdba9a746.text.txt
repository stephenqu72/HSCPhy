### Answer
The correct answer is **B. Electromagnetic waves travelled at the speed of light**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we need to consider the historical context of James Clerk Maxwell's work in the mid-19th century. 

1.  **Maxwell’s Equations:** Maxwell derived a set of equations that described how electric and magnetic fields behave and interact. From these equations, he mathematically predicted the existence of waves of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that propagate through space.
2.  **The Velocity Calculation:** When Maxwell calculated the theoretical speed at which these electromagnetic waves would travel in a vacuum, he used the constants $\epsilon_0$ (permittivity of free space) and $\mu_0$ (permeability of free space). The formula he derived is:
    $$v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$$
3.  **The Connection:** Upon calculating the numerical value for $v$, Maxwell found that it was approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s. This value was remarkably close to the experimentally determined speed of light ($c$) at the time.
4.  **The Conclusion:** Because the theoretical speed of the predicted electromagnetic waves matched the measured speed of light, Maxwell concluded that light itself must be an electromagnetic wave.

While electromagnetic waves are indeed transverse (A) and non-mechanical (D), it was specifically the **matching of the calculated velocity** to the known speed of light that provided the empirical "aha!" moment for Maxwell to identify light as an electromagnetic phenomenon.
