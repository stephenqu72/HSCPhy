To calculate the orbital speed of the International Space Station (ISS), we use the physics of circular motion under gravity.

### Answer
The orbital speed is approximately $7.67 \times 10^3 \text{ m/s}$ (or $7.67 \text{ km/s}$).

### Explanation
To solve this, follow these steps:

**1. Identify the given values and constants:**
*   Altitude above Earth ($h$): $4.00 \times 10^2 \text{ km} = 4.00 \times 10^5 \text{ m}$.
*   Radius of Earth ($R_E$): $6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$ (standard physics data).
*   Mass of Earth ($M$): $5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$ (standard physics data).
*   Gravitational constant ($G$): $6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2$.

**2. Determine the total orbital radius ($r$):**
The orbital radius must be measured from the **center** of the Earth, not the surface.
$$r = R_E + h = 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m} + 0.40 \times 10^6 \text{ m} = 6.77 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$$

**3. Use the orbital velocity formula:**
The centripetal force required to keep the satellite in orbit is provided by Earth's gravitational force. Equating these gives the formula for orbital speed:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

**4. Perform the calculation:**
Substitute the values into the formula:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg})}{6.77 \times 10^6 \text{ m}}}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{3.982 \times 10^{14}}{6.77 \times 10^6}}$$
$$v = \sqrt{5.88 \times 10^7} \approx 7669 \text{ m/s}$$

Rounding to three significant figures, we get $7.67 \times 10^3 \text{ m/s}$.
