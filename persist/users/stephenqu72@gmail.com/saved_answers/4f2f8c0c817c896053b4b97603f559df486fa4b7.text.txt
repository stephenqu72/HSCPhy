To determine which test flight produces the greatest range, we need to understand the physics of projectile motion.

### Explanation

The horizontal range $R$ of a projectile launched from ground level is given by the formula:
$$R = v_x \cdot t$$
Where $t$ is the time of flight. The time of flight is determined by the vertical component of velocity ($v_y$) and gravity ($g$):
$$t = \frac{2v_y}{g}$$
Substituting $t$ into the range formula gives:
$$R = v_x \cdot \left(\frac{2v_y}{g}\right) = \frac{2}{g}(v_x \cdot v_y)$$

Since $g$ is constant, the range is directly proportional to the product of the horizontal and vertical velocity components ($v_x \cdot v_y$). We need to calculate this product for each test flight:

*   **Test 1:** $120 \times 207 = 24,840$
*   **Test 2:** $167 \times 172 = 28,724$
*   **Test 3:** $183 \times 154 = 28,182$
*   **Test 4:** $207 \times 120 = 24,840$

**Conceptual Shortcut:**
Mathematically, for a fixed initial speed $v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$, the product $v_x \cdot v_y$ is maximized when $v_x = v_y$ (i.e., a launch angle of $45^\circ$). 
*   Test 1 and Test 4 have the same product.
*   Test 2 and Test 3 are closer to each other in magnitude (more balanced), and Test 2 is the closest to the $45^\circ$ equilibrium where $v_x = v_y$. 

Comparing the products, Test 2 yields the highest value.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B. Test 2**.
