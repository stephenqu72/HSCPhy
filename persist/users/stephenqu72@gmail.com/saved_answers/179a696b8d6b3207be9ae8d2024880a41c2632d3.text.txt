### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the process of **Big Bang nucleosynthesis** and the life cycle of stars:

1.  **Composition of the Early Universe:** Immediately following the Big Bang, the universe consisted primarily of hydrogen and helium, with trace amounts of lithium. There were no heavier elements (often referred to as "metals" in astrophysics) present, as these are synthesized inside stars through nuclear fusion.
2.  **Population III Stars:** The first stars, known as Population III stars, were formed from this pristine primordial gas. Because they were born before any other stars had lived and died, they contained virtually no heavy elements (elements with an atomic number greater than 3, like carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, or iron).
3.  **Spectral Analysis:** The absorption lines in a star's spectrum correspond to the specific elements present in the star's atmosphere. Since the first stars lacked heavy elements, their spectra would show absorption lines for hydrogen, helium, and perhaps lithium, but would **lack the absorption lines of heavier elements** that we see in later, "metal-rich" stars like our Sun.
4.  **Why other options are incorrect:**
    *   (A) is incorrect because the composition of the universe has changed significantly over time due to nucleosynthesis.
    *   (C) is incorrect because atoms in these stars certainly had electrons; they were neutral atoms capable of producing absorption spectra.
    *   (D) is incorrect because the number of absorption lines depends on the chemical complexity of the atmosphere, not just temperature. In fact, a star with fewer heavy elements will generally show a "cleaner" spectrum with fewer absorption lines.
