The correct answer is **(D) Random error**.

### Explanation

To solve this, we must distinguish between the different types of experimental errors:

1.  **Systematic error:** These are consistent, repeatable errors usually caused by faulty equipment or a flawed experimental design (e.g., a scale that is always $0.5\text{ g}$ off). If the initial velocity were consistently too high or too low by the same amount every time, this would be a systematic error.
2.  **Random error:** These are unpredictable variations that occur in experimental measurements. They cause data points to fluctuate around a true value. Because the student's launcher is "homemade," it likely lacks precision, meaning the mechanism varies slightly from trial to trial. If the velocity is inconsistent (sometimes faster, sometimes slower), the resulting data will be scattered. This lack of precision and repeatability is a classic characteristic of random error.
3.  **Human error:** This refers to mistakes made by the observer, such as misreading a scale or parallax error. While human operation of the launcher could contribute, the term "inconsistent initial velocity" refers to the output of the apparatus itself, making "random error" the more scientifically accurate classification for the variation in the launch.
4.  **Instrumental error:** This is a sub-category of systematic error relating to the calibration of equipment. Since the launcher's performance is fluctuating rather than being calibrated incorrectly in a fixed direction, it is best described as random.

Because the velocity is changing unpredictably across trials, it introduces uncertainty into the measurements that can only be minimized by repeating trials and averaging the results, which is the standard approach to dealing with random errors.
