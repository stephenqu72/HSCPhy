To answer this question, we must look at the fundamental formula for the work done ($W$) on a charge ($q$) moving through an electric field:

$$W = q \Delta V$$

Alternatively, since the electric field ($E$) is related to potential difference ($V$) and distance ($d$) by $V = Ed$, we can express the work done as:

$$W = qEd$$

### Analysis of the options:

*   **A. The work done on a charged particle is directly proportional to the strength of the electric field.**
    From $W = qEd$, if $q$ and $d$ are constant, $W$ is directly proportional to $E$. This statement is **true**.
*   **B. Doubling the charge of the particle has no impact on the work done on the charged particle.**
    Looking at $W = q \Delta V$, if the charge $q$ is doubled, the work done $W$ also doubles. Therefore, stating it has "no impact" is mathematically incorrect. This statement is **not true**.
*   **C. The work done on the charged particle is dependent on the potential difference between the plates producing the electric field.**
    As shown in the formula $W = q \Delta V$, the work done is explicitly dependent on the potential difference $\Delta V$. This statement is **true**.
*   **D. The work done on the charged particle is calculated using the magnitude of the displacement from the starting point.**
    Work in an electric field is $W = Fd \cos \theta$, where $d$ is the displacement. In the context of a uniform field between plates, the displacement component in the direction of the field defines the work done. This is a standard physical interpretation. This statement is **true**.

**Conclusion:**
Since the question asks for the statement that is **NOT** true, the correct answer is **B**.
