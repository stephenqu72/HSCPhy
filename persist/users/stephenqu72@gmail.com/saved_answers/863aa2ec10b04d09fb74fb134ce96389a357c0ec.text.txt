### Answer
The correct answer is **D. Its translational velocity**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we apply the principles of the **Doppler Effect** in astrophysics:

1.  **Understanding Redshift:** When an object (like a star) moves away from an observer, the light waves it emits are "stretched" to longer wavelengths. Because the red end of the visible spectrum has the longest wavelengths, this phenomenon is called **redshift**.
2.  **The Doppler Effect:** The Doppler effect relates the shift in observed wavelength ($\Delta \lambda$) to the velocity of the source relative to the observer. Specifically, the change in wavelength provides information about the **radial velocity** (a component of translational velocity) of the star along the line of sight.
3.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **A. Density:** While spectral line broadening can sometimes indicate high pressure/density, the actual shift of the lines is due to motion, not density.
    *   **B. Rotational velocity:** This is determined by the *broadening* of spectral lines (due to one side of the star moving toward us and the other away), not by a collective redshift of the entire spectrum.
    *   **C. Surface temperature:** This is determined by the peak wavelength of the continuous spectrum (Wien’s Law) or the presence of specific absorption lines, not by the shifting of those lines.
    *   **D. Translational velocity:** Since a redshift indicates the star is moving away from the observer, it directly provides information about the star's translational velocity (specifically the radial velocity component).

Therefore, observing a redshift allows astronomers to determine the velocity at which a star is moving away from the Earth.
