To solve this problem, we need to calculate the value of $X$ (Jupiter's orbital radius in Astronomical Units) and $Y$ (Jupiter's orbital period in days).

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Finding $X$ (Orbital radius in AU):**
One Astronomical Unit (AU) is defined as the average orbital radius of the Earth. Given that the orbital radius of Earth is $150,000,000 \text{ km} = 1.0 \text{ AU}$, we can find $X$ by dividing Jupiter's orbital radius by Earth's:
$$X = \frac{778,000,000 \text{ km}}{150,000,000 \text{ km}}$$
$$X \approx 5.19 \text{ AU}$$

**2. Finding $Y$ (Orbital period in days):**
We use Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion, which states that the ratio of the square of the orbital period ($T$) to the cube of the orbital radius ($r$) is constant for all planets orbiting the same star:
$$\frac{T^2}{r^3} = k$$
Since we are using units relative to Earth ($T_{Earth} = 1 \text{ year}$, $r_{Earth} = 1 \text{ AU}$), the constant is $1$. Therefore:
$$\frac{T_J^2}{r_J^3} = \frac{T_E^2}{r_E^3} \implies T_J^2 = r_J^3$$
Where $r_J = 5.19 \text{ AU}$ and $T_J$ is in Earth years:
$$T_J^2 = (5.19)^3$$
$$T_J^2 \approx 139.78$$
$$T_J \approx \sqrt{139.78} \approx 11.82 \text{ years}$$

Now, convert the orbital period from years into days (assuming 1 year = 365 days):
$$Y = 11.82 \times 365 \text{ days}$$
$$Y \approx 4314 \text{ days}$$

**Summary of Answers:**
*   $X \approx 5.19$
*   $Y \approx 4314 \text{ days}$
