To determine which statement does **not** describe $g$, we must first understand its definition based on the provided physics principles.

### Analysis of the Physics
The gravitational field strength $g$ is defined by the formula $g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$. 
From Newton's Second Law ($F = ma$) and the law of universal gravitation ($F = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$), we can equate the forces:
$$mg = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

This demonstrates that $g$ represents:
1. **The gravitational field strength:** The force per unit mass exerted by the Earth at a specific distance.
2. **Acceleration due to gravity:** Since $F = mg$, a free-falling body with mass $m$ experiences an acceleration of $a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{mg}{m} = g$.
3. **Force per unit mass:** If $m = 1\text{ kg}$, then $F = g \times 1\text{ kg} = g$. Therefore, $g$ is numerically equal to the force experienced by a $1\text{ kg}$ mass.

### Evaluating the Options
*   **(A)** "g is the gravitational field at the surface of Earth": **Correct.** This is the definition of $g$ as a field strength.
*   **(B)** "g is the force that a mass m feels at the surface of Earth": **Incorrect.** The force felt by a mass $m$ is $F = mg$. $g$ is the *acceleration* or the *force per unit mass*, not the force itself (which depends on the magnitude of $m$).
*   **(C)** "g is the acceleration of a free body at the surface of Earth": **Correct.** This is the standard definition of acceleration due to gravity.
*   **(D)** "g is the force experienced by a mass of 1 kg at the surface of Earth": **Correct.** By definition, the gravitational field strength is the force exerted on a unit mass ($1\text{ kg}$).

Since statement (B) confuses the constant $g$ (field strength/acceleration) with the total force $F$ (which is dependent on the specific mass $m$), it is the statement that does not correctly describe $g$.

**Correct Answer: (B)**
