To answer this question, we must look at the force components providing the necessary **centripetal force** ($F_c$) to keep the cars moving in a circle.

### Explanation and Solution

1.  **Horizontal Road:** The only horizontal force acting towards the center of the curve is the static friction ($f$) between the tyres and the road. The centripetal force is provided solely by this friction: $F_c = f = \frac{mv^2}{r}$. The maximum speed is limited by the maximum static friction coefficient $\mu$.
2.  **Banked Road:** On a banked track, the normal force ($N$) is perpendicular to the road surface. We can resolve this normal force into two components:
    *   A vertical component: $N \cos \theta$ (which balances weight $mg$).
    *   A horizontal component: $N \sin \theta$ (which acts towards the center of the curve).
    
Because the horizontal component of the normal force ($N \sin \theta$) contributes to the centripetal force, the car does not rely solely on friction to stay in the circular path. This effectively increases the "available" centripetal force at any given speed, allowing for a higher maximum velocity before sliding occurs.
