To solve this question, we must apply our understanding of field theory as taught in the HSC Physics curriculum regarding the fundamental sources of gravitational, magnetic, and electric fields.

### Solution

| Field type | Only monopoles | Only dipoles | Both monopoles and dipoles |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Gravitation** | $\checkmark$ | | |
| **Magnetism** | | $\checkmark$ | |
| **Electricity** | | | $\checkmark$ |

### Explanation

1.  **Gravitation**: According to the standard model of classical mechanics and general relativity, mass is the source of gravitational fields. Mass always creates an attractive field (a monopole source). There is no observed "negative mass" that could create a repulsive gravitational pole to form a dipole, so it is strictly described by **only monopoles**.
2.  **Magnetism**: In classical electromagnetism (specifically Gauss’s Law for magnetism), there are no magnetic monopoles. Magnetic fields are always generated by current loops or intrinsic spin, which essentially act as **only dipoles** (having a North and a South pole). Even if you break a magnet, you simply create two smaller magnets, each with a North and South pole.
3.  **Electricity**: Electric fields are created by electric charges (positive or negative). A single positive or negative charge is a monopole. However, we can also have electric dipoles, which consist of a positive and a negative charge separated by a small distance. Since both individual charges and pairs of charges exist, electricity is associated with **both monopoles and dipoles**.
