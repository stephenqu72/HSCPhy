### Solutions and Explanations

#### Part (a)
**Task:** Calculate the frequency ($f$) of incident light given a stopping voltage ($V_s$) of $3.4\text{ V}$ and a work function ($\phi$) of $2.3\text{ eV}$.

**Step 1:** Use the photoelectric equation: $E_k = hf - \phi$. Since $E_k = eV_s$, we can write:
$$hf = eV_s + \phi$$

**Step 2:** Convert all units to Joules.
$eV_s = 3.4\text{ eV} = 3.4 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J} = 5.447 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$
$\phi = 2.3\text{ eV} = 2.3 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J} = 3.685 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$

**Step 3:** Calculate $hf$:
$$hf = 5.447 \times 10^{-19} + 3.685 \times 10^{-19} = 9.132 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$$

**Step 4:** Solve for $f$ using Planck's constant $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J s}$:
$$f = \frac{9.132 \times 10^{-19}}{6.626 \times 10^{-34}} \approx 1.38 \times 10^{15}\text{ Hz}$$

#### Part (b)
**Task:** Explain the relationship between work function ($\phi$) and threshold frequency ($f_0$).

**Explanation:** The work function ($\phi$) is defined as the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the surface of a metal. The threshold frequency ($f_0$) is the minimum frequency of incident radiation required to eject an electron. They are directly proportional, related by the equation:
$$\phi = hf_0$$
This means that if the incident light frequency is below $f_0$, the photon energy is less than the work function, and no photoelectrons will be emitted regardless of the intensity of the light.

#### Part (c)
**Task:** Calculate the maximum velocity ($v_{max}$) for a $133\text{ nm}$ source.

**Step 1:** Calculate photon energy ($E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$):
$$E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})(3.00 \times 10^8)}{133 \times 10^{-9}} \approx 1.494 \times 10^{-18}\text{ J}$$

**Step 2:** Calculate kinetic energy ($E_k = E - \phi$):
Convert $\phi = 2.3\text{ eV}$ to Joules again: $3.685 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$.
$$E_k = 1.494 \times 10^{-18} - 0.3685 \times 10^{-18} = 1.1255 \times 10^{-18}\text{ J}$$

**Step 3:** Use the classical kinetic energy formula ($E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$):
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m_e}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 1.1255 \times 10^{-18}}{9.109 \times 10^{-31}}} \approx \sqrt{2.47 \times 10^{12}}$$
$$v \approx 1.57 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$$
