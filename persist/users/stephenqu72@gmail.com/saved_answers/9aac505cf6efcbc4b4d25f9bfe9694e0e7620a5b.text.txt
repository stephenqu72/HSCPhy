To solve this problem, we apply Snell's Law to determine the angle of refraction and then use trigonometry to calculate the displacement $OX$.

### Part a: Calculation of distance OX

**1. Determine the angle of incidence ($\theta_1$):**
The angle given ($40.00^\circ$) is with respect to the *surface*. The angle of incidence used in Snell’s Law must be measured from the *normal* (the dotted vertical line).
$$\theta_1 = 90^\circ - 40.00^\circ = 50.00^\circ$$

**2. Use Snell's Law to find the angle of refraction ($\theta_2$):**
The formula is $n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2 \sin(\theta_2)$, where $n_1 = 1.000$ (air) and $n_2 = 1.335$ (water).
$$1.000 \cdot \sin(50.00^\circ) = 1.335 \cdot \sin(\theta_2)$$
$$\sin(\theta_2) = \frac{\sin(50.00^\circ)}{1.335} \approx \frac{0.7660}{1.335} \approx 0.5738$$
$$\theta_2 = \arcsin(0.5738) \approx 35.01^\circ$$

**3. Calculate distance $OX$ using trigonometry:**
In the right-angled triangle formed by the depth of the tank ($80.00 \text{ cm}$) and the refracted ray, $OX$ is the opposite side to the angle of refraction $\theta_2$.
$$\tan(\theta_2) = \frac{OX}{\text{depth}}$$
$$OX = 80.00 \times \tan(35.01^\circ)$$
$$OX \approx 80.00 \times 0.7003 \approx 56.02 \text{ cm}$$

### Part b: Effect of white sunlight

When white light enters the water, **dispersion** occurs because different wavelengths of light have different refractive indices in water (the refractive index is higher for shorter wavelengths like violet/blue and lower for longer wavelengths like red).

*   **Left of point X:** Violet/Blue light. These colors have a higher refractive index than green light, meaning they refract (bend) more towards the normal, resulting in a smaller angle of refraction and landing to the left of the green light's position.
