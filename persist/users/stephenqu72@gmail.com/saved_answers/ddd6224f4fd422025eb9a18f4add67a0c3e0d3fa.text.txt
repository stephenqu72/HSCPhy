To solve this problem, we must identify the physical origin of each type of spectrum presented:

1.  **Spectrum I**: This shows distinct, bright lines on a dark background. This is an **emission spectrum**, which is produced by hot, low-density gases, such as those found in a **gas discharge tube**.
2.  **Spectrum II**: This shows a continuous background interrupted by dark lines. This is an **absorption spectrum**. Sunlight is the classic example, where the hot interior of the Sun produces a continuous spectrum, and the cooler gases in the Sun's atmosphere absorb specific wavelengths, creating these "Fraunhofer lines."
3.  **Spectrum III**: This shows a smooth, continuous band of color (or intensity) without any lines. This is a **continuous spectrum**, which is produced by hot, dense objects, such as an **incandescent filament** (like a traditional light bulb).

Matching these to the options:
*   I = gas discharge tube
*   II = sunlight
*   III = incandescent filament

Looking at the options provided:
*   (A) incorrect
*   (B) incorrect
*   (C) matches: I = gas discharge tube, II = sunlight, III = incandescent filament
*   (D) incorrect

**Answer**: The correct option is **(C)**.

### How to solve this:
*   **Identify the signature of each spectrum**: 
    *   Discrete bright lines = Emission (Gas discharge).
    *   Continuous band with dark gaps = Absorption (Sunlight).
    *   Solid, unbroken band = Continuous (Hot solid/filament).
*   **Elimination**: Start with the easiest identification. For most students, the continuous spectrum (III) is the most recognizable as an incandescent source. This immediately narrows the choices to (C) or (D). Then, differentiate between the line spectra (I) and the absorption spectrum (II) to confirm (C).
