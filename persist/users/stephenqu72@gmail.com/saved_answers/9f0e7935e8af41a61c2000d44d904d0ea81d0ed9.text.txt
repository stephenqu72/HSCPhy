To answer this question, we must look at the transition from the Bohr model of the atom to the quantum mechanical model proposed by Erwin Schrödinger.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) removed the certainty about the position of electrons in their orbits.**

### Explanation
1.  **The Bohr Model:** Niels Bohr proposed that electrons move in fixed, circular "orbits" around the nucleus at specific distances, similar to planets orbiting the sun. This model treated electrons as discrete particles with definite positions and trajectories.
2.  **Schrödinger’s Contribution:** Erwin Schrödinger developed wave mechanics, where he described the electron using a wavefunction ($\psi$). His equation showed that we cannot know both the exact position and momentum of an electron simultaneously (linked to Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle).
3.  **From Orbits to Orbitals:** Instead of fixed orbits, Schrödinger introduced the concept of "orbitals." An orbital is a three-dimensional region of space where there is a high **probability** (usually 90%) of finding an electron. 
4.  **Why the others are incorrect:**
    *   (B) is incorrect because Schrödinger's model specifically *removed* the notion of fixed, certain paths.
    *   (C) is partially true in concept (wave-particle duality was largely attributed to de Broglie), but Schrödinger's *advancement* to atomic theory specifically focused on the mathematical description of the electron's position as a probability cloud rather than a wave-only statement.
    *   (D) is incorrect because quantum mechanics acknowledges wave-particle duality; electrons exhibit properties of both waves and particles.

By replacing definite orbits with probability clouds, Schrödinger fundamentally removed the certainty of an electron's exact position.
