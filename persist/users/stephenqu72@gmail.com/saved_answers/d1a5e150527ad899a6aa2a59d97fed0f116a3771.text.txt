To solve this problem, we must apply the two fundamental principles governing the transmission of light through polarizers:

### 1. Step-by-Step Explanation

**Step 1: Determine the intensity after the first polarizer**
When unpolarized light with intensity $I_0$ passes through an ideal polarizer, the intensity of the transmitted light ($I_1$) is exactly half of the incident intensity, regardless of the orientation of the polarizer.
$$I_1 = \frac{1}{2} I_0$$
Given $I_0 = 20\text{ W m}^{-2}$:
$$I_1 = \frac{1}{2} \times 20 = 10\text{ W m}^{-2}$$

**Step 2: Apply Malus's Law for the second polarizer**
When polarized light of intensity $I_1$ passes through a second polarizer (the analyzer) with a transmission axis at an angle $\theta$ relative to the first, the transmitted intensity $I_2$ is governed by Malus's Law:
$$I_2 = I_1 \cos^2(\theta)$$
We are given the final transmitted intensity $I_2 = 4\text{ W m}^{-2}$. Substituting our values:
$$4 = 10 \cos^2(\theta)$$

**Step 3: Solve for $\theta$**
Rearrange the equation to solve for $\theta$:
$$\cos^2(\theta) = \frac{4}{10} = 0.4$$
$$\cos(\theta) = \sqrt{0.4}$$
$$\theta = \arccos(\sqrt{0.4})$$
$$\theta \approx \arccos(0.63245)$$
$$\theta \approx 50.77^\circ$$

Rounding to the nearest degree, we get $51^\circ$.

### 2. Conclusion
Comparing our result to the given options:
(A) $51^\circ$
(B) $63^\circ$
(C) $66^\circ$
(D) $78^\circ$

The correct answer is **(A)**.
