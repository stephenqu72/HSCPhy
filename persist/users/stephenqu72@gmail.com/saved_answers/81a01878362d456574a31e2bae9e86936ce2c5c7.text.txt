To solve this problem, we apply the concept of **time dilation** from Einstein's theory of Special Relativity.

### Answer
The time interval in the quasar's frame of reference is approximately **14.18 hours**.

---

### Step-by-Step Explanation

**1. Identify the given variables:**
*   Observed time interval on Earth ($\Delta t$): $20\text{ hours}$
*   Lorentz factor ($\gamma$): $1.41$
*   We need to find the proper time interval ($\Delta t_0$), which is the time measured in the frame of reference where the event is stationary (the quasar's frame).

**2. Recall the Time Dilation Formula:**
The relationship between the observed time ($\Delta t$) and the proper time ($\Delta t_0$) is:
$$\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$$

**3. Rearrange the formula to solve for $\Delta t_0$:**
$$\Delta t_0 = \frac{\Delta t}{\gamma}$$

**4. Perform the calculation:**
$$\Delta t_0 = \frac{20}{1.41}$$
$$\Delta t_0 \approx 14.18439...$$

Rounding to two decimal places, we get:
$$\Delta t_0 \approx 14.18\text{ hours}$$

**Teacher's Tip:** Always ensure you identify which time is the "proper time" ($\Delta t_0$) and which is the "dilated time" ($\Delta t$). The proper time is always the shorter interval when observed from a frame moving relative to the clock. Since the Earth observes the quasar moving at a relativistic speed, the 20 hours observed on Earth is the dilated time, meaning the actual time elapsed in the quasar's frame must be smaller.
