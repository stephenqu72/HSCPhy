### Answer
The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
To determine the correct answer, we must analyze the physics demonstrated by the apparatus shown in the diagram:

1.  **Understanding the Apparatus:** The image displays a **Maltese cross/paddle wheel tube**. This experiment was famously used by J.J. Thomson and others to investigate the properties of cathode rays (which we now know are electrons).
2.  **Evaluating the Evidence:** When the cathode rays strike the vanes of the paddle wheel, the wheel begins to rotate. For an object to be set into motion, a force must be applied. In classical physics, an object gains kinetic energy and moves when it is struck by particles possessing mass and velocity—this combination provides **momentum** ($p = mv$).
3.  **Eliminating Alternatives:**
    *   **A:** While cathode rays do travel in straight lines, this is demonstrated by using a solid object (like a Maltese cross) to cast a shadow on the end of the tube, not by a rotating paddle wheel.
    *   **B:** While cathode rays require a low-pressure environment, the paddle wheel does not specifically prove that they can *only* be produced in a vacuum; it proves the physical nature of the rays themselves.
    *   **D:** If cathode rays were waves, they would reflect off the surface of the paddle or pass through/around it without necessarily imparting momentum to rotate the wheel. The rotation is strong evidence for the **particle nature** of the rays.

By observing the mechanical rotation of the paddle wheel, we conclude that the cathode rays must consist of particles with mass, confirming they possess momentum.
