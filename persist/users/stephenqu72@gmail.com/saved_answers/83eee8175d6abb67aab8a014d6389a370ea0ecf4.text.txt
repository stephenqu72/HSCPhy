The correct answer is **(B) Conservation of Energy**.

### Explanation:

James Chadwick’s discovery of the neutron in 1932 relied on analyzing the collisions between unknown neutral radiation (emitted from beryllium bombarded by alpha particles) and various target materials, such as hydrogen and nitrogen gas.

1.  **The Problem:** Scientists initially hypothesized that the neutral radiation consisted of high-energy gamma rays. However, when they calculated the energy required to eject protons from hydrogen gas using this assumption, the values were inconsistent with the experimental results.
2.  **The Physics:** Chadwick applied the **Law of Conservation of Energy** and the **Law of Conservation of Momentum** to the collision of the neutral particles with the nuclei of the target atoms. 
3.  **The Deduction:** By treating the collision as an elastic scattering event between two masses, he calculated the mass of the neutral particle. He demonstrated that if the radiation were gamma rays, the laws of conservation of energy and momentum would be violated. However, if the radiation consisted of particles with a mass approximately equal to that of a proton, the experimental data fit the conservation laws perfectly.

While "Conservation of Mass-Energy" (Einstein's $E=mc^2$) is a fundamental concept in nuclear physics, Chadwick’s specific experimental proof was based on the classical mechanical conservation of energy and momentum applied to the collision kinematics of particles. Therefore, **(B)** is the most precise answer in the context of this HSC Physics curriculum question.
