### Answer
The correct answer is **A. The mass of an electron.**

### Explanation
To solve this, we need to look at the historical context of J.J. Thomson’s work and Robert Millikan’s experiment:

1.  **Thomson’s contribution:** Before Millikan, J.J. Thomson had already determined the **charge-to-mass ratio** ($q/m$) of the electron using cathode ray tubes. However, because $q$ and $m$ were linked as a ratio, he could not determine the specific value of either variable on its own.
2.  **Millikan’s contribution:** Millikan’s oil drop experiment provided the missing piece of the puzzle: the exact elementary charge ($q$) of the electron, which is approximately $-1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}$.
3.  **The synthesis:** Once physicists knew both the ratio $q/m$ (from Thomson) and the individual charge $q$ (from Millikan), they could algebraically rearrange the ratio to solve for the mass ($m$):
    $$m = \frac{q}{(q/m)}$$

By substituting the known charge into the previously established ratio, scientists were finally able to calculate the absolute mass of an electron.
