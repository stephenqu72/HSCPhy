### Explanation
To understand escape velocity, we must look at the conservation of energy principle. 

An object on the surface of a planet has:
1. **Gravitational Potential Energy ($U$):** Which is negative, defined as $U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$.
2. **Kinetic Energy ($K$):** Defined as $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

The "escape velocity" is defined as the minimum initial velocity required for an object to travel infinitely far away from a massive body (like Earth) and come to rest only at an infinite distance. At an infinite distance ($r = \infty$), the gravitational potential energy is defined as $0$, and if the object just barely "escapes," its final kinetic energy is also $0$.

According to the law of **Conservation of Mechanical Energy**:
$$E_{initial} = E_{final}$$
$$K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f$$
$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \left(-\frac{GMm}{r}\right) = 0 + 0$$
$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{r} = 0$$

Therefore, the total mechanical energy is $0$. Option (D) correctly identifies this physical requirement.

**Why the others are incorrect:**
*   **(A)** describes "orbital velocity," which is the speed needed to maintain a closed circular path around a body, not to escape its gravity entirely.
*   **(B)** is a simplified, colloquial way of thinking about it, but it lacks the scientific precision of (D), which explains *why* the object can fly away forever (the total energy reaches the threshold of zero).
*   **(C)** is a common misconception; escaping the atmosphere is merely a matter of overcoming atmospheric drag and doesn't require reaching the gravitational escape velocity.
