To determine the change in speed required for a satellite to move to a lower orbit, we must use the formula for orbital velocity:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

Where:
- $G$ is the gravitational constant ($6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3 \text{ kg}^{-1} \text{ s}^{-2}$)
- $M$ is the mass of the Earth ($5.972 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$)
- $r$ is the orbital radius ($r = R_E + h$, where $R_E \approx 6371 \text{ km}$)

### Step-by-Step Calculation:

1.  **Calculate the initial radius ($r_1$) and final radius ($r_2$):**
    - $r_1 = 6371 \text{ km} + 850 \text{ km} = 7221 \text{ km} = 7.221 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$
    - $r_2 = 6371 \text{ km} + 650 \text{ km} = 7021 \text{ km} = 7.021 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$

2.  **Calculate the initial velocity ($v_1$):**
    $$v_1 = \sqrt{\frac{(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})}{7.221 \times 10^6}} \approx 7436 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

3.  **Calculate the final velocity ($v_2$):**
    $$v_2 = \sqrt{\frac{(6.674 \times 10^{-11})(5.972 \times 10^{24})}{7.021 \times 10^6}} \approx 7541 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

4.  **Determine the change in velocity ($\Delta v$):**
    $$\Delta v = v_2 - v_1 = 7541 - 7436 = +105 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

**Explanation:** 
According to the orbital velocity formula, the closer a satellite is to Earth (smaller $r$), the higher its orbital velocity must be to maintain a stable circular orbit. When moving from a higher orbit to a lower orbit, the satellite must increase its speed to remain in a stable circular path at that new, lower altitude. Therefore, the satellite must **increase** its speed by approximately **$105 \text{ m s}^{-1}$**.
