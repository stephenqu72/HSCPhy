To find the horizontal distance $s$ traveled by the projectile when it reaches its maximum height, we follow these steps:

### 1. Identify Initial Components
The projectile is launched with velocity $u$ at an angle of $\theta = 45^\circ$. We resolve this into horizontal and vertical components:
- Horizontal velocity: $u_x = u \cos(45^\circ) = u \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$
- Vertical velocity: $u_y = u \sin(45^\circ) = u \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$

### 2. Find Time to Reach Maximum Height ($t$)
At maximum height, the vertical velocity component becomes zero ($v_y = 0$). Using the equation $v_y = u_y - gt$:
$$0 = u \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - gt$$
$$gt = u \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$$
$$t = \frac{u \sqrt{2}}{2g}$$

### 3. Calculate Horizontal Distance ($s$)
Since there is no horizontal acceleration, the horizontal distance is simply $s = u_x \times t$:
$$s = \left( u \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \right) \times \left( \frac{u \sqrt{2}}{2g} \right)$$
$$s = \frac{u^2 \times (\sqrt{2})^2}{4g}$$
$$s = \frac{u^2 \times 2}{4g}$$
$$s = \frac{u^2}{2g}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, the correct answer is **(A)**.

### Explanation Summary
To solve projectile motion problems, always treat horizontal and vertical components independently. The horizontal velocity remains constant throughout the flight, while the vertical velocity changes due to gravity. By finding the time it takes for the vertical component of velocity to reach zero, you determine the time to reach the peak. Multiplying this time by the constant horizontal velocity provides the horizontal displacement at that specific point in time.
