To solve this problem, we apply the principles of Einstein’s Special Relativity regarding relativistic energy.

### Answer
The correct option is **(B)**.

---

### Explanation

**1. Understand the Relationship:**
In special relativity, the total energy ($E_{total}$) of an object is the sum of its rest energy ($E_{rest}$) and its kinetic energy ($E_k$).
$$E_{total} = E_{rest} + E_k$$
This can be rearranged to find the kinetic energy:
$$E_k = E_{total} - E_{rest}$$

**2. Relate Mass to Energy:**
We are given that the relativistic mass ($m_v$) increases by a factor of 22 compared to the rest mass ($m_0$). That is:
$$m_v = 22 m_0$$
Using Einstein's mass-energy equivalence ($E = mc^2$):
*   $E_{total} = m_v c^2 = 22 m_0 c^2$
*   $E_{rest} = m_0 c^2$

**3. Calculate Kinetic Energy:**
Substitute these into our kinetic energy equation:
$$E_k = 22 m_0 c^2 - m_0 c^2 = 21 m_0 c^2$$

**4. Plug in the Constants:**
The rest mass of an electron ($m_0$) is approximately $9.109 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$, and the speed of light ($c$) is $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.
Alternatively, using the rest energy of an electron in Joules:
$$E_{rest} \approx 8.187 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J}$$
Now calculate $E_k$:
$$E_k = 21 \times (8.187 \times 10^{-14} \text{ J})$$
$$E_k \approx 1.719 \times 10^{-12} \text{ J}$$

Rounding to significant figures, we get $1.72 \times 10^{-12} \text{ J}$, which matches option (B).
