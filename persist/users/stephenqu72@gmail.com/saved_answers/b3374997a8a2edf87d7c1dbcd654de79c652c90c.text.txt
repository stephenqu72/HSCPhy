### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
To graph the data, plot **Frequency ($f$)** on the x-axis and **Voltage ($V$)** on the y-axis.

1.  **Axes:** Ensure the axes are labelled with units ($10^{14}$ Hz for x-axis, V for y-axis). Choose a scale that utilizes most of the grid.
2.  **Points:** Plot the coordinate pairs: $(3.5, 1.3), (4.8, 1.7), (5.3, 1.9), (7.0, 2.6)$.
3.  **Line of Best Fit:** Draw a straight line through the origin $(0,0)$—or as close to it as the points allow—ensuring it passes through the center of the distribution of points to balance residuals above and below the line.

#### Part (b)
**Justification:** Using a single data point (like point 1) is a poor method because it is highly susceptible to random errors and measurement uncertainties present in that specific reading. If that single point is an outlier or measured incorrectly, the calculated value for $h$ will be significantly inaccurate.

**Better Method:** The superior approach is to determine the **gradient (slope)** of the line of best fit. 
From the provided equation:
$$V = \left( \frac{h}{q_e} \right) f$$
This matches the linear form $y = mx + c$, where the gradient $m = \frac{h}{q_e}$. 
1.  Calculate the gradient from the graph using two points $(f_1, V_1)$ and $(f_2, V_2)$ that lie on the line of best fit: $m = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta f}$.
2.  Once the gradient is found, calculate Planck's constant using $h = m \times q_e$, where $q_e \approx 1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C.
3.  **Benefit:** This method accounts for all data points collectively, which reduces the impact of random error and provides a much more reliable and representative value for $h$.
