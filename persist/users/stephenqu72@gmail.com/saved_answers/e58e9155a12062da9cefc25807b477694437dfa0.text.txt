To determine the value of mass $M$, we must analyze the forces acting on both masses when the system is in equilibrium.

### 1. Analyze mass $M$
Mass $M$ is hanging vertically and is stationary. Therefore, the forces acting on it must be balanced. The tension $T$ in the string pulling upwards must equal the gravitational force pulling downwards:
$$T = Mg$$

### 2. Analyze mass $m$
Mass $m$ is moving in a horizontal circle of radius $r$ with constant speed $v$. The tension $T$ in the string provides the necessary centripetal force to keep mass $m$ in its circular path:
$$T = F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

### 3. Equate and Solve
Since the string is continuous and friction is negligible, the tension $T$ is the same throughout the string. We can set the two expressions for $T$ equal to each other:
$$Mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

To solve for $M$, divide both sides by $g$:
$$M = \frac{mv^2}{gr}$$

Comparing this result to the given options:
(A) $\frac{mv^2}{r}$ (This is the tension $T$, not mass $M$)
(B) $mv^2rg$ (Incorrect dimensions)
(C) $\frac{mgv^2}{r}$ (Incorrect)
(D) $\frac{mv^2}{gr}$ (Matches our derivation)

**Correct Answer: (D)**

### How to solve this type of problem:
1. **Identify the force of interest:** Recognize that the string tension provides the connection between the vertical equilibrium of mass $M$ and the circular motion of mass $m$.
2. **Apply Newton's Second Law to each object:** For static objects, use $\sum F = 0$. For objects in uniform circular motion, use $\sum F = \frac{mv^2}{r}$ toward the center of the circle.
3. **Link the components:** Use the shared variable (in this case, tension $T$) to equate the equations.
4. **Algebraic isolation:** Rearrange the final equation to solve for the requested variable.
