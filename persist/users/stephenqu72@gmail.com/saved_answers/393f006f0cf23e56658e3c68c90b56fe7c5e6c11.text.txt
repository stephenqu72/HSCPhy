**Answer:**
The correct answer is **C. Leptons**.

---

### Explanation

To solve this question, we must understand the hierarchy of the Standard Model of particle physics:

1.  **Fundamental Particles vs. Composite Particles:** The Standard Model classifies elementary particles into fermions (which include quarks and leptons) and gauge bosons (force carriers). 
2.  **Hadrons (Mesons and Baryons):** These are composite particles made of quarks held together by the strong nuclear force.
    *   **Baryons** (like protons and neutrons) are made of three quarks.
    *   **Mesons** are made of a quark-antiquark pair.
3.  **Leptons:** These are fundamental (elementary) particles that do not participate in the strong nuclear force. The most familiar lepton is the electron. Other leptons include the muon ($\mu^-$) and the tau ($\tau^-$), as well as their corresponding neutrinos.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A. Mesons:** These are composite particles made of quarks, not leptons.
*   **B. Baryons:** These are composite particles made of three quarks, not leptons.
*   **D. Muons are not a specific particle:** This is incorrect because the muon is indeed a specific, elementary particle within the lepton family of the Standard Model.

In the HSC Physics curriculum, it is essential to memorize the "family tree" of particles to quickly identify that while mesons and baryons are composite, muons, electrons, and neutrinos are all fundamental particles classified as **leptons**.
