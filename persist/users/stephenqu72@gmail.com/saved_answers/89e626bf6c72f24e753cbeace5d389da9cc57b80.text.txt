To determine the work function from the experimental data, we use Einstein’s photoelectric equation:
$$E_k = hf - \Phi$$

Where:
*   $E_k$ is the maximum kinetic energy.
*   $h$ is Planck's constant (represented by the gradient of the line).
*   $f$ is the frequency of incident light.
*   $\Phi$ is the work function (the absolute value of the y-intercept).

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Plotting the data:** Plot the given pairs $(f, E_k)$ on the provided axes:
    $(11.2, 0.6), (13.5, 1.3), (15.2, 2.3), (18.6, 3.3), (20.0, 4.2)$.
2.  **Drawing the Line of Best Fit:** Draw a straight line through the points. While there may be slight variations, the line should aim to balance the points above and below.
3.  **Determining the y-intercept:** Extend the line of best fit back to the y-axis (where $f = 0$). Alternatively, use the equation $y = mx + c$:
    *   Pick two points on your line of best fit, for example, $(12, 0.8)$ and $(20, 4.2)$.
    *   Calculate gradient ($m$): $m = \frac{4.2 - 0.8}{20 - 12} = \frac{3.4}{8} = 0.425$
    *   Find intercept ($c$): $4.2 = 0.425(20) + c \rightarrow 4.2 = 8.5 + c \rightarrow c = -4.3$.
4.  **Work Function:** The work function $\Phi$ is the magnitude of the y-intercept. Based on the linear regression of these points, the y-intercept is approximately $-4.3\text{ eV}$. Therefore, the work function $\Phi \approx 4.3\text{ eV}$.

### Explanation:
The photoelectric effect equation follows the linear form $y = mx + c$, where $E_k$ is the y-axis and $f$ is the x-axis. The work function represents the energy required to remove an electron from the surface. When $E_k = 0$, the light frequency equals the threshold frequency ($f_0$), and the energy of the photon equals the work function ($hf_0 = \Phi$). By finding the y-intercept of the plotted graph, we identify the negative value of the work function, representing the "deficit" of energy at zero frequency.
