To determine the correct answer, we must apply the principles of Einstein’s photoelectric effect.

### Answer
The correct answer is **d) Photocurrent magnitude.**

---

### Explanation
To solve this, we need to distinguish between the effects of **light intensity** and **light frequency** (or wavelength):

1.  **Light Intensity and Photocurrent:** Intensity is defined as the number of photons hitting the metal surface per unit area per unit time. According to the particle model of light, each photon interacts with one electron. Therefore, if the intensity of the light increases, the number of photons striking the metal surface increases. This leads to a greater number of electrons being ejected from the metal, which directly results in an increase in the **photocurrent magnitude**.

2.  **Why the others are incorrect:**
    *   **a) Photon Energy:** This is determined by the frequency ($E = hf$). Changing the intensity (brightness) does not change the frequency of the light, so the individual photon energy remains constant.
    *   **b) Photon Wavelength:** Like frequency, wavelength is a property of the light source color/type, not its intensity. Changing the brightness does not change the wavelength of the light.
    *   **c) Electron kinetic energy:** The maximum kinetic energy of the ejected electrons is determined by the equation $E_k = hf - \Phi$, where $\Phi$ is the work function. Since kinetic energy depends only on the frequency ($f$) of the incident light and the work function of the metal, changing the intensity has no effect on the energy of the individual electrons.

In summary, increasing intensity increases the *quantity* of electrons (current), while increasing frequency increases the *quality* (energy) of the electrons.
