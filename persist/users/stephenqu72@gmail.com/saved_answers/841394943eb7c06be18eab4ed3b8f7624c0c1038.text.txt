### Answer
The correct answer is **D**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand how bathroom scales work and what happens during orbital motion:

1.  **How Scales Work:** Bathroom scales do not measure "gravity" directly; they measure the **normal force** ($F_N$) exerted by the surface against the object standing on them. According to Newton's Third Law, this normal force is equal in magnitude to the force the person exerts on the scale (their "apparent weight").
2.  **State of Orbit:** An astronaut orbiting the Earth is in a state of **free-fall**. Both the astronaut and the space capsule are accelerating toward the Earth's center at the same rate due to gravity ($g$). 
3.  **Apparent Weightlessness:** Because the astronaut and the scale are both falling together at the same acceleration, there is no contact force between the astronaut's feet and the surface of the scale. Since the astronaut is not pushing down on the scale, the scale does not push back up. Therefore, the normal force $F_N$ is $0$, resulting in a scale reading of zero.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** Mass is an intrinsic property of matter and does not change due to location or motion.
*   **B:** The Earth's gravitational field is still strong in low-Earth orbit (it is approximately $8.9 \text{ m/s}^2$ at an altitude of $400 \text{ km}$, which is quite close to $9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$ at the surface).
*   **C:** The Moon is too far away to exert a balancing pull on the astronaut that would counteract Earth's gravity in this way; the astronaut's orbital motion is determined almost entirely by the Earth's gravitational field.
