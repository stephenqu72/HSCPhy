### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must apply the fundamental definitions of kinematics in uniform circular motion:

1.  **Instantaneous Velocity ($\vec{v}$):** In circular motion, the velocity vector is always directed tangent to the circular path at the point of interest. Looking at position $P$ on the left side of the circle, and noting the counter-clockwise direction of motion indicated by the arrow on the circle's perimeter, the tangent at $P$ points vertically downward. Therefore, $\vec{v}$ must point straight down.

2.  **Centripetal Acceleration ($\vec{a}$):** An object in uniform circular motion is constantly accelerating because its direction of motion is changing. This acceleration is known as centripetal acceleration, and by definition, it is always directed toward the center of the circular path. Since position $P$ is on the left side of the circle, the center is directly to the right of $P$. Therefore, $\vec{a}$ must point horizontally to the right.

Comparing these conclusions to the provided options:
*   $\vec{v}$ is downward.
*   $\vec{a}$ is to the right.

This configuration is perfectly represented by option **D**.
