### Answer
To show why the de Broglie wavelength of an everyday object is not observable, we use the de Broglie equation:
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{mv}$$
where:
*   $\lambda$ is the wavelength
*   $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$)
*   $m$ is the mass of the object
*   $v$ is the velocity of the object

**Example:**
Consider a cricket ball with a mass of $0.16 \text{ kg}$ moving at a speed of $30 \text{ m/s}$.

Substituting these values into the equation:
$$\lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{0.16 \times 30}$$
$$\lambda \approx 1.38 \times 10^{-34} \text{ m}$$

**Conclusion:**
This wavelength is unimaginably small—far smaller than the diameter of an atomic nucleus. Because this value is so infinitesimally small compared to the physical dimensions of the object (or any measuring device), wave properties like diffraction and interference cannot be observed. Therefore, everyday objects behave purely as particles.
