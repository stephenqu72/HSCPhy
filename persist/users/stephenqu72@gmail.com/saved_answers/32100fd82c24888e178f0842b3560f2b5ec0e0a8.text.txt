### Answer and Explanation

To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of mass-energy equivalence. In nuclear fusion, the "mass defect" ($\Delta m$) is the difference between the mass of the reactants and the mass of the products. Any "missing" mass is converted into energy according to Einstein’s equation, $E = \Delta m c^2$.

**Constants provided/required:**
*   $1 \text{ u} = 931.5 \text{ MeV}/c^2$
*   $1 \text{ u} = 1.66 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$
*   $c = 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$

#### (a) Calculate the mass defect in MeV

1.  **Identify reactants and products:**
    Reactants: $^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{1}\text{H}$
    Products: $^{13}\text{N}$ and $\gamma$ (gamma ray, which has zero mass).
2.  **Calculate mass of reactants:**
    $m_{\text{reactants}} = 12.000 \text{ u} + 1.0078 \text{ u} = 13.0078 \text{ u}$
3.  **Calculate mass defect ($\Delta m$):**
    $\Delta m = m_{\text{reactants}} - m_{\text{products}}$
    $\Delta m = 13.0078 \text{ u} - 13.057 \text{ u} = -0.0492 \text{ u}$
    *(Note: In many physics contexts, "mass defect" refers to the magnitude of the difference. Here, the negative value indicates energy is absorbed or the specific nuclear reaction pathway requires energy/is endothermic based on these provided values, though typically CNO cycle reactions are exothermic. Strictly following the provided numbers:)*
    Mass defect magnitude $= 0.0492 \text{ u}$
4.  **Convert to MeV:**
    $E (\text{MeV}) = 0.0492 \text{ u} \times 931.5 \text{ MeV/u} \approx 45.83 \text{ MeV}$

#### (b) Calculate the energy released in Joules

Using the energy calculated in part (a), we convert MeV to Joules ($1 \text{ MeV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-13} \text{ J}$):

$E = 45.83 \text{ MeV} \times 1.602 \times 10^{-13} \text{ J/MeV}$
$E \approx 7.34 \times 10^{-12} \text{ J}$
