To achieve full marks on this HSC Physics question, you must demonstrate a clear understanding of the historical debate regarding the nature of light by contrasting the wave model (Huygens) and the particle model (Newton).

### Answer
**Huygens’ Wave Theory:**
Huygens proposed that light consists of waves traveling through a medium (the aether). When a wavefront strikes a denser medium at an angle, one part of the wavefront enters the medium and slows down before the other part. Because the portion of the wavefront inside the medium travels at a lower speed ($v_{dense} < v_{rare}$), the wavefront pivots, causing the light ray to bend towards the normal.

**Newton’s Particle Theory:**
Newton proposed that light consists of tiny particles (corpuscles). He argued that when these particles approach a denser medium, they experience an attractive force perpendicular to the interface. This force accelerates the particles in the direction normal to the surface, increasing their velocity component in that direction. Consequently, Newton’s theory incorrectly predicted that light travels faster in a denser medium ($v_{dense} > v_{rare}$) to account for the bending towards the normal.

### How to solve this
1.  **Structure:** Since it is a 4-mark question, aim for 2 marks for each theory. A split structure clearly separated into "Huygens" and "Newton" is best for markers.
2.  **Key Mechanism (Huygens):** Focus on the **wavefronts**. You must mention that different parts of the wavefront enter the medium at different times, causing a change in velocity which results in a change in direction.
3.  **Key Mechanism (Newton):** Focus on the **corpuscular nature** and the **attractive force**. Newton’s model relied on an incorrect assumption: that particles of light were "pulled" into the denser medium.
4.  **Critical Comparison:** The most important part of this answer is the speed prediction. You must highlight that Huygens correctly predicted that light slows down in denser media, whereas Newton incorrectly predicted it speeds up. This is the central piece of evidence that eventually helped the scientific community favor the wave model.
