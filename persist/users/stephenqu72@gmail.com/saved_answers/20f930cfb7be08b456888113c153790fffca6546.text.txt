### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to recall the historical development of the model of light, specifically Isaac Newton’s corpuscular (particle) theory.

1.  **Understand the model:** Newton proposed that light consisted of small particles (corpuscles) that traveled in straight lines.
2.  **Explain Refraction:** When these particles moved from a rarer medium (like air) into a denser medium (like glass or water), Newton theorized that the particles of the denser medium exerted a **gravitational-like force of attraction** on the light particles. 
3.  **Resulting Change:** This force acted on the light particles, causing them to accelerate and change their direction of travel (bending toward the normal). In this model, the change in direction was a direct result of this force accelerating the light particles as they entered the denser material.

*   **Why (B) is incorrect:** While Newton's theory correctly predicted that light would travel faster in a denser medium, he did not define refraction by the act of "slowing down." In fact, his model required the particles to speed up to explain the bending toward the normal.
*   **Why (C) is incorrect:** This is a historical distractor and does not explain the theoretical mechanism of the particle model.
*   **Why (D) is incorrect:** Scattering is a wave-like or particle-collision phenomenon that does not describe the ordered bending of light defined as refraction.

By understanding that Newton attributed the change in the path of light to a force exerted by the particles of the medium, we can identify (A) as the intended mechanism.
