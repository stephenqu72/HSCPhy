To answer this question, we need to apply the physics of DC motors, specifically the relationship between back electromotive force (back EMF), mechanical load, and current.

### Answer
i. **Slower speed:** As the bicycle moves up a hill, the gravitational potential energy increases, resulting in an increased mechanical load (opposing force) on the motor. This load causes the motor to rotate at a lower angular velocity (slower speed).

ii. **Increased current:** A rotating DC motor acts as a generator, producing a back EMF ($\varepsilon_{back}$) that opposes the supply voltage ($V$). The back EMF is proportional to the speed of rotation ($\varepsilon_{back} \propto \omega$). When the motor slows down due to the hill, the back EMF decreases. According to Ohm's law for a motor circuit, $I = \frac{V - \varepsilon_{back}}{R}$, where $R$ is the internal resistance. As $\varepsilon_{back}$ decreases, the numerator ($V - \varepsilon_{back}$) increases, resulting in a higher current ($I$) drawn from the battery.
