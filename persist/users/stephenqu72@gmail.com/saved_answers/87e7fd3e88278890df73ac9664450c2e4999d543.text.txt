To solve this problem, we apply the principles of special relativity, specifically **time dilation**.

### 1. Understanding the physics
The heartbeats of the space traveller are measured in their own **proper time** ($\Delta t_0$), which is $72$ years. From the perspective of an observer on Earth (the stationary frame), the traveller is moving at a velocity $v = 0.600c$. Due to time dilation, the time experienced by the traveller appears to pass more slowly to the stationary observer.

The relationship between the time measured in the stationary frame ($\Delta t$) and the proper time ($\Delta t_0$) is given by:
$$\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0$$
where $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor:
$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

### 2. Calculation
**Step 1: Calculate the Lorentz factor ($\gamma$)**
Given $v = 0.600c$:
$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (0.600)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.36}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.64}} = \frac{1}{0.8} = 1.25$$

**Step 2: Determine the total heartbeats**
The heart rate (beats per year) is constant for the traveller in their own frame. Even though the stationary observer perceives the traveller's life to last longer ($72 \times 1.25 = 90$ years), the traveller *only experiences* $72$ years of biological time. 

Because the heartbeats are a biological process occurring within the traveller's own reference frame, the total number of heartbeats depends strictly on the **proper time** experienced by the traveller. Since the traveller is born and dies on the spaceship, they experience their full lifespan of 72 years.
