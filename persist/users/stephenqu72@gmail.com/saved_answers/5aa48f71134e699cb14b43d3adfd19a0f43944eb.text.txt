To determine the correct statement regarding the net force acting on the ball, we must analyze the forces involved while the ball is in contact with the smooth, vertical wall.

### Analysis of Forces
1.  **Gravity ($F_g$):** This force always acts vertically downwards with a constant magnitude of $mg$.
2.  **Normal Force ($N$):** Because the wall is "smooth" (frictionless), the wall can only exert a force **perpendicular** to its surface. Since the wall is vertical and semi-circular, the normal force acts in the horizontal plane, directed towards the center of curvature of the wall.
3.  **Net Force ($F_{net}$):** According to Newton's Second Law, the net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on the object:
    $$\vec{F}_{net} = \vec{F}_g + \vec{N}$$
    Since $\vec{F}_g$ is constant and vertical, and $\vec{N}$ is horizontal and changes direction as the ball moves along the curved path, the total net force is a vector sum of a constant vertical component and a varying horizontal component.

### Evaluating the Options
*   **A: The magnitude of the net force remains constant.** Incorrect. As the ball moves, the normal force $\vec{N}$ changes direction. Furthermore, the speed of the ball changes as it gains height and loses it, which affects the required centripetal force provided by $\vec{N}$. Thus, $F_{net} = \sqrt{(mg)^2 + N^2}$ changes over time.
*   **B: The direction of the net force is vertically downwards.** Incorrect. While gravity is vertical, the normal force $\vec{N}$ provides a horizontal component while the ball is in contact with the wall.
*   **C: The direction of the net force is perpendicular to the wall.** Incorrect. The net force is the vector sum of gravity (downwards) and the normal force (perpendicular to the wall). This resultant force vector will point downwards and inwards, meaning it is not strictly perpendicular to the wall.
*   **D: The magnitude of the net force reaches a minimum when the ball is at its highest point.** Correct. At the highest point of the trajectory, the vertical velocity component is zero. Throughout the motion, the ball's speed is changing. The normal force $N$ is required to provide the centripetal acceleration $N = \frac{mv^2}{r}$. At the highest point, the vertical speed is zero, and the kinetic energy (and thus velocity $v$) is at its minimum for the trajectory. Since both $mg$ and $N$ (proportional to $v^2$) are at their relative minimums, the total magnitude of the net force is at its minimum.

### Conclusion
The correct statement is **D**.
