To explain the variations in the current, we must apply the principles of electromagnetism, specifically **back electromotive force (back EMF)** and the behavior of DC motors.

### Answer
The variations in the current occur due to changes in the back EMF generated by the rotating motor:

1.  **Initial Current Spike ($5.5\text{ A}$):** When the drill is first switched on, the rotor is stationary. At this moment, the back EMF is zero. According to Ohm's Law ($V = IR$), the current is limited only by the low resistance of the motor coils, resulting in a high "inrush" current.
2.  **Steady State ($0.9\text{ A}$):** As the motor gains speed, the rotating coils cut through the magnetic field, inducing a back EMF that opposes the supply voltage. As the motor reaches its free-running speed, the back EMF approaches the supply voltage, significantly reducing the effective voltage across the coils and causing the current to drop to a low, steady value.
3.  **Drilling Load ($2.1\text{ A}$):** When the drill is used to drill a hole, the physical load slows down the rotation of the motor. This reduction in speed decreases the back EMF. Since the back EMF opposes the supply voltage, a lower back EMF allows more current to flow through the motor to provide the additional torque required to overcome the mechanical resistance of the material being drilled.

### How to solve this question
When answering "explain" questions in HSC Physics regarding motors:

*   **Link Physics Principles to the Scenario:** Always mention back EMF. A common pitfall is to discuss torque without explaining *why* the current changes (it is the change in back EMF that dictates the change in current).
*   **Structure your answer chronologically:** Break your response into the three distinct phases identified in the prompt (Start-up, Steady State, Loaded).
*   **Use the formula:** Keep the concept of $I = \frac{V - \varepsilon}{R}$ in mind (where $V$ is supply voltage, $\varepsilon$ is back EMF, and $R$ is resistance). Use this to explain that as the motor slows down, $\varepsilon$ decreases, which forces $I$ to increase.
