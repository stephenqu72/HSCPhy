To solve this problem, we must analyze the relationship between terminal velocity and mass by interpreting the provided linear graph and applying the principles of dynamics.

### Part (a)
**Answer:** The graph shows a linear relationship between the square of the terminal velocity ($v^2$) and the mass ($m$). The equation for a straight line is $y = mx + c$.
1. Slope ($k$): Using points $(100, 2.2)$ and $(300, 6.6)$, $k = \frac{6.6 - 2.2}{300 - 100} = \frac{4.4}{200} = 0.022$.
2. The y-intercept is approximately $0$.
Thus, $v^2 \propto m$, which implies $v \propto \sqrt{m}$.

**Explanation:** In physics, a straight-line graph through the origin indicates that the variable on the y-axis is directly proportional to the variable on the x-axis. Since $v^2$ is proportional to $m$, taking the square root of both sides shows that the terminal velocity is proportional to the square root of the mass.

### Part (b)
**Answer:** At terminal velocity, acceleration $a = 0$. Therefore, the net force is zero: $F_{net} = F_{weight} - F_{air} = 0$.
This means $F_{air} = F_{weight} = mg$.
Since $v^2 \propto m$ from part (a), we can write $m = kv^2$ (where $k$ is a constant).
Substituting this into the force equation: $F_{air} = (kv^2)g = (kg)v^2$.

**Explanation:** By applying Newton’s Second Law ($\Sigma F = ma$), we recognize that at terminal velocity, weight must equal air resistance. Since the graph demonstrates that mass is proportional to the square of velocity, the drag force must scale with $v^2$ to maintain equilibrium for varying masses.

### Part (c)
