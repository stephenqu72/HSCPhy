To solve this problem, we use the transformer equation, which relates the input (primary) voltage and number of turns to the output (secondary) voltage and number of turns:

$$\frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{N_p}{N_s}$$

Where:
- $V_p$ is the primary voltage
- $V_s$ is the secondary (output) voltage
- $N_p$ is the number of turns on the primary coil
- $N_s$ is the number of turns on the secondary coil

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1. **Analyze the current setup:** We have an input $V_p = 240\text{ V}$ and an output $V_s = 6\text{ V}$. Substituting into the formula:
   $$\frac{240}{6} = \frac{N_p}{N_s} \implies \frac{N_p}{N_s} = 40$$
   This means the primary coil has 40 times more turns than the secondary coil.

2. **Determine the desired outcome:** We want to increase the output voltage $V_s$ from $6\text{ V}$ to $12\text{ V}$ while keeping $V_p$ at $240\text{ V}$.
   $$\frac{240}{12} = \frac{N_p}{N_s} \implies \frac{N_p}{N_s} = 20$$

3. **Identify the necessary change:** To change the ratio $\frac{N_p}{N_s}$ from $40$ to $20$, we have two choices:
   - Decrease $N_p$ (the number of turns on the primary coil).
   - Increase $N_s$ (the number of turns on the secondary coil).

4. **Evaluate the options:** Looking at the choices provided:
   - Option A: Increasing $N_p$ would decrease the output voltage, which is the opposite of what we want.
   - Option B: Decreasing $N_p$ will decrease the primary-to-secondary ratio, thereby increasing the output voltage.
   - Options C and D: Resistance does not change the voltage ratio of an ideal transformer.

Therefore, the correct answer is **B**.
