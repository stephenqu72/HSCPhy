To answer this question, we must understand the physical meaning of the constant $k$ in Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion.

### Answer
The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
Kepler’s Third Law is given by the formula:
$$T^2 = kR^3$$

To derive the value of the constant $k$, we equate the centripetal force required to keep a planet in orbit to the gravitational force exerted by the star:
$$\frac{mv^2}{R} = \frac{GMm}{R^2}$$
Substituting the orbital velocity $v = \frac{2\pi R}{T}$ into the equation:
$$\frac{m (\frac{2\pi R}{T})^2}{R} = \frac{GMm}{R^2}$$
$$\frac{4\pi^2 R}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{R^2}$$
Rearranging for $T^2$:
$$T^2 = \left( \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} \right) R^3$$

By comparing this to $T^2 = kR^3$, we can see that:
$$k = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$$

Where:
- $G$ is the universal gravitational constant.
- $M$ is the **mass of the central star** being orbited.

Because $k$ depends entirely on the mass of the central star ($M$), the value of $k$ will only change if the object being orbited is changed. 

*   **Options A and B:** These describe changes to the planet's orbit ($T$ or $R$). Kepler's Law dictates that $T^2$ and $R^3$ change proportionally to keep the ratio $k$ constant for a given star.
*   **Option C:** If the planet orbits a **different star** with a different mass ($M$), then $k$ must change.
*   **Option D:** The mass of the orbiting planet ($m$) cancels out during the derivation of the formula. Therefore, the mass of the planet does not affect the value of $k$.
