### Sample Answer
Historically, SI units were based on physical artifacts, such as the International Prototype of the Kilogram (a platinum-iridium cylinder). However, these are subject to degradation, contamination, and instability over time. Scientific progress and the development of quantum and relativistic theories have allowed us to redefine these units based on universal physical constants, which are invariant across space and time.

Key examples include:
1.  **The Second:** Formerly defined by Earth’s rotation, it is now defined by the transition frequency of the cesium-133 atom ($9,192,631,770$ cycles), grounding time in quantum mechanics.
2.  **The Meter:** Formerly defined by a metal bar, it is now defined by the distance light travels in a vacuum in $1/299,792,458$ of a second, linking it to the universal constant $c$ (the speed of light).
3.  **The Kilogram:** Previously tied to a physical object, it is now defined by fixing the numerical value of the Planck constant ($h$) to $6.62607015 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$, derived from the Kibble balance experiment.
4.  **The Ampere:** Now defined by fixing the elementary charge ($e$) to $1.602176634 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$, ensuring the unit of current is based on the fundamental charge of an electron rather than a theoretical force between wires.
