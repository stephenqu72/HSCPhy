To determine the net force acting on conductor $Q$, we must calculate the individual forces exerted by conductors $P$ and $R$ and then sum them vectorially.

### 1. The Physics Principle
The force per unit length between two parallel current-carrying wires is given by:
$$\frac{F}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi r}$$
Given $k = \frac{\mu_0}{2\pi}$, the formula simplifies to:
$$F = \frac{k I_1 I_2 L}{r}$$

**Rules for direction:**
*   Currents in the **same direction** attract.
*   Currents in **opposite directions** repel.

### 2. Force from $P$ on $Q$ ($F_{PQ}$)
*   Current in $P = 2I$, Current in $Q = I$.
*   Distance = $2d$.
*   Since both currents are directed upwards, the force is **attractive** (pulling $Q$ toward $P$, which is **to the left**).
$$F_{PQ} = \frac{k(2I)(I)L}{2d} = \frac{2k I^2 L}{2d} = \frac{k I^2 L}{d}$$

### 3. Force from $R$ on $Q$ ($F_{RQ}$)
*   Current in $R = I$ (downwards), Current in $Q = I$ (upwards).
*   Distance = $d$.
*   Since currents are in opposite directions, the force is **repulsive** (pushing $Q$ away from $R$, which is **to the left**).
$$F_{RQ} = \frac{k(I)(I)L}{d} = \frac{k I^2 L}{d}$$

### 4. Net Force
Since both forces act to the left, we add their magnitudes:
$$F_{net} = F_{PQ} + F_{RQ}$$
$$F_{net} = \frac{k I^2 L}{d} + \frac{k I^2 L}{d} = \frac{2k I^2 L}{d}$$
The direction is to the left.

Comparing this to the given options, the correct answer is **(A)**.
