Hello! As an HSC Physics teacher, I'm here to walk you through this orbital mechanics problem. These are classic questions that test your ability to convert units correctly and use the physics of circular motion.

---

### Part (a) Calculate the angular speed ($\omega$)
**Formula:** $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$

1. **Convert time to SI units:** $T = 97 \text{ minutes} \times 60 \text{ seconds/minute} = 5820 \text{ seconds}$.
2. **Calculate:**
   $$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{5820} \approx 1.08 \times 10^{-3} \text{ rad s}^{-1}$$

---

### Part (b)(i) Calculate the radius ($r$)
To solve this, we use the relationship between the orbital velocity of a satellite and its orbital radius: $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$. Since $v = \omega r$, we can substitute to get $\omega = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r^3}}$.
*(Note: Use $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ Nm}^2\text{kg}^{-2}$ and $M_{\text{Earth}} = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$)*

1. **Rearrange for $r$:** $r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{GM}{\omega^2}}$
2. **Calculate:**
   $$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}{(1.08 \times 10^{-3})^2}}$$
   $$r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{3.98 \times 10^{14}}{1.166 \times 10^{-6}}} \approx \sqrt[3]{3.41 \times 10^{20}} \approx 6.99 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$$

---

### Part (b)(ii) Calculate the centripetal force ($F_c$)
**Formula:** $F_c = mr\omega^2$

1. **Substitute known values:**
   $$F_c = (1.1 \times 10^4 \text{ kg}) \times (6.99 \times 10^6 \text{ m}) \times (1.08 \times 10^{-3} \text{ rad s}^{-1})^2$$
   $$F_c = (7.689 \times 10^{10}) \times (1.166 \times 10^{-6})$$
   $$F_c \approx 8.97 \times 10^4 \text{ N}$$

*(Alternatively, you could use $F_c = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$, which yields the same result.)*

---

### How to solve these problems:
1. **Always use SI units:** The most common mistake is forgetting to convert minutes to seconds.
2. **Keep precision:** Keep intermediate calculations in your calculator memory to avoid rounding errors until the final step.
3. **Check your logic:** Does your radius make sense? The Earth's radius is $\approx 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$, so a Hubble orbit at $\approx 7.0 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$ is physically realistic.
