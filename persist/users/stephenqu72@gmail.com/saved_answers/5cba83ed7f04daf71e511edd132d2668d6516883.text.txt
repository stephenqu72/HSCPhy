### Answer
The life cycle of a star is fundamentally determined by its initial mass, which dictates its position on the Main Sequence.

1.  **Low to Medium Mass Stars (up to ~8 $M_{\odot}$):**
    These stars start in the lower-right to middle section of the Main Sequence. Upon exhausting their core hydrogen, they evolve into Red Giants. They eventually shed their outer layers to form a Planetary Nebula. The remaining core contracts to become a dense White Dwarf, which eventually cools into a Black Dwarf.

2.  **High Mass Stars (> 8 $M_{\odot}$):**
    These stars begin on the upper-left of the Main Sequence (hot, luminous, blue stars). Due to their high mass, they consume their fuel rapidly. They expand into Red Supergiants and undergo successive fusion stages until iron is formed in the core. This leads to a catastrophic Type II Supernova explosion.

3.  **Final Remnants of High Mass Stars:**
    *   If the remaining core is between approximately 1.4 $M_{\odot}$ and 3 $M_{\odot}$ (the Chandrasekhar limit), it collapses into an incredibly dense Neutron Star (or Pulsar).
    *   If the remaining core exceeds ~3 $M_{\odot}$ (the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit), gravity overcomes all pressure, resulting in a Black Hole, from which not even light can escape.
