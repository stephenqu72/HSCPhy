### Explanation

1.  **Understanding Binding Energy and Mass Defect:** 
    The binding energy ($BE$) of a nucleus is the energy required to disassemble a nucleus into its constituent protons and neutrons. Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, given by $E = mc^2$, links this energy to the "missing mass" known as the mass defect ($\Delta m$):
    $$BE = \Delta m \cdot c^2$$
    Where:
    - $BE$ is the binding energy.
    - $\Delta m$ is the mass defect (the difference between the mass of the individual nucleons and the actual mass of the nucleus).
    - $c$ is the speed of light.

2.  **Evaluating Options C and D:**
    Since $BE$ and $\Delta m$ are directly proportional to each other ($BE \propto \Delta m$), if Nucleus $X$ has a greater binding energy than Nucleus $Y$, it must mathematically have a greater mass defect. This makes **C** a correct statement.

3.  **Why A and B are incorrect:**
    Stability is determined by the **binding energy per nucleon** ($BE/A$), not the total binding energy of the nucleus. A large nucleus (like Uranium) may have a very high total binding energy because it has many nucleons, but it is less stable than a medium-sized nucleus (like Iron) which has a higher binding energy *per* nucleon. Because we do not know the number of nucleons in $X$ and $Y$, we cannot determine which is more stable.
