To answer this question effectively for 7 marks, you need to provide a clear, logical experimental procedure that demonstrates an understanding of Malus' Law, which states:
$$I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$$
where $I$ is the transmitted intensity, $I_0$ is the incident intensity, and $\theta$ is the angle between the transmission axes of the two polarisers.

### Experimental Method

1.  **Equipment:** Use a light source (laser or LED), two polarising filters (a polariser and an analyser), a light sensor (connected to a data logger), and a rotating mount with a degree scale.
2.  **Setup:** Place the light source, the first polariser (fixed), the second polariser (analyser on a rotating mount), and the light sensor in a straight line on an optical bench.
3.  **Calibration:** Align the polarisers so that they are parallel ($\theta = 0^\circ$). Ensure the light sensor is calibrated to read intensity in arbitrary units or Watts per square meter.
4.  **Procedure:** 
    *   Rotate the analyser through $180^\circ$ in steps (e.g., every $10^\circ$).
    *   At each increment, record the intensity $I$ displayed by the light sensor.
5.  **Data Analysis:** 
    *   Calculate $\cos^2(\theta)$ for each angle $\theta$.
    *   Plot a graph of $I$ (y-axis) versus $\cos^2(\theta)$ (x-axis).
6.  **Verification:** If Malus' Law holds, the graph should result in a straight line passing through the origin. The gradient of this line represents $I_0$.
7.  **Reliability:** Repeat the measurements three times to calculate an average for each angle and reduce random error. Ensure the ambient light is minimized by performing the experiment in a darkened room or using a light shield.
