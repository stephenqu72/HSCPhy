### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to recall the results of the **Rutherford gold foil experiment** (also known as the Geiger-Marsden experiment). 

Rutherford, Geiger, and Marsden fired alpha particles ($\alpha$ particles, which are helium nuclei with a $2+$ charge) at a very thin sheet of gold foil. Based on the "plum pudding model" of the atom accepted at the time, they expected the particles to pass straight through with only minor deflections. However, their observations were revolutionary:

1.  **Most $\alpha$ particles passed straight through the foil:** This indicated that the atom is mostly empty space.
2.  **A small fraction of $\alpha$ particles were deflected at large angles:** This indicated that the positive charge and most of the mass of the atom are concentrated in a very small, dense region called the **nucleus**.
3.  **A very small number of particles bounced almost directly backward:** This confirmed that the nucleus is highly concentrated and positively charged, causing strong electrostatic repulsion of the approaching $\alpha$ particles.

Evaluating the options:
*   **A** is incorrect because while most passed through, some *were* significantly deflected.
*   **B** is incorrect because only a small minority were deflected at large angles, not all of them.
*   **C** is the correct description of the experimental data, which leads to the modern nuclear model of the atom.
*   **D** is incorrect as polarization is a property of transverse waves (like light), not massive particles like alpha particles.
