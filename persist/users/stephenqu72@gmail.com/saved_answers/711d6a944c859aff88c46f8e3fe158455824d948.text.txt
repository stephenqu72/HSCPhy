### Explanation:
To determine the quark composition of subatomic particles, we look at their electric charges. Quarks carry fractional charges:
*   The **up (u) quark** has a charge of $+\frac{2}{3}e$.
*   The **down (d) quark** has a charge of $-\frac{1}{3}e$.

A **neutron** is electrically neutral, meaning its total charge must sum to $0$. We can verify the composition of each option by adding the charges:

*   **Option A (up-up-down):** 
    $+\frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = +\frac{3}{3} = +1$. This is the composition of a **proton**.
*   **Option B (up-down-down):** 
    $+\frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = 0$. This confirms the neutron is neutral.
*   **Option C (up-up-up):** 
    $+\frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} = +2$. This is not a standard baryon found in atomic nuclei.
*   **Option D (down-down-down):** 
    $-\frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = -1$. This corresponds to a $\Delta^-$ baryon.

Therefore, the neutron consists of one up quark and two down quarks.
