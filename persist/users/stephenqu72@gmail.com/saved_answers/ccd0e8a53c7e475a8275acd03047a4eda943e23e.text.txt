To solve these problems, we must treat the projectile motion as two independent components: horizontal motion (constant velocity) and vertical motion (constant acceleration due to gravity, $g \approx 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$ or $10\text{ m s}^{-2}$).

### Question 9
**Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
For projectile motion, the horizontal distance $d$ is covered at a constant horizontal velocity $v_x$. The formula for distance is:
$$d = v_x \times t$$
Given that Lucy is running horizontally at $6\text{ m s}^{-1}$, the horizontal velocity $v_x$ is $6\text{ m s}^{-1}$. Substituting this into the formula gives:
$$d = 6t$$
Therefore, the expression for the horizontal distance $d$ is $6t$.

### Question 10
**Answer: B**

**Explanation:**
To find the time $t$ to reach the water, we look at the vertical motion. Lucy starts with an initial vertical velocity of $0\text{ m s}^{-1}$ (since she runs horizontally off the platform) and falls a vertical displacement of $h = 8.0\text{ m}$. Using the equation of motion:
$$h = u_y t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
Since $u_y = 0$, this simplifies to:
$$h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
Using $g \approx 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$:
$$8.0 = 0.5 \times 9.8 \times t^2$$
$$8.0 = 4.9 \times t^2$$
$$t^2 = \frac{8.0}{4.9} \approx 1.633$$
$$t = \sqrt{1.633} \approx 1.28\text{ s}$$
Rounding to the nearest option provided, $1.3\text{ s}$ is the closest answer. (Note: If using $g = 10\text{ m s}^{-2}$, $t^2 = 1.6$, resulting in $t \approx 1.26\text{ s}$, which also rounds to $1.3\text{ s}$).
