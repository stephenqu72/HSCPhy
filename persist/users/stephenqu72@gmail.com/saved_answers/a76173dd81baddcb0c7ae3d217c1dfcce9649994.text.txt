### Part a: Show that the gravitational field strength is $0.48\text{ N kg}^{-1}$

**Solution:**
The gravitational field strength $g$ is given by $g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$. Since $GM$ is constant for a planet, we can use the ratio method based on the inverse square law:
$$\frac{g_1}{g_2} = \frac{r_2^2}{r_1^2}$$
Given:
- $r_1 (\text{surface}) = 3390\text{ km} = 3.39 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
- $r_2 (\text{orbit}) = 3390\text{ km} + 6000\text{ km} = 9390\text{ km} = 9.39 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
- $g_1 = 3.72\text{ N kg}^{-1}$

$$g_2 = g_1 \times \left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)^2 = 3.72 \times \left(\frac{3390}{9390}\right)^2 \approx 3.72 \times 0.1303 \approx 0.4847\text{ N kg}^{-1}$$
Rounding to two significant figures, we get $0.48\text{ N kg}^{-1}$.

### Part b: Calculate the orbital period of Phobos

**Solution:**
We use the formula for orbital velocity $v$ and equate it to $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$. Since $g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$, we know $GM = g \cdot r^2$.
For a circular orbit, $\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$, so $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$.
Substituting $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$:
$$\frac{2\pi r}{T} = \sqrt{\frac{gr^2}{r}} = \sqrt{gr} \implies T = \frac{2\pi r}{\sqrt{gr}}$$
Using $r = 9.39 \times 10^6\text{ m}$ and $g = 0.4847\text{ m/s}^2$:
$$T = \frac{2 \pi (9.39 \times 10^6)}{\sqrt{0.4847 \times 9.39 \times 10^6}} = \frac{5.899 \times 10^7}{2132.3} \approx 27665\text{ seconds}$$
Rounding to significant figures, $T \approx 2.8 \times 10^4\text{ s}$.

### Part c: Explain the change in orbital period

**Solution:**
The orbital period will **become shorter**.
**Reasoning:** According to Kepler's Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$), the orbital period is directly related to the orbital radius. As the orbital radius $r$ decreases, the required orbital period $T$ must also decrease. Physically, to maintain a circular orbit at a lower altitude where the gravitational field is stronger, the satellite must travel at a higher orbital velocity. A shorter path length (smaller circumference) combined with a higher orbital velocity results in a shorter orbital period.
