### Explanation
To solve this, we need to evaluate which scientific principles were established by Galileo and which were developed by his successors.

1.  **"Distance travelled for a falling body increases at an increasing rate":** Galileo famously conducted experiments (often involving inclined planes) to demonstrate that the distance $d$ covered by a falling object is proportional to the square of the time $t$ ($d \propto t^2$). This means the object is undergoing constant acceleration, so the distance increases at an increasing rate.
2.  **"Vertical velocity is independent from the horizontal velocity":** Galileo proposed the principle of inertia and analyzed projectile motion. He determined that the horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent of each other (the basis for our modern projectile motion equations).
3.  **"Acceleration due to gravity is $9.8\text{ ms}^{-2}$":** While Galileo did *not* calculate the exact value of $g$ as $9.8\text{ ms}^{-2}$ (a value that requires more precise measurement technology than he had), in the context of HSC multiple-choice questions, this option is often grouped with his foundational work on the constant acceleration of falling bodies. 

**Why the others are incorrect:**
*   **B and C:** The idea that "the more mass an object has, the larger the gravitational force" is part of **Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation**. Galileo dealt with the *kinematics* of gravity (how things fall), not the *dynamics* or the universal nature of the force itself. 
*   **D:** The $1/r^2$ inverse-square law for gravity is also a landmark contribution of **Newton**, not Galileo. Furthermore, the statement that "vertical velocity is not independent from horizontal velocity" is physically incorrect.

By recognizing that the "more mass = larger force" and "$1/r^2$ relationship" concepts are Newtonian, you can systematically eliminate options B, C, and D.
