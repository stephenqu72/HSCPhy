To solve this problem, we use **Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion**, which relates the orbital period ($T$) of a satellite to its orbital radius ($r$):

$$T^2 \propto r^3$$

Alternatively, written as an equation:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the variables:**
    *   For the Rosetta Probe: Period $T_1 = 5T$, Radius $= r_1$.
    *   For Philae: Period $T_2 = T$, Radius $= r_2$.
    *   Note: The mass of the satellite ($m$ or $1/2m$) is irrelevant to the orbital period, as it cancels out in the derivation of Kepler's Third Law.

2.  **Set up the ratio:**
    Since both orbit the same comet (mass $M$ is constant), we can set up a ratio based on Kepler's Third Law:
    $$\frac{r_1^3}{T_1^2} = \frac{r_2^3}{T_2^2}$$
    $$\frac{r_1^3}{r_2^3} = \frac{T_1^2}{T_2^2}$$

3.  **Substitute the values:**
    $$\left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)^3 = \frac{(5T)^2}{T^2}$$
    $$\left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)^3 = \frac{25T^2}{T^2}$$
    $$\left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)^3 = 25$$

4.  **Solve for the ratio:**
    $$\frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt[3]{25}$$

    Calculating the value:
    *   $2^3 = 8$
    *   $3^3 = 27$
    Since $25$ is very close to $27$, the cube root of $25$ is approximately $2.92$. Looking at the provided options, the ratio is closest to $3:1$.

**Answer:**
The correct option is **c) 3:1**.
