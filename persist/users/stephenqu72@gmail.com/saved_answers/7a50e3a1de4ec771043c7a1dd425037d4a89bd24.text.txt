To answer this question effectively, you must compare the predictions of J.J. Thomson’s "plum pudding" model against the empirical evidence observed by Geiger and Marsden.

### Answer
The results of the Geiger-Marsden experiment falsified J.J. Thomson’s Model 1 and provided evidence for Rutherford’s Model 2 by demonstrating that the positive charge and mass of an atom are concentrated in a tiny, dense central nucleus rather than being diffused throughout the atom.

### Explanation
To solve this, you need to structure your response by contrasting the two models based on the observed data:

1.  **Thomson’s Model (The "Plum Pudding" Model):** Thomson proposed that an atom consisted of a diffuse sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded throughout. If this were true, positively charged alpha particles would only experience very weak electrostatic forces as they passed through the atom. Consequently, they should have passed through the gold foil with, at most, very minor deflections.
2.  **The Observation:** Geiger and Marsden observed that while most alpha particles passed straight through the foil, a significant number were deflected at large angles, and a very small fraction (approximately 1 in 8,000) were deflected backwards (rebounded).
3.  **The Conclusion (Model 2):** These large-angle deflections could only occur if the alpha particles encountered a very strong, localized electrostatic repulsive force. This indicated that the entire positive charge and almost all the mass of the atom must be concentrated in a tiny, dense core—the **nucleus**. 
4.  **Scientific Shift:** The experiment forced scientists to reject the "pudding" model in favor of the nuclear model, where electrons orbit a small, positively charged nucleus with a large volume of empty space in between.
