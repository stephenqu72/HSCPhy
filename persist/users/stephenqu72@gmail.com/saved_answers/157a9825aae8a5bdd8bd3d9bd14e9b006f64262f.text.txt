### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
To solve for the speed on a banked curve without friction (icy conditions), we use the physics of circular motion. The only forces acting on the car are the normal force ($N$) and gravity ($mg$). The horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force ($F_c$).

**Given:**
*   Radius $r = 20\text{ m}$
*   Angle $\theta = 15^\circ$
*   Gravity $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$

**Physics derivation:**
1.  Vertical equilibrium: $N\cos\theta = mg$
2.  Centripetal force: $N\sin\theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$
3.  Dividing the equations: $\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$
4.  Rearranging for speed: $v = \sqrt{rg\tan\theta}$

**Calculation:**
$$v = \sqrt{20 \times 9.8 \times \tan(15^\circ)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{196 \times 0.2679}$$
$$v = \sqrt{52.51} \approx 7.25\text{ m/s}$$

The car must travel at approximately **$7.25\text{ m/s}$** to navigate the curve safely.

---

#### Part (b)
In "normal" conditions, the surface is not icy, allowing for the inclusion of **static friction** ($f$). 

**Explanation:**
*   **Icy conditions:** The centripetal force is provided solely by the horizontal component of the normal force ($N\sin\theta$). The car is limited to one specific "design speed."
*   **Normal conditions:** Friction acts parallel to the road surface. At speeds higher than the design speed, friction acts down the slope to provide additional centripetal force.
*   **Vector Diagram:** In your diagram, draw the weight vector ($mg$ downwards), the Normal force ($N$ perpendicular to the road), and the Friction force ($f$ pointing down the slope). The vector sum of $N\sin\theta + f\cos\theta$ points toward the center of the curve. Because the car can now utilize the maximum static friction force, the resultant net force is significantly larger, allowing for a higher centripetal acceleration ($a = v^2/r$) and thus a higher maximum velocity.
