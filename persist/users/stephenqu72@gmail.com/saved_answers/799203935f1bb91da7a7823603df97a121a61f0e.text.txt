To determine which change makes the results more **reliable**, we must look at how we can reduce random error in this specific practical investigation.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(D)**.

### Explanation
In physics experiments, **reliability** refers to the consistency of your results—specifically, whether repeating the experiment yields the same findings. 

1.  **Analyzing (D):** Timing a single swing with a stopwatch introduces significant **reaction time error**. Because a human's reaction time is roughly $0.1$ to $0.2$ seconds, if the period of the pendulum is, for example, $2.0$ seconds, a $0.2$s error represents a $10\%$ uncertainty. By timing, for example, $10$ oscillations and dividing the total time by $10$, you distribute that single reaction time error over the $10$ swings, reducing the uncertainty in the period of a single swing by a factor of $10$. This significantly improves the reliability of the measurement.

2.  **Evaluating other options:**
    *   **(A)** Reducing the string length does not inherently make the result more reliable; it merely changes the period. 
    *   **(B)** Keeping the amplitude small is required for the simple harmonic motion approximation ($T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$) to be valid (it improves **accuracy/validity**), but it does not specifically address the human error associated with timing individual oscillations.
    *   **(C)** Using a lighter thread reduces the mass of the string, which is a good practice to ensure the assumption of a "massless string" holds (improving **validity**), but it does not address the precision of the timing measurement itself as effectively as option (D).

Therefore, timing multiple swings is the standard method for reducing random error caused by human reaction time, thereby increasing the reliability of the data set.
