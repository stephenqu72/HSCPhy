### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the scope and the failures of the Bohr model of the atom:

*   **Bohr’s Model context:** Bohr developed his model specifically to explain the stability of the hydrogen atom and the observed emission line spectra of hydrogen. It successfully utilized quantized energy levels to predict these specific wavelengths.
*   **Why (A) is incorrect:** The Bohr model explicitly includes a central, positively charged nucleus. In fact, it assumes the electron orbits this nucleus in circular paths.
*   **Why (B) is incorrect:** The Bohr model is very successful at explaining both emission and absorption spectra for hydrogen, as both processes involve the same energy transitions between the same discrete energy levels (e.g., $E = hf$).
*   **Why (C) is incorrect:** Bohr’s model explains all transitions between defined energy levels (Rydberg states) for hydrogen, not just the first few.
*   **Why (D) is correct:** This is a classic limitation. The Bohr model relies on the assumption of a single electron interacting with a nucleus. When applied to atoms with more than one electron (multi-electron atoms), the model fails to account for the **electron-electron repulsions** and the complexity of the electric fields created by those electrons. Consequently, it cannot accurately predict the spectral lines for any element other than hydrogen (or hydrogen-like ions with one electron, such as $He^+$).

Therefore, the inability to accurately model multi-electron systems is a well-documented failure of the Bohr theory, which eventually led to the development of the more robust Quantum Mechanical (Schrödinger) model.
