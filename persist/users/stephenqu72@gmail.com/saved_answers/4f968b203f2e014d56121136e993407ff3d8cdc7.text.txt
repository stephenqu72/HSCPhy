To solve this problem, we must apply **Faraday’s Law of Induction**, which states that the induced electromotive force (emf), denoted by $\varepsilon$, is equal to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux ($\Phi$) over time ($t$):

$$\varepsilon = - \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$

The problem asks for the **magnitude** of the induced emf, which means we are looking for the absolute value: $|\varepsilon| = | \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} |$. In graphical terms, the magnitude of the emf is equal to the **absolute value of the gradient (slope)** of the $\Phi$ vs. $t$ graph.

### Step-by-Step Analysis:

1.  **Interval $0$ to $t_1$:** The flux increases linearly. The slope is positive and constant. Therefore, the magnitude of the emf is a non-zero constant.
2.  **Interval $t_1$ to $t_2$:** The flux decreases linearly to zero. The slope is negative and constant. Because we are looking for the **magnitude**, the absolute value of this negative slope remains a constant positive value. Note that the slope in the first interval ($0$ to $t_1$) is steeper than the slope in the second interval ($t_1$ to $t_2$). Thus, the magnitude of the emf will be higher for the first interval and lower for the second.
3.  **Interval $t_2$ to $t_3$:** The flux is zero and constant. The slope is $0$, meaning the induced emf is $0$.
4.  **Interval $t_3$ to $t_4$:** The flux increases linearly, but with a shallower gradient than the first interval. This results in a small, constant non-zero magnitude for the emf.

### Conclusion:
- From $0$ to $t_1$: High constant emf.
- From $t_1$ to $t_2$: Lower constant emf.
- From $t_2$ to $t_3$: Zero emf.
- From $t_3$ to $t_4$: Small constant emf.
