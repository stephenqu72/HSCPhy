### Explanation
To determine which experimental setup allows a physicist to measure the elementary charge of an electron ($q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$), we must identify the historical experiments represented by each image:

*   **Option A: Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment.** This is the correct setup. Robert Millikan used this apparatus to measure the charge of individual oil droplets suspended in a uniform electric field. By balancing the gravitational force ($mg$) with the electric force ($qE$), he determined that the charge on the droplets was always an integer multiple of a fundamental value, which he identified as the charge of the electron.
*   **Option B: Rutherford’s Gold Foil Experiment.** This experiment involved firing alpha particles at a thin gold foil. It provided evidence for the existence of a dense, positively charged nucleus within the atom, rather than measuring the charge of an electron.
*   **Option C: Chadwick’s Neutron Discovery.** This experiment shows alpha radiation striking a beryllium target, which then emits radiation that knocks protons out of paraffin wax. This led to the discovery of the neutron.
*   **Option D: Thomson’s Cathode Ray Tube (CRT).** While this setup (a CRT with electric and magnetic fields) was used by J.J. Thomson to determine the **charge-to-mass ratio** ($q/m$) of the electron, it could not determine the charge of the electron in isolation without knowing the mass or having an independent measurement of the charge.

Therefore, **A** is the only experiment designed to measure the elementary charge of the electron.
