### Answer
The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of **work-energy** and **centre of mass** dynamics in rotational systems.

1.  **The Physics of Pumping a Swing:** Increasing the speed of a swing requires the rider to perform work on the system. When a rider is at the top of the swing's arc (where velocity is momentarily zero and potential energy is at a maximum), their velocity is low, but their potential energy is high. 
2.  **Raising the Centre of Mass:** By straightening their legs and pulling on the ropes, the rider effectively shifts their body mass further away from the seat and closer to the pivot point (or effectively raises their centre of mass). 
3.  **Work and Potential Energy:** When the rider moves their body weight upward (raising their centre of mass) at the high point of the arc, they are doing work against gravity. Because they are at the top, this movement increases their gravitational potential energy ($GPE = mgh$).
4.  **Conservation of Energy:** As the rider swings back down towards the equilibrium position, this additional potential energy is converted into kinetic energy ($KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$). By repeating this rhythmic motion, the rider continuously adds energy to the system, thereby increasing the amplitude and speed of the swing.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** Straightening your legs at the top *does* increase the system's potential energy, but the phrasing "adds to available kinetic energy" is imprecise; the movement itself is the work done to increase the total mechanical energy.
*   **C:** Pulling on the ropes does not provide a torque that significantly contributes to the pendulum-like motion in the way the shifting of the centre of mass does; the pivot is fixed, and the torque provided by the rider's internal forces doesn't generate the angular acceleration required to increase speed.
*   **D:** While centripetal force is involved in circular motion, the mechanism for increasing speed on a swing is fundamentally about changing the potential energy by altering the height of the centre of mass, not by simply increasing centripetal acceleration.
