### Part (a)
To find the initial velocity ($v$), we use the horizontal range formula for projectile motion on flat ground:
$$R = \frac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$$

**Given values:**
- Range $R = 650 \text{ m}$
- Angle $\theta = 25^\circ$
- Acceleration due to gravity $g = 9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$

**Rearranging for $v$:**
$$v^2 = \frac{R \cdot g}{\sin(2\theta)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{650 \cdot 9.8}{\sin(50^\circ)}}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{6370}{0.76604...}} \approx \sqrt{8315.4...} \approx 91.19 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

Rounding to appropriate significant figures (consistent with the provided data), the firing velocity is approximately **$91 \text{ m s}^{-1}$**.

### Part (b)
To evaluate the accuracy and validity:
*   **Accuracy:** The calculation is mathematically accurate based on the model chosen. However, the result is likely to be **inaccurate** in a real-world setting. In reality, air resistance (drag) would act against the cannonball, significantly reducing its horizontal range. Therefore, the actual firing velocity required to reach $650 \text{ m}$ would need to be higher than our calculated $91 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.
*   **Validity:** The model has **low validity** for a professional production environment. By neglecting air resistance, we are using an "idealised" physics model. For a practical application involving safety (real actors), this model is insufficient because it fails to account for terminal velocity, drag forces proportional to the square of the velocity, and potential variations in local gravity or air density. A more valid approach would involve computational fluid dynamics or empirical testing to account for the actual trajectory of the projectile.
