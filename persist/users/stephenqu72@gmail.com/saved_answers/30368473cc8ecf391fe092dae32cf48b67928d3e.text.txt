To show that muons can reach the Earth's surface, we must demonstrate that due to time dilation, the "dilated" lifetime of the moving muon allows it to travel a distance greater than or equal to $9\text{ km}$.

### Step-by-Step Explanation

**1. Identify the given values:**
*   Velocity ($v$) = $0.998c$
*   Proper lifetime ($t_0$) = $2.2 \times 10^{-6}\text{ s}$
*   Distance to travel ($d$) = $9 \times 10^3\text{ m}$

**2. Calculate the Lorentz factor ($\gamma$):**
The Lorentz factor is given by $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$.
Substituting $v = 0.998c$:
$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (0.998)^2}} \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.996004}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.003996}} \approx 15.82$$

**3. Calculate the dilated lifetime ($t_v$):**
From the perspective of an observer on Earth, the muon's lifetime is dilated:
$$t_v = \gamma t_0$$
$$t_v = 15.82 \times 2.2 \times 10^{-6}\text{ s} \approx 3.48 \times 10^{-5}\text{ s}$$

**4. Calculate the distance the muon travels ($d_{travel}$):**
Using $d = vt$:
$$d_{travel} = (0.998 \times 3 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}) \times (3.48 \times 10^{-5}\text{ s})$$
$$d_{travel} \approx 10,420\text{ m} \approx 10.4\text{ km}$$

**Conclusion:**
Since the distance the muon can travel in its dilated lifetime ($10.4\text{ km}$) is greater than the altitude of $9\text{ km}$, the muons are able to reach the surface of the Earth.
