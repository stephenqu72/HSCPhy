### Part (a) Solution
To find the temperature, we use Wien’s Displacement Law, which states that the wavelength of peak emission ($\lambda_{max}$) is inversely proportional to the temperature ($T$):

$$\lambda_{max} \cdot T = 2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}\cdot\text{K}$$

1. **Identify $\lambda_{max}$ from the graph:** Looking at the peak of the curve, the intensity is highest at approximately $500\text{ nm}$ ($5.0 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$).
2. **Calculate $T$:**
   $$T = \frac{2.898 \times 10^{-3}}{\lambda_{max}} = \frac{2.898 \times 10^{-3}}{5.0 \times 10^{-7}}$$
   $$T \approx 5796\text{ K}$$
3. **Conclusion:** This value is approximately $5800\text{ K}$, consistent with the question.

### Part (b) Solution
The dark lines in the Sun's spectrum are known as **Fraunhofer lines** (absorption lines). They are caused by the following mechanism:

1. **Continuous Spectrum:** The core of the Sun produces a continuous blackbody spectrum due to its high density and temperature.
2. **Atmospheric Absorption:** As this light passes through the cooler, lower-density outer layers of the Sun's atmosphere (the photosphere and chromosphere), specific atoms and ions absorb photons.
3. **Quantized Energy:** Electrons in these atoms absorb photons that match the exact energy difference ($\Delta E = hf$) between their energy levels. 
4. **Re-emission:** While the atoms do re-emit these photons, they do so in random directions, meaning the intensity of light at those specific wavelengths is significantly reduced when viewed from Earth, resulting in dark absorption lines.
