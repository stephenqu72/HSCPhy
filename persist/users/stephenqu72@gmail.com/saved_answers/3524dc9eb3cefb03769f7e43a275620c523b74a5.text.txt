### Answer

**a) Name the two types of spectra shown above.**
*   **A:** Continuous spectrum
*   **B:** Emission line spectrum

**b) Describe how the two spectra shown can be produced and observed in a science classroom.**
*   **Continuous spectrum (A):** Produced by a hot, dense light source (e.g., an incandescent light bulb filament). The light is passed through a prism or diffraction grating, which disperses the white light into a smooth, unbroken band of all visible wavelengths (colors).
*   **Emission spectrum (B):** Produced by a low-pressure gas excited by electricity (e.g., a spectral discharge tube containing neon or hydrogen). When the high-energy electrons in the gas atoms drop to lower energy levels, they emit specific, discrete wavelengths of light. When observed through a spectroscope (containing a prism or diffraction grating), this appears as distinct, bright colored lines on a black background.

### How to solve this

1.  **Analyze the Visuals:**
    *   **Spectrum A** shows a "rainbow" with no breaks. This indicates that every wavelength is present. This is the hallmark of a **continuous spectrum**.
    *   **Spectrum B** shows a black background with specific, sharp colored lines. This indicates that only specific, discrete wavelengths are present. This is the hallmark of an **emission spectrum**.

2.  **Recall the Physics Principles:**
    *   **Continuous spectra** result from "blackbody radiation." When matter is dense (like a solid filament), atoms are crowded together and constantly colliding. This smears out their individual energy levels, allowing for the emission of light at all possible frequencies.
    *   **Emission spectra** result from quantum transitions. In a dilute gas, atoms are far apart. Electrons jump between specific, quantized energy levels ($E_n$). When an electron falls from a high energy state to a lower one, it releases a photon with energy equal to the difference: $E_{photon} = E_{high} - E_{low} = hf$. Since only specific energy differences exist for a given element, only specific colors (wavelengths) appear.

3.  **Constructing the Response:**
    *   For part (a), keep it concise.
    *   For part (b), remember the "three-part" requirement for an HSC answer: identify the source (the light), identify the mechanism (thermal excitation vs. electronic transition), and identify the method of observation (prism/diffraction grating).
