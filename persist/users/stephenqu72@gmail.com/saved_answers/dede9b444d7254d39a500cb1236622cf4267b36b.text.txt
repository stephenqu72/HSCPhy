To solve this problem, we use the following standard constants for Earth:
- Mass of Earth, $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$
- Radius of Earth, $R = 6.37 \times 10^6\text{ m}$
- Gravitational constant, $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ Nm}^2/\text{kg}^2$
- Satellite mass, $m = 100\text{ kg}$
- Altitude, $h = 300\text{ km} = 3 \times 10^5\text{ m}$
- Orbital radius, $r = R + h = 6.67 \times 10^6\text{ m}$

### (a) Calculate the predicted period of the satellite
Use Kepler’s Third Law: $\frac{T^2}{r^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$.
$$T = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}} = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi^2 (6.67 \times 10^6)^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}} \approx 5430\text{ seconds} \approx 90.5\text{ minutes}$$

### (b) Determine the total energy of the satellite
The total energy $E$ in a circular orbit is given by $E = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$.
$$E = -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(100)}{2(6.67 \times 10^6)} \approx -2.98 \times 10^9\text{ J}$$

### (c) Calculate the average orbital speed
The orbital speed $v$ is given by $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$.
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}{6.67 \times 10^6}} \approx 7730\text{ m/s} \text{ (or } 7.73\text{ km/s)}$$

### (d) Calculate the minimum energy required to lift the satellite
The energy required is the difference between the total energy at the orbital height and the potential energy at the Earth's surface.
Initial potential energy at surface: $U_i = -\frac{GMm}{R} = -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})(100)}{6.37 \times 10^6} \approx -6.25 \times 10^9\text{ J}$.
Final total energy in orbit (from part b): $E_f = -2.98 \times 10^9\text{ J}$.
Energy required $\Delta E = E_f - U_i = (-2.98 \times 10^9) - (-6.25 \times 10^9) \approx 3.27 \times 10^9\text{ J}$.

***

### Explanation of approach:
1. **Orbital Mechanics:** Always convert altitude to orbital radius by adding the Earth's radius.
2. **Kepler's Third Law:** Used for orbital period calculations.
3. **Energy:** Remember that total energy is negative (bound state). Potential energy at the surface is defined relative to infinity ($U = -GMm/r$).
4. **Energy required:** This is the difference between the final energy state (orbit) and the initial energy state (stationary on surface).
