### Part (a)

**(i) Suggest why the law specifies the average orbital radius.**
Planets follow elliptical orbits, not perfect circles. Therefore, the distance between the planet and the Sun changes throughout the orbit; the "average orbital radius" is used to represent the semi-major axis of the ellipse.

**(ii) State the name of the force that causes the acceleration.**
Gravitational force (or centripetal force).

**(iii) State an expression for the magnitude $F$.**
$$F = \frac{GM_S m}{R^2}$$

**(iv) Hence deduce... $K = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM_S}$.**
The gravitational force provides the centripetal force required for circular motion:
$$\frac{GM_S m}{R^2} = \frac{m v^2}{R}$$
Since $v = \frac{2\pi R}{T}$, we substitute this into the equation:
$$\frac{GM_S}{R^2} = \frac{(2\pi R / T)^2}{R} = \frac{4\pi^2 R^2}{T^2 R} = \frac{4\pi^2 R}{T^2}$$
Rearranging for $T^2$:
$$T^2 = \left( \frac{4\pi^2}{GM_S} \right) R^3$$
Comparing this to $T^2 = KR^3$, we see that $K = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM_S}$.

### Part (b)

**(i) Deduce that the gravitational field strength $g$ is approximately $0.1\text{ N kg}^{-1}$.**
For circular motion, the acceleration (gravitational field strength $g$) is given by $g = \frac{v^2}{R}$.
First, calculate the orbital velocity $v$:
$$v = \frac{2\pi R}{T} = \frac{2 \times \pi \times 1.1 \times 10^9}{6.2 \times 10^5} \approx 11153\text{ m/s}$$
Now calculate $g$:
$$g = \frac{v^2}{R} = \frac{(11153)^2}{1.1 \times 10^9} \approx 0.113 \approx 0.1\text{ N kg}^{-1}$$

**(ii) Estimate the mass of Jupiter.**
Using the gravitational field strength formula $g = \frac{GM}{R^2}$:
$$M = \frac{g R^2}{G}$$
$$M = \frac{0.113 \times (1.1 \times 10^9)^2}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}}$$
$$M \approx \frac{0.113 \times 1.21 \times 10^{18}}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}} \approx 2.05 \times 10^{27}\text{ kg}$$

### How to solve it
1. **Conceptual Understanding:** Understand that orbital mechanics are governed by the balance between gravitational attraction and centripetal acceleration.
2. **Derivation:** When asked to "deduce," always start from fundamental physical principles (Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation and the definition of centripetal force).
3. **Calculation:** When calculating constants, ensure your units are consistent (SI units: meters, seconds, kilograms). Use intermediate values to show your working clearly for markers.
