To determine which position results in the maximum magnetic flux through the conducting loop, we must consider the definition of magnetic flux:
$$\Phi = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int B \cos(\theta) \, dA$$
where $\mathbf{B}$ is the magnetic field produced by the straight conductor, $d\mathbf{A}$ is the area vector perpendicular to the plane of the loop, and $\theta$ is the angle between the magnetic field vector and the area vector.

### Analysis of the options:

1.  **Option A, B, and C:** The conductor lies in the same horizontal plane as the circular loop. The magnetic field lines produced by a straight current-carrying wire wrap around the wire in concentric circles. If the wire is in the same plane as the loop, the magnetic field lines are either entering the plane on one side of the wire and exiting on the other, or they lie entirely within the plane of the loop. Specifically, the magnetic field vectors $\mathbf{B}$ are perpendicular to the area vector $\mathbf{A}$ (which points vertically out of the horizontal plane). Since $\cos(90^{\circ}) = 0$, the net magnetic flux through the loop is **zero** for these cases.
2.  **Option D:** The conductor is placed in a vertical plane passing through the loop. The magnetic field lines produced by the current in the vertical wire will curve such that they pass through the area enclosed by the loop. Because the field lines are no longer perpendicular to the area vector (they have a component parallel to the area vector), there is a net flux through the loop. 

### Conclusion:
In positions A, B, and C, the magnetic field produced by the current lies within the same plane as the loop, meaning the field lines are parallel to the loop's surface and do not "pierce" it. In position D, the orientation of the wire relative to the loop allows the magnetic field to pass through the area of the loop, resulting in a non-zero (maximum) magnetic flux.

**The correct answer is D.**
