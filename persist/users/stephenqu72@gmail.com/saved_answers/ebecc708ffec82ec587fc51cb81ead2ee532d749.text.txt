### Sample Answer

**Historical Method: Fizeau’s Method (1849)**
Fizeau used a rotating cogwheel and a distant mirror. A beam of light was directed through a gap in the cogwheel to a mirror located several kilometers away. The reflected beam would return to the wheel. By varying the rotation speed of the wheel, Fizeau ensured the returning light was blocked by a tooth. Knowing the distance ($d$) to the mirror and the angular velocity of the wheel, he calculated the time taken for the light to travel the round trip. This was a significant improvement over earlier astronomical methods as it was the first non-astronomical measurement of the speed of light on Earth, yielding approximately $3.13 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.

**Contemporary Method: Resonance in a Cavity or Laser Interferometry**
Contemporary methods utilize the relationship $c = f\lambda$. By using highly stable lasers (such as an iodine-stabilized HeNe laser), scientists can measure the frequency ($f$) and the wavelength ($\lambda$) of light with extreme precision using an optical frequency comb. Because the speed of light is now defined as exactly $299,792,458 \text{ m/s}$, these experiments are used to define the standard meter. This method has an uncertainty of nearly zero, representing a vast improvement in accuracy compared to historical mechanical methods.
