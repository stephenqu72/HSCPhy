### Answer

**a) Explain the experimental evidence that led to Rutherford's model of the atom.**

Rutherford’s model was derived from the Geiger-Marsden experiment (the gold foil experiment). In this experiment, alpha particles were fired at a thin sheet of gold foil. The evidence showed that:
1.  **Most alpha particles passed straight through:** This suggested the atom consists mostly of empty space.
2.  **Some particles were deflected at small angles:** This indicated the presence of a concentrated positive charge within the atom.
3.  **A very small number of particles were reflected directly back (large angle scattering):** This provided evidence for a dense, positively charged central core (the nucleus) containing almost all of the atom's mass.

**b) Explain the importance of the Balmer series in Bohr's explanation for his atomic model.**

The Balmer series provided the empirical mathematical foundation for Bohr's quantum model. The wavelengths of the visible light emitted by hydrogen follow the Rydberg formula:
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \frac{1}{2^2} - \frac{1}{n^2} \right)$$
where $n = 3, 4, 5...$. Bohr recognized that the Balmer series represented transitions of an electron from higher energy levels to the $n=2$ energy level. This allowed him to postulate that electrons exist in stable, quantized orbits and that light is emitted when an electron "jumps" between these discrete energy levels, with the energy of the photon corresponding to the difference in energy between the two states: $E = \Delta E = hf$.

*   **For part (b):** Focus on the bridge between classical physics and quantum mechanics.
