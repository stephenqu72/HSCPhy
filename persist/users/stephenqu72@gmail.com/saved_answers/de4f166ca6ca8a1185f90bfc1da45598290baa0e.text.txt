To find the decay constant ($\lambda$) for the radioactive isotope, we use the concept of **half-life** ($t_{1/2}$), which is the time required for the mass of the isotope to decrease to half of its initial value.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Identify the initial mass ($N_0$):**
    Looking at the graph, at $t = 0$, the mass is $400 \text{ g}$.

2.  **Determine the half-life ($t_{1/2}$):**
    We need to find the time it takes for the mass to reach half of $400 \text{ g}$, which is $200 \text{ g}$.
    Locating $200 \text{ g}$ on the y-axis and moving across to the data point, we see it corresponds to $t = 100 \text{ years}$.
    Therefore, the half-life is $t_{1/2} = 100 \text{ years}$.

3.  **Calculate the decay constant ($\lambda$):**
    The relationship between the decay constant and half-life is given by the formula:
    $$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$$
    Substituting the value of $t_{1/2}$:
    $$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{100}$$
    $$\lambda \approx \frac{0.6931}{100}$$
    $$\lambda \approx 0.00693 \text{ years}^{-1}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, the correct choice is **B**.

### Explanation of the approach:
*   **Data extraction:** The graph provides discrete points representing exponential decay. By identifying the initial value and the point where the value is halved, we can easily determine the half-life.
*   **Mathematical link:** In HSC Physics, radioactive decay follows the law $N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$. Deriving the relationship between the decay constant and half-life is a standard requirement. Knowing that $t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(2)}{\lambda}$ allows for a quick conversion from graphical time data to the decay constant.
