To solve this physics problem, we must analyze the forces acting on the car. In a frictionless banked track, the only two forces acting on the car are the gravitational force ($mg$) acting downwards and the normal force ($N$) acting perpendicular to the banked surface.

### (a) i. Determine the magnitude of the Normal force ($N$)
Since the car is moving in a horizontal circle, there is no vertical acceleration. Therefore, the vertical component of the normal force must balance the gravitational force:
$$N \cos(\theta) = mg$$
$$N = \frac{mg}{\cos(\theta)}$$
Substituting the values $m = 1000\text{ kg}$, $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$, and $\theta = 30^\circ$:
$$N = \frac{1000 \times 9.8}{\cos(30^\circ)} = \frac{9800}{0.866} \approx 11316\text{ N}$$
The magnitude of the normal force is approximately **$1.13 \times 10^4\text{ N}$**.

### (a) ii. Determine the speed of the car
The horizontal component of the normal force provides the necessary centripetal force ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$):
$$N \sin(\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
We know $N = \frac{mg}{\cos(\theta)}$, so substituting this into the equation:
$$\left(\frac{mg}{\cos(\theta)}\right) \sin(\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$mg \tan(\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$v^2 = rg \tan(\theta)$$
$$v = \sqrt{rg \tan(\theta)} = \sqrt{60 \times 9.8 \times \tan(30^\circ)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{588 \times 0.577} \approx \sqrt{339.48} \approx 18.42\text{ m/s}$$
The speed of the car is approximately **$18.4\text{ m/s}$**.

### (b) Explain why the car must move higher up
For a frictionless banked track, the centripetal force is provided by the horizontal component of the normal force ($N \sin \theta$). If the speed $v$ is doubled, the required centripetal force ($mv^2/r$) increases by a factor of 4.
To maintain the path without sliding (sideways friction), the horizontal component of the normal force must increase. On a conical track, moving higher up the incline effectively changes the effective radius and/or the required banking angle balance. More importantly, if the track is a surface of constant slope, the car would normally slide outwards. To maintain equilibrium at a higher speed, the car must effectively transition to a steeper part of the bank or a larger radius; since $v \propto \sqrt{r}$, increasing the speed requires a larger radius $r$ to maintain the same bank angle, or a steeper angle to support the increased centripetal requirement.
