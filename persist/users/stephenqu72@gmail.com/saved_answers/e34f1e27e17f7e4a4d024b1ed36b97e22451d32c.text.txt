### Sample Answer

Scientific models are developed to explain observed phenomena and are validated through their ability to make testable predictions that align with experimental evidence.

**Development:**
Christiaan Huygens developed his wave model of light in 1678 to address limitations in Newton’s particle (corpuscular) model. Huygens proposed that light traveled as a longitudinal wave through a hypothetical medium called the "luminiferous aether." His model was developed to explain observable wave properties such as reflection and refraction. He introduced the principle that every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary wavelets, which successfully predicted the geometry of wavefront propagation.

**Validation:**
Validation occurs when a model explains existing data and successfully predicts new phenomena. Initially, Huygens' model was validated because it provided a mathematical explanation for Snell’s Law of refraction, which the particle model struggled to explain as convincingly. However, the scientific process is iterative. While Huygens' model was validated for reflection and refraction, it was later challenged (e.g., the lack of observed polarization). It was further validated when Thomas Young’s Double Slit experiment provided empirical evidence for interference—a phenomenon explained by the wave model—thereby confirming the wave nature of light. Ultimately, models are validated or superseded based on their predictive power against rigorous experimental testing.

1.  **Understand the Verb:** The prompt asks you to "explain." This means you must provide a clear logical sequence showing *how* things happen. Do not just list facts; connect the development of the model to the process of validation.
2.  **Define the Structure:** Your answer should have two distinct parts as requested by the prompt:
    *   **Development:** Explain the context (e.g., the need to explain phenomena like refraction or reflection) and the core assumption (light as a wave).
    *   **Validation:** Explain the importance of empirical evidence. Mention how the model was checked against experiments (like Snell’s Law) and how its ability to predict future observations (like Young’s interference) solidified its acceptance.
3.  **HSC Exam Tip:** The examiners want to see that you understand that science is not static. Mention that models are accepted when they align with evidence, but can be modified or replaced if new evidence arises.
