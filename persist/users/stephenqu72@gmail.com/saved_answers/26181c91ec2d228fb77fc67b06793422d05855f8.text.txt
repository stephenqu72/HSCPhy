### Answer
The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation

To arrive at this answer, let’s analyze the historical context of James Clerk Maxwell’s work in the late 19th century:

1.  **The Core Contribution:** Maxwell unified the laws of electricity and magnetism into a single, cohesive set of equations. From these equations, he mathematically derived the existence of transverse waves that travel at the speed of light.
2.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **(A) The link between magnetism and electrons:** While Maxwell's equations describe fields, the concept of the "electron" as a discrete particle was established later by J.J. Thomson in 1897, after Maxwell's primary work.
    *   **(B) The discovery of radio waves:** This is a common distractor. Maxwell *predicted* the existence of electromagnetic waves, but it was Heinrich Hertz who later provided the experimental *discovery* (proof) of radio waves.
    *   **(C) The prediction of an electromagnetic spectrum:** This is correct. By calculating the theoretical velocity of electromagnetic waves, $v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$, Maxwell realized that this value matched the measured speed of light. This led him to the profound conclusion that light itself is an electromagnetic wave, and that there exists a broader spectrum of such waves beyond visible light.
    *   **(D) That light was composed of photons:** This is incorrect. The photon model is a feature of Quantum Mechanics, proposed by Albert Einstein in 1905 to explain the photoelectric effect, decades after Maxwell's work.

**Key takeaway for HSC Physics:** Always distinguish between the *theoretical prediction* (Maxwell) and the *experimental confirmation* (Hertz) when studying the history of electromagnetic waves.
