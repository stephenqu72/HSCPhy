### Answer
Particle accelerators are vital tools in high-energy physics, serving primarily to probe the fundamental nature of matter and the forces governing the universe. Their roles include:

1.  **Testing the Standard Model:** Accelerators smash subatomic particles (like protons or electrons) together at near-light speeds. By analysing the debris from these high-energy collisions, physicists can detect new particles, such as the Higgs boson, and verify the predictions of the Standard Model of particle physics.
2.  **Probing Matter at Small Scales:** According to the de Broglie hypothesis, wavelength is inversely proportional to momentum: $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$. By accelerating particles to extremely high energies, their de Broglie wavelength becomes incredibly small. This allows physicists to "resolve" or see structures within nucleons and study quarks and gluons.
3.  **Simulating Early Universe Conditions:** High-energy collisions create extreme temperatures and densities similar to those that existed milliseconds after the Big Bang. This helps physicists understand the evolution of the universe and the behaviour of fundamental forces at high energy levels.
4.  **Verification of Mass-Energy Equivalence:** Accelerators provide experimental evidence for Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$. The kinetic energy imparted to the particles by electric fields is converted into mass upon collision, allowing for the creation of heavy, short-lived particles that do not exist in stable matter.
5.  **Practical Applications:** Beyond fundamental research, accelerators are used in medicine (e.g., proton therapy for cancer) and materials science (e.g., using synchrotron radiation to analyse molecular structures).
