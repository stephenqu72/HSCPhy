To solve this problem, we must use the formula for the force per unit length ($F/L$) between two parallel current-carrying conductors:

$$ \frac{F}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2 \pi d} $$

Where:
*   $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space.
*   $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the currents in the two wires.
*   $d$ is the distance between the wires.

We are looking for the option that does **not** result in the force increasing by a factor of 4.

### Analysis of the options:

**A. Doubling the current in both wires:**
$I_1$ becomes $2I_1$ and $I_2$ becomes $2I_2$.
New force $\propto (2I_1)(2I_2) = 4 I_1 I_2$.
This increases the force by a factor of **4**.

**B. Doubling the current in one wire and halving the distance:**
$I_1$ becomes $2I_1$ and $d$ becomes $0.5d$.
New force $\propto \frac{(2I_1)(I_2)}{0.5d} = \frac{2}{0.5} \times \frac{I_1 I_2}{d} = 4 \times \frac{I_1 I_2}{d}$.
This increases the force by a factor of **4**.

**C. Decreasing the distance between the wires by a factor of 4:**
$d$ becomes $\frac{d}{4}$.
New force $\propto \frac{I_1 I_2}{d/4} = 4 \times \frac{I_1 I_2}{d}$.
This increases the force by a factor of **4**.

**D. Halving the current in both wires and decreasing the distance by a factor of 8:**
$I_1$ becomes $0.5I_1$, $I_2$ becomes $0.5I_2$, and $d$ becomes $\frac{d}{8}$.
New force $\propto \frac{(0.5I_1)(0.5I_2)}{d/8} = \frac{0.25}{1/8} \times \frac{I_1 I_2}{d} = (0.25 \times 8) \times \frac{I_1 I_2}{d} = 2 \times \frac{I_1 I_2}{d}$.
This increases the force by a factor of **2**, not 4.

**Conclusion:** Option D does not increase the force by a factor of 4.
