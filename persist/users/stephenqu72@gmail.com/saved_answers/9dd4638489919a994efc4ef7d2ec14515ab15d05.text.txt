### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must apply Faraday's Law of Induction and the principle of Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM).

1.  **Electromagnetic Induction**: The induced electromotive force (EMF) in a conductor moving through a magnetic field is given by the formula:
    $$\varepsilon = Blv$$
    where $B$ is the magnetic field strength, $l$ is the length of the bar, and $v$ is the instantaneous velocity of the bar.

2.  **Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)**: When the bar is released from a displaced position, it undergoes SHM. The position $x$ of an object in SHM can be modeled as:
    $$x(t) = A\cos(\omega t)$$
    The velocity $v(t)$ is the derivative of position with respect to time:
    $$v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = -A\omega\sin(\omega t)$$

3.  **Analyzing the Graphs**:
    *   At the moment of release ($t = 0$), the bar is held stationary at its maximum displacement. Therefore, the initial velocity $v(0) = 0$. Consequently, the initial EMF must be $0$. This eliminates options B and D.
    *   Immediately after release, the bar accelerates upwards (in the negative $x$ direction if we define down as positive). As the bar gains velocity upwards, an EMF is generated.
    *   Following the velocity function $v(t) = -A\omega\sin(\omega t)$, the EMF $\varepsilon(t) = Blv(t) = -BlA\omega\sin(\omega t)$.
    *   At $t=0$, the slope of the sine function is positive, so the EMF will initially increase to a positive value (or negative depending on the defined coordinate system and Lenz's Law). 
    *   Looking at the options, Graph A shows the EMF starting at $0$ and increasing, consistent with the standard representation of a velocity-time graph for an object released from rest at a maximum displacement.

Graph A represents a sine function starting at $0$, which matches the velocity profile of the oscillating bar.
