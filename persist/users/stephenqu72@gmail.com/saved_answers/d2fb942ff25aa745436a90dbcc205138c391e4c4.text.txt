To achieve full marks in this 9-mark HSC Physics question, you must demonstrate a deep understanding of the historical transition from classical to quantum physics by linking specific experimental evidence to the theoretical developments made by Planck, Einstein, and Bohr.

### Answer Breakdown

**1. Max Planck (Blackbody Radiation)**
*   **Experimental Evidence:** Classical physics (Rayleigh-Jeans Law) failed to explain the "ultraviolet catastrophe," where theory predicted infinite energy emission at short wavelengths for blackbody radiators.
*   **Theoretical Development:** Planck proposed that electromagnetic energy is emitted in discrete "quanta" rather than a continuous stream. He introduced the constant $h$ ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$), stating energy is proportional to frequency: $E = hf$. This solved the ultraviolet catastrophe by limiting the energy available for high-frequency emission.

**2. Albert Einstein (Photoelectric Effect)**
*   **Experimental Evidence:** The photoelectric effect showed that the kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons depended on the *frequency* of incident light, not its intensity. Furthermore, there was a "threshold frequency" below which no emission occurred, regardless of intensity.
*   **Theoretical Development:** Einstein took Planck’s quantization idea and applied it to light itself, proposing light exists as particles called "photons." He formulated the equation: $hf = \Phi + E_k$, where $\Phi$ is the work function. This successfully explained why frequency—not intensity—determined energy, as one photon interacts with one electron.

**3. Niels Bohr (Atomic Spectra)**
*   **Experimental Evidence:** Emission and absorption spectra of gases (like hydrogen) showed discrete lines rather than a continuous spectrum. Balmer’s empirical formula predicted these wavelengths but lacked a physical model.
*   **Theoretical Development:** Bohr proposed that electrons occupy stable, quantized orbits around the nucleus. When an electron transitions between these levels, it emits or absorbs a photon with energy equal to the difference between the levels: $\Delta E = hf$. This explained the discrete spectral lines as evidence of energy level quantization, fundamentally changing our model of the atom.
