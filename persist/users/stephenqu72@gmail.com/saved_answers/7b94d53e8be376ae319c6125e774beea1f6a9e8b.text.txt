### Explanation
To solve this problem, you need to understand the physics of stellar spectroscopy, which is a key component of the HSC Physics "Astrophysics" module.

1.  **Understanding Absorption Spectra:** A star emits a continuous spectrum of light from its hot core (the photosphere). As this light passes through the cooler, thinner gas of the star’s atmosphere, specific atoms and ions absorb specific wavelengths of light. This corresponds to the energy required to excite electrons to higher energy levels. These missing wavelengths appear as dark lines (Fraunhofer lines) against the continuous rainbow background.
2.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **(A)** Incorrect. While the Doppler effect causes a shift in the position of spectral lines (redshift for moving away, blueshift for moving towards), the mere presence of dark lines themselves does not indicate the motion of the star.
    *   **(B)** Incorrect. Atoms *do* re-emit the absorbed photons, but they do so in random directions. Because the observer is only looking at a tiny fraction of the light emitted by the star, the light scattered *out* of our line of sight significantly outweighs the light scattered *into* our line of sight from the surrounding gas, resulting in the dark line.
    *   **(C) Correct.** Each element has a unique "fingerprint" of energy levels, meaning each element absorbs light at very specific, characteristic wavelengths. By identifying the position of these dark lines (as labeled in the diagram: Ca, H, Fe, Mg, Na, etc.), astrophysicists can determine which chemical elements are present in the star's atmosphere.
    *   **(D)** Incorrect. All atoms present in the atmosphere contribute to the spectrum; they just interact with light at different wavelengths.

**Summary:** The dark lines are the result of elements in the star's atmosphere absorbing photons at specific resonant frequencies, allowing us to perform elemental analysis of the star.
