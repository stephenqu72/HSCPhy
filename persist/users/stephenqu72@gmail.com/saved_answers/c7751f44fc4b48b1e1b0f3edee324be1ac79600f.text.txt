### Answer
The correct answer is **C. several elements are present in the star's atmosphere.**

### Explanation
To solve this, we need to understand how stellar absorption spectra are formed:

1.  **Continuous Spectrum:** The hot, dense core of the star emits a continuous range of wavelengths (blackbody radiation).
2.  **Absorption Process:** As this light travels outward, it passes through the cooler, thinner gases in the star's atmosphere (the photosphere/chromosphere). The atoms of the specific elements present in the atmosphere absorb specific photons that correspond exactly to the energy transitions (excited states) of their electrons.
3.  **The Resulting Lines:** Because these specific wavelengths are removed from the continuous spectrum, we see dark lines at those positions. Each element has a unique "fingerprint" of absorption lines based on its electron energy levels.
4.  **Why C is correct:** By observing the positions of the dark lines (such as the lines labeled $Ca$, $H$, $Fe$, $Mg$, $Na$, and $O_2$ in the diagram), astronomers can identify which chemical elements are present in the stellar atmosphere. The presence of multiple different labels across the spectrum confirms that the star's atmosphere contains a variety of elements.

**Why the others are incorrect:**
*   **A:** This refers to the *Doppler Effect* (Redshift/Blueshift). While movement shifts the entire spectrum, it does not explain why the dark lines exist in the first place.
*   **B:** This is technically incomplete. Atoms *do* re-emit the photons, but they re-emit them in *random directions*. Because the observer is only looking at a tiny fraction of the sky, the intensity at those specific wavelengths appears significantly lower, creating the dark line.
*   **D:** All atoms in the atmosphere interact with the light; the lines are simply evidence of the specific elements present.
