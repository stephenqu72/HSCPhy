To derive the concept of escape velocity, we use the principle of the **Conservation of Mechanical Energy**.

### Answer
Escape velocity is defined as the minimum speed required for an object to break free from the gravitational attraction of a massive body without further propulsion.

To "escape" to infinity, the object must have enough kinetic energy to reach a point where its gravitational potential energy is zero and its final velocity is zero.

1. The total mechanical energy at the surface of the planet is the sum of its kinetic energy and gravitational potential energy:
$$E_{initial} = K + U = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{r}$$
where $M$ is the mass of the planet, $m$ is the mass of the object, $G$ is the gravitational constant, and $r$ is the radius of the planet.

2. At the "escape" point (infinity), the potential energy is $0$ and we set the kinetic energy to $0$ to find the *minimum* velocity:
$$E_{final} = 0 + 0 = 0$$

3. By conservation of energy ($E_{initial} = E_{final}$):
$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{r} = 0$$
$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{GMm}{r}$$
$$v^2 = \frac{2GM}{r}$$
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}$$

### How to solve this
To maximize your marks in an HSC Physics exam for a derivation question:

*   **State the Principle:** Explicitly mention that you are using the "Conservation of Mechanical Energy." This shows the examiner you understand the physical basis of the derivation.
*   **Define the Variables:** Always define your terms (e.g., $M$ = mass of planet, $r$ = distance from centre).
*   **Logic at Infinity:** The crucial conceptual step is explaining that at the limit of escape, the object's distance becomes infinite ($r \to \infty$) and its velocity becomes zero. This forces the total energy to equal zero.
*   **Algebraic Rigour:** Ensure you cancel the mass of the object ($m$) from both sides, illustrating that escape velocity is independent of the object's own mass.
