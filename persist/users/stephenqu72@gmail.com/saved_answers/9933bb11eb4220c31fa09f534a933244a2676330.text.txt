To solve this problem, we need to calculate the orbital velocity of a satellite at two different altitudes and find the difference between them.

### 1. Conceptual Understanding
The orbital velocity $v$ of a satellite is given by the formula:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$
Where:
- $G$ is the gravitational constant ($6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3 \text{kg}^{-1} \text{s}^{-2}$)
- $M$ is the mass of the Earth ($5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$)
- $r$ is the orbital radius from the centre of the Earth ($r = R_E + h$)
- $R_E$ is the radius of the Earth ($\approx 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$)
- $h$ is the altitude of the satellite

**Key Principle:** As the orbital radius $r$ decreases (moving to a lower orbit), the denominator in the equation decreases, meaning the required orbital velocity $v$ must **increase**.

### 2. Calculation
**Step 1: Calculate the radius for both orbits.**
- $r_1 = 6,370 \text{ km} + 850 \text{ km} = 7,220 \text{ km} = 7.22 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$
- $r_2 = 6,370 \text{ km} + 650 \text{ km} = 7,020 \text{ km} = 7.02 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$

**Step 2: Calculate the orbital velocities.**
Using $GM \approx 3.986 \times 10^{14} \text{ m}^3 \text{s}^{-2}$:
- $v_1 = \sqrt{\frac{3.986 \times 10^{14}}{7.22 \times 10^6}} \approx 7,436 \text{ m s}^{-1}$
- $v_2 = \sqrt{\frac{3.986 \times 10^{14}}{7.02 \times 10^6}} \approx 7,541 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

**Step 3: Find the difference.**
- $\Delta v = v_2 - v_1 = 7,541 - 7,436 = 105 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

Since the satellite is moving to a lower orbit where a higher speed is required for a stable orbit, the speed must **increase by $105 \text{ m s}^{-1}$**.
