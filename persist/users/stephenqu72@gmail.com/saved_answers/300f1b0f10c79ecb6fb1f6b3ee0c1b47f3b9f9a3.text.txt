To determine the correct vector diagram, we must analyze the forces acting on the cyclist.

### Physics Analysis
1.  **Gravity ($mg$):** Acts vertically downwards (the green arrow in the diagrams).
2.  **Normal Force ($N$):** Acts perpendicular to the surface of the banked track (the red arrow in the diagrams).
3.  **Friction ($f$):** Since the cyclist is travelling **faster** than the design speed, they have a tendency to slide **up** the track. To prevent this, static friction must act **down the slope** of the track to provide the additional centripetal force required for the higher speed.

### Evaluating the Net Force
The resultant centripetal force ($F_c$) must be directed horizontally towards the center of the turn. The centripetal force is the vector sum of the horizontal components of the Normal force and the Friction force.
*   If the cyclist were at the design speed, the horizontal component of the normal force alone would provide exactly the required centripetal force.
*   Because the cyclist is moving faster, they need a greater net centripetal force. The downward-slope friction force has a horizontal component that points towards the center of the circle, adding to the horizontal component of the normal force.

### Comparing the Options
*   **Diagram (A):** Shows a force (blue) pointing down the slope. This represents friction. When you sum the vertical gravity vector, the perpendicular normal force (red), and this friction vector (blue), the resultant is a horizontal force directed towards the center of the turn. This is the physically correct configuration.
*   **Diagram (C):** Shows a force pointing up the slope, which would occur if the cyclist were travelling *slower* than the design speed.

Therefore, **Diagram (A)** is the correct illustration because the friction force must act down the slope to supply the extra centripetal force needed for the increased speed.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A)**.
