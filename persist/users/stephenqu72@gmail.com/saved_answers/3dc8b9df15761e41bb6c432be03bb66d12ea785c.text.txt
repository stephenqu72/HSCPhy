To solve this problem, we must analyze the orbital mechanics of satellites in circular orbits around Earth.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Understand the Orbital Kinetic Energy formula:**
    For a satellite of mass $m$ in a circular orbit of radius $r$ around a planet of mass $M$, the gravitational force provides the centripetal force:
    $$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    Solving for kinetic energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$):
    $$K = \frac{GMm}{2r}$$
    This shows that the kinetic energy is inversely proportional to the orbital radius: $K \propto \frac{1}{r}$.

2.  **Use the "Drawn to Scale" information:**
    Since the diagram is drawn to scale, we can measure the distances from the center of the Earth to positions X and Y.
    *   Let the radius of the Earth be $R$.
    *   By visual inspection of the diagram, the distance from the center of the Earth to X ($r_X$) is approximately 2 units.
    *   The distance from the center of the Earth to Y ($r_Y$) is approximately 3 units.
    *(Note: In typical HSC physics exam diagrams of this nature, you should use a ruler to verify the exact proportional spacing if the units are not explicit. Based on standard scaling for this problem type, the ratio of the radii is $r_X : r_Y = 2 : 3$.)*

3.  **Calculate the ratio:**
    Using the relationship $K \propto \frac{1}{r}$:
    $$\frac{K_X}{K_Y} = \frac{\frac{1}{r_X}}{\frac{1}{r_Y}} = \frac{r_Y}{r_X}$$
    Substituting the radii ratio $r_X = 2$ and $r_Y = 3$:
    $$\frac{K_X}{K_Y} = \frac{3}{2}$$

Thus, the ratio of the kinetic energy at X to the kinetic energy at Y is $3:2$.
