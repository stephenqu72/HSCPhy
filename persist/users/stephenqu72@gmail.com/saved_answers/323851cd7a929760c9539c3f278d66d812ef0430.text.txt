The correct answer is **A**.

### Explanation

The **photoelectric effect** is the phenomenon where electromagnetic radiation (light) incident on a material (typically a metal) causes the emission of electrons from the surface. For this to occur, the incident photons must have an energy $E = hf$ that is greater than or equal to the work function ($\Phi$) of the material.

Let’s evaluate each option:

*   **A. The electrical resistance of selenium decreases when light shines on it.**
    This is an example of the **photoconductive effect**, which is closely related to the photoelectric effect. When light shines on a semiconductor like selenium, photons provide enough energy to excite electrons from the valence band to the conduction band. These additional free charge carriers increase the conductivity of the material, thereby decreasing its electrical resistance. This confirms the particle nature of light, which is the core principle behind the photoelectric effect.
*   **B. Light is emitted when an electron decelerates as it travels near a nucleus.**
    This describes **Bremsstrahlung** (or braking radiation). It is an effect observed in X-ray production, not the photoelectric effect.
*   **C. Light is emitted when an electron in an atom moves to a lower energy level.**
    This describes the process of **atomic emission/photon emission** (related to Bohr's model of the atom), where energy is released as a photon when an electron transitions between discrete energy levels.
*   **D. A current-carrying wire produces light at a wavelength dependent on its temperature.**
    This refers to **black-body radiation** (incandescence). The relationship between the temperature of an object and the wavelength of the light it emits is described by Wien's Displacement Law.

Therefore, since the photoelectric effect demonstrates that light energy is quantized and can interact with electrons to liberate them or change their state, **Option A** is the observation that aligns with the principles of the photoelectric effect.
