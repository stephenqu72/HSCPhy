To solve this problem, we use the formula for the magnetic force per unit length between two long, straight, parallel current-carrying wires.

### 1. Identify the relevant formula
The magnitude of the force per unit length ($F/L$) between two parallel wires is given by the formula:
$$\frac{F}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2 \pi d}$$

Where:
*   $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space, which is approximately $4 \pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ T m A}^{-1}$.
*   $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the currents in the two wires (in Amperes).
*   $d$ is the separation distance between the wires (in metres).

### 2. Substitute the given values
From the question, we have:
*   $I_1 = 1 \text{ A}$
*   $I_2 = 1 \text{ A}$
*   $d = 1 \text{ m}$

Plugging these into the formula:
$$\frac{F}{L} = \frac{(4 \pi \times 10^{-7}) \times 1 \times 1}{2 \pi \times 1}$$

### 3. Calculate the result
Cancel out the $\pi$ terms and simplify the constants:
$$\frac{F}{L} = \frac{4 \pi \times 10^{-7}}{2 \pi}$$
$$\frac{F}{L} = 2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ N m}^{-1}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, it corresponds to **(A)**.

### Conclusion
The force per metre length between the wires is $2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ N m}^{-1}$.
