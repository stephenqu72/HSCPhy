The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we must understand the physics behind Millikan’s oil drop experiment. 

1.  **The Equilibrium Condition:** When an oil drop is suspended, the downward gravitational force ($F_g = mg$) is exactly balanced by the upward electric force ($F_e = qE = q\frac{V}{d}$). Setting these equal gives:
    $$mg = q\frac{V}{d}$$
    where $m$ is mass, $g$ is gravity, $q$ is the charge on the drop, $V$ is the potential difference, and $d$ is the distance between the plates.

2.  **The Problem with a Single Drop:** Calculating the absolute charge $q$ on a single drop is extremely difficult because it requires precise knowledge of the drop's mass ($m$), which depends on its density and radius (determined via Stokes' Law). Small errors in calculating radius lead to large errors in mass and thus charge.

3.  **Millikan’s Breakthrough:** Millikan realized that the total charge $q$ on a drop must be an integer multiple of the elementary charge ($e$), such that $q = ne$. He observed how the potential difference $V$ had to change to keep a drop suspended if its charge changed (e.g., due to X-ray ionization). 

4.  **Why (C) is correct:** Millikan looked at the **differences** in charge between various states of a single drop. By analyzing the change in the potential difference required to maintain equilibrium as the charge on the drop changed by integer amounts of $e$, he found the greatest common divisor of all the measured charges. This allowed him to conclude that all charges were multiples of a fundamental unit of charge ($e$), effectively cancelling out the uncertainties associated with the mass/radius of the drops.

*   (B) is irrelevant to the static suspension equilibrium.
