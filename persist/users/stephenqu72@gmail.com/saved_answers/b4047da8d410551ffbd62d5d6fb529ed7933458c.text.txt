To solve this problem, we must analyze the forces acting on the driver in both the "peak" (hill) and "trough" (valley) positions using the principles of circular motion.

### 1. Identify the Forces at the Peak
At the peak of the hill, the net force providing the centripetal acceleration is the difference between the gravitational force ($mg$) and the normal force ($F_N$):
$$F_{net} = mg - F_{N, peak} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Given:
*   $m = 80\text{ kg}$
*   $g \approx 10\text{ m/s}^2$ (standard approximation for HSC Physics)
*   $F_{N, peak} = 400\text{ N}$
*   $mg = 80 \times 10 = 800\text{ N}$

Substituting these values:
$$800 - 400 = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$400 = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

### 2. Identify the Forces at the Trough
At the bottom of the valley, the normal force acts upward (against gravity) to provide the centripetal acceleration. The net force is the difference between the normal force ($F_N$) and the gravitational force ($mg$):
$$F_{net} = F_{N, valley} - mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Since the speed ($v$) and radius ($r$) are constant, the value of $\frac{mv^2}{r}$ is the same as calculated above ($400\text{ N}$):
$$F_{N, valley} - 800 = 400$$

### 3. Solve for $F_{N, valley}$
$$F_{N, valley} = 400 + 800 = 1200\text{ N}$$

The magnitude of the normal force experienced by the driver at the bottom of the valley is **1200 N**.

The correct option is **(B)**.
