To determine the correct set of axes to produce a linear graph, we must first derive the mathematical relationship between the horizontal range ($s_x$) and the vertical height ($h$) for a horizontally launched projectile.

### Step 1: Derive the equations of motion
For a projectile launched horizontally from a height $h$ with an initial velocity $u_x$:

1.  **Vertical motion:** Using the displacement formula $s_y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2} a_y t^2$, where $u_y = 0$, $s_y = h$, and $a_y = g$:
    $$h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
    Solving for time $t$:
    $$t^2 = \frac{2h}{g} \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}$$

2.  **Horizontal motion:** Since there is no acceleration in the horizontal direction, the range $s_x$ is:
    $$s_x = u_x t$$

### Step 2: Relate $s_x$ and $h$
Substitute the expression for $t$ into the horizontal displacement equation:
$$s_x = u_x \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}$$

To see the relationship more clearly, square both sides:
$$s_x^2 = u_x^2 \left( \frac{2h}{g} \right)$$
$$s_x^2 = \left( \frac{2u_x^2}{g} \right) h$$

### Step 3: Analyze the linear form
A linear graph follows the form $y = mx + c$. Comparing our equation $s_x^2 = (\frac{2u_x^2}{g})h$ to this:
*   $y = s_x^2$
*   $x = h$
*   $m = \frac{2u_x^2}{g}$ (constant slope)

This shows that plotting $s_x^2$ on the y-axis against $h$ on the x-axis will result in a straight line passing through the origin.

Looking at the options provided:
*   Option C represents the axes $s_x^2$ vs $h$.

**Answer: C**
