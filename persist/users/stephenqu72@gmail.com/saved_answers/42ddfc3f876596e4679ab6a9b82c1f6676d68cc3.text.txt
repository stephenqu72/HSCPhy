### Model Answer

**The Motor Effect and Torque**
The motor effect occurs when a current-carrying conductor placed in an external magnetic field experiences a force. The magnitude is given by $F = BIl \sin(\theta)$. Torque ($\tau$) is the turning effect of this force, defined as $\tau = Fd$, where $d$ is the perpendicular distance from the pivot. By maximizing the perpendicular distance and the force (by increasing $B$, $I$, or $n$ turns), we produce continuous rotation.

**DC Motors**
In a DC motor, current flows through a coil placed in a magnetic field. According to the motor effect, the opposite sides of the coil experience forces in opposite directions, creating a couple that exerts torque. A split-ring commutator reverses the current direction every half-turn, ensuring the torque always acts in the same direction, maintaining continuous rotation.

**Induction Motors**
Induction motors also rely on the motor effect but lack physical connections to the rotor. A rotating magnetic field (RMF) is produced by the stator coils. This RMF cuts the bars of the "squirrel-cage" rotor, inducing an EMF (Faraday’s Law) and a resulting current (Lenz’s Law). The rotor bars, now carrying current within the stator's magnetic field, experience a force via the motor effect. This force exerts a torque that causes the rotor to "chase" the RMF.
