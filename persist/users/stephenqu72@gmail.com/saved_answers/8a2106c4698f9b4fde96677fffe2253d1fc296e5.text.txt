To solve this problem, we must apply Newton's Second Law of Motion ($F_{net} = ma$) to a mass suspended in an elevator (lift) under different acceleration conditions. Let $m$ be the mass, $T$ be the tension in the cord (the measured weight force), and $g$ be the acceleration due to gravity.

### 1. Stationary ($W_S$)
When the lift is stationary (or moving at constant velocity), the net force is zero.
$$T - mg = 0 \implies T = mg$$
So, $W_S = mg$.

### 2. Accelerating Upwards ($W_U$)
When the lift accelerates upwards with acceleration $a$, the net force must be upward:
$$T - mg = ma \implies T = m(g + a)$$
Since $a > 0$, it follows that $W_U = m(g + a) > mg$. Therefore, **$W_U > W_S$**.

### 3. Accelerating Downwards ($W_D$)
When the lift accelerates downwards with acceleration $a$, the net force must be downward:
$$mg - T = ma \implies T = m(g - a)$$
Since $a > 0$, it follows that $W_D = m(g - a) < mg$. Therefore, **$W_D < W_S$**.

### Conclusion
Combining these inequalities:
$$W_D < W_S < W_U$$

This corresponds to option **(A)**.

---

### Explanation Summary
When you are in a lift:
*   **Moving up:** You feel "heavier" because the floor/ceiling needs to exert extra force to accelerate you upward against gravity.
*   **Moving down:** You feel "lighter" because gravity is already helping the downward acceleration, requiring less force to hold you up.
*   **Stationary:** You feel your "true" weight ($mg$).

Comparing these, the upward acceleration produces the highest measured weight, and downward acceleration produces the lowest.
