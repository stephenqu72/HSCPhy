To solve this problem, we must recognise that a satellite cannot orbit closer than the Earth's surface (i.e., its radius $r$ cannot be less than the Earth's radius $R_E$).

### Explanation
The period of a satellite depends on its orbital radius $r$. As the radius $r$ decreases, the orbital period $T$ decreases. The minimum possible period occurs when the satellite is at the lowest possible orbital altitude, which is right at the Earth's surface ($r = R_E$). Using Kepler’s Third Law or equating centripetal force to gravitational force, we find the period for a circular orbit at the surface. Any orbit smaller than this would require the satellite to travel through the Earth, which is physically impossible for a stable orbit.

### Calculation
We use the formula for orbital period:
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM}}$$

Where:
- $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$
- $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$ (mass of Earth)
- $r = R_E = 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$ (radius of Earth)

Substitute the values:
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{(6.37 \times 10^6)^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}}$$
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{2.58 \times 10^{20}}{3.98 \times 10^{14}}}$$
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{648241} \approx 2\pi \times 805.13 \approx 5057 \text{ seconds}$$

Convert seconds to minutes:
$$T \approx \frac{5057}{60} \approx 84.3 \text{ minutes}$$

Rounding to standard values, this gives approximately 85 minutes. Since any orbit must be at a radius $r \ge R_E$, the period $T$ must be $\ge 85$ minutes.
