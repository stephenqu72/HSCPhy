To solve this problem, we must first recall the formula for the separation between adjacent bright fringes (maxima) in a double-slit interference pattern, which is given by:

$$\Delta x = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$$

Where:
- $\Delta x$ is the distance between maxima (the distance between $A$ and $B$).
- $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light.
- $L$ is the distance from the slits to the screen (labeled $y$ in the diagram).
- $d$ is the distance between the slits.

### The Question
The goal is to keep $\Delta x$ constant when the wavelength ($\lambda$) is **reduced**. Since $\Delta x$ is directly proportional to $\lambda$, reducing $\lambda$ will cause $\Delta x$ to decrease. To keep $\Delta x$ constant, we must adjust the other variables ($L$ or $d$) to counteract this decrease.

### Solution

**Method 1: Decrease the slit separation ($d$)**
From the formula, $\Delta x \propto \frac{1}{d}$. If we decrease $d$, the value of $\Delta x$ will increase. By decreasing $d$ in the same proportion as we decrease $\lambda$, the ratio $\frac{\lambda}{d}$ remains constant, keeping the distance between maxima constant.

**Method 2: Increase the distance to the screen ($L$ or $y$)**
From the formula, $\Delta x \propto L$. If we increase the distance $L$, the value of $\Delta x$ will increase. By increasing the screen distance $y$ in the same proportion as we decrease the wavelength, the product $\lambda L$ remains constant, keeping the distance between maxima constant.
