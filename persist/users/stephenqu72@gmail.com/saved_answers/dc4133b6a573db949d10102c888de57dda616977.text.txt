To solve this problem, we use Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, defined by the formula:

$$E = mc^2$$

Where:
- $E$ is the energy in Joules (J).
- $m$ is the mass lost in kilograms (kg).
- $c$ is the speed of light in a vacuum ($3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$).

### Step-by-Step Calculation:

**1. Calculate the energy in Joules:**
Given the mass lost $m = 3.07 \times 10^{-28} \text{ kg}$, we substitute this into the equation:
$$E = (3.07 \times 10^{-28} \text{ kg}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})^2$$
$$E = (3.07 \times 10^{-28}) \times (9.00 \times 10^{16})$$
$$E = 2.763 \times 10^{-11} \text{ Joules}$$

**2. Convert Joules to electron-volts (eV):**
To convert Joules to eV, we divide the energy in Joules by the charge of an electron ($1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/eV}$):
$$\text{Energy in eV} = \frac{2.763 \times 10^{-11} \text{ J}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/eV}}$$
$$\text{Energy in eV} \approx 1.7247 \times 10^8 \text{ eV}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, we find it matches option **D**.

**Teacher's Tip:** Always remember that Einstein's formula $E=mc^2$ outputs energy in **Joules** because the mass must be in **kg**. If a question asks for energy in **eV**, you must perform the conversion at the final step by dividing by the elementary charge ($1.602 \times 10^{-19}$).
