To determine the correct graph, we must look at the physics formula for the total time of flight ($t$) of a projectile launched from level ground.

### Explanation

1.  **The Formula:** The time of flight for a projectile launched with an initial velocity $v$ at an angle $\theta$ is given by:
    $$t = \frac{2v \sin(\theta)}{g}$$
    where $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity.

2.  **The Relationship:** Assuming the initial velocity ($v$) is constant, the time of flight $t$ is directly proportional to $\sin(\theta)$. 
    *   At a launch angle of $\theta = 0^\circ$, $\sin(0^\circ) = 0$, so $t = 0$.
    *   At a launch angle of $\theta = 90^\circ$, $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$, which gives the maximum time of flight.
    *   For angles between $0^\circ$ and $90^\circ$, the value of $\sin(\theta)$ increases as $\theta$ increases.

3.  **Analyzing the Graphs:**
    *   **Graph (A):** Shows a linear relationship. While $\sin(\theta)$ is somewhat linear for small angles, it is not linear across the full range to $90^\circ$.
    *   **Graph (B):** This graph implies that as the angle increases from $0^\circ$ to $90^\circ$, the time increases and then decreases back to zero. This is incorrect for the range $0^\circ$ to $90^\circ$.
    *   **Graph (C):** While the sine function is non-linear, it actually "curves" downward (concave down) as it approaches $90^\circ$, not upward as shown in (C). However, in many introductory physics contexts, if students are asked to sketch the relationship between $\sin(\theta)$ and time, it would be linear. Since we are graphing against $\theta$ directly, we are looking for the shape of the sine curve from $0$ to $\pi/2$.
