### Explanation
To solve this, you need to understand the historical context of late 19th-century physics:

1.  **The Nature of Waves:** During the 19th century, physicists like Thomas Young (with his double-slit experiment) provided strong evidence that light behaved as a wave, exhibiting phenomena such as interference and diffraction.
2.  **The Mechanical Requirement:** At the time, all known waves (such as sound waves or water waves) were **mechanical waves**. By definition, mechanical waves require a physical medium to transfer energy from one point to another. 
3.  **The Logic Gap:** Since light was observed to travel through the vacuum of space (e.g., from the Sun to the Earth), and physicists could not conceive of a "wave" existing without a medium, they hypothesized the existence of a mysterious, invisible substance that permeated the entire universe. They called this substance the **luminiferous aether**.
4.  **Evaluating the distractors:**
    *   **A is incorrect** because the constancy of the speed of light is a postulate of Einstein's Special Relativity, which eventually *disproved* the necessity of the aether.
    *   **B is incorrect** because "relative to the motion of the aether" is a concept that came out of the failed attempts to detect the aether (like the Michelson-Morley experiment), not the reason the model was adopted in the first place.
    *   **D is incorrect** because it misrepresents historical observations; light was not thought to slow down due to a particulate nature in the context of the aether theory.

In summary, the aether was a theoretical construct created to satisfy the requirement that all waves must travel through a medium.
