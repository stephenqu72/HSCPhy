$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

### Analysis of the Graph and Options
The graph shows a **linear relationship** (a straight line passing through the origin) between the gravitational field strength ($g$) and the variable on the $x$-axis. This means we are looking for a relationship of the form $y = mx$, where $y = g$ and $x$ is some function of $r$.

1.  **Look at the options:** The options involve $r$ and $M$. We know the standard inverse-square law is $g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$.
2.  **Rearrange the formula:** If we want a linear graph, we can treat $g$ as the dependent variable ($y$) and identify what the $x$-axis must be. 
    *   If we plot $g$ against $\frac{1}{r^2}$, the equation becomes:
        $$g = (GM) \cdot \left(\frac{1}{r^2}\right)$$
    *   Comparing this to $y = mx$:
        *   $y = g$
        *   $x = \frac{1}{r^2}$ (which is the inverse square of the distance)
        *   $m$ (the gradient) $= GM$

3.  **Evaluate the options:**
    *   **Option A:** Suggests $x = r$. If $x = r$, $g$ is proportional to $1/r^2$, which would be a curve, not a straight line.
    *   **Option B:** Suggests $x = \frac{1}{r^2}$ and Gradient $= GM$. This matches our derivation perfectly.

    *   **Option D:** Suggests $x = M$. While $g \propto M$, the gradient would be $\frac{G}{r^2}$, not $\frac{G}{r}$.

### Conclusion
The correct answer is **B**. By plotting gravitational field strength against the inverse square of the distance, the relationship becomes linear with a gradient equal to the product of the universal gravitational constant and the mass of the planet ($GM$).
