To solve this problem, we need to determine if the current $I$ required to produce a torque $\tau = 2.95\text{ Nm}$ exceeds the $5\text{ A}$ limit of the circuit breaker.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Determine the angular velocity ($\omega$) at $\tau = 2.95\text{ Nm}$:**
Looking at the graph, the line passes through $(0, 160)$ and $(4, 80)$. We can find the equation of the line using the gradient-intercept form $y = mx + c$:
- Gradient ($m$) = $\frac{80 - 160}{4 - 0} = \frac{-80}{4} = -20$
- Equation: $\omega = -20\tau + 160$

Substitute $\tau = 2.95\text{ Nm}$ into the equation:
$$\omega = -20(2.95) + 160$$
$$\omega = -59 + 160 = 101\text{ rad s}^{-1}$$

**2. Calculate the required current ($I$):**
Using the provided efficiency formula:
$$\tau = \frac{V I \eta}{\omega}$$
Rearrange to solve for $I$:
$$I = \frac{\tau \omega}{V \eta}$$

Substitute the known values ($V = 240\text{ V}$, $\eta = 0.3$, $\tau = 2.95\text{ Nm}$, $\omega = 101\text{ rad s}^{-1}$):
$$I = \frac{2.95 \times 101}{240 \times 0.3}$$
$$I = \frac{297.95}{72}$$
$$I \approx 4.14\text{ A}$$

**3. Conclusion:**
Since the calculated current $I \approx 4.14\text{ A}$ is less than the circuit breaker limit of $5\text{ A}$, the circuit breaker will **not** trip. The motor will continue to operate normally at this torque.
