### Solution and Explanation

#### (a) Identify stars undergoing proton-proton chain nucleosynthesis
**Answer:** The stars on the **Main Sequence** (the diagonal line of points running from top-left to bottom-right). Specifically, these are stars like **T** and **U**.

**Explanation:** The proton-proton chain is the primary fusion process for stars on the Main Sequence that have masses similar to or less than our Sun. Stars like R (supergiant) or B (giant) have core temperatures high enough for other processes (like the CNO cycle or helium burning).

#### (b) Calculate energy released
**Step 1: Calculate the mass defect ($\Delta m$) for both reactions.**
First reaction: $^1\text{H} + ^1\text{H} \rightarrow ^2\text{H} + e^+ + \nu$
Initial mass: $2 \times 1.00784 = 2.01568 \text{ amu}$
Final mass: $2.014 + 0.000548756 = 2.014548756 \text{ amu}$
$\Delta m_1 = 2.01568 - 2.014548756 = 0.001131244 \text{ amu}$

Second reaction: $^2\text{H} + ^1\text{H} \rightarrow ^3\text{He} + \gamma$
Initial mass: $2.014 + 1.00784 = 3.02184 \text{ amu}$
Final mass: $3.016029 \text{ amu}$
$\Delta m_2 = 3.02184 - 3.016029 = 0.005811 \text{ amu}$

**Step 2: Total mass defect and energy conversion.**
Total $\Delta m = 0.001131244 + 0.005811 = 0.006942244 \text{ amu}$
Using the conversion $1 \text{ amu} = 931.5 \text{ MeV}$:
Energy $= 0.006942244 \times 931.5 \approx 6.467 \text{ MeV}$

#### (c) ii. Compare and contrast B and S
**Comparison:**
*   **Luminosity:** Star B has a very high absolute magnitude (very luminous), whereas Star S has a low absolute magnitude (low luminosity).
*   **Temperature:** Star B is a red star (cooler surface temperature, located on the right side of the H-R diagram). Star S is a white dwarf (hot surface temperature, located on the bottom-left of the H-R diagram).
*   **Size:** Star B is physically massive (a supergiant), while Star S is extremely small (roughly Earth-sized).
*   **Evolutionary Stage:** Star B is an evolved star that has left the Main Sequence to burn heavier elements; Star S is the final evolutionary stage of a low-to-medium mass star, no longer undergoing fusion.
