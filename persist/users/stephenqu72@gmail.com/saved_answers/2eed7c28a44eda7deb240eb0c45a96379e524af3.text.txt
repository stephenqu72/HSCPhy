To determine which satellite requires the greatest escape velocity, we must refer to the physics of gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy.

### Answer
The correct answer is **D**.

---

### Explanation
The escape velocity ($v_e$) is defined as the minimum speed required for an object to escape the gravitational influence of a massive body, such as Earth. We derive this by equating the kinetic energy of the satellite to the magnitude of its gravitational potential energy at a distance $r$ from the center of the Earth:

$$\frac{1}{2}mv_e^2 = \frac{GMm}{r}$$

Where:
*   $m$ is the mass of the satellite.
*   $M$ is the mass of the Earth.
*   $G$ is the gravitational constant.
*   $r$ is the distance from the center of the Earth.

Notice that the mass of the satellite ($m$) appears on both sides of the equation and cancels out:

$$\frac{1}{2}v_e^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$$
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}$$

From this formula, we can conclude two vital points:
1.  **Mass Independence:** The escape velocity is **independent** of the mass of the satellite. Therefore, the differences in mass ($1600\text{ kg}$, $900\text{ kg}$, $500\text{ kg}$, $200\text{ kg}$) are "distractors" and do not affect the result.
2.  **Distance Dependence:** The escape velocity is inversely proportional to the square root of the distance from the center of the Earth ($v_e \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}$). 

To achieve the **greatest** escape velocity, we need the **smallest** value of $r$. 
*   In option A, $r = 4r$
*   In option B, $r = 3r$
*   In option C, $r = 2r$
*   In option D, $r = r$

Since option D has the smallest distance from the center of the Earth, the satellite in option D requires the greatest escape velocity.
