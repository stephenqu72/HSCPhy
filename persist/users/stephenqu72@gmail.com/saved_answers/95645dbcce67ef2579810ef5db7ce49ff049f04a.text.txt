### Answer
The correct answer is **a) The apparent magnitude of a star measured from a distance of 10 parsecs.**

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to recall the standard astrophysical definitions regarding stellar brightness and distance:

1.  **Apparent Magnitude ($m$):** This is how bright a star appears to an observer on Earth. It depends on both the intrinsic luminosity of the star and its distance from us. Because stars are at different distances, apparent magnitude does not allow for a direct comparison of their true power output (luminosity).
2.  **Absolute Magnitude ($M$):** To compare the true luminosities of stars, astronomers use a standardized "benchmark" distance. Absolute magnitude is defined as the apparent magnitude a star would have if it were placed at a standard distance of exactly $10 \text{ parsecs}$ ($32.6 \text{ light-years}$) from the observer.

**Evaluating the options:**
*   **a) Correct:** This aligns exactly with the standard definition of absolute magnitude. By placing all stars at the same distance ($10 \text{ pc}$), we eliminate the effect of distance, allowing $M$ to serve as a measure of the star's intrinsic luminosity.
*   **b) Incorrect:** This is the definition of *apparent* magnitude.
*   **c) Incorrect:** This refers to a different standard distance (1 parsec is sometimes used for the definition of parsec itself, but not for the definition of absolute magnitude).
*   **d) Incorrect:** This describes luminosity or bolometric energy output, not the magnitude scale.
