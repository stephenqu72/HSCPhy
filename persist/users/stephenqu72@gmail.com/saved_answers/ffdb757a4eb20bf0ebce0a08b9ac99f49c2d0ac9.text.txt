### Model Answer

The evolution of physics is driven by the interaction between theory and experimental evidence.

**1. Abandoning Outdated Models: The Michelson-Morley Experiment**
The 19th-century "luminiferous aether" theory proposed that light waves required a medium to propagate. The Michelson-Morley experiment (1887) used an interferometer to detect the Earth's motion through this aether. The null result—finding no shift in interference patterns regardless of the orientation of the apparatus—directly contradicted the aether model. This provided the essential empirical evidence that led to the abandonment of the aether and paved the way for Einstein’s Theory of Special Relativity, which postulates that the speed of light $c$ is constant in all inertial frames.

**2. Developing New Models: The Photoelectric Effect**
Classical wave theory could not explain why light below a certain threshold frequency failed to eject electrons from a metal surface, regardless of intensity. In 1905, Einstein analyzed experimental data on the photoelectric effect and proposed that light consists of discrete quanta, or photons, with energy $E = hf$. This new model successfully explained the experimental observations (e.g., the relationship between the kinetic energy of emitted electrons and the frequency of incident radiation) and fundamentally shifted physics toward quantum theory.

**3. Validating Existing Models: The Hafele-Keating Experiment**
While Special Relativity provided a new model, it required rigorous validation. The Hafele-Keating experiment (1971) confirmed the predictions of time dilation. By transporting atomic clocks on commercial airliners and comparing them to stationary clocks, researchers observed that the flying clocks ticked at different rates, consistent with the relativistic calculations for velocity and gravity. This confirmed the validity of Einstein’s General and Special Relativity under real-world conditions, strengthening their status as fundamental models of the universe.

### How to solve this type of question
1.  **Structure your essay:** Use clear subheadings or paragraphs for each requirement (Create, Validate, Abandon).
2.  **Be specific:** Do not talk in generalizations. Name the experiments (e.g., Michelson-Morley) and the specific physical principles (e.g., aether vs. constant speed of light).
3.  **Link evidence to the model:** You must explain *why* the result changed the theory. For example, mention that the null result of Michelson-Morley forced physicists to discard the necessity of a medium for light propagation.
4.  **Use correct terminology:** Use terms like "empirical evidence," "falsifiability," "relativistic," and "quantum nature" to demonstrate your depth of knowledge.
