To determine the mass of the Sun using Kepler’s Third Law, we start with the standard equation:

$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

Rearranging this to solve for $T^2$, we get:

$$T^2 = \left( \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} \right) r^3$$

### Explanation of the Method
In physics experiments, plotting data is the most reliable way to determine a constant because it allows you to visualize the relationship, identify outliers, and calculate a gradient that represents the line of best fit, which effectively averages out experimental errors.

Comparing the equation above to the linear equation $y = mx + c$:
*   **$y$** is the dependent variable: $T^2$ (square of the orbital period).
*   **$x$** is the independent variable: $r^3$ (cube of the orbital radius).
*   **$m$** (the gradient) is: $\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$.

By plotting $T^2$ on the y-axis and $r^3$ on the x-axis, the graph will be a straight line passing through the origin. The gradient ($m$) of this line will be equal to $\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$. Therefore, we can calculate the mass of the Sun ($M$) by rearranging the gradient formula:

$$M = \frac{4\pi^2}{G \cdot \text{gradient}}$$
