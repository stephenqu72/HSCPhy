### Answer
The correct answer is **(A)**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the shift from the Bohr model of the atom to the quantum mechanical model proposed by Erwin Schrödinger:

1.  **The Bohr Model:** Niels Bohr proposed that electrons move in fixed, defined circular orbits at specific energy levels around the nucleus. This implied that at any given time, an electron had a definite "position."
2.  **Schrödinger’s Advancement:** Schrödinger applied the concept of wave-particle duality (de Broglie's hypothesis) to electrons. He developed a wave equation that treats the electron as a wave function. This approach indicates that we cannot know both the precise position and momentum of an electron simultaneously (which is also supported by the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle).
3.  **From Orbits to Orbitals:** Instead of fixed orbits, Schrödinger’s model introduced **orbitals**, which are mathematical regions in space where there is a high probability ($90\%$) of finding an electron. By moving away from fixed paths, Schrödinger **removed the certainty** of an electron's exact position, replacing it with the concept of probability clouds.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(B)** is the opposite of the truth; Schrödinger introduced probability, thereby removing certainty.
*   **(C)** is partially true in concept (electrons exhibit wave-like behavior), but it is incomplete and does not define the *advancement* in atomic theory as accurately as the shift in the understanding of position.
*   **(D)** is incorrect because modern physics recognizes the electron as having both wave and particle characteristics (wave-particle duality), not that it is "not a particle."
