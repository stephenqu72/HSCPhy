### Answer

The student’s observation that increasing the intensity of light does not trigger photoelectric emission—when the light frequency is below the threshold frequency—provides critical evidence for the **particle (photon) model of light** over the classical wave model.

1.  **Classical Wave Theory Failure:** According to classical wave theory, light is a continuous wave. If intensity (amplitude) were increased, the energy delivered to the metal surface per unit area would increase. One would expect that if an electron were given enough time to absorb sufficient energy from the wave, it would eventually be emitted, regardless of the frequency. The fact that emission does *not* occur proves this theory incorrect for the photoelectric effect.
2.  **Photon Model Success:** Einstein proposed that light consists of discrete "packets" of energy called photons. The energy of a single photon is given by the equation $E = hf$, where $h$ is Planck's constant and $f$ is the frequency. 
3.  **Threshold Frequency ($f_0$):** Photoelectric emission occurs only if a single photon has enough energy to overcome the metal's work function ($\Phi$). If the frequency is below the threshold frequency ($f < f_0$), then $hf < \Phi$. 
4.  **Evidence:** Increasing the intensity simply increases the *number* of photons hitting the surface per second; it does not change the energy of individual photons. Since no single photon has enough energy to eject an electron, increasing the number of "insufficiently energetic" photons does nothing to cause emission. This confirms that light interacts with matter as discrete particles, not as a continuous wave.
