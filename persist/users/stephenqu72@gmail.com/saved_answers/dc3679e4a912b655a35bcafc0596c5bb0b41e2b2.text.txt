Hello! As an HSC Physics teacher, I'm happy to walk you through this circular motion problem. This is a classic question that tests your understanding of centripetal force and how gravity interacts with tension in vertical systems.

---

### Part (a)
To find the maximum speed, we use the formula for centripetal force:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Given: $m = 0.50\ kg$, $r = 1.2\ m$, and maximum tension $F = 5.0\ N$.
Setting the tension equal to the centripetal force:
$$5.0 = \frac{0.50 \cdot v^2}{1.2}$$
$$5.0 \cdot 1.2 = 0.50 \cdot v^2$$
$$6.0 = 0.50 \cdot v^2$$
$$v^2 = 12$$
$$v = \sqrt{12} \approx 3.46\ m/s$$

---

### Part (b)
When the string breaks at point $P$, the centripetal force disappears. According to Newton’s First Law, the ball will continue to move in a straight line at its current velocity. Therefore, you should draw a **straight line tangent to the circle** at point $P$, pointing in the direction of the ball's instantaneous motion.

---

### Part (c)
The tension is not uniform because gravity acts on the ball differently depending on its position in the vertical circle.

1.  **At the bottom of the circle:** The string must provide the centripetal force *and* overcome the weight of the ball.
    $$T - mg = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies T = \frac{mv^2}{r} + mg$$
    This is where tension is **maximum**.
2.  **At the top of the circle:** Both tension and gravity act towards the center to provide the centripetal force.
    $$T + mg = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies T = \frac{mv^2}{r} - mg$$
    This is where tension is **minimum**.

**Diagram suggestion:** Draw a circle showing the ball at the top and bottom. At the bottom, draw a long arrow for $T$ upwards and a short arrow for $mg$ downwards. At the top, draw a short arrow for $T$ downwards and an arrow for $mg$ downwards.
