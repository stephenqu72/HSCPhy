### Answer
The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
This problem is a classic application of **Galilean Relativity** (or the principle of relative velocity).

1.  **Understand the frame of reference:** The archer is moving horizontally at a speed $v$ relative to the ground. When the archer fires the arrow, the arrow inherits this initial horizontal velocity $v$ because it is moving along with the horse and rider.
2.  **Vector analysis:**
    *   The archer aims the arrow toward the target. In the archer's reference frame, the arrow travels in a straight line toward the bullseye.
    *   However, from the perspective of the target (the ground frame), the arrow has two velocity components:
        *   $v_y$: The velocity directed toward the target (the intended path).
        *   $v_x$: The velocity inherited from the horse's motion, directed in the direction the horse is traveling.
3.  **Resultant motion:** Because the arrow has a forward component of velocity $v_x$, it will "drift" in the direction of the horse's travel while it is in the air. If the archer aimed directly at the bullseye (as shown in option C), the arrow would strike the target at a point displaced forward in the direction of the horse's motion.
4.  **Compensating for the drift:** To hit the bullseye, the archer must aim "behind" the target (relative to the direction of motion). By aiming at the point shown in **B**, the forward velocity $v_x$ carries the arrow forward during its flight, causing it to arrive exactly at the center of the bullseye.
