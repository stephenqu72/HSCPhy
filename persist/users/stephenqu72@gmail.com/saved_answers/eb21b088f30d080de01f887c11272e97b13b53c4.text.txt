### Explanation
To solve this, we must compare the characteristics of the forces involved in both types of motion:

1.  **Projectile Motion:** In the absence of air resistance, the only force acting on a projectile is gravity. Gravity acts vertically downwards. Relative to the ground (our frame of reference), this direction is **constant** (always pointing towards the center of the Earth).
2.  **Uniform Circular Motion:** For an object to move in a circle at a constant speed, it requires a centripetal force directed toward the center of the circle. Because the object is moving along the circumference, the direction of its velocity is constantly changing (tangent to the circle). The centripetal force must always be **perpendicular** to this instantaneous velocity vector to change its direction without changing its magnitude (speed).

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** In projectile motion, the *velocity* is constantly changing (because of the downward acceleration), so the claim that motion is in a "constant direction" is physically imprecise.
*   **C:** In projectile motion, the acceleration (gravity) is vertical, while the motion is parabolic. The acceleration is rarely in the same direction as the motion (except at the very peak of the trajectory if we look at the horizontal component, but even then, it is not "in the same direction" as the instantaneous velocity).
*   **D:** While it is true that horizontal velocity is constant in projectile motion, "vertical motion is constant" in circular motion is incorrect; the vertical component of an object's velocity in a vertical circle changes continuously.

By identifying the fundamental nature of the forces—gravity being a constant field force and centripetal force being a dynamic, position-dependent vector—we confirm that **B** is the best scientific statement.
