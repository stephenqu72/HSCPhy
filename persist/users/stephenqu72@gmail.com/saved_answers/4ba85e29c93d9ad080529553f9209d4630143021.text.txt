### Model Answer

The analysis of stellar spectra provides critical data about a star's characteristics:

1.  **Chemical Composition:** Stars produce absorption spectra. As light from the hot, dense core passes through the cooler outer atmosphere, specific wavelengths are absorbed by atoms and ions. By comparing these dark absorption lines to known laboratory spectra, astronomers can identify the elements present in the stellar atmosphere.
2.  **Surface Temperature:** The continuous part of the spectrum (the blackbody curve) determines the peak wavelength ($\lambda_{max}$). According to **Wien's Law** ($\lambda_{max} T = \text{constant}$), the peak wavelength is inversely proportional to temperature. Furthermore, the presence or absence of specific absorption lines (e.g., strong Hydrogen lines in A-type stars vs. molecules in M-type stars) is used to classify stars into spectral classes ($O, B, A, F, G, K, M$).
3.  **Radial Velocity:** The **Doppler Effect** causes a shift in the absorption lines. A "redshift" indicates the star is moving away from Earth, while a "blueshift" indicates it is moving toward Earth. The magnitude of the shift allows for the calculation of the star’s radial velocity.
4.  **Stellar Rotation:** Rapid rotation of a star causes the absorption lines to broaden due to the Doppler shift of light from the side of the star rotating toward the observer versus the side rotating away. This is known as pressure/rotational line broadening.
