### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) Random error**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the difference between the types of errors commonly encountered in HSC Physics depth studies:

1.  **Systematic Error:** These are errors that shift all measurements in a consistent direction (e.g., a scale that is always $5\text{ g}$ too heavy). If the launcher was consistently firing too fast or too slow due to a calibration fault, it would be a systematic error.
2.  **Random Error:** These are unpredictable variations that occur in measurements. They result from fluctuations in the environment or the apparatus that the experimenter cannot fully control. Because the launcher is "homemade" and does not produce a *consistent* initial velocity (meaning it fluctuates from shot to shot), the output is unpredictable. This variability in the independent variable makes the results inconsistent, which is the definition of a random error.
3.  **Human Error:** This is a broad term often used for mistakes (like misreading a scale), but in formal scientific analysis, we differentiate between systematic and random components rather than labeling them as "human error."
4.  **Instrumental Error:** While the launcher is an instrument, the *nature* of the inconsistency (fluctuation) classifies the *type* of error as random.

Since the initial velocity is not consistent (i.e., it is varying unpredictably), it introduces scatter into the data set, which is a hallmark of **random error**.
