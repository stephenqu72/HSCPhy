To earn full marks on this HSC Physics question, you need to demonstrate an understanding of the wave-particle duality of matter and the probabilistic nature of quantum mechanics.

### Answer
The observations demonstrate the **wave-particle duality** of electrons. 

1. **Particle nature:** Each individual electron arrives as a discrete, localized point on the detection screen, confirming that electrons behave as particles upon detection.
2. **Wave nature:** As the number of electrons ($N$) increases, the accumulated points form a characteristic interference pattern consisting of alternating maxima and minima. This indicates that each electron behaves as a wave that interferes with itself (or traverses both slits simultaneously as a probability wave).
3. **Probabilistic nature:** The intensity of the interference pattern at any point on the screen is proportional to the square of the amplitude of the electron's probability wavefunction, $|\psi|^2$. Initially, with small $N$, the pattern is obscured by randomness. As $N$ increases, the probability distribution emerges, confirming that while individual electron impacts are random, the collective behavior is deterministic and governed by wave mechanics.

### How to solve this
To structure your response for 4 marks, follow this checklist:

*   **Identify the duality:** State clearly that electrons exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties.
*   **Explain the "Particle" observation:** Reference the early images ($N=10, 100$) where individual hits appear random. This shows electrons are discrete entities (particles).
*   **Explain the "Wave" observation:** Reference the later images ($N=20,000, 70,000$) where a clear interference pattern emerges. This confirms electrons exhibit diffraction and interference, which are wave phenomena.
*   **Explain the Probability link:** Connect the two concepts by stating that the interference pattern represents the *probability distribution* of the electrons. The bright fringes are regions of high probability (constructive interference), and dark fringes are regions of low probability (destructive interference).
