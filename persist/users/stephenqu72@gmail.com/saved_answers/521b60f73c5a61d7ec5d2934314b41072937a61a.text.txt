The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation

To understand "weightlessness" in an orbiting spacecraft, we must look at the physics of freefall.

1.  **Define Weight:** In physics, "weight" is defined as the **normal force** ($F_N$) exerted on an object by a surface (like the floor of a spacecraft). When you stand on a scale, it measures the normal force pushing back against you.
2.  **The State of Orbit:** A spacecraft in orbit is in a state of **constant freefall**. Both the spacecraft and the astronaut inside are under the influence of Earth's gravity. According to Newton’s second law, $F = ma$, the acceleration of any object in a gravitational field is independent of its mass:
    $$a = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$
    Because both the spacecraft and the astronaut are at the same distance $r$ from the Earth's center, they both experience the exact same acceleration $a$ toward the Earth.
3.  **Why it feels "weightless":** Since both objects are accelerating downward at the same rate, there is no force being exerted by the floor of the spacecraft on the astronaut. Because the spacecraft is not "pushing" on the astronaut, the normal force $F_N$ is zero. Therefore, the astronaut perceives this state as "weightlessness."

**Analysis of other options:**
*   **(A) is incorrect** because Earth's gravity is very strong at orbital altitudes (often $80-90\%$ of gravity on the surface).
*   **(B) is incorrect** because the force Earth exerts on an object depends on its mass ($F = mg$). Since the spacecraft and astronaut have different masses, the gravitational forces on them are different.
*   **(D) is incorrect** because while they do exert forces on each other (Newton’s Third Law), this is not what causes the sensation of weightlessness; in fact, the lack of these forces is precisely why weightlessness is felt.
