To solve this problem efficiently, we use the **Law of Conservation of Mechanical Energy**. Since we are ignoring air resistance, the total mechanical energy of the stone at the top of the cliff is equal to the total mechanical energy just before it strikes the sea.

### 1. Identify the Energy at the Top
At the top of the cliff, the stone has both kinetic energy ($E_k$) and gravitational potential energy ($E_p$):
- Mass $m = 2.0\text{ kg}$
- Initial velocity $v_i = 20\text{ m s}^{-1}$
- Height $h = 15\text{ m}$
- Acceleration due to gravity $g \approx 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$

$$E_{total} = E_{k, \text{top}} + E_{p, \text{top}}$$
$$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 + mgh$$

Substituting the values:
$$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2} \times 2.0 \times (20)^2 + (2.0 \times 9.8 \times 15)$$
$$E_{total} = 400 + 294 = 694\text{ J}$$

### 2. Identify the Energy at the Bottom
Just before striking the sea, the height $h = 0$, so the gravitational potential energy is zero. All the mechanical energy is now in the form of kinetic energy ($E_{k, \text{bottom}}$):
$$E_{total} = E_{k, \text{bottom}} + 0$$
$$E_{k, \text{bottom}} = 694\text{ J}$$

**Final Answer:** The kinetic energy of the stone immediately before it strikes the sea is **$694\text{ J}$**.

*(Note: If your school uses $g = 9.81\text{ m s}^{-2}$, the result is $400 + 294.3 = 694.3\text{ J}$. Both are typically accepted in HSC physics.)*

---
### Explanation of Strategy
- **Why avoid kinematics?** You could calculate the vertical and horizontal components of velocity to find the final velocity vector, then use $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. However, energy conservation is significantly faster and less prone to algebraic error.
- **Key concept:** In a system with no non-conservative forces (like air resistance), $E_{initial} = E_{final}$. This is a classic HSC "shortcut" technique that saves time for more complex questions.
