### Answer
The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must apply the standard classification of subatomic particles within the Standard Model of particle physics:

1.  **Hadrons** are composite particles made of quarks held together by the strong nuclear force. Hadrons are further divided into two groups:
    *   **Baryons:** These are composite particles made of **three quarks** ($qqq$). Common examples include protons ($uud$) and neutrons ($udd$).
    *   **Mesons:** These are composite particles made of a quark and an antiquark pair ($q\bar{q}$).

2.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **(A)** is incorrect because a particle made of two quarks is not a standard classification; a meson is made of one quark and one antiquark.
    *   **(B)** is incorrect because leptons (like the muon) are fundamental particles, not composed of quarks.
    *   **(C)** is **correct** because, by definition, a baryon is a hadron made of three quarks.
    *   **(D)** is incorrect because baryons are fermions (half-integer spin), not bosons (integer spin).

In the HSC Physics curriculum, it is essential to distinguish between fundamental particles (like quarks and leptons) and composite particles (hadrons). Remembering the "three-quark" rule for baryons is a key requirement for success in the "From the Universe to the Atom" module.
