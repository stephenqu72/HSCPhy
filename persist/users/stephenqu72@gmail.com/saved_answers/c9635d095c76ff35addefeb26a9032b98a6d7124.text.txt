To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of **Special Relativity**, specifically **time dilation**.

### 1. Identify the given information
*   Time elapsed on Earth (stationary frame): $t = 17.6 \text{ years}$
*   Time elapsed for the astronaut (moving frame): $t_0 = 9.2 \text{ years}$

### 2. Explain the physics
An observer on Earth sees the clock on the spaceship moving slower than their own clock. This is time dilation. The relationship is given by:
$$t = \gamma t_0$$
Where $\gamma$ is the Lorentz factor: $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$.

However, to find the distance as measured by an observer on Earth ($d$), we use the speed of the ship as seen from Earth: $d = v \times t$. Since $v = \frac{d_0}{t_0}$ (where $d_0$ is the length contracted distance), we can simply determine the velocity first.

### 3. Step-by-Step Calculation

**Step A: Find the velocity ($v$) of the spaceship**
Using the time dilation formula:
$$\frac{t_0}{t} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$
$$\frac{9.2}{17.6} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$
$$0.5227 = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$
Squaring both sides:
$$0.2732 = 1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}$$
$$\frac{v^2}{c^2} = 1 - 0.2732 = 0.7268$$
$$\frac{v}{c} = \sqrt{0.7268} \approx 0.8525$$
So, $v \approx 0.8525c$.

**Step B: Calculate the distance from Earth's perspective**
From the perspective of the observer on Earth, the ship travels at velocity $v$ for time $t = 17.6 \text{ years}$.
$$d = v \times t$$
$$d = (0.8525c) \times (17.6 \text{ years})$$
Since $c = 1 \text{ light-year/year}$, we can simplify:
$$d = 0.8525 \times 17.6$$
$$d \approx 15.0 \text{ light-years}$$

**Final Answer:** The distance to the star, as measured by an observer on Earth, is approximately **15.0 light-years**.
