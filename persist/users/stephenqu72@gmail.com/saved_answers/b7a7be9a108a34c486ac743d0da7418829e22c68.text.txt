To solve this problem, we need to determine the number of photons emitted per second by finding the total power (energy per second) and dividing it by the energy of a single photon.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the given information:**
    *   Power ($P$) = $1.50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ W}$ (which is $1.50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ Joules per second}$).
    *   Wavelength ($\lambda$) = $5.30 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$.
    *   Planck's constant ($h$) = $6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$.
    *   Speed of light ($c$) = $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.

2.  **Calculate the energy of a single photon ($E$):**
    The energy of a photon is given by the formula $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$.
    $$E = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})}{5.30 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}}$$
    $$E \approx 3.75 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

3.  **Calculate the number of photons per second ($n$):**
    Since Power represents total energy emitted per second, the number of photons $n$ is the total power divided by the energy of one photon:
    $$n = \frac{P}{E}$$
    $$n = \frac{1.50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ J/s}}{3.75 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J/photon}}$$
    $$n = 4.0 \times 10^{15} \text{ photons/s}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, the correct answer is **(A)**.

### Answer:
(A) $4.0 \times 10^{15}$
