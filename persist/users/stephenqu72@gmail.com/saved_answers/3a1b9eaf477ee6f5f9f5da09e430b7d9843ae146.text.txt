### Answer

(a) **Sun** (or Sirius A)
(b) **Rigel A** (or Sirius A)
(c) **Rigel A**

### Explanation

To solve these questions, you need to understand the structure of the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram and the stages of stellar evolution:

**(a) Identifying stars fusing hydrogen to helium:**
Stars that are currently fusing hydrogen in their cores are located on the **Main Sequence**, which is the diagonal band stretching from the top-left (hot, luminous) to the bottom-right (cool, dim) of the H-R diagram. From the provided graph, the **Sun** and **Sirius A** lie on this sequence. Barnard’s Star is also on the main sequence, so it would also be an acceptable answer.

**(b) Identifying stars where the CNO cycle is predominant:**
The CNO (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen) cycle is the dominant hydrogen fusion process in stars more massive than about $1.3$ times the mass of the Sun, which corresponds to stars with surface temperatures greater than approximately $17,000\text{ K}$. Looking at the x-axis (temperature), **Rigel A** is significantly hotter than $17,000\text{ K}$, making it the clear choice. Sirius A is also hot enough that the CNO cycle contributes significantly to its energy production.

**(c) Determining the brightest star as viewed from Earth:**
The apparent brightness of a star as viewed from Earth depends on its **luminosity** ($L$) and its distance from Earth. The question asks which star would be the brightest if it replaced the Sun (implying they are all placed at the same distance, $1\text{ AU}$, from Earth). In this scenario, apparent brightness is directly proportional to luminosity. Looking at the y-axis ($L/L_\odot$), **Rigel A** has the highest luminosity (approximately $10^5$ to $10^6$ times that of the Sun). Therefore, it would be the brightest star in the sky.
