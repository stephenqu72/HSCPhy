To find the impulse experienced by the tennis ball, we must calculate the area under the Force-time ($F-t$) graph. The impulse ($J$) is given by the integral of force with respect to time:

$$J = \int F \, dt$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Understand the Shape:** The graph represents a triangular-like pulse. While it is curved, we can approximate the area by treating it as a triangle. The formula for the area of a triangle is:
    $$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$$

2.  **Identify the Dimensions from the Graph:**
    *   **Height ($F_{max}$):** The peak of the graph aligns with $250\text{ N}$.
    *   **Base ($\Delta t$):** The pulse starts at $t = 0.03\text{ s}$ and ends at $t = 0.08\text{ s}$. 
        $$\text{Base} = 0.08\text{ s} - 0.03\text{ s} = 0.05\text{ s}$$

3.  **Calculate the Impulse ($J$):**
    $$J \approx \frac{1}{2} \times 0.05\text{ s} \times 250\text{ N}$$
    $$J \approx 0.5 \times 12.5\text{ N s}$$
    $$J \approx 6.25\text{ N s}$$

4.  **Compare with Options:**
    *   A. $0.50\text{ N s}$
    *   B. $5.0\text{ N s}$
    *   C. $10\text{ N s}$
    *   D. $50\text{ N s}$

Given the curvature of the actual graph (which is more "filled" than a perfect triangle), the area will be slightly larger than our triangular approximation. Among the choices provided, $5.0\text{ N s}$ (Option B) is the closest value. 

*Note: If we treat the shape as a rectangle of height $250\text{ N}$ and width $0.025\text{ s}$ (the average width), we get a similar magnitude.*

**Correct Option: B**
