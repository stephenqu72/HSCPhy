To answer this question, we must apply **Malus's Law**, which describes the intensity of light transmitted through two polarising filters.

### Explanation
When unpolarised light passes through the first polariser, it becomes linearly polarised. When this light subsequently passes through a second polariser (often called an analyser), the intensity $I$ of the transmitted light is given by:
$$I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$$
where:
*   $I_0$ is the intensity of light entering the second polariser.
*   $\theta$ is the angle between the transmission axes of the two polarisers.

The graph of $I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$ follows a squared cosine wave pattern. 
1.  **When $\theta = 0^\circ$**: The polarisers are parallel, so $\cos^2(0^\circ) = 1$. The intensity $I$ is at its **maximum**.
2.  **When $\theta = 90^\circ$**: The polarisers are perpendicular (crossed), so $\cos^2(90^\circ) = 0$. The intensity $I$ is at its **minimum (zero)**.
3.  **When $\theta = 180^\circ$**: The polarisers are parallel again, so $\cos^2(180^\circ) = (-1)^2 = 1$. The intensity $I$ returns to its **maximum**.

*   **(D)** correctly shows the pattern where the intensity starts at a maximum (parallel), drops to zero as it reaches $90^\circ$ (perpendicular), and returns to a maximum as it rotates to $180^\circ$ (parallel).
