As an HSC teacher, I would guide you through this classic physics problem, which describes James Chadwick’s 1932 discovery of the neutron.

### Answer

**(a) Explain why the hypothesis was proposed and then rejected, with reference to the observations.**

*   **Why it was proposed:** Gamma radiation ($\gamma$) was the best-known neutral radiation at the time. Since the unknown radiation was not deflected by an electric field, it was known to have no charge, making gamma rays a plausible candidate.
*   **Why it was rejected:** 
    *   **Energy/Momentum constraints:** If the radiation were gamma rays, the protons ejected from the paraffin would require an impossibly high energy (far exceeding the energy of the incident alpha particles) to satisfy conservation of energy and momentum.
    *   **Photoelectric effect:** Gamma rays are high-energy photons and should easily produce the photoelectric effect. The observation that the radiation *could not* produce the photoelectric effect contradicted the properties of gamma radiation.
    *   **Conclusion:** The radiation was actually a neutral particle with a mass similar to a proton, now known as the **neutron**.

**(b) How did these experiments change the model of the atom?**

*   Before this discovery, the nucleus was thought to consist only of protons (which created a mass-to-charge ratio problem, as the mass of the nucleus was roughly double its atomic number).
*   The discovery of the neutron provided the missing mass in the nucleus without adding extra charge. This led to the **proton-neutron model of the nucleus**, completing the standard model of the atom as consisting of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons surrounded by orbiting electrons.

---

### How to solve this
1.  **Analyze the "Why proposed":** In physics history questions, always look for the property that matches the hypothesis. The "neutral charge" (no deflection) is the key link to gamma rays.
2.  **Analyze the "Why rejected":** Focus on the provided experimental evidence. 
    *   Think about **conservation laws**: Scattering a heavy proton requires a particle of similar mass to the proton (like a neutron). A massless photon would struggle to transfer that much kinetic energy.
    *   Think about **particle properties**: Connect "no photoelectric effect" to the fact that neutrons do not interact via the electromagnetic force like photons do.
3.  **Synthesize the "Model change":** Always link discovery to the structure. Remember that before 1932, we had a "proton-only" nucleus model, which couldn't explain isotopes or mass discrepancies. The neutron "filled the gap."
