This question relates to the physics of electromagnetic induction, specifically **Eddy Currents** and **Lenz’s Law**.

### Answer
This extra step is performed to remove non-ferrous, conductive metals (such as aluminum or copper) from the waste stream. As these conductive materials slide down the incline over the permanent magnets, the changing magnetic flux induces eddy currents within the metals. According to Lenz's Law, these eddy currents create magnetic fields that oppose the motion of the metal objects. This interaction exerts a magnetic force that slows down or deflects the non-ferrous metals differently than non-conductive waste (like plastic or paper), allowing them to be separated from the rest of the refuse.

### How to solve it
To solve this, follow these logical steps:

1.  **Identify the physical phenomenon:** The scenario describes a conductive material moving through a non-uniform magnetic field. This is the classic setup for generating eddy currents.
2.  **Apply Lenz's Law:** Recall that Lenz’s Law states that an induced current will flow in a direction that creates a magnetic field opposing the change in magnetic flux that produced it.
3.  **Explain the force:** Because the conductive waste is moving relative to the magnet, the eddy currents generate a magnetic force (specifically, a combination of drag and Lorentz force components). This force provides a mechanical "sorting" mechanism.
4.  **Connect to the context:** Since the prompt specifies the initial removal of *ferrous* substances, the "extra step" must be targeting *non-ferrous* but *conductive* metals. Non-conductive waste (glass, paper) will not experience these forces and will slide down normally, while conductive items will experience a braking or deflecting force, allowing for separation.
