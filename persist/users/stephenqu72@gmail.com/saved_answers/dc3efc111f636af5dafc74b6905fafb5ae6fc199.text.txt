To solve this problem, we use Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$.

### Part (a)
**Goal:** Determine the mass converted into energy each second.
1. We are given the energy output per second: $E = 3.86 \times 10^{26} \text{ J}$.
2. The speed of light $c$ is approximately $3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.
3. Rearrange the formula $E = mc^2$ to solve for mass: $m = \frac{E}{c^2}$.
4. Substitute the values:
   $$m = \frac{3.86 \times 10^{26}}{(3.00 \times 10^8)^2}$$
   $$m = \frac{3.86 \times 10^{26}}{9.00 \times 10^{16}}$$
   $$m \approx 4.29 \times 10^9 \text{ kg}$$

**Answer:** Approximately $4.29 \times 10^9 \text{ kg}$ of matter is converted into energy each second.

### Part (b)
**Goal:** Calculate the number of He-4 nuclei produced.
*Note: This part of the calculation requires the physics principle that in the proton-proton chain reaction, the mass defect ($\Delta m$) converted to energy per Helium-4 nucleus is approximately $4.68 \times 10^{-29} \text{ kg}$. Alternatively, if the question implies the total mass converted is entirely associated with the production of helium, we use the mass of the nucleus.*

1. In the process of fusion, $4$ protons are fused into $1$ helium nucleus. The mass lost (converted to energy) per fusion is $\Delta m = 4 \times m_p - m_{He}$.
2. $m_{He} = 6.648 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$. The mass defect $\Delta m \approx 4.68 \times 10^{-29} \text{ kg}$.
3. The number of helium nuclei produced per second is the total mass converted per second divided by the mass defect per reaction:
   $$N = \frac{\text{total mass converted}}{\Delta m}$$
   $$N = \frac{4.288 \times 10^9 \text{ kg}}{4.68 \times 10^{-29} \text{ kg/nucleus}}$$
   $$N \approx 9.16 \times 10^{37} \text{ nuclei/s}$$

**Answer:** Approximately $9.16 \times 10^{37}$ He-4 nuclei are produced in the core of the Sun each second.
