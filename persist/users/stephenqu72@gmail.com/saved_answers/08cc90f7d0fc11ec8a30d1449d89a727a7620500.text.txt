To solve this problem, we use the de Broglie wavelength formula, which relates the wavelength ($\lambda$) of a particle to its momentum ($p$):

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$

### Given values:
*   Planck's constant, $h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$
*   Mass of an electron, $m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
*   Velocity of the electron, $v = 1.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

### Calculation:
Substitute the values into the formula:

$$\lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{(9.1 \times 10^{-31}) \times (1.0 \times 10^7)}$$

First, calculate the denominator (momentum):
$$p = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times 1.0 \times 10^7 = 9.1 \times 10^{-24} \text{ kg m s}^{-1}$$

Now, divide $h$ by the momentum:
$$\lambda = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{9.1 \times 10^{-24}}$$
$$\lambda \approx 0.728 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m}$$
$$\lambda \approx 7.28 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}$$

Rounding to two significant figures, we get $7.3 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}$.
