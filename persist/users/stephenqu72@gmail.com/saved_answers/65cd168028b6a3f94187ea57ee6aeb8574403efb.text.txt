### Model Answer

The Geiger-Marsden experiment (Rutherford’s gold foil experiment) was fundamental in dismantling the Thomson "plum pudding" model of the atom and establishing the nuclear model. 

**1. Observations and Inferences:**
The experiment involved firing $\alpha$-particles at a thin gold foil. While most passed through undeflected, a small proportion experienced significant deflections, and some were reflected back toward the source. According to the "plum pudding" model, the positive charge was spread throughout the atom, which would only cause small, negligible deflections. The observation of large-angle scattering proved that the atom contained a small, dense, positively charged core—the nucleus—which concentrated the mass and positive charge.

**2. Shift in the Model:**
This experiment led to the Rutherford model, where electrons orbit a central, positively charged nucleus, with the vast majority of the atom being empty space. This successfully explained the scattering data that Thomson’s model could not account for.

**3. Limitations and Analysis:**
Despite its brilliance, the new model faced significant theoretical limitations. According to classical electrodynamics, an electron accelerating in an orbit should continuously lose energy as electromagnetic radiation. This implies that electrons should spiral into the nucleus within a fraction of a second, leading to the collapse of the atom. Furthermore, the model could not account for the discrete line spectra observed in atoms (like hydrogen), which suggested that electron orbits were quantized rather than continuous. These limitations necessitated the development of the Bohr model and, eventually, quantum mechanics.

2.  **Structure:** 
    *   **Introduction:** Define the experiment and the model it replaced.
    *   **The Evidence:** Explicitly link the experimental results (large-angle scattering) to the physical inference (dense positive nucleus).
    *   **The Transition:** Briefly describe the Rutherford model.
    *   **The Critique:** Address the limitations. Mention "classical physics," "accelerating charges," "energy loss," and "atomic stability." This is crucial for achieving high marks.
3.  **Use Terminology:** Use key terms like "$\alpha$-particles," "electrostatic repulsion," "quantization," and "classical electrodynamics."
