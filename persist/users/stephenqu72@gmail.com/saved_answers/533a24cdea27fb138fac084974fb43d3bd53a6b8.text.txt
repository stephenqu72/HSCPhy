To solve this problem, we must apply the principle of **Conservation of Mechanical Energy** for an object in a gravitational field.

### Explanation

1.  **Total Mechanical Energy ($E_{total}$):**
    The total mechanical energy of an object in a gravitational field is the sum of its gravitational potential energy ($U$) and its kinetic energy ($K$):
    $$E_{total} = U + K$$
    Since $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ and $K$ must be $\ge 0$, the total energy of the object must always be greater than or equal to its current gravitational potential energy $U$. On the graph, this means any point representing the total energy must lie at or above the curve at a given distance $r$.

2.  **Scenario 1: At the top of its trajectory**
    At the highest point of the trajectory, the mass momentarily stops. Therefore, its velocity $v = 0$, which means its kinetic energy $K = 0$.
    Since $K=0$, the total energy must be exactly equal to the gravitational potential energy at that point. Looking at the graph at distance $R$, the point on the curve is **B**. Therefore, at the top of the trajectory, the total energy must be at point **B**.

3.  **Scenario 2: The mass is falling towards the Earth**
    When the mass is moving (falling), it has kinetic energy ($K > 0$). Therefore, the total energy must be greater than the gravitational potential energy ($E_{total} > U$).
    Looking at the line $XY$ at distance $R$, points above the curve represent states where $E_{total} > U$. Point **C** is above point **B**, which satisfies this condition. Points **D** and **E** are also above the curve; however, we must consider the initial conditions. When the mass was launched from $R_E$, it had a fixed amount of total energy. As it falls, energy is conserved, meaning the total energy remains constant. Point **B** represents the potential energy at distance $R$, and point **C** represents a possible total energy value that is higher than the potential energy at that specific location. Points D and E are significantly higher energy states that would not match the specific total energy the mass possessed when it was launched. Therefore, **C** is the correct identification for a point representing total energy during the fall.

Comparing these conclusions to the options provided, row (B) correctly identifies **B** for the top of the trajectory and **C** for the mass falling.

### Answer
The correct option is **(B)**.
