To answer this question effectively, you need to address two distinct historical developments in physics: the resolution of the "ultraviolet catastrophe" and the subsequent impact on the atomic model.

### Answer
Max Planck solved the problem presented by the black-body radiation graphs (the ultraviolet catastrophe) by proposing that energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously, but in discrete "packets" or "quanta". He introduced the equation $E = nhf$, where $E$ is energy, $n$ is an integer, $h$ is Planck’s constant, and $f$ is the frequency of radiation. This quantized assumption limited the intensity of high-frequency (short-wavelength) radiation, matching the experimental curve perfectly and preventing the infinite energy prediction of classical wave theory.

Thirteen years later, in 1913, Niels Bohr used this concept of quantization to develop his model of the hydrogen atom. Bohr proposed that electrons occupy stable, discrete energy levels (stationary states) and can only gain or lose energy by jumping between these allowed levels, emitting or absorbing a photon with energy equal to the difference between the levels ($\Delta E = hf$). This successfully explained the stability of the atom and the observed spectral lines of hydrogen, moving beyond the classical Rutherford model.
