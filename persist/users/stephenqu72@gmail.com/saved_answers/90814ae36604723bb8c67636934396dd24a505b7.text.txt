### Model Answer

The spectrum of a star is a vital tool in astrophysics, as it acts as a "fingerprint" containing information about the star's physical properties.

1.  **Chemical Composition:** By observing the absorption lines (Fraunhofer lines) in a star’s spectrum, astronomers can identify the elements present in the star's photosphere. Each element absorbs light at specific, characteristic wavelengths. Comparing these to laboratory standards allows for the identification of elements and, by analyzing the intensity of these lines, the relative abundance of elements can be determined.
2.  **Surface Temperature:** A star acts approximately like a black body. The continuous spectrum of a star has a peak wavelength ($\lambda_{max}$) determined by its surface temperature, described by Wien’s Displacement Law: 
    $$\lambda_{max} = \frac{b}{T}$$
    By identifying the peak of the spectral curve, the surface temperature can be calculated. Additionally, the presence of specific ionisation states (e.g., strong hydrogen lines vs. metal lines) allows stars to be classified into spectral classes ($O, B, A, F, G, K, M$).
3.  **Radial Velocity:** The Doppler effect causes the spectral lines of a star to shift. If a star is moving away from Earth, its spectral lines shift toward the red end (redshift), and if moving toward Earth, toward the blue end (blueshift). The radial velocity ($v$) can be calculated using:
    $$\frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda_0} = \frac{v}{c}$$
4.  **Rotational Velocity:** Rotation causes the spectral lines to broaden. One side of the star rotates toward the observer (blueshift) and the other away (redshift), causing the line to smear out. The degree of broadening provides an estimate of the star’s rotational speed.
