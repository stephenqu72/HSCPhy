### Answer
Einstein’s principle of mass-energy equivalence, expressed by the equation $E = mc^2$, was fundamental to the acceptance of the Big Bang Theory because it provided the theoretical mechanism to explain the origin of matter in the early universe.

In the extremely high-energy environment of the early universe, energy could be converted into mass. According to the Big Bang Theory, the universe began as a point of infinite density and heat. As the universe expanded and cooled, the intense radiation (energy) present was able to spontaneously undergo pair production, transforming into matter and antimatter particles. Without the understanding that energy can condense into mass, the Big Bang model could not account for how a universe initially composed of pure energy could populate itself with the massive particles (protons, neutrons, electrons) that form the stars and galaxies we observe today.

### How to solve this question
1.  **Identify the Core Concept:** Recognise that $E = mc^2$ establishes that mass and energy are interchangeable.
2.  **Link to Cosmology:** Connect this concept to the early universe. The early universe was not composed of "stuff" in the traditional sense, but of high-energy radiation.
3.  **Explain the Mechanism:** Use the term "pair production" or the concept of energy condensing into mass to explain how matter formed as the universe cooled. 
4.  **Conclude on Significance:** Emphasize that the Big Bang Theory requires a source for the matter in the universe; Einstein’s equation provides the physical law that makes that "creation" of matter from energy scientifically valid.
