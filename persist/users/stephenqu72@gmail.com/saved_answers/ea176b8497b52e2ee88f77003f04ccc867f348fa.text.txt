To solve this problem, we must apply **Wien’s Displacement Law** to find the peak wavelength of emission and then sketch the characteristic black-body radiation curve.

### 1. Calculation
Wien’s Displacement Law is given by:
$$\lambda_{max} = \frac{b}{T}$$
Where:
*   $b \approx 2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}\cdot\text{K}$ (Wien's constant)
*   $T = 2800 \text{ K}$

Substituting the values:
$$\lambda_{max} = \frac{2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}\cdot\text{K}}{2800 \text{ K}} \approx 1.035 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m} = 1035 \text{ nm}$$

This result tells us the peak of the emission spectrum is in the **near-infrared** region (just outside the visible spectrum of roughly $400\text{--}700 \text{ nm}$).

### 2. Plotting the Curve
When sketching the graph (Intensity vs. Wavelength):

*   **Peak:** Mark the peak of the curve at $\lambda \approx 1035 \text{ nm}$.
*   **Shape:** The curve should start at the origin $(0,0)$, rise steeply to the peak at $1035 \text{ nm}$, and then tail off slowly towards longer wavelengths (following the characteristic Planck distribution shape).
*   **Context:** Note that because $1035 \text{ nm}$ is in the infrared, the visible portion of the spectrum (the "tail" on the left side of the peak) is what gives the bulb its warm/yellowish glow.
