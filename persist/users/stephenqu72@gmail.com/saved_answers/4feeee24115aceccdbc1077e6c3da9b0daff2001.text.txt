### Answer and Explanation

To solve this problem, we use the photoelectric effect equation: $E_k = hf - \Phi$ and the kinematics of charged particles in an electric field.

**Constants:**
- Work function $\Phi = 2.36\text{ eV} = 2.36 \times 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J} = 3.78 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$
- Mass of electron $m_e = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$
- Planck’s constant $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
- Visible light range: $\lambda \approx 380\text{ nm}$ to $750\text{ nm}$ ($f \approx 4.0 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$ to $7.9 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$)

---

#### Part (a)
The total kinetic energy of an electron striking the anode is the sum of its initial kinetic energy from the photoelectric effect and the energy gained from the potential difference $V = 5\text{ V}$.
$$E_{total} = (hf - \Phi) + eV_{accel}$$
The range depends on the photon frequency $f$.
1. **Minimum energy:** The threshold frequency is $f_0 = \frac{\Phi}{h} = \frac{3.78 \times 10^{-19}}{6.626 \times 10^{-34}} \approx 5.7 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$. At this frequency, $E_k = 0$, so $E_{total} = 5\text{ eV}$.
   $$v_{min} = \sqrt{\frac{2(5 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19})}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}} \approx 1.33 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$$
2. **Maximum energy:** The highest visible frequency is $\approx 7.9 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$.
   $hf_{max} = (6.626 \times 10^{-34})(7.9 \times 10^{14}) \approx 5.23 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J} \approx 3.27\text{ eV}$.
   $E_{total} = (3.27 - 2.36) + 5 = 5.91\text{ eV} \approx 9.47 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$.
   $$v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{2(9.47 \times 10^{-19})}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}} \approx 1.44 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$$

**Range:** Approximately $1.33 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$ to $1.44 \times 10^6\text{ m/s}$.

---

#### Part (b)
We need the wavelength $\lambda$ that causes an electron to travel $d = 0.15\text{ m}$ in $t = 200\times 10^{-9}\text{ s}$ under a potential $V = 5\text{ V}$.
The acceleration $a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{eV}{md} = \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(5)}{(9.11 \times 10^{-31})(0.15)} \approx 5.85 \times 10^{12}\text{ m/s}^2$.
Using $d = v_i t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$:
$$0.15 = v_i(2 \times 10^{-7}) + 0.5(5.85 \times 10^{12})(2 \times 10^{-7})^2$$
$$0.15 = v_i(2 \times 10^{-7}) + 0.117 \implies v_i = \frac{0.033}{2 \times 10^{-7}} = 1.65 \times 10^5\text{ m/s}$$
Now find the photon energy: $hf = \Phi + \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2$
$$hf = 3.78 \times 10^{-19} + 0.5(9.11 \times 10^{-31})(1.65 \times 10^5)^2 \approx 3.90 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{E} = \frac{(6.63 \times 10^{-34})(3 \times 10^8)}{3.90 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 5.1 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m} = 510\text{ nm}$$
