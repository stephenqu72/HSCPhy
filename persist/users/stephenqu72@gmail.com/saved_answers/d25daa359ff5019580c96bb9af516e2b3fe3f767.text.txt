### Answer

**(a) Historical Method: Fizeau’s Experiment**
In 1849, Hippolyte Fizeau used a rotating toothed wheel to measure the speed of light. Light was directed through a gap between the teeth of a rapidly rotating gear, reflected off a distant mirror (located $8\text{ km}$ away), and returned to the observer. By increasing the speed of rotation, Fizeau found the point where the returning light was blocked by the next tooth in the wheel. Knowing the distance to the mirror and the rotational frequency of the wheel, he calculated the speed of light as $v = \frac{2d}{t}$.

*   **Limitations:** The primary limitation was the human difficulty in accurately determining the exact rotational speed at which the light was fully obscured (the "extinction" point) due to the gradual dimming of the image and the precision limits of mechanical rotational equipment at the time.

**(b) Relationship of the Speed of Light to Distance and Time**
Since 1983, the speed of light ($c$) has been defined as an exact constant: $c = 299\,792\,458 \text{ m/s}$. Because $c$ is fixed by definition, the **metre** is no longer defined by a physical artifact (like a platinum-iridium bar), but rather as the distance light travels in a vacuum during a time interval of $1/299\,792\,458$ of a second. Essentially, distance is now derived from the measurement of time and the universal constant $c$.
