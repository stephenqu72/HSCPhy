To answer this question effectively for an HSC Physics exam, you need to link the experimental design to the specific predictions made by Einstein's theory of special relativity (specifically time dilation).

### Answer
The Hafele-Keating experiment validated the theory of special relativity by demonstrating time dilation resulting from relative velocity. In 1971, highly accurate atomic clocks were flown on commercial airliners in both eastward and westward directions around the Earth. According to special relativity, the moving clocks should experience "time dilation" compared to a stationary reference clock on the ground. The results showed that the clocks recorded different elapsed times consistent with the Lorentz factor $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$, thereby confirming that time is not absolute but depends on the relative velocity of the observer.

### How to solve this
When approaching a 3-mark "Explain" question like this, follow these steps:

1.  **Identify the core principle (1 mark):** State clearly that the experiment tests **time dilation** due to relative velocity, a key prediction of special relativity.
2.  **Describe the experimental setup (1 mark):** Mention the use of high-precision atomic clocks placed on moving platforms (aircraft) compared to stationary clocks on the ground.
3.  **Link to the theory/results (1 mark):** Explain that the difference in elapsed time measured by the clocks matched the calculations derived from the time dilation formula:
    $$\Delta t' = \gamma \Delta t = \frac{\Delta t}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$
    Mentioning that this confirmed time is relative (not absolute) provides the final analytical "hook" to secure full marks.
