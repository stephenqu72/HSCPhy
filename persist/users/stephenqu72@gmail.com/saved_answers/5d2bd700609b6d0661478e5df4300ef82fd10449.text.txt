To solve this problem, we rely on the law of universal gravitation, where the acceleration due to gravity $g$ at a distance $r$ from the center of a planet is given by:
$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$
Since the x-axis is plotted as $1/r^2$, the graph represents a linear relationship $g = (GM) \cdot (\frac{1}{r^2})$, where the slope of the line is $GM$.

### (a) Determine $g$ at $r = 3.0 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$
1. Calculate the value of $\frac{1}{r^2}$:
   $$\frac{1}{(3.0 \times 10^6)^2} = \frac{1}{9.0 \times 10^{12}} \approx 1.11 \times 10^{-13} \text{ m}^{-2}$$
2. Locate $1.11 \times 10^{-13}$ on the x-axis. Using a line of best fit through the origin and the data points, at $x = 1.11$, the corresponding $y$ value ($g$) is approximately **$2.4 \text{ m/s}^2$**.

### (b) Determine the mass of Mercury ($M$)
1. Find the slope of the line: $m = \frac{\Delta g}{\Delta (1/r^2)}$.
   Using a point on the line, e.g., $g = 2.0$ at $1/r^2 = 0.93 \times 10^{-13}$:
   $$\text{Slope} = \frac{2.0}{0.93 \times 10^{-13}} \approx 2.15 \times 10^{13} \text{ m}^3\text{s}^{-2}$$
2. Since $\text{Slope} = GM$:
   $$M = \frac{\text{Slope}}{G} = \frac{2.15 \times 10^{13}}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}} \approx 3.2 \times 10^{23} \text{ kg}$$

### (c) Will the rock fall back down?
To determine this, compare the ejection velocity $v$ to the escape velocity $v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}$.
1. Calculate $v_e$ using $G \approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11}$, $M \approx 3.2 \times 10^{23} \text{ kg}$, and $r = 2.4 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$:
   $$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \cdot 3.2 \times 10^{23}}{2.4 \times 10^6}} = \sqrt{1.77 \times 10^7} \approx 4200 \text{ m/s}$$
2. The ejection velocity is $1.5 \times 10^3 \text{ m/s} = 1500 \text{ m/s}$.
3. Since $1500 \text{ m/s} < 4200 \text{ m/s}$, the rock does not reach escape velocity and **will fall back down** to the surface of Mercury.
