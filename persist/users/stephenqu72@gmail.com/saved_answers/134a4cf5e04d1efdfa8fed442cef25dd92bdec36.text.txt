### Answer and Explanation

To find the magnitude of the average induced EMF, we use **Faraday's Law of Induction**, which states that the induced EMF ($\varepsilon$) is equal to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux ($\Phi$):

$$\varepsilon = - \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$

Since we are looking for the magnitude, we ignore the negative sign:
$$|\varepsilon| = \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$

**Step 1: Calculate the change in magnetic flux ($\Delta \Phi$)**
Magnetic flux is defined as $\Phi = B \cdot A \cdot \cos(\theta)$. In this problem, the loop moves from outside the field (where flux is $0$) to fully inside the field (where the area $A$ is $0.05\text{ m}^2$).
- Initial flux ($\Phi_i$): $0\text{ Wb}$
- Final flux ($\Phi_f$): $B \cdot A = (3.5 \times 10^{-4}\text{ T}) \times (0.05\text{ m}^2)$
- $\Delta \Phi = \Phi_f - \Phi_i = 1.75 \times 10^{-5}\text{ Wb}$

**Step 2: Calculate the induced EMF**
Given the time interval $\Delta t = 0.20\text{ s}$:
$$|\varepsilon| = \frac{1.75 \times 10^{-5}\text{ Wb}}{0.20\text{ s}}$$
$$|\varepsilon| = 8.75 \times 10^{-5}\text{ V}$$

Rounding this to two significant figures gives $8.8 \times 10^{-5}\text{ V}$.

Comparing this result to the given options:
A. $0\text{ V}$
B. $3.5 \times 10^{-6}\text{ V}$
C. $8.8 \times 10^{-3}\text{ V}$
D. $8.8 \times 10^{-5}\text{ V}$

The correct option is **D**.
