### Answer

**Comparison of Spectra**
*   **Discharge tubes** produce **emission spectra**. These consist of bright, discrete coloured lines on a black background, created as excited electrons drop to lower energy levels, releasing photons with specific energies corresponding to $E = hf$.
*   **Stars** produce **absorption spectra**. A star's dense core produces a continuous spectrum, but as this light passes through the cooler, thinner gases in the star's atmosphere, specific wavelengths are absorbed by atoms, creating dark lines (Fraunhofer lines) against the continuous rainbow background.
*   **Contrast:** One represents emission of energy (bright lines), while the other represents selective absorption of energy (dark lines) from a continuous source. Both, however, are unique "fingerprints" of the elements present.

**Features revealed by stellar spectra**
1.  **Chemical Composition:** By matching the dark absorption lines to known emission spectra of elements in a laboratory, we can identify which elements are present in the star's atmosphere.
2.  **Surface Temperature:** By analyzing the peak wavelength of the continuous spectrum (Wien’s Law) or the presence of specific ionisation states of atoms, we can determine the star’s effective surface temperature (Spectral Classification: O, B, A, F, G, K, M).
3.  **Radial Velocity:** The Doppler effect causes absorption lines to shift towards the red end (redshift) if the star is moving away, or the blue end (blueshift) if it is moving towards Earth.
4.  **Rotational Velocity:** The broadening of absorption lines indicates the speed at which the star is rotating.
5.  **Density/Pressure:** Pressure broadening of spectral lines can provide information about the density and atmospheric pressure of the star.
