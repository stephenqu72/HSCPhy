### Answer
The correct answer is **A. The wave nature of light**.

### Explanation

To solve this, we need to understand the underlying physical phenomenon being observed.

1.  **Identify the Phenomenon:** The image displays a series of alternating bright and dark fringes. This is a classic **interference pattern** produced by Young's double-slit experiment.
2.  **Why it occurs:** Interference occurs when light waves originating from two slits overlap. When the waves meet in phase, they undergo **constructive interference** (bright fringes), and when they meet out of phase, they undergo **destructive interference** (dark fringes).
3.  **Evaluate the Options:**
    *   **A. The wave nature of light:** This is the correct choice because interference is a property unique to waves. Only a wave-like entity can superimpose to create regions of reinforcement and cancellation.
    *   **B. The ratio of the width of the slits and the distance between them:** While these parameters affect the spacing of the fringes ($\Delta y = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$), they are conditions for the setup, not the fundamental physical nature that *allows* the pattern to exist in the first place.
    *   **C. Light being composed of packets of photons:** This refers to the *particle* model of light (quantum theory). If light behaved only as a stream of particles, we would expect to see two distinct lines on the screen corresponding to the two slits, rather than an interference pattern.
    *   **D. The use of a coherent light source:** Coherence is a requirement for the interference pattern to be *stable and observable* (it ensures a constant phase relationship), but coherence itself does not explain *why* light produces the pattern; the wave nature is what causes the superposition of waves.

Therefore, the wave nature of light is the fundamental principle that accounts for the interference pattern observed.
