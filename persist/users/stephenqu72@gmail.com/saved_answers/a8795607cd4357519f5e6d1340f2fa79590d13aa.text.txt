### Answer
The correct answer is **C. only transverse waves.**

### Explanation
To solve this, we need to recall the fundamental difference between wave types and what the phenomenon of "polarisation" actually represents:

1.  **Define Polarisation:** Polarisation refers to the restriction of the oscillations of a wave to a single plane. For a wave to be polarised, its oscillation must occur in a direction perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation.
2.  **Transverse Waves:** In a transverse wave, the particles of the medium (or the field vectors, in the case of electromagnetic waves like light) oscillate perpendicular to the direction of travel. Because there is a "plane" of oscillation, we can use a filter (like a polarising lens) to block all oscillations except those occurring in one specific orientation. Therefore, transverse waves *can* be polarised.
3.  **Longitudinal Waves:** In a longitudinal wave (such as sound waves), the particles oscillate parallel to the direction of travel (compression and rarefaction). Because the oscillation is in the same direction as the energy transfer, there is no "side-to-side" or "up-and-down" orientation to filter out. Consequently, longitudinal waves cannot be polarised.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** Incorrect, because longitudinal waves (like sound) cannot be polarised.
*   **B:** Incorrect, because sound is a longitudinal wave, and therefore it cannot be polarised.
*   **D:** Incorrect, because longitudinal waves are defined by their inability to be polarised.

**HSC Tip:** Always remember the mnemonic: **Polarisation is the definitive test to distinguish between transverse and longitudinal waves.** If a wave can be polarised, it is definitively transverse.
