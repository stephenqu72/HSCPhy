### Answer

**(a)** According to particle theory, light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons. The energy of an individual photon is given by $E = hf$, where $h$ is Planck’s constant and $f$ is the frequency. When a single photon interacts with an electron in the metal, it transfers its entire energy to that electron in a one-to-one interaction. The work function ($\phi$) represents the minimum energy required to eject an electron from the metal surface. The maximum kinetic energy ($K_{max}$) of the emitted electron is the difference between the incident photon energy and the work function: $K_{max} = hf - \phi$. Since $\phi$ is constant for a specific metal, an increase in the frequency $f$ of the incoming light results in a higher photon energy, which consequently increases the maximum kinetic energy of the ejected electrons.

**(b)** The photoelectric equation can be expressed in the form $y = mx + c$:
$$K_{max} = hf - \phi$$
In this equation, $K_{max}$ corresponds to the y-axis, $f$ corresponds to the x-axis, and $\phi$ is the y-intercept. The slope of the line is defined by the constant $h$ (Planck’s constant). Since $h$ is a universal constant and does not depend on the type of metal used, the slope for both lines remains identical. Because both lines share the same slope, they must be parallel.
