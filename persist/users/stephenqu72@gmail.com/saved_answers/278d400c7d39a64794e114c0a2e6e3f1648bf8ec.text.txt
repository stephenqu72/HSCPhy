The correct answer is **B**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the fundamental link between atomic structure and spectroscopy:

1.  **Atomic Structure:** According to the Bohr model of the atom, electrons occupy specific, discrete energy levels. Because every element has a different number of protons and a different electron configuration, the electrostatic forces acting on these electrons are unique to that element.
2.  **Quantum Transitions:** When an electron moves between two energy levels, it must gain or lose an exact amount of energy equal to the difference between those levels ($\Delta E$). This energy is released or absorbed as a photon, where the energy of the photon is given by $E = hf$ (or $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$).
3.  **Why Spectroscopy works:** Because each element has a unique "fingerprint" of energy levels, the specific wavelengths ($\lambda$) of light emitted or absorbed are also unique to that element. This results in a unique line spectrum for each element, allowing scientists to identify them.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A:** Elements do not produce continuous spectra; they produce line spectra (emission or absorption spectra). A continuous spectrum is produced by hot dense objects (like the core of a star).
*   **C:** While true that absorption spectra show dark lines, this describes *what* we see on the plate, not *why* the lines correspond to specific elements. It is an observation, not the fundamental cause.
*   **D:** While true that electrons emit energy when moving to lower levels, this is a general property of atoms and does not explain *why* different elements produce *different* spectra.
