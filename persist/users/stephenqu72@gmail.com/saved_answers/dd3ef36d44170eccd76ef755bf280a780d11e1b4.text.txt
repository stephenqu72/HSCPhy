To solve this physics problem, we need to analyze the trajectories of the three projectiles ($X, Y, Z$) based on their vertical and horizontal components of motion.

### Step-by-Step Analysis

1.  **Greatest Height:**
    The vertical height $h$ reached by a projectile is given by the formula $h = \frac{v_y^2}{2g}$. Since all projectiles have the same initial speed $v$, the one launched at the steepest angle will have the largest vertical component ($v_y = v \sin\theta$). Looking at the graph, curve $X$ reaches the highest point on the y-axis, followed by $Y$, then $Z$. Therefore, **$X$ has the greatest height**.

2.  **Greatest Time of Flight:**
    The time of flight $t$ is determined by how long the projectile stays in the air, which depends on its initial vertical velocity: $t = \frac{2v_y}{g} = \frac{2v \sin\theta}{g}$. Because projectile $X$ has the largest vertical component (steepest angle), it remains in the air the longest. Visually, the curve $X$ is the widest/highest, meaning it takes longer to return to the ground. Therefore, **$X$ has the greatest time of flight**.

3.  **Greatest Range:**
    The range $R$ of a projectile is given by $R = \frac{v^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}$. The range is maximized at an angle of $45^\circ$.
    *   $X$ is launched at a very high angle (closer to $90^\circ$), which results in a high peak but a short range.
    *   $Z$ is launched at a shallow angle. 
    *   Looking at the x-axis intercepts (where the projectiles land), curve $Z$ travels the furthest distance horizontally. Therefore, **$Z$ has the greatest range**.

### Conclusion
