To solve this problem, we use the principles of electromagnetic induction and ideal transformers. An ideal transformer assumes no energy loss, meaning input power equals output power ($P_p = P_s$).

### Part a: Calculate the peak voltage in the secondary coil
The provided voltage of $12\text{ V}_{\text{RMS}}$ is the root-mean-square (RMS) voltage. For a sinusoidal wave, the relationship between peak voltage ($V_{\text{peak}}$) and RMS voltage ($V_{\text{RMS}}$) is:
$$V_{\text{peak}} = V_{\text{RMS}} \times \sqrt{2}$$

**Calculation:**
$$V_{\text{peak}} = 12 \times \sqrt{2} \approx 16.97\text{ V}$$
Rounding to an appropriate number of significant figures (usually 2 or 3 based on the input), we get **$17.0\text{ V}$**.

---

### Part b: Determine the ratio of the number of turns $\frac{N_p}{N_s}$
For an ideal transformer, the turns ratio is equal to the ratio of the voltages:
$$\frac{N_p}{N_s} = \frac{V_p}{V_s}$$

**Calculation:**
Given $V_p = 240\text{ V}$ and $V_s = 12\text{ V}$:
$$\frac{N_p}{N_s} = \frac{240}{12} = 20$$
The ratio is **$20:1$** (or simply **$20$**).

---

### Part c: Calculate the RMS current in the primary coil
Since the transformer is ideal, the power input to the primary coil equals the power delivered to the secondary coil.
$$P_p = P_s = 0.90\text{ W}$$
Power is calculated using the formula $P = V_{\text{RMS}} \times I_{\text{RMS}}$. Rearranging for the primary current ($I_p$):
$$I_p = \frac{P_p}{V_p}$$

**Calculation:**
$$I_p = \frac{0.90\text{ W}}{240\text{ V}} = 0.00375\text{ A}$$
This can also be expressed as **$3.75\text{ mA}$**.

### Summary of Answers:
*   **a:** $17.0\text{ V}$
*   **b:** $20$
*   **c:** $0.00375\text{ A}$ (or $3.75\text{ mA}$)
