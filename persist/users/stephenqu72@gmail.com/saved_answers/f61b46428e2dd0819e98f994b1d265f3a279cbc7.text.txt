To solve this problem, we must relate the physics of a car on a banked track to the linear relationship shown in the graph.

### Physics Explanation
When a car travels on a banked track and the centripetal force is provided entirely by the horizontal component of the normal force (i.e., no friction is required), the governing equation is derived from:
$$\sum F_x = N \sin(\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$\sum F_y = N \cos(\theta) = mg$$

Dividing the two equations gives:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$
Rearranging to isolate $v^2$:
$$v^2 = (g \tan(\theta)) \cdot r$$

This equation is in the form of $y = mx$, where $y = v^2$ and $x = r$. Therefore, the gradient (slope) of the provided graph is equal to $g \tan(\theta)$.

### Calculation
1. **Determine the gradient ($m$) of the graph:**
   Pick two points on the line. Using $(0, 0)$ and $(100, 160)$:
   $$m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{160 - 0}{100 - 0} = 1.6 \, \text{m/s}^2/\text{m}$$

2. **Calculate the angle ($\theta$):**
   Set the gradient equal to $g \tan(\theta)$, using $g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2$:
   $$1.6 = 9.8 \tan(\theta)$$
   $$\tan(\theta) = \frac{1.6}{9.8} \approx 0.16327$$
   $$\theta = \arctan(0.16327)$$
   $$\theta \approx 9.27^\circ$$
