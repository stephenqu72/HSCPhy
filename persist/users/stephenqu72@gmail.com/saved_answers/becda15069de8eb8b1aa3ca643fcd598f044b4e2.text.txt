To solve this problem, we must analyze the equilibrium state of wire **A**. For wire **A** to hover above wire **B**, the magnetic force of repulsion must exactly balance the gravitational force acting on wire **A**.

### 1. Direction of the Currents
The magnetic force between two parallel current-carrying wires is attractive if the currents flow in the same direction and repulsive if they flow in opposite directions. Since wire **A** is hovering *above* wire **B** (counteracting gravity), the magnetic force must be **repulsive**. Therefore, the currents must flow in **opposite directions**.

### 2. Calculating the Height
The formula for the magnetic force per unit length $F/L$ between two parallel wires separated by a distance $r$ is:
$$\frac{F}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi r}$$
The gravitational force per unit length acting on wire **A** is:
$$\frac{F_g}{L} = \mu g$$
where $\mu$ is the mass per unit length. At equilibrium, $F/L = F_g/L$:
$$\frac{\mu_0 I^2}{2\pi r} = \mu g$$
Given:
- $I = 100\text{ A}$
- $\mu = 20.0\text{ g m}^{-1} = 0.020\text{ kg m}^{-1}$
- $g = 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$
- $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\text{ T m A}^{-1}$

Rearranging for $r$:
$$r = \frac{\mu_0 I^2}{2\pi \mu g}$$
$$r = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}) \times (100)^2}{2\pi \times 0.020 \times 9.8}$$
$$r = \frac{2 \times 10^{-7} \times 10000}{0.020 \times 9.8}$$
$$r = \frac{0.002}{0.196} \approx 0.0102\text{ m}$$

Comparing our results:
- Height = $0.0102\text{ m}$
- Direction = Opposite directions

This corresponds to option **(C)**.
