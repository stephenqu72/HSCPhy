To determine under which conditions it is safe to drive around the bend, we must calculate the **centripetal force** required for the car to maintain its path and compare it to the available frictional force provided in the table.

### 1. Identify the physics principles
When a car moves around a circular bend at a constant speed, the force providing the necessary centripetal acceleration is the static friction between the tires and the road. For the car to safely navigate the bend, the available frictional force ($F_f$) must be greater than or equal to the required centripetal force ($F_c$):
$$F_f \geq F_c$$
The required centripetal force is calculated using the formula:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

### 2. Perform the calculation
Given values:
*   Mass ($m$) = $2200\text{ kg}$
*   Speed ($v$) = $10.5\text{ m s}^{-1}$
*   Radius ($r$) = $23\text{ m}$

Substitute these values into the formula:
$$F_c = \frac{2200 \times (10.5)^2}{23}$$
$$F_c = \frac{2200 \times 110.25}{23}$$
$$F_c = \frac{242550}{23} \approx 10545.65\text{ N}$$

### 3. Evaluate the conditions
The car requires a minimum centripetal force of approximately **$10546\text{ N}$** to stay on the road at that speed. Now, compare this to the maximum frictional force available under each condition:
*   **Dry:** $9500\text{ N}$ ($9500 < 10546$) $\rightarrow$ Not safe
*   **Heavy rain:** $8000\text{ N}$ ($8000 < 10546$) $\rightarrow$ Not safe
*   **Chemical spill:** $6500\text{ N}$ ($6500 < 10546$) $\rightarrow$ Not safe

Since the required force ($10546\text{ N}$) exceeds the available frictional force in every listed condition, the car will skid in all of these scenarios. Therefore, it is safe under **none of the conditions**.
