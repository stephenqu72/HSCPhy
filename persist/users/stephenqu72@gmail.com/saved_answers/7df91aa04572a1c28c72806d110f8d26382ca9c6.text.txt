### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) Dark lines would appear in the spectrum.**

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we apply Kirchhoff's laws of spectroscopy, which are fundamental concepts in the HSC Physics syllabus regarding the nature of light and atoms.

1.  **The Source:** An incandescent lamp acts as a black-body radiator. A black-body provides a **continuous spectrum**, meaning it emits light at all wavelengths (producing a complete "rainbow" of colors).
2.  **The Interaction:** When this light passes through a **cool gas**, the atoms in the gas are in their ground state. They will absorb specific photons from the continuous spectrum that correspond exactly to the energy transitions required to excite their electrons to higher energy levels.
3.  **The Result:** Because the gas absorbs these specific wavelengths, they are "missing" from the light that eventually reaches the observer. When you view the resulting spectrum, you see the original continuous spectrum with thin, dark gaps at the specific wavelengths absorbed by the gas. This is known as an **absorption spectrum** (or dark-line spectrum).

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(B)** Bright lines appear in an *emission spectrum*, which occurs when a hot, low-density gas is viewed against a dark background, not when a cool gas is placed in front of a continuous source.
*   **(C)** A continuous spectrum only exists if there is no intervening gas to absorb specific frequencies.
*   **(D)** Blue-shifting is a Doppler effect caused by the relative motion of the source toward the observer, which is not mentioned in this scenario.
