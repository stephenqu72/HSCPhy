To solve this problem, we apply **Faraday's Law of Induction**, which states that the induced electromotive force (emf) in a closed circuit is equal to the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the loop.

### Explanation:

1.  **Understand Faraday's Law:** The magnitude of the induced emf ($\varepsilon$) is given by the formula:
    $$\varepsilon = \left| \frac{\Delta \Phi_B}{\Delta t} \right|$$
    where $\Delta \Phi_B$ is the change in magnetic flux and $\Delta t$ is the time interval.

2.  **Identify the Given Variables:**
    *   The problem states that the "rate of change of the magnetic flux" is $R$. 
    *   Mathematically, this means:
        $$\frac{\Delta \Phi_B}{\Delta t} = R$$
    *   The area $S$ is mentioned, but in this specific context, the rate of change of flux is already defined as $R$ (which typically incorporates the magnetic field change over that area). The definition of magnetic flux is $\Phi_B = B \cdot A$ (or $BS$). Since $R$ is explicitly given as the rate of change of the flux ($\frac{\Delta \Phi_B}{\Delta t}$), the area $S$ is already accounted for within the value $R$.

3.  **Determine the emf:**
    *   Substituting the given rate into Faraday's Law:
        $$\varepsilon = \left| \frac{\Delta \Phi_B}{\Delta t} \right| = R$$

4.  **Conclusion:** The induced emf is simply equal to the rate of change of the magnetic flux, which is $R$.

Comparing this result to the given options:
*   A: $R$
*   B: $RS$
*   C: $RST$
*   D: $\frac{RS}{T}$

The correct option is **A**.
