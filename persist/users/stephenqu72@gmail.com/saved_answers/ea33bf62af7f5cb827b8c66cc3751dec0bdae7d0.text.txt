### Answer
In a constant circular orbit, the satellite maintains a constant orbital radius and a constant orbital speed. Mechanical energy ($E$) is the sum of kinetic energy ($K$) and gravitational potential energy ($U$), where $E = K + U$. Since the radius remains constant, the potential energy ($U = -G\frac{Mm}{r}$) does not change. Since the speed remains constant, the kinetic energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) does not change. Because there is no change in the total mechanical energy ($\Delta E = 0$), the net work done on the satellite must be zero, as stated by the work-energy theorem.

### Explanation
To solve this, you need to connect the Work-Energy Theorem to the specific properties of circular orbital motion. Here is the step-by-step logic:

1.  **Define Mechanical Energy:** Recall that mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic energy ($K$) and potential energy ($U$).
    $$E = K + U = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{r}$$
2.  **Analyze the components:**
    *   **Potential Energy ($U$):** In a constant circular orbit, the distance from the center of the Earth ($r$) is constant. Therefore, the gravitational potential energy remains constant.
    *   **Kinetic Energy ($K$):** The orbital speed ($v$) for a satellite in a circular orbit is determined by $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$. Since $M$, $G$, and $r$ are constant, $v$ must be constant. Therefore, the kinetic energy remains constant.
3.  **Synthesize:** Since both $K$ and $U$ are constant, the total mechanical energy $E$ of the satellite does not change over time ($\Delta E = 0$).
4.  **Apply the Work-Energy Theorem:** The Work-Energy Theorem states that the net work done on an object is equal to the change in its mechanical energy ($\text{Work} = \Delta E$). Since $\Delta E = 0$, the total work done on the satellite must be zero.

**Exam Tip:** In HSC Physics, always explicitly link the "constant radius" to "constant potential energy" and "constant orbital speed" to "constant kinetic energy" to ensure you secure both marks.
