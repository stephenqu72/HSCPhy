### Physics Analysis
The horizontal range $R$ of a projectile launched horizontally from a height $h$ with an initial velocity $v$ is given by:
1.  **Vertical motion:** The time of flight $t$ depends on the height $h$:
    $$h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}$$
2.  **Horizontal motion:** The range $R$ is determined by the horizontal velocity $v$ and the time of flight $t$:
    $$R = v \cdot t = v \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}}$$

For the experiment to be **valid**, we are investigating the relationship between $v$ (independent variable) and $R$ (dependent variable). In a controlled experiment, any other variables that could affect the dependent variable ($R$) must be kept constant.

*   **Height ($h$):** If $h$ changes, the time of flight $t$ changes, which directly alters the range $R$ even if the launch velocity $v$ remains constant. Therefore, $h$ must be kept constant.
*   **Distance ($d$):** In this specific setup, $d$ represents the distance the ball travels along the table before launching. While $d$ might be related to the *method* of achieving a specific $v$, if we vary $v$, we are essentially looking at how the launch speed affects the range. However, if $d$ is changed while $h$ is constant, it may alter the kinematics of the launcher or the release point. To ensure the launch height remains exactly $h$ above the floor and that the projectile trajectory is consistent, both dimensions are geometric parameters of the apparatus that define the "environment" of the motion. 

Specifically, if the experimenter changes the launch velocity $v$, they must ensure that the height $h$ from which the projectile leaves the table does not change. Furthermore, the distance $d$ defines the starting position; if $d$ were allowed to vary randomly, it might introduce inconsistencies in how the ball is released or its final velocity. For a controlled experiment, all external factors that influence the outcome must be fixed. Both $d$ and $h$ define the physical constraints of the launch and must remain constant to ensure that any change in the range is solely due to the change in launch velocity.

### Conclusion
Both $d$ and $h$ are environmental variables that affect the projectile path. To isolate the effect of $v$ on the range $R$, these variables must be held constant throughout the trials.
