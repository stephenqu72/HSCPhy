To solve this problem, we use the fundamental principles of the photoelectric effect and wave properties of light.

### Part (a): Calculate the frequency of the light

**Formula:** The relationship between the speed of light ($c$), wavelength ($\lambda$), and frequency ($f$) is given by the wave equation:
$$c = f \lambda \implies f = \frac{c}{\lambda}$$

**Given:**
- $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
- $\lambda = 550 \text{ nm} = 550 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}$

**Calculation:**
$$f = \frac{3.00 \times 10^8}{550 \times 10^{-9}}$$
$$f \approx 5.45 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$$

---

### Part (b): Will electrons be ejected?

**Concept:** For photoelectric emission to occur, the energy of an individual photon ($E$) must be greater than or equal to the work function ($\Phi$) of the metal.
**Formula:** The energy of a photon is calculated using Planck’s constant ($h$):
$$E = hf$$

**Given:**
- $h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
- $f = 5.45 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$
- Work function $\Phi = 5 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$

**Calculation:**
$$E = (6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (5.45 \times 10^{14})$$
$$E \approx 3.61 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

**Conclusion:**
Comparing the photon energy to the work function:
$$3.61 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} < 5 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$
Since the energy of the incident photons is **less than** the minimum energy (work function) required to eject electrons, **no electrons will be ejected** from the metal.
