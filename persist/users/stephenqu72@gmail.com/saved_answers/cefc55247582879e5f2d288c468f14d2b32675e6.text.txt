To solve this problem, we must identify the physics principle governing a car moving around a curved path: circular motion.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the Force:** The centripetal force ($F_c$) required to keep a car moving in a circle of radius $r$ at a speed $v$ is provided by the friction (traction) force between the tyres and the road.
    The formula for centripetal force is:
    $$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

2.  **Set up the Equality:** Since the friction force provides the centripetal force, we equate the maximum traction force ($F_{max}$) to the expression:
    $$F_{max} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

3.  **Analyze the Variables:**
    *   $F_{max}$ is constant for both cars.
    *   $r$ is constant for the curved road.
    *   Let $m_A$ be the mass of car A and $m_B$ be the mass of car B. We are told $m_A = 1.5 m_B$.
    *   Let $v_A = v$ be the max speed of car A, and $v_B$ be the max speed of car B.

4.  **Solve for $v$:** Rearranging the formula for speed gives:
    $$v = \sqrt{\frac{F_{max} \cdot r}{m}}$$
    This shows that the maximum speed $v$ is inversely proportional to the square root of the mass:
    $$v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}$$

5.  **Compare Car A and Car B:**
    $$\frac{v_B}{v_A} = \sqrt{\frac{m_A}{m_B}}$$
    Substitute $m_A = 1.5 m_B$:
    $$\frac{v_B}{v} = \sqrt{\frac{1.5 m_B}{m_B}} = \sqrt{1.5}$$
    Calculating the value:
    $$\sqrt{1.5} \approx 1.22$$

Rounding to the closest provided option, we get $1.2v$.

**Correct Option: (B)**
