To solve this problem, we need to understand the relationship between centripetal acceleration ($a_c$), the radius of rotation ($r$), and the period ($T$).

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the relevant formula:** 
    The centripetal acceleration of an object moving in a circle is given by:
    $$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
    Since $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$, we can substitute this into the formula to express $a_c$ in terms of the period $T$:
    $$a_c = \frac{(\frac{2\pi r}{T})^2}{r} = \frac{4\pi^2 r^2}{T^2 r} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}$$

2.  **Analyze the relationship:**
    From the derived formula $a_c = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}$, we can see that for a fixed radius $r$, the centripetal acceleration is inversely proportional to the square of the period:
    $$a_c \propto \frac{1}{T^2}$$

3.  **Apply the condition:**
    We want the new centripetal acceleration ($a_c'$) to be triple the original acceleration ($a_c$):
    $$a_c' = 3a_c$$
    Using the proportionality derived in step 2:
    $$\frac{1}{(T')^2} = 3 \times \frac{1}{T^2}$$

4.  **Solve for $T'$:**
    Rearranging the equation to solve for the new period $T'$:
    $$(T')^2 = \frac{T^2}{3}$$
    Taking the square root of both sides:
    $$T' = \frac{T}{\sqrt{3}}$$
