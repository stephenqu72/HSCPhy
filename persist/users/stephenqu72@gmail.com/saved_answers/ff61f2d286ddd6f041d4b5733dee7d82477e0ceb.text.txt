To find the angular velocity ($\omega$) of the Earth in its orbit around the Sun, we use the relationship between angular velocity and the period of rotation ($T$):

$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Identify the period ($T$):** The Earth takes approximately 1 year to complete one orbit around the Sun. We must convert this time into seconds (SI unit for time).
    *   $1 \text{ year} \approx 365.25 \text{ days}$
    *   $1 \text{ day} = 24 \text{ hours} \times 60 \text{ minutes} \times 60 \text{ seconds} = 86,400 \text{ seconds}$
    *   $T = 365.25 \times 86,400 \approx 31,557,600 \text{ seconds}$ (or roughly $3.16 \times 10^7 \text{ s}$)

2.  **Calculate the angular velocity:** Plug the period into the formula:
    $$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{3.156 \times 10^7 \text{ s}}$$
    $$\omega \approx \frac{6.283}{3.156 \times 10^7}$$
    $$\omega \approx 1.99 \times 10^{-7} \text{ rad s}^{-1}$$

3.  **Compare with the options:**
    Rounding our result gives approximately $2.0 \times 10^{-7} \text{ rad s}^{-1}$, which corresponds to option **(A)**.

**Note:** Option (B) is often a distractor as it represents the angular velocity of the Earth spinning on its own axis (one rotation every 24 hours). Ensure you distinguish between *orbital* motion (around the Sun) and *rotational* motion (around the axis).

The correct answer is **(A)**.
