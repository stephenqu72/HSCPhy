To analyze the motion of the magnet using the law of conservation of energy, we must compare the mechanical energy at different stages of the fall.

### Analysis of Motion

**1. Free Fall (Position $X$ to $Y$):**
In this region, the only force acting on the magnet is gravity. Mechanical energy is conserved as potential energy is converted into kinetic energy.
*   **Initial potential energy:** $PE_X = mgh = 0.04 \times 9.8 \times 0.78 = 0.30576\text{ J}$.
*   **Velocity at $Y$:** Using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$, where $u=0$ and $s=0.78\text{ m}$:
    $v_Y = \sqrt{2 \times 9.8 \times 0.78} \approx 3.91\text{ m/s}$.

**2. Inside the Cylinder (Position $Y$ to $Z$):**
As the magnet moves through the copper cylinder, the changing magnetic flux induces eddy currents in the copper (Lenz’s Law). These eddy currents create a magnetic field that opposes the motion of the magnet.
*   **Energy Transformation:** The mechanical energy (gravitational potential + kinetic) of the magnet is no longer conserved. Some energy is dissipated as thermal energy due to the resistance of the copper ($I^2R$ heating).
*   **Quantitative Observation:** According to the graph, the magnet enters the cylinder at $t \approx 0.4\text{ s}$ and exits at $t \approx 0.9\text{ s}$ (duration $0.5\text{ s}$). The gradient (velocity) is significantly lower inside the cylinder compared to free fall, indicating a loss in kinetic energy.
*   **Calculation:**
    *   Change in $PE$ inside the cylinder: $\Delta PE = mg\Delta h = 0.04 \times 9.8 \times 0.20 = 0.0784\text{ J}$.
    *   Velocity inside the cylinder: $v = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.20}{0.5} = 0.4\text{ m/s}$.
    *   Kinetic energy inside: $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = 0.5 \times 0.04 \times (0.4)^2 = 0.0032\text{ J}$.
    *   Since the $KE$ at $Y$ (approx $0.306\text{ J}$) is much higher than the $KE$ inside the cylinder, a large amount of mechanical energy has been converted into heat in the copper.

### Summary
The motion demonstrates that while gravity does work to increase the magnet's energy, the electromagnetic induction within the copper acts as a non-conservative force. The "braking" effect is a direct result of the system converting mechanical energy into thermal energy, consistent with the First Law of Thermodynamics and Lenz's Law.
