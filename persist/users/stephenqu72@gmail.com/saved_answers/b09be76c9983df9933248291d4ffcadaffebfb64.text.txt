### Answer

**Historical Method: Fizeau’s Experiment (1849)**
Hippolyte Fizeau determined the speed of light using a terrestrial method. He directed a beam of light through the gap between teeth of a rapidly rotating cogwheel. The light traveled to a distant mirror (located at Suresnes, approx. 8 km away) and reflected back. If the cogwheel rotated at the correct speed, the returning light would be blocked by the next tooth. By knowing the distance ($d$) to the mirror, the number of teeth on the wheel, and the frequency of rotation ($f$), Fizeau could calculate the speed of light ($v$) using the relationship $v = \frac{2d}{t}$, where $t$ is the time taken for the light to travel the round trip.

**Contemporary Method: Laser Interferometry**
Modern measurements utilize lasers to determine the frequency ($f$) and wavelength ($\lambda$) of light in a vacuum. By applying the wave equation $c = f\lambda$, scientists can determine the speed of light with extreme precision. Techniques often involve stabilizing a laser frequency using atomic transitions (e.g., iodine or methane) and measuring the wavelength using a frequency comb or an interferometer.

**Definition of the Metre**
Since 1983, the speed of light ($c$) has been defined as a fixed universal constant: exactly $299\,792\,458 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. Consequently, the SI unit of length, the **metre**, is now defined as the distance light travels in a vacuum during a time interval of exactly $1/299\,792\,458$ of a second. This links our standard of length directly to the constancy of the speed of light and the precision of atomic clocks.
