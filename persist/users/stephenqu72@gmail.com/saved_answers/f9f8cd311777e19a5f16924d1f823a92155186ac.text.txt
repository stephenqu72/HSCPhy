### Answer
An electromagnetic wave is produced by an accelerating electric charge (such as an electron oscillating in an antenna). This acceleration creates a time-varying electric field, which, according to Maxwell’s equations, induces a time-varying magnetic field. 

The propagation of the wave occurs because these oscillating electric and magnetic fields are mutually self-sustaining: the changing electric field induces a changing magnetic field, and the changing magnetic field induces a changing electric field, allowing the wave to travel through space (or a vacuum) at the speed of light, $c$. The electric and magnetic field vectors are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of wave propagation.

2. A sinusoidal wave on the $xz$-plane representing the Magnetic field ($B$).
3. The waves should be in phase, with the direction of propagation along the $z$-axis.

### How to solve it
To score the full 3 marks on this HSC-style question, you need to structure your response to address both the "production" and "propagation" components clearly:

1.  **Production (1 mark):** Identify that the source is an **accelerating charge**. Mentioning oscillation is also key, as this creates the time-varying nature required to generate the fields.
2.  **Propagation (1 mark):** Explain the self-sustaining mechanism. Use the concept of induction: a changing $E$-field produces a $B$-field, and a changing $B$-field produces an $E$-field. Mention that this occurs at the speed of light.
3.  **Diagram/Communication (1 mark):** The diagram is mandatory. Ensure your diagram shows the $E$ and $B$ fields oscillating perpendicular to each other and perpendicular to the direction of propagation (transverse nature). Labeling your axes is essential for full marks.

**Teacher's Tip:** When describing electromagnetic waves, always use the term **"self-propagating"** and ensure you explicitly state that the oscillation of the charge is the fundamental cause.
