To solve these problems, we use the principles of conservation of momentum and the nature of collisions in classical mechanics.

### Question 8
**Answer: C. $4.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$**

**Explanation:**
This is an inelastic collision where the two objects join together after impact. According to the **Law of Conservation of Momentum**, the total momentum before the collision must equal the total momentum after the collision:
$$\sum p_{\text{before}} = \sum p_{\text{after}}$$
$$(m_X \times v_X) + (m_Y \times v_Y) = (m_X + m_Y) \times v_f$$

Given:
- $m_X = 10\text{ tonnes}$
- $v_X = 6.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$
- $m_Y = 5.0\text{ tonnes}$
- $v_Y = 0\text{ m s}^{-1}$ (stationary)

Substituting the values:
$$(10 \times 6.0) + (5.0 \times 0) = (10 + 5.0) \times v_f$$
$$60 = 15 \times v_f$$
$$v_f = \frac{60}{15} = 4.0\text{ m s}^{-1}$$

### Question 9
**Answer: B. kinetic energy is not conserved but momentum is conserved.**

**Explanation:**
1.  **Momentum:** In all isolated collisions (where no external forces like friction are considered), linear momentum is always conserved.
2.  **Kinetic Energy:** In a perfectly inelastic collision—where two objects stick together—kinetic energy is **not** conserved. Much of the initial kinetic energy is converted into other forms, such as internal energy (heat), sound, or deformation energy during the collision process. Therefore, the system loses kinetic energy.
