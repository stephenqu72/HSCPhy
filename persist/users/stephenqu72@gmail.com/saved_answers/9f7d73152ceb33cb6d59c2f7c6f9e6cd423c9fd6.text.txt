### Suggested Answer

**1. The Ampere and Experimental Measurement**
The definition of the Ampere reflects the progression from macroscopic, mechanical definitions to fundamental physical constants. Originally, the Ampere was defined through the force between two parallel current-carrying wires (Ampere's force law). This relied on the experimental measurement of $F = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2 L}{2\pi r}$. However, experimental realization of this was difficult and imprecise. 

In 2019, the SI system shifted to a definition based on the elementary charge $e$. This change was only possible because experimental measurements (using devices like the single-electron pump and the Kibble balance) reached such high precision that the value of $e$ could be fixed as a constant. This illustrates the role of experiment in moving physics from "device-dependent" definitions to "nature-dependent" definitions, increasing universal accuracy.

**2. The Speed of Light ($c$)**
Our understanding of $c$ evolved from unsuccessful attempts to prove it was finite (Galileo) to precise measurements (Fizeau, Foucault, Michelson). These experiments initially sought to determine a speed, but as precision increased, the measurement of $c$ became limited by the definition of the meter. 

Crucially, in 1983, the relationship $c = f\lambda$ allowed the scientific community to redefine the meter based on the fixed value of $c$. Experimental measurements moved from *measuring* the speed of light to *using* the constant speed of light to define length. This highlights how experiments drive theoretical shifts: once $c$ was measured with sufficient consistency, it became a fundamental anchor for the SI system itself.

**Conclusion**
In both cases, experimental measurement is the "gatekeeper" of scientific truth. It does not merely confirm theory; it acts as the necessary catalyst for redefining the very standards of human measurement, moving science toward greater objectivity and stability.
