To test the hypothesis that the gravitational force between the ISS and the capsule accounts for the circular motion, we must determine if the required centripetal force ($F_c$) matches the gravitational force ($F_g$) between the two objects.

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Calculate the required centripetal force ($F_c$):**
The centripetal force required to maintain the capsule in a circular orbit is given by the formula:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
Given:
- $m = 1.2 \times 10^4 \text{ kg}$
- $v = 0.233 \text{ m s}^{-1}$
- $r = 200 \text{ m}$

$$F_c = \frac{(1.2 \times 10^4) \times (0.233)^2}{200}$$
$$F_c = \frac{12000 \times 0.054289}{200} = 60 \times 0.054289 \approx 3.26 \text{ N}$$

**2. Calculate the gravitational force ($F_g$):**
Using Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation:
$$F_g = \frac{G M m}{r^2}$$
Given:
- $G \approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2 \text{ kg}^{-2}$
- $M = 4.2 \times 10^5 \text{ kg}$
- $m = 1.2 \times 10^4 \text{ kg}$
- $r = 200 \text{ m}$

$$F_g = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (4.2 \times 10^5) \times (1.2 \times 10^4)}{200^2}$$
$$F_g = \frac{3.36 \times 10^{-1}}{40000} \approx 8.4 \times 10^{-6} \text{ N}$$

**3. Conclusion:**
Comparing the two values:
- Required centripetal force $F_c \approx 3.26 \text{ N}$
- Gravitational force $F_g \approx 8.4 \times 10^{-6} \text{ N}$

Since $F_c \gg F_g$, the gravitational force between the capsule and the ISS is significantly too small to provide the centripetal force required for this orbit. Therefore, the hypothesis is **rejected**. The motion must be maintained by active propulsion (thrusters) rather than gravity.
