### Explanation for HSC Students
In the context of the HSC Physics syllabus, **reliability** refers to the consistency of a measurement or experiment. An experiment is considered reliable if repeating it under the same conditions yields the same or very similar results.

1.  **Why (c) is the correct answer:** Robert Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment is a classic example of scientific rigour. Because the experimental setup involved individual oil drops subject to various forces (gravity, electric force, viscous drag, and buoyancy), there were many sources of random error. To ensure that his measured values for the elementary charge $q$ were not just a fluke, Millikan performed thousands of individual measurements over several years. By observing that all the measured charges were integer multiples of a fundamental value ($q = ne$), the consistency of these repetitions proved the reliability of his data.
2.  **Why the others are incorrect:**
    *   **a)** While controlling variables is important for *validity*, reliability is specifically concerned with the repetition of trials to reduce the impact of random errors.
    *   **b)** This is logically circular. Millikan was trying to *determine* the charge of an electron; it was not a "known" value that he could compare against to establish his experiment's reliability at the time.
    *   **d)** Using water would not act as a control in the same way; water evaporates too quickly to be measured in this experimental apparatus, making it an impractical and ineffective modification.

In summary, when you see "reliability" in a multiple-choice question, you should immediately look for options that mention **repetition, large sample sizes, or consistency of results.**
