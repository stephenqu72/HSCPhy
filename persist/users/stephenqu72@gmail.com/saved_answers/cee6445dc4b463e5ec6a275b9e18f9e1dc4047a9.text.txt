### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a): Explain how mass defect and binding energy relate to atoms.

**Answer:**
Mass defect ($\Delta m$) is the difference between the mass of an atomic nucleus and the sum of the individual masses of its constituent protons and neutrons (nucleons). When nucleons come together to form a stable nucleus, a small amount of mass is converted into energy and released. This energy is known as the binding energy ($BE$). Binding energy is the energy required to completely disassemble a nucleus into its constituent nucleons, or conversely, the energy released when the nucleus is formed. The relationship between the two is defined by Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, given by the equation $BE = \Delta m c^2$. A higher binding energy per nucleon indicates a more stable nucleus.

**How to solve:**
To gain full marks for a 3-mark question, you must hit these key points:
1. Define mass defect correctly (the mass "lost").
2. Define binding energy as the energy equivalent of that mass defect.
3. Explicitly link them using Einstein's relation ($E=mc^2$).

#### Part (b): Calculate the binding energy for the Carbon 12 atom.

**Note:** To calculate this, we use the standard values:
- Mass of a proton ($m_p$) $\approx 1.007276 \, \text{u}$
- Mass of a neutron ($m_n$) $\approx 1.008665 \, \text{u}$
- Mass of a Carbon-12 atom $\approx 12.000000 \, \text{u}$
- Conversion factor: $1 \, \text{u} \approx 931.5 \, \text{MeV}/c^2$

**Step-by-step Calculation:**
1. **Identify the constituents:** A Carbon-12 nucleus has $Z=6$ protons and $N=6$ neutrons.
2. **Calculate expected mass:**
   $$\text{Mass}_{\text{expected}} = (6 \times 1.007276) + (6 \times 1.008665) = 12.095646 \, \text{u}$$
3. **Calculate mass defect ($\Delta m$):**
   $$\Delta m = 12.095646 - 12.000000 = 0.095646 \, \text{u}$$
4. **Calculate binding energy in MeV:**
   $$BE = 0.095646 \, \text{u} \times 931.5 \, \text{MeV/u} \approx 89.10 \, \text{MeV}$$

**How to solve:**
1. State the number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
2. Find the sum of the individual masses of these nucleons.
3. Subtract the actual mass of the nucleus from the sum calculated in step 2 to find the mass defect.
4. Multiply the result by the conversion factor ($931.5 \, \text{MeV}$) to convert atomic mass units into energy units.
