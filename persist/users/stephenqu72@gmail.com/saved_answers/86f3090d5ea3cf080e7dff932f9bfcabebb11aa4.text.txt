To determine the intensity of the transmitted light in Diagram II, we use **Malus's Law**, which states:

$$I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$$

Where:
- $I$ is the transmitted intensity.
- $I_0$ is the intensity of the incident polarized light.
- $\theta$ is the angle between the transmission axis of the polarizer and the plane of polarization of the incident light.

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Analyze Diagram I:**
    The incident intensity is $I_0 = 5\text{ W m}^{-2}$ and the transmitted intensity is $I = 2\text{ W m}^{-2}$. While we could calculate the initial angle $\theta_1$ using $\cos^2(\theta_1) = \frac{2}{5}$, we actually don't need to know the specific value of $\theta_1$ to solve the problem for the rotation.

2.  **Analyze the Rotation:**
    The question states that in Diagram II, the polarizer has been rotated through $90^\circ$ relative to its position in Diagram I.
    If the initial angle of the transmission axis relative to the incident light polarization was $\theta_1$, the new angle $\theta_2$ becomes $\theta_1 + 90^\circ$.

3.  **Apply Malus's Law to Diagram II:**
    The new intensity $I_2$ is:
    $$I_2 = I_0 \cos^2(\theta_1 + 90^\circ)$$

    Using the trigonometric identity $\cos(\theta + 90^\circ) = -\sin(\theta)$, we get:
    $$I_2 = I_0 (-\sin(\theta_1))^2 = I_0 \sin^2(\theta_1)$$

4.  **Connect the two intensities:**
    We know that for any angle $\theta$, $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$, which implies $\sin^2(\theta) = 1 - \cos^2(\theta)$.
    From Diagram I, we know $\cos^2(\theta_1) = \frac{I_1}{I_0} = \frac{2}{5} = 0.4$.
    Therefore, $\sin^2(\theta_1) = 1 - 0.4 = 0.6$.

5.  **Calculate the final intensity:**
    $$I_2 = I_0 \times \sin^2(\theta_1) = 5 \times 0.6 = 3\text{ W m}^{-2}$$

Alternatively, a quick conceptual check: If a polarizer is rotated by $90^\circ$, the new intensity is simply $I_0 - I_1 = 5 - 2 = 3\text{ W m}^{-2}$.
