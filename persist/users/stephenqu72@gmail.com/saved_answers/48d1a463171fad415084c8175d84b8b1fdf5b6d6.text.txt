To solve this problem, we need to apply the concepts of uniform circular motion, work, and conservation of energy.

### Analysis

1.  **Object A (Horizontal Circular Motion):**
    *   In uniform circular motion on a horizontal plane, the speed of the object remains constant. Because the speed is constant, the kinetic energy ($E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) remains constant.
    *   Since the motion is horizontal, the height of the object does not change, meaning the gravitational potential energy ($E_p = mgh$) remains constant.
    *   Because both kinetic and potential energy are constant, the **Total Mechanical Energy is constant**.
    *   By the Work-Energy Theorem, the net work done on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy. Since the speed (and thus kinetic energy) does not change, the **net work done is zero**.

2.  **Object B (Vertical Circular Motion):**
    *   The problem states this is "uniform circular motion," which implies a constant angular velocity and constant speed.
    *   If the object moves in a vertical circle at a constant speed, its kinetic energy is constant. However, as it moves, its height $h$ changes. Therefore, its gravitational potential energy ($E_p = mgh$) varies throughout the orbit.
    *   Since Total Mechanical Energy = $E_k + E_p$, and $E_p$ is changing while $E_k$ remains constant, the **Total Mechanical Energy changes**.
    *   Regarding work: If the speed is constant, the change in kinetic energy is zero, so the **net work done is zero**. 

### Conclusion
*   Object A: Net work = Zero, Total Mechanical Energy = Constant.
*   Object B: Net work = Zero, Total Mechanical Energy = Changes.
