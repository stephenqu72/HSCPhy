To solve this problem, we need to calculate the total energy emitted by the source per second, which by definition is the power in Watts (since $1\text{ W} = 1\text{ J/s}$).

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

**1. Identify the given information:**
*   Wavelength ($\lambda$) = $550\text{ nm} = 550 \times 10^{-9}\text{ m}$
*   Number of photons per second ($n$) = $2.8 \times 10^{16}$
*   Planck's constant ($h$) $\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J s}$
*   Speed of light ($c$) $\approx 3.0 \times 10^8\text{ m/s}$

**2. Calculate the energy of a single photon ($E_p$):**
Using the formula $E_p = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$:
$$E_p = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.0 \times 10^8)}{550 \times 10^{-9}}$$
$$E_p \approx \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{550 \times 10^{-9}} \approx 3.614 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$$

**3. Calculate the total power ($P$):**
The total power is the energy of one photon multiplied by the number of photons emitted per second:
$$P = n \times E_p$$
$$P = (2.8 \times 10^{16}) \times (3.614 \times 10^{-19})$$
$$P \approx 1.012 \times 10^{-2}\text{ W}$$

**4. Conclusion:**
Comparing our result to the options provided, $1.012 \times 10^{-2}\text{ W}$ is closest to option A.

**Correct Option: A**
