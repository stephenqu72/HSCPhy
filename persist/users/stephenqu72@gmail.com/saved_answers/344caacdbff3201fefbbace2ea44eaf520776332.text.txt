### Teacher's Explanation

To solve this problem, we must resolve the forces acting on the car in two different scenarios: the static case (parking) and the dynamic case (turning at maximum speed).

**Part (a): Angle of banking**
When the car is stationary on the slope, it is on the verge of sliding down. The forces acting on it are gravity ($mg$ downward), the normal force ($N$ perpendicular to the slope), and the maximum static friction ($f = \mu N$ up the slope).
For equilibrium:
1. Perpendicular to the slope: $N = mg \cos \theta$
2. Parallel to the slope: $f = mg \sin \theta$

Given $f = \frac{1}{6}N$, substitute these expressions:
$$\frac{1}{6}(mg \cos \theta) = mg \sin \theta$$
$$\tan \theta = \frac{1}{6} \implies \theta = \arctan(1/6) \approx 9.46^\circ$$

**Part (b): Maximum speed**
When moving at maximum speed $v$, the car is on the verge of sliding *up* the slope. Friction now acts down the slope ($f = \mu N$). The centripetal force is provided by the horizontal components of $N$ and $f$.
Resolving forces:
- Vertical: $N \cos \theta - f \sin \theta = mg$
- Horizontal: $N \sin \theta + f \cos \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$

Substitute $f = \frac{1}{6}N$ and divide the equations to eliminate $N$ and $m$:
$$\frac{v^2}{rg} = \frac{\sin \theta + \mu \cos \theta}{\cos \theta - \mu \sin \theta} = \frac{\tan \theta + \mu}{1 - \mu \tan \theta}$$
With $\tan \theta = 1/6$ and $\mu = 1/6$:
$$\frac{v^2}{rg} = \frac{1/6 + 1/6}{1 - (1/6)(1/6)} = \frac{2/6}{35/36} = \frac{1}{3} \times \frac{36}{35} = \frac{12}{35}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{12}{35} \times 66.0 \times 9.8} \approx \sqrt{221.76} \approx 14.89\text{ m/s}$$

---

### Answers

**(a)** The angle of banking $\theta$:
$$\tan \theta = \mu = \frac{1}{6}$$
$$\theta = \arctan(0.1667) = 9.46^\circ$$

**(b)** The maximum speed $v$:
Using the formula $v = \sqrt{rg \left( \frac{\tan \theta + \mu}{1 - \mu \tan \theta} \right)}$:
$$v = \sqrt{66.0 \times 9.8 \times \left( \frac{1/6 + 1/6}{1 - 1/36} \right)}$$
$$v = \sqrt{646.8 \times \frac{12}{35}} \approx 14.89\text{ m/s}$$
