### Teacher's Solution and Explanation

To solve these problems, we must apply the principles of **circular motion** and **Newton’s Second Law**. At any point on a curved track, the net force providing the centripetal acceleration ($F_c$) is the vector sum of the normal force ($F_N$) and the component of gravity acting towards the center of the circle.

#### (a) Determine the magnitude of the Normal force at point B
At point B, the track is at the bottom of a dip. The center of the curvature is above the car. The forces acting on the car are the Normal force ($F_N$) pointing upward and the gravitational force ($mg$) pointing downward. 

The net centripetal force is:
$$F_{net} = F_N - mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$F_N = m \left(g + \frac{v^2}{r}\right)$$

**Given:** $m = 540\text{ kg}$, $v = 14.1\text{ m s}^{-1}$, $r = 8.0\text{ m}$, and using $g \approx 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$:
$$F_N = 540 \times \left(9.8 + \frac{14.1^2}{8.0}\right)$$
$$F_N = 540 \times (9.8 + 24.85) = 540 \times 34.65$$
$$F_N \approx 18,711\text{ N}$$

#### (b) Determine the maximum speed at point A
Point A is the crest of a hill. The center of curvature is below the car. The forces acting on the car are gravity ($mg$) downward and the Normal force ($F_N$) upward. The net centripetal force is:
$$mg - F_N = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
The car "leaves the track" when $F_N = 0$. At this limit:
$$mg = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies v_{max} = \sqrt{gr}$$

**Calculation:**
$$v_{max} = \sqrt{9.8 \times 7.5} = \sqrt{73.5} \approx 8.57\text{ m s}^{-1}$$

**Assessment:**
The predicted speed at point A is $10\text{ m s}^{-1}$. Since $10\text{ m s}^{-1} > 8.57\text{ m s}^{-1}$, the car will lose contact with the track at this speed. Therefore, the predicted speed of $10\text{ m s}^{-1}$ is **too high**.
