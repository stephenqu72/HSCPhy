### Answer
The phenomenon observed is due to the principles of **polarization**. The left-hand lens is a polarizing filter oriented such that its transmission axis is parallel to the polarization direction of the incident light (or the incident light is unpolarized and the lens acts as a consistent filter), meaning its orientation relative to the light source does not change its ability to transmit light intensity. 

Conversely, the right-hand lens is part of a system involving two polarizers (the light source/environment and the lens). As the angle of inclination changes, the angle $\theta$ between the transmission axis of the lens and the polarization plane of the incident light changes. According to **Malus's Law**, the transmitted intensity $I$ is given by:
$$I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$$
As the angle increases, the value of $\cos^2(\theta)$ decreases, leading to the observed reduction in the amount of light passing through the right-hand lens.
