### Solution and Explanation

To solve this problem, we treat the rocket's motion after it runs out of fuel as a **projectile motion** problem. We use the equations of motion with constant acceleration due to gravity, $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$. Let the point where the fuel runs out be the origin for this segment of the motion, where initial vertical displacement $s_y = -260\text{ m}$ (downward) and time $t = 12\text{ s}$.

#### (a) What was the speed $U$ of the rocket?
The vertical component of velocity is $v_{0y} = U\sin(65^\circ)$.
Using the displacement formula $s_y = v_{0y}t + \frac{1}{2}a_y t^2$:
$$-260 = (U\sin 65^\circ)(12) + \frac{1}{2}(-9.8)(12)^2$$
$$-260 = 10.876U - 705.6$$
$$445.6 = 10.876U$$
$$U \approx 40.97\text{ m/s}$$

#### (b) What is the distance $X$?
The horizontal velocity is constant: $v_x = U\cos(65^\circ) = 40.97 \times \cos(65^\circ) \approx 17.32\text{ m/s}$.
The total horizontal distance covered while coasting is $\Delta x = v_x t = 17.32 \times 12 = 207.84\text{ m}$.
The total distance $X$ from the launch point is the initial offset plus this distance:
$$X = 52 + 207.84 = 259.84\text{ m} \approx 260\text{ m}$$

#### (c) Why would this approach fail for a height of 100 km?
The calculation assumes that the acceleration due to gravity ($g$) is constant at $9.8\text{ m/s}^2$. 
1. **Gravitational Field Strength:** At a height of 100 km, the rocket is significantly further from the Earth's center. According to Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation, $g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$, the value of $g$ decreases as $r$ increases. 
2. **Curvature of the Earth:** Over such a large displacement, the assumption that the Earth's surface is a flat plane is no longer valid. The "horizontal" direction changes as the rocket moves, and a spherical model would be required.
