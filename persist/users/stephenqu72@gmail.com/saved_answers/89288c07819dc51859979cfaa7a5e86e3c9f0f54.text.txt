To answer this question, we must apply the principles of Einstein's Special Theory of Relativity.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A)**.

### Explanation
In physics, the **Principle of Relativity** states that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference. A fundamental consequence of this is that every observer perceives their own "local" time as passing at the "normal" rate (i.e., one second per second).

When we discuss **time dilation**, we are comparing two different frames of reference:
1. **Proper time ($\Delta t_0$):** This is the time interval measured by an observer who is at rest relative to the events occurring. For both the stationary person and the person moving at a constant velocity, they each consider themselves to be "at rest" in their own frame of reference. Therefore, in their own frames, they perceive time passing normally.
2. **Relativistic time dilation:** If an outside observer looks at a clock moving relative to them, they will observe that moving clock to be ticking slower. Specifically, using the Lorentz transformation formula:
   $$\Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$
   Where $\Delta t$ is the time measured by the stationary observer and $\Delta t_0$ is the time measured by the moving observer. 

However, the question asks what each person **observes** regarding the flow of time in their own frame. Because both people are in their own inertial frames of reference, they both observe their own time to be passing at the standard rate. The "slowing down" is an observation made by one observer *about* the other.

*Note: In reality, an object with mass cannot reach the speed of light ($c$), but according to the logic of special relativity, even if one were to approach $c$, one's own wristwatch would always appear to function normally from one's own perspective.*
