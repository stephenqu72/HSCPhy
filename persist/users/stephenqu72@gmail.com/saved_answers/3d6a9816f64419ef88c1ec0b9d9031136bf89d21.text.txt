To solve this projectile motion problem, we must treat horizontal and vertical components of motion independently.

**Given:**
*   Horizontal velocity $v_x = 8.00\text{ ms}^{-1}$
*   Time to return to original height (time of flight for the symmetric part) $t_1 = 3.00\text{ s}$
*   Additional time to reach the ground $t_2 = 0.11\text{ s}$
*   Total time of flight $T = 3.00 + 0.11 = 3.11\text{ s}$
*   Acceleration due to gravity $g = 9.8\text{ ms}^{-2}$ (acting downwards)

---

### (a) Calculate the horizontal range
The range $R$ is the total horizontal distance covered during the total time of flight.
$$R = v_x \times T = 8.00 \times 3.11 = 24.88\text{ m}$$

### (b) Calculate the vertical component of initial velocity ($v_{iy}$)
In projectile motion, returning to the original height takes $3.00\text{ s}$. The time to reach the peak is half of this, $t_{peak} = 1.50\text{ s}$. At the peak, the vertical velocity is $0$.
Using $v_f = v_{iy} + at$:
$$0 = v_{iy} + (-9.8 \times 1.50)$$
$$v_{iy} = 14.7\text{ ms}^{-1}$$

### (c) Calculate the initial angle of projection ($\theta$)
The angle $\theta$ is found using the relationship between the horizontal and vertical components of the initial velocity:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v_{iy}}{v_x} = \frac{14.7}{8.00} = 1.8375$$
$$\theta = \tan^{-1}(1.8375) \approx 61.4^\circ$$

### (d) Calculate the height at which Lauren threw the ball ($h$)
The ball falls below its original release point during the final $0.11\text{ s}$. At the moment it passes the release height (at $t = 3.00\text{ s}$), its vertical velocity is $-v_{iy}$ (due to symmetry).
So, at $t = 3.00\text{ s}$, $v_{y} = -14.7\text{ ms}^{-1}$.
Now, look at the final $0.11\text{ s}$ interval:
$u = -14.7\text{ ms}^{-1}$, $t = 0.11\text{ s}$, $a = -9.8\text{ ms}^{-2}$
$$\Delta y = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$
$$\Delta y = (-14.7 \times 0.11) + \frac{1}{2}(-9.8)(0.11)^2$$
$$\Delta y = -1.617 - 0.05929 = -1.67629\text{ m}$$
The height $h$ is the magnitude of this displacement: $h \approx 1.68\text{ m}$.
