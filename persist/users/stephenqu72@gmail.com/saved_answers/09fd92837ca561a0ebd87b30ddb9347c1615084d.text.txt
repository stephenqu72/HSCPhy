### Part (a)
**Answer/Explanation:**
To determine the direction of the induced current, we apply **Lenz’s Law**, which states that the induced current will create a magnetic field that opposes the change in magnetic flux.

1.  **Change in Flux:** As the North pole of the bar magnet approaches the solenoid from above, the downward magnetic flux through the coil increases.
2.  **Opposing Field:** To oppose this increase, the coil must create an upward-pointing magnetic field (North pole at the top of the coil).
3.  **Right-Hand Grip Rule:** Point your right thumb upward (the direction of the induced magnetic field). Your fingers curl around the coil in the direction of the conventional current.
4.  **Drawing:** On the diagram, draw arrows on the front of the coil windings pointing to the **left**. This current will flow out of the top wire and into the bottom wire (or vice versa depending on your orientation, but the relative direction must be consistent with the field pointing up).

### Part (b)
**Answer/Explanation:**
This is a classic demonstration of electromagnetic damping caused by **eddy currents**.

*   **The Unslotted Pipe:** As the magnet falls, the changing magnetic flux induces circular "eddy" currents in the conductive copper pipe. According to Lenz’s Law, these currents create their own magnetic fields that oppose the motion of the falling magnet. This results in an upward magnetic force (magnetic braking), causing the magnet to fall **slowly**.
*   **The Slotted Pipe:** The vertical slit breaks the path for the circular eddy currents. Because the currents cannot complete a full loop around the pipe circumference, the magnitude of the eddy currents is significantly reduced. Consequently, the opposing magnetic force is much weaker, and the magnet falls **significantly faster**, closer to the rate of free-fall.
