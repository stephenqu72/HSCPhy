To find the momentum of the electron, we use the **de Broglie hypothesis**, which relates the wavelength ($\lambda$) of a particle to its momentum ($p$).

### The Formula
The de Broglie wavelength is defined as:
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$$
Where:
*   $\lambda$ is the wavelength ($3.33 \times 10^{-10} \text{ m}$)
*   $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$)
*   $p$ is the momentum

### Step-by-Step Calculation
1.  **Rearrange the formula** to solve for momentum ($p$):
    $$p = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$

2.  **Substitute the known values**:
    $$p = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{3.33 \times 10^{-10}}$$

3.  **Perform the division**:
    $$p \approx 1.98978 \times 10^{-24} \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m/s}$$

4.  **Compare with the given options**:
    Rounding our result to three significant figures gives $1.99 \times 10^{-24}$. This matches option **(B)**.

**Conclusion:** The correct answer is **(B)**.
