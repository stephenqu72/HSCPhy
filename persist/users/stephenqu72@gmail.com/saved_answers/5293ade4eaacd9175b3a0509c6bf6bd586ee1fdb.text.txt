### Answers

**(a)** Stars **A**, **B**, and **E**. 
*Reasoning:* These stars are on the Main Sequence. Stars with masses roughly between $0.08$ and $8$ solar masses will eventually exhaust their fuel, shed their outer layers as planetary nebulae, and leave behind a white dwarf core.

**(b)** Star **B** is a Red Giant. In this stage, two processes occur:
1. **Hydrogen shell burning:** Fusing hydrogen into helium in a shell surrounding the inert helium core.
2. **Helium fusion (the Triple-Alpha process):** Fusing helium nuclei into carbon within the core.

**(c)(i)**
* **Proton-Proton (P-P) Chain:** Dominates in stars with core temperatures below approximately $15$ million Kelvin. It involves the direct fusion of hydrogen nuclei into helium-4.
* **CNO Cycle:** Dominates in stars with core temperatures above approximately $17$ million Kelvin. It uses Carbon, Nitrogen, and Oxygen as catalysts to fuse hydrogen into helium-4. It is much more temperature-sensitive than the P-P chain.

**(c)(ii)**
The **core temperature** of the star determines which process dominates. This, in turn, is primarily determined by the **mass** of the star (higher mass leads to higher gravitational pressure and higher core temperatures).

### Teacher's Explanation

*   **Part (a):** Understand the life cycle of stars. The "Main Sequence" (where A, E, and B are) represents the longest phase of a star's life. Low to intermediate-mass stars ($< 8M_{\odot}$) evolve into Red Giants and eventually leave behind white dwarfs. Star C is a Supergiant (likely a high-mass star that will go supernova), and D is already a White Dwarf.
*   **Part (b):** You must recognise Star B's position on the HR diagram as the "Red Giant Branch." Once a star leaves the main sequence, its core contracts (heating up) while its envelope expands. This allows for helium fusion in the core and hydrogen fusion in the surrounding shell.
*   **Part (c)(i):** This is a comparison question. Focus on the *mechanism* (direct vs. catalytic) and the *temperature requirement*. The CNO cycle is a much more efficient energy producer but requires the significantly higher thermal energy found in massive stars to overcome the electrostatic repulsion of the heavier Carbon/Nitrogen/Oxygen nuclei.
*   **Part (c)(ii):** Remember the link: **Mass $\rightarrow$ Core Pressure $\rightarrow$ Core Temperature $\rightarrow$ Dominant Fusion Rate.** The core temperature is the fundamental driver of the reaction rate kinetics.
