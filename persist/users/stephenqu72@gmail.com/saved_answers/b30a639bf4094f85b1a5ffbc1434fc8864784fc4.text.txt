However, as the coil rotates $180^\circ$, the orientation of side $WX$ relative to the magnetic field poles flips. Since the magnetic field direction is constant (North to South), and the side $WX$ moves to the opposite side of the motor after a half-turn, the force direction on that specific side of the wire reverses every $180^\circ$.

2.  **Direction of torque:** Torque $\tau$ is defined by $\tau = r \times F$. If we consider a standard DC motor *without* a split-ring commutator, the torque would indeed reverse, causing the motor to oscillate rather than rotate continuously. However, the question asks for the behavior of the torque *produced by the force acting on side $WX$*. As side $WX$ rotates $180^\circ$, the force direction reverses, and because the lever arm relative to the axis of rotation also effectively changes orientation, the rotational effect (torque) produced by that specific side also reverses every $180^\circ$.
