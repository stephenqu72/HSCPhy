#### Part b) Calculation
To show that the charge $q = 2\ \mu C$ ($2 \times 10^{-6}\ C$), we use Coulomb’s Law:
$$F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$
Given that the objects are identical, $q_1 = q_2 = q$, so $F = \frac{kq^2}{r^2}$. Rearranging to solve for $q$:
$$q = \sqrt{\frac{F \cdot r^2}{k}}$$

1.  Substitute the values:
    $$q = \sqrt{\frac{39.95 \times (0.03)^2}{8.99 \times 10^9}}$$
2.  Calculate the numerator:
    $$39.95 \times 0.0009 = 0.035955$$
3.  Divide by $k$:
    $$\frac{0.035955}{8.99 \times 10^9} \approx 4.0 \times 10^{-12}$$
4.  Take the square root:
    $$q = \sqrt{4.0 \times 10^{-12}} = 2.0 \times 10^{-6}\ C = 2\ \mu C$$
