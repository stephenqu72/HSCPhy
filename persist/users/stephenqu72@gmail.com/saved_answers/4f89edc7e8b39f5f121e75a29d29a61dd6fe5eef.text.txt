### Answer
The correct answer is **D. $t_S$**.

### Explanation
This question relates to the **Rømer method** for measuring the speed of light, which relies on the observation of the eclipses of Jupiter's moon, Io.

1.  **The Physics:** The "observed" orbital period is the actual period of Io plus (or minus) the time it takes for light to travel the change in distance between Jupiter and Earth. Because Earth is moving in its orbit, the distance between Earth and Jupiter is constantly changing.
2.  **Light Travel Time:** If Earth is moving **away** from Jupiter, the light from each successive eclipse has a longer distance to travel than the previous one, making the observed interval between eclipses *longer* than the true orbital period. If Earth is moving **towards** Jupiter, the distance decreases, making the observed interval *shorter*.
3.  **Analyzing the Positions:** 
    *   Look at the arrows on Earth's orbit. 
    *   At position $P$ (near $P_1$ to $P_2$), Earth is moving generally across/towards the Jupiter system.
    *   At position $Q$, Earth is moving towards Jupiter.
    *   At position $R$, Earth is moving across the orbit.
    *   At position $S$, Earth is moving **away** from Jupiter. 
4.  **Conclusion:** When Earth is moving away from Jupiter (at position $S$), the light from the moon has to cover an increasing distance to reach the observer. This causes the signal to arrive later than it would if the Earth were stationary, resulting in the longest measured time interval ($t_S$) between consecutive orbits.
