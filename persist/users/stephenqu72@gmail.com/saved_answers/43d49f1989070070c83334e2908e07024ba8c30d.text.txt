To answer this question, we must look at the principles of uniform circular motion.

### Answer
The correct option is **(C) Directed inward toward the centre.**

### Explanation
When an object moves in a circular path at a constant speed, it is undergoing **uniform circular motion**. 

1.  **Acceleration in Circular Motion:** Even though the speed (the magnitude of velocity) is constant, the *direction* of the velocity vector is constantly changing as the car moves around the track. Because acceleration is defined as the rate of change of velocity ($a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$), a change in direction means there must be an acceleration. This is known as **centripetal acceleration**.
2.  **Newton’s Second Law:** According to Newton’s Second Law, $F_{net} = ma$. Since there is an acceleration directed toward the centre of the circle (centripetal acceleration), there must be a corresponding **net force** acting in that same direction.
3.  **Why the others are incorrect:**
    *   **(A)** A force tangent to the circle would cause the car to change its *speed* (tangential acceleration), but the question states the speed is constant.
    *   **(B)** An outward force (often confused with "centrifugal force") is a fictitious force that does not exist in an inertial frame of reference; it is not the net force acting on the object.
    *   **(D)** If the net force were zero, the car would move in a straight line at a constant velocity, according to Newton’s First Law (Law of Inertia).

Therefore, for any object moving in a circle, the net force must be the **centripetal force**, which is always directed toward the centre of the circular path.
