### Answer and Explanation

**a. Describe what the absorption spectra reveal about the motion of star P.**
*   **Answer:** The absorption spectrum of Star P is redshifted compared to the laboratory spectrum. All absorption lines have shifted toward longer wavelengths (e.g., the line near $400\text{ nm}$ in the lab is at a higher value in Star P). This indicates that the star is moving away from Earth.
*   **Explanation:** Compare the position of the lines. Since the lines in the star's spectrum appear at higher wavelengths than the stationary laboratory reference, the light has undergone a Doppler shift. Specifically, a shift toward the red end of the spectrum (longer wavelengths) confirms the star is exhibiting **recession**.

**b. Describe what changes will occur to the absorption spectrum of P as it continues onto the next evolutionary stage as a star.**
*   **Answer:** As Star P (a Main Sequence star) evolves into a Red Giant, it will expand and its surface temperature will decrease. This decrease in temperature will lead to changes in the ionization states of the atoms in its atmosphere, causing some spectral lines to fade and new lines (corresponding to molecules like titanium oxide or neutral metals) to appear or become more prominent. Additionally, the luminosity class change may lead to narrower absorption lines due to a decrease in surface gravity (pressure broadening).
*   **Explanation:** Moving off the main sequence toward the giant branch involves cooling. Wien's Law suggests the peak wavelength will shift toward the red, but the *absorption lines* are defined by the temperature-dependent excitation and ionization levels of the elements in the stellar photosphere (Saha-Boltzmann equations).

**c. Explain why there are relatively fewer Main Sequence stars in spectral classes B and A.**
*   **Answer:** Stars in spectral classes B and A are very massive and hot. According to the mass-luminosity relationship ($L \propto M^{3.5}$), these stars consume their hydrogen fuel at an extremely rapid rate. Consequently, their lifespan is significantly shorter compared to cooler, less massive stars (like M-class stars).
*   **Explanation:** This is a classic "Initial Mass Function" and stellar evolution question. Because O, B, and A stars "live fast and die young," they spend a very small fraction of the total galactic lifetime on the Main Sequence, meaning we are statistically less likely to observe them at any given time compared to long-lived, low-mass stars.
