### The Explanation

J.J. Thomson used a **cathode ray tube (CRT)** to determine the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$) of the electron. The core of the experiment relies on firing electrons through a region where they are subjected to both an electric field ($E$) and a magnetic field ($B$).

1.  **The setup:** Electrons are accelerated from a cathode towards an anode by a potential difference. A narrow beam enters a region containing parallel metal plates (providing an electric field) and Helmholtz coils (providing a uniform magnetic field perpendicular to the electric field).
2.  **Balancing forces:** Thomson adjusted the electric and magnetic fields until the electron beam passed through undeflected. At this state, the electric force ($F_e = qE$) is equal and opposite to the magnetic force ($F_m = qvB$):
    $$qE = qvB \implies v = \frac{E}{B}$$
3.  **Deflection:** Once the velocity ($v$) is known, the electric field is turned off. The magnetic field alone causes the electrons to move in a circular path with radius $r$. The magnetic force provides the centripetal force:
    $$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
4.  **Derivation:** By rearranging for $q/m$:
    $$\frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br} = \frac{E}{B^2r}$$
    By measuring $E$, $B$, and the radius of curvature $r$, the ratio $q/m$ is calculated.
