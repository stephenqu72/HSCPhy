### Answer

**a) Explain the relationship between rotation, magnetic flux, and back emf as the motor turns. (4 marks)**

As the coil of the motor rotates within the external magnetic field, the magnetic flux $\Phi$ passing through the coil changes continuously. According to Faraday’s Law, the induced electromotive force (emf) is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux:
$$\varepsilon = -N \frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}$$
As the motor turns, this changing flux induces a voltage within the coil that opposes the applied voltage (Lenz’s Law). This is known as **back emf**. 

The magnitude of the back emf depends on the rate of rotation; as the motor speeds up, the rate of change of flux increases, leading to a higher back emf. This back emf acts to limit the net current flowing through the motor, which prevents the motor from overheating and helps it reach a steady-state operating speed where the torque produced matches the mechanical load.

**b) Explain why AC induction motors are widely used in industries. (3 marks)**

AC induction motors are preferred in industrial applications for several key reasons:
1. **Durability and low maintenance:** They have a simple, robust construction (particularly the squirrel-cage rotor) with no brushes or commutators, which reduces mechanical wear and the need for frequent servicing.
2. **Efficiency and Reliability:** They are highly efficient and can operate in harsh industrial environments (such as dusty or volatile conditions) because they do not produce sparks, unlike DC motors with brushes.
3. **Cost-effectiveness:** They are relatively inexpensive to manufacture, simple to control via modern variable frequency drives (VFDs), and can operate directly from the standard AC power supply available in industrial facilities.
