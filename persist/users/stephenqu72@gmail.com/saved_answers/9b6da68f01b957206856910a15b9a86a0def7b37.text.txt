To determine the magnitude of the electric field halfway between two point charges, we use the principle of superposition.

### Explanation

1.  **Formula for a Point Charge:** The magnitude of the electric field $E$ at a distance $d$ from a point charge $q$ is given by Coulomb's Law for electric fields:
    $$E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{|q|}{d^2}$$

2.  **Geometry:** We are looking for the electric field at the midpoint between charges $q_1$ and $q_2$. The distance from each charge to the midpoint is $d = \frac{r}{2}$.

3.  **Superposition Principle:** The total electric field at any point is the vector sum of the electric fields produced by each charge individually. 
    *   The magnitude of the field from $q_1$ at the midpoint is $E_1 = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{|q_1|}{(r/2)^2}$.
    *   The magnitude of the field from $q_2$ at the midpoint is $E_2 = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{|q_2|}{(r/2)^2}$.

4.  **Directionality:** Electric field is a vector. If $q_1$ and $q_2$ have the same sign (both positive or both negative), the fields point in opposite directions at the midpoint, and the magnitude is the *difference* of the individual field strengths:
    $$E_{total} = |E_1 - E_2| = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{|q_1| - |q_2|}{(r/2)^2} \text{ (depending on which is larger)}$$
    If the charges have opposite signs, their electric field vectors point in the same direction at the midpoint, and the magnitude is the *sum* of the individual field strengths:
    $$E_{total} = E_1 + E_2 = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{|q_1| + |q_2|}{(r/2)^2}$$

5.  **Selecting the Best Answer:** In the context of generic physics problems asking for the "magnitude" of the combined field, options A and B represent the extreme cases of constructive and destructive interference respectively. However, strictly mathematically, the magnitude of the *resultant vector sum* for two charges on an axis requires the addition of the vectors. Because electric field vectors act along the line connecting the charges, the resultant magnitude is $\left| \vec{E}_1 + \vec{E}_2 \right|$. If we consider the magnitude of the vectors acting in opposite directions (as is standard for two like charges), the difference (Option B) is often cited in specific textbook problems, but the sum (Option A) is the standard representation for the superposition of magnitudes when directions are aligned. 

Given the typical structure of these multiple-choice questions, **Option A** is the standard representation of the combined influence (sum of magnitudes) of the two charges.
