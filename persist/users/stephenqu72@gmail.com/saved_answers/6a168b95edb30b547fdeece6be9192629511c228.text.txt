To determine which option results in a decrease in overall mass, we must apply Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, given by the famous equation:

$$E = mc^2$$

This principle states that mass and energy are interchangeable. Specifically, when a system releases energy to its surroundings (an exothermic process), the total mass of the system must decrease by an amount $\Delta m = \frac{\Delta E}{c^2}$.

### Explanation of Options:

*   **(A) Splitting a helium nucleus:** Splitting a helium nucleus (an alpha particle) is an endothermic process. Because helium is extremely stable (it has a very high binding energy per nucleon), "unbinding" its nucleons requires an input of energy. According to $E=mc^2$, adding energy to a system increases its mass. Thus, this results in an *increase* in mass.
*   **(B) Lifting an object off the ground:** Lifting an object increases its gravitational potential energy. While this adds energy to the system and theoretically increases its mass by an infinitesimal amount ($\Delta m = \frac{mgh}{c^2}$), this is usually considered an external interaction or a change in potential energy state rather than a internal mass-energy conversion.
*   **(C) An exothermic reaction:** An exothermic reaction releases energy into the surroundings. If the system loses energy ($\Delta E < 0$), it must also lose mass ($\Delta m < 0$) to satisfy the conservation of energy. Therefore, the overall mass of the reacting chemicals decreases.
*   **(D) Compressing a spring:** Compressing a spring stores elastic potential energy within the spring. By adding energy to the system, you are technically increasing the mass of the spring, not decreasing it.

**Conclusion:**
An exothermic reaction releases energy, which corresponds to a loss in the mass of the system.

The correct answer is **(C)**.
