To solve this problem, we must analyze the forces acting on the disc as it undergoes uniform circular motion. The two forces acting on the disc are gravity ($mg$ downwards) and the normal force ($N$ perpendicular to the surface).

### Part a: Calculate the speed of the disc
1. **Identify the forces:** The vertical component of the normal force must balance gravity: $N \cos(\theta) = mg$. The horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force: $N \sin(\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{r}$.
2. **Derive the formula:** Dividing the two equations gives $\tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{rg}$, where $r = 5.00$ m, $\theta = 30^\circ$, and $g \approx 9.8$ m/s$^2$.
3. **Calculate:**
   $$v = \sqrt{rg \tan(\theta)} = \sqrt{5.00 \times 9.8 \times \tan(30^\circ)}$$
   $$v = \sqrt{49 \times 0.57735} \approx \sqrt{28.29} \approx 5.32 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

**Answer:** $5.32 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

### Part b: Calculate the change in height
1. **Find the new radius:** When the speed changes to $v_{new} = 6.00$ m/s, the new radius $r_{new}$ is:
   $$r_{new} = \frac{v_{new}^2}{g \tan(\theta)} = \frac{6.00^2}{9.8 \times \tan(30^\circ)} = \frac{36}{5.658} \approx 6.3626 \text{ m}$$
2. **Relate radius to height:** The geometry of the cone dictates the height $h$ from the apex. Since $\tan(\theta) = \frac{r}{h_{dist}}$, where $h_{dist}$ is the vertical distance from the apex, then $h_{dist} = \frac{r}{\tan(\theta)}$.
3. **Calculate the change:** The height $h$ is proportional to the radius ($h \propto r$).
   $$h_1 = \frac{5.00}{\tan(30^\circ)} \approx 8.660 \text{ m}$$
   $$h_2 = \frac{6.3626}{\tan(30^\circ)} \approx 11.020 \text{ m}$$
   $$\Delta h = 11.020 - 8.660 = 2.36 \text{ m}$$

**Answer:** $2.36 \text{ m}$
