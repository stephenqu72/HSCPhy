### Answer
J.J. Thomson used this cathode ray tube to determine that cathode rays were negatively charged particles of matter significantly smaller than atoms.

**1. Experimental Procedure:**
*   **Electric Field Deflection:** Thomson applied a potential difference across the parallel plates. The cathode rays were deflected toward the positive plate, indicating that the rays consisted of negatively charged particles.
*   **Magnetic Field Deflection:** By applying a magnetic field (using the magnet), he observed a deflection of the rays. Using the right-hand rule (adjusted for negative charges), he confirmed the particles were indeed moving charges.
*   **Balancing Fields:** By adjusting the electric field strength ($E$) and magnetic field strength ($B$) simultaneously, Thomson could make the rays strike the screen at point B (zero net deflection). This satisfies the condition $F_e = F_m$, where $qE = qvB$. This allowed him to calculate the velocity ($v$) of the particles: $v = E/B$.
*   **Charge-to-Mass Ratio:** With the velocity known, he removed the electric field and measured the deflection caused solely by the magnetic field. By equating the magnetic force to the centripetal force ($qvB = mv^2/r$), he calculated the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$).

**2. Interpretation and Impact:**
*   **Discovery of the Electron:** Thomson found that the $q/m$ ratio for cathode rays was approximately 1800 times larger than that of the hydrogen ion. Since $q$ was expected to be similar, he concluded that the mass ($m$) of the particle must be much smaller than the mass of an atom.
*   **Subatomic Particles:** This result provided the first evidence that atoms were not indivisible "billiard balls" but were composed of smaller subatomic components.
*   **Structure of Matter:** His findings directly challenged Dalton's atomic theory, leading to his "Plum Pudding Model," where negatively charged "corpuscles" (electrons) were embedded in a sphere of positive charge. This laid the foundation for modern atomic physics.
