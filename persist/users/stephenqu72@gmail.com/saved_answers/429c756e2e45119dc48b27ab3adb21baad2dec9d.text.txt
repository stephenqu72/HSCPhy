The correct answer is **(A)**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must use the universal formula for gravitational potential energy ($U$), which defines the energy of an object of mass $m$ at a distance $r$ from the center of a planet with mass $M$:

$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$

1.  **Dependence on mass:** From the formula, we can clearly see that the gravitational potential energy is directly proportional to both the mass of the planet ($M$) and the mass of the space mission ($m$). Therefore, any option stating it is "independent" of either mass is incorrect. This eliminates (C) and (D).
2.  **Direction of change:** Because the potential energy is negative, as the space mission moves away from the planet, $r$ increases. 
    *   As $r \to \infty$, the fraction $\frac{GMm}{r}$ approaches $0$.
    *   Since the value is negative, moving from a large negative value (e.g., $-1000$ J) to a smaller negative value closer to zero (e.g., $-100$ J) represents an **increase** in value.
3.  **Conclusion:** As the object moves away, the gravitational potential energy increases towards a maximum value of zero (which is defined as the potential energy at an infinite distance from the planet).

Thus, option (A) is the only statement that correctly identifies the dependence on both masses and the correct mathematical trend of the energy.
