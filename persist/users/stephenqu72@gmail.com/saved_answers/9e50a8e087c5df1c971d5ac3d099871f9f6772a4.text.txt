To solve this problem, we must apply the principle of mass-energy equivalence ($E = mc^2$) to the process of electron-positron annihilation.

### Answer
The correct answer is **C. 0.5 MeV**.

---

### Explanation
1.  **Understand the process:** When an electron and a positron collide, they annihilate each other. Because both particles have mass, that mass must be converted into energy, specifically into two gamma-ray photons (to satisfy the conservation of momentum).
2.  **Calculate the total mass-energy:** The rest mass of an electron (and a positron) is approximately $0.511 \text{ MeV}/c^2$. 
    For one electron and one positron, the total mass is:
    $$0.511 \text{ MeV}/c^2 + 0.511 \text{ MeV}/c^2 \approx 1.022 \text{ MeV}/c^2$$
    Using $E = mc^2$, the total energy released is approximately $1.022 \text{ MeV}$.
3.  **Determine energy per photon:** To conserve momentum, this total energy is split equally between two photons traveling in opposite directions.
    $$\text{Energy per photon} = \frac{1.022 \text{ MeV}}{2} \approx 0.511 \text{ MeV}$$
4.  **Conclusion:** Comparing this to the provided options, $0.5 \text{ MeV}$ is the standard approximate value used in HSC Physics curricula for the energy of a single photon resulting from this annihilation.
