To find the work function of the cathode, we use Einstein’s photoelectric equation:
$$E_k(\text{max}) = hf - \Phi$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Identify given values:**
*   Frequency of light, $f = 6.6 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz}$
*   Stopping voltage, $V_s = 1.60 \text{ V}$
*   Planck’s constant, $h = 4.136 \times 10^{-15} \text{ eV s}$ (using this constant directly allows us to work in eV)

**2. Calculate the maximum kinetic energy:**
The maximum kinetic energy in electron-volts is numerically equal to the stopping voltage in volts:
$$E_k(\text{max}) = e \times V_s = 1.60 \text{ eV}$$

**3. Calculate the energy of the incident photons ($E = hf$):**
$$E = (4.136 \times 10^{-15} \text{ eV s}) \times (6.6 \times 10^{14} \text{ Hz})$$
$$E \approx 2.73 \text{ eV}$$

**4. Rearrange the photoelectric equation to solve for the work function ($\Phi$):**
$$\Phi = hf - E_k(\text{max})$$
$$\Phi = 2.73 \text{ eV} - 1.60 \text{ eV}$$
$$\Phi = 1.13 \text{ eV}$$

**Final Answer:**
The work function of the cathode metal is **$1.13 \text{ eV}$**.

***

### Explanation:
*   **Concepts:** The photoelectric effect describes how light hitting a surface can eject electrons. The energy of the incoming photon ($hf$) is used to overcome the binding energy of the metal (the work function, $\Phi$) and provide kinetic energy to the ejected electron ($E_k$).
*   **Stopping Voltage:** The stopping voltage ($V_s$) is the precise amount of negative potential required to stop even the most energetic electrons. Therefore, $E_k(\text{max})$ is exactly equal to $eV_s$.
*   **Constants:** Using $h$ in units of $\text{eV s}$ is a strategic shortcut in HSC Physics to avoid converting between Joules and electron-volts, which minimizes calculation errors.
