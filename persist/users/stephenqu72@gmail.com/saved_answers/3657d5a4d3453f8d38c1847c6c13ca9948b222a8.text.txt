### Answer

Einstein’s theory of Special Relativity is supported by two key postulates: the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference, and the speed of light in a vacuum, $c$, is constant for all observers.

**1. Time Dilation and Muon Decay:**
Muons are subatomic particles created in the upper atmosphere by cosmic rays. They have a very short mean lifetime ($\approx 2.2 \mu s$). Classically, even traveling at near-light speed, they should decay long before reaching the Earth's surface. However, experiments (such as those by Rossi and Hall) detect a high flux of muons at sea level. This is explained by time dilation: from the Earth's frame of reference, the muon's internal clock is slowed down by a factor of the Lorentz factor, $\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$. This allows the muon to survive the journey, providing direct evidence that time is relative.

**2. Length Contraction:**
From the perspective of the muon, the atmosphere itself undergoes length contraction. The distance the muon must travel is shortened, allowing it to reach the ground within its short lifespan. This complements the time dilation observation and confirms the relativity of space and time.

**3. Atomic Clocks and Global Positioning Systems (GPS):**
The Hafele-Keating experiment involved placing ultra-precise atomic clocks on commercial airliners and flying them around the world. Upon return, the clocks showed a measurable difference in time compared to clocks kept on the ground. Modern GPS satellites also require constant adjustments for both Special Relativity (time dilation due to high orbital velocity) and General Relativity (gravitational time dilation) to maintain accuracy. Without accounting for these relativistic effects, GPS systems would lose accuracy by several kilometers per day.
