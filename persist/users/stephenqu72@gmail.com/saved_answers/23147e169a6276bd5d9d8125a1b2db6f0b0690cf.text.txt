### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) Removed the certainty about the position of electrons in their orbits.**

---

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the shift from the Bohr model to the quantum mechanical model of the atom proposed by Erwin Schrödinger:

1.  **Contextual Background:** Niels Bohr’s model described electrons moving in fixed, circular **orbits** with definite paths, similar to planets orbiting the sun. This model suggested we could know exactly where an electron was at any given time.
2.  **Schrödinger’s Contribution:** Erwin Schrödinger developed wave equations that describe the probability of finding an electron in a specific region of space (called **orbitals**). His model essentially replaced the concept of deterministic "orbits" with probabilistic "electron clouds."
3.  **Heisenberg and Schrödinger:** This shift aligns with the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, which states that it is fundamentally impossible to know both the exact position and the exact momentum of an electron simultaneously. By treating electrons as wave functions, Schrödinger's work established that we can only determine the probability of finding an electron in a specific region, thereby **removing the certainty** regarding their exact position.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(B)** is the opposite of what Schrödinger achieved; his model introduced probabilistic uncertainty.
*   **(C)** is partially true in that electrons exhibit wave-particle duality (as proposed by de Broglie), but Schrödinger's *specific* advancement in atomic theory was the mathematical framework defining the spatial probability of the electron, not just the declaration of wave properties.
*   **(D)** is incorrect because the quantum mechanical model maintains that electrons exhibit both wave and particle characteristics (wave-particle duality), not that they are "not particles."
