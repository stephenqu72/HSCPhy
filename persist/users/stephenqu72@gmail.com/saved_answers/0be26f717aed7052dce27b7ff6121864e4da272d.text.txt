### Sample Answer (Investigation: Determining $g$ using a simple pendulum)

**The Process:** 
I investigated the relationship between the length of a pendulum ($l$) and the period of oscillation ($T$). I hung a small metal sphere from a string of length $l$ to a retort stand. I displaced the pendulum at a small angle (less than $10^\circ$ to maintain simple harmonic motion) and used a stopwatch to time 10 full oscillations. I repeated this for five different lengths, calculating the period $T$ for each. I then plotted $T^2$ against $l$. Given $T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}}$, the gradient of the line of best fit represents $\frac{4\pi^2}{g}$.

**Ensuring Accuracy:**
To minimize systematic error, I used a digital timer to reduce human reaction time and ensured the pendulum was measured from the pivot point to the centre of mass of the sphere using a metre ruler with a precision of $\pm 1 \text{ mm}$. I used a protractor to ensure the release angle remained constant and small, preventing error associated with the small-angle approximation.

**Ensuring Reliability:**
To improve reliability, I performed three trials for each length and calculated the mean period. This allowed me to identify and remove any anomalous data points. By using a range of lengths, I increased the validity of the line of best fit, allowing for a more accurate calculation of $g$ from the gradient.

**Ensuring Safety:**
A hazard identified was the heavy mass potentially falling on a student's foot. The risk was managed by ensuring the mass was securely tied to the string and the retort stand was stabilized with a heavy base or a G-clamp. Additionally, I cleared the floor of bags to prevent tripping while moving around the equipment.
