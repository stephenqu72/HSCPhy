### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must understand the historical context of the **Geiger-Marsden experiment** (often called the Rutherford gold foil experiment):

1.  **The Context:** Before this experiment, the accepted model of the atom was J.J. Thomson’s "Plum Pudding" model (represented by diagrams C and D), which proposed that positive charge and mass were spread out uniformly throughout the entire volume of the atom.
2.  **The Prediction:** If the Plum Pudding model were true, alpha particles (which are positively charged and relatively massive) should pass through the thin gold foil with minimal deflection, as the electrostatic forces would be too weak to cause significant changes in their paths.
3.  **The Result:** Geiger and Marsden observed that while most alpha particles passed straight through, a small fraction of them were deflected at large angles, and some were even reflected directly backward. 
4.  **The Conclusion:** This evidence proved that the positive charge and the majority of the mass of the atom are concentrated in a tiny, dense central region called the **nucleus**. 
    *   Diagram **A** correctly depicts the planetary/nuclear model where a dense nucleus causes significant electrostatic repulsion (deflection) when an alpha particle passes close to it.
    *   Diagram **B** shows alpha particles passing through undisturbed, which does not account for the large-angle scattering observed.
    *   Diagrams **C and D** represent the outdated "Plum Pudding" model, which fails to account for the observed large-angle deflections.

Therefore, diagram **A** is the only one that represents the model consistent with the Rutherford-Geiger-Marsden experimental findings.
