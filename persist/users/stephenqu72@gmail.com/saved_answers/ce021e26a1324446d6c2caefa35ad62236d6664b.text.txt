To solve this problem, we use the principles of projectile motion. 

**Given data:**
*   Launch angle: $\theta = 25^\circ$
*   Time interval between frames: $\Delta t = 0.1 \text{ s}$
*   Time to reach maximum height: $t_{up} = 3.5 \text{ frames} \times 0.1 \text{ s/frame} = 0.35 \text{ s}$
*   Acceleration due to gravity: $g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

---

### a) Calculate the maximum vertical displacement
At maximum height, the vertical velocity $v_y = 0$. We use the formula $v_y = u_y - gt$.
Setting $v_y = 0$: $u_y = gt = 9.8 \times 0.35 = 3.43 \text{ m/s}$.
The displacement is $s_y = u_y t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$:
$$s_y = (3.43)(0.35) - 0.5(9.8)(0.35)^2 = 1.2005 - 0.60025 = 0.60 \text{ m}$$

### b) Calculate the student's launch velocity
We know the vertical component $u_y = u \sin\theta$.
$$u = \frac{u_y}{\sin\theta} = \frac{3.43}{\sin(25^\circ)} \approx \frac{3.43}{0.4226} \approx 8.12 \text{ m/s}$$

### c) Calculate the student's range
First, find total time of flight $T$. Since the jump is symmetric (ignoring height difference between launch and landing): $T = 2 \times t_{up} = 0.7 \text{ s}$.
Horizontal velocity $v_x = u \cos\theta = 8.12 \times \cos(25^\circ) \approx 7.36 \text{ m/s}$.
$$Range = v_x \times T = 7.36 \times 0.7 = 5.15 \text{ m}$$

### d) State ONE assumption
One common assumption is that **air resistance is negligible** or that the **take-off and landing heights are the same** (level ground).
