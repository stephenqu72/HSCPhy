To determine the number of different wavelengths of light emitted when an electron de-excites from the $n = 5$ energy level, we need to count every possible downward transition the electron can make to reach the ground state ($n = 1$).

### Explanation
When an electron is in the $n = 5$ state, it can transition to any lower energy level ($n = 4, 3, 2, 1$). Each unique transition corresponds to a photon with a specific energy and wavelength (since $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$).

We can map out all possible paths:
1.  **Transitions starting from $n=5$:**
    *   $5 \rightarrow 4$
    *   $5 \rightarrow 3$
    *   $5 \rightarrow 2$
    *   $5 \rightarrow 1$
2.  **Transitions starting from $n=4$:**
    *   $4 \rightarrow 3$
    *   $4 \rightarrow 2$
    *   $4 \rightarrow 1$
3.  **Transitions starting from $n=3$:**
    *   $3 \rightarrow 2$
    *   $3 \rightarrow 1$
4.  **Transitions starting from $n=2$:**
    *   $2 \rightarrow 1$

Summing these up, we have $4 + 3 + 2 + 1 = 10$ possible transitions. 

Alternatively, you can use the formula for the number of spectral lines emitted when an electron drops from level $n$ to the ground state:
$$N = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$
Substituting $n = 5$:
$$N = \frac{5(5-1)}{2} = \frac{5 \times 4}{2} = \frac{20}{2} = 10$$

Since each transition results in a unique energy change, there are 10 different wavelengths produced.

**Correct Option: (A) 10**
