To determine the torque applied to the disc, we use the fundamental definition of torque:

$$\tau = rF\sin(\phi)$$

Where:
*   $r$ is the distance from the pivot to the point of application of the force (the radius).
*   $F$ is the magnitude of the force applied.
*   $\phi$ is the angle between the position vector $\vec{r}$ and the force vector $\vec{F}$.

### Step-by-step Solution:

**1. Identify the variables:**
*   $r = 100\text{ cm} = 1.0\text{ m}$ (Standard SI units must be used).
*   $F = 100\text{ N}$.
*   The angle $\theta = 30^\circ$ given in the diagram is the angle between the force vector and the *radial line* extended outwards. In the formula for torque, the angle $\phi$ is defined as the angle between the position vector $\vec{r}$ and the force vector $\vec{F}$. Looking at the diagram, the force is directed such that the angle between the radial vector $\vec{r}$ and the force vector $\vec{F}$ is exactly the angle $\theta = 30^\circ$.

**2. Perform the calculation:**
$$\tau = (1.0\text{ m}) \times (100\text{ N}) \times \sin(30^\circ)$$

Since $\sin(30^\circ) = 0.5$:
$$\tau = 100 \times 0.5$$
$$\tau = 50\text{ Nm}$$

**3. Conclusion:**
The magnitude of the torque is $50\text{ Nm}$. Comparing this to the given options, it matches option (A).

**Correct Answer:** **(A)**

### How to approach similar questions:
*   **Convert units:** Always ensure your radius is in meters ($100\text{ cm} \rightarrow 1.0\text{ m}$) before calculating torque.
*   **Identify the correct angle:** The formula $\tau = rF\sin(\phi)$ requires the angle between the lever arm (position vector) and the force vector. If the diagram shows the angle between the force and the perpendicular, you would use $\cos(\theta)$ instead, but here the angle is clearly defined relative to the radial extension.
*   **Physics Concept:** Torque is the rotational analogue of force. It represents the "turning effect" of a force. Only the component of the force perpendicular to the radius contributes to the rotation.
