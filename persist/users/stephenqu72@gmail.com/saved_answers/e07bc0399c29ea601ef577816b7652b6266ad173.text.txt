To solve this problem, we need to calculate the gravitational potential energy ($U$) of the satellite using the standard physics formula:

$$U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$

### 1. Identify the given values
*   Mass of satellite ($m$) = $6.30 \times 10^3 \text{ kg}$
*   Altitude ($h$) = $1.88 \times 10^4 \text{ km} = 1.88 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$
*   Mass of Earth ($M$) $\approx 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$ (Standard physical constant)
*   Gravitational constant ($G$) $\approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2$
*   Radius of Earth ($R_E$) $\approx 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$ (Standard physical constant)

### 2. Determine the orbital radius ($r$)
The distance $r$ from the centre of the Earth is the sum of the Earth's radius and the altitude of the satellite:
$$r = R_E + h$$
$$r = 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m} + 1.88 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$$
$$r = 0.637 \times 10^7 \text{ m} + 1.88 \times 10^7 \text{ m} = 2.517 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$$

### 3. Calculate Potential Energy ($U$)
Substitute the values into the potential energy formula:
$$U = -\frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (6.30 \times 10^3)}{2.517 \times 10^7}$$

First, calculate the numerator:
$$(6.67 \times 5.97 \times 6.30) \times 10^{(-11 + 24 + 3)} = 250.7 \times 10^{16} = 2.507 \times 10^{18}$$

Now divide by the radius ($r$):
$$U = -\frac{2.507 \times 10^{18}}{2.517 \times 10^7} \approx -0.996 \times 10^{11} \text{ J}$$

Rounding this to significant figures, we get approximately $-1.00 \times 10^{11} \text{ J}$.

Comparing this to the given options:
(A) $-1.00 \times 10^{11} \text{ J}$
(B) $-1.00 \times 10^{14} \text{ J}$
(C) $-3.95 \times 10^{11} \text{ J}$
(D) $-1.34 \times 10^{14} \text{ J}$

The correct answer is **(A)**.
