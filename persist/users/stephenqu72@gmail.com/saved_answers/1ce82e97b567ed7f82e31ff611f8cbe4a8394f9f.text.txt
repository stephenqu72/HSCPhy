To determine the angular speed ($\omega$) of the Earth, we use the fundamental formula for rotational motion:

$$\omega = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t}$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

1.  **Understand the Variables:**
    *   One full rotation of the Earth corresponds to an angle of $2\pi$ radians.
    *   The time period ($T$) for one full rotation is 24 hours.

2.  **Convert Time to Seconds:**
    Since the unit requested is $rad \cdot s^{-1}$, we must convert 24 hours into seconds:
    $$\Delta t = 24 \text{ hours} \times 60 \text{ minutes/hour} \times 60 \text{ seconds/minute}$$
    $$\Delta t = 86,400 \text{ seconds}$$

3.  **Calculate Angular Speed:**
    Substitute these values into the formula:
    $$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{86,400}$$
    $$\omega \approx \frac{6.283185}{86,400}$$
    $$\omega \approx 7.2722 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad} \cdot s^{-1}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, it matches option (B).

**Correct Answer: (B)**

Even though we colloquially say "24 hours," in high-precision physics problems, remember that the Earth actually completes a rotation relative to the stars (a sidereal day) in approximately 23 hours and 56 minutes. However, based on the specific prompt of "24 hours," option (B) is the mathematically correct derivation of that value.
