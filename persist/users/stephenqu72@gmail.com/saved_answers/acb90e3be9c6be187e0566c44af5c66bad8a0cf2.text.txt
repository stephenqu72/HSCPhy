To determine the direction of the friction force, we must compare the actual speed of the car to the "design speed" of the banked track.

### The Answer
When the car travels at $8\text{ ms}^{-1}$, which is **slower** than the design speed of $10\text{ ms}^{-1}$, the car has a tendency to slide **down** the slope. To maintain a constant radius, static friction must act **up the slope** (parallel to the track surface).

**The forces you should draw are:**
1.  **Weight ($mg$):** A vertical arrow pointing straight down from the center of mass of the car.
2.  **Normal Force ($N$):** An arrow pointing perpendicular to the surface of the track, pointing up and to the left.
3.  **Friction ($f$):** An arrow pointing **up the slope** (parallel to the surface of the track), pointing up and to the left.

### Explanation
1.  **Understand the Design Speed:** At $10\text{ ms}^{-1}$, the horizontal component of the normal force provides exactly the required centripetal force ($F_c$) to keep the car in circular motion without any help from friction.
2.  **The Effect of Speed:** The required centripetal force is given by $F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$.
    *   If the car moves **slower** than $10\text{ ms}^{-1}$, the required centripetal force is less than the horizontal component provided by the normal force alone. The car "wants" to slip down the incline. Friction must act **up the slope** to prevent this downward slip.
    *   If the car moves **faster** than $10\text{ ms}^{-1}$, the required centripetal force is greater than the horizontal component provided by the normal force. The car "wants" to slip up the incline. Friction must act **down the slope** to prevent this outward slip.
3.  **Summary:** Since $8\text{ ms}^{-1} < 10\text{ ms}^{-1}$, the car is moving too slowly for the bank angle. Friction acts up the slope to provide the necessary support to keep the car on its circular path.
