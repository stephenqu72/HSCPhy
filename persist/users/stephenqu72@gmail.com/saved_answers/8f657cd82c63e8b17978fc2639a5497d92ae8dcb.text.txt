To determine which star is a white dwarf, we must look at where these objects typically sit on the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B. GD 61**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we rely on our understanding of stellar classification and the H-R diagram:

1.  **Understand White Dwarf characteristics:** White dwarfs are the remnant cores of low-to-medium mass stars. Because they are the size of a planet but contain the mass of a star, they are extremely dense and have very low luminosity (high absolute magnitude). However, because they are hot cores left over from fusion, they maintain relatively high surface temperatures. On an H-R diagram, they are found in the bottom-left corner—**hot but dim**.

2.  **Evaluate the options using the table:**
    *   **119 Tauri:** Absolute magnitude $-5.2$ (very bright), temperature $3,800\text{ K}$ (cool). This places it in the upper-right section of the H-R diagram, consistent with a **red supergiant**.
    *   **GD 61:** Absolute magnitude $+15$ (very dim), temperature $17,000\text{ K}$ (very hot). This combination of low luminosity and high temperature is the signature location for a **white dwarf**.
    *   **LP 944-20:** Absolute magnitude $+20$ (extremely dim), temperature $2,700\text{ K}$ (very cool). This corresponds to a **brown dwarf** (substellar object).
    *   **Zeta Puppis:** Absolute magnitude $-6.2$ (very bright), temperature $40,000\text{ K}$ (extremely hot). This places it in the upper-left of the H-R diagram, consistent with a **massive blue main-sequence star or supergiant**.

By identifying that a white dwarf must have a very low luminosity (high magnitude) and a high surface temperature, we can conclude that GD 61 is the only candidate that fits these criteria.
