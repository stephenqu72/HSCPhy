To solve this problem, we need to account for two physical phenomena: the **Inverse Square Law** (for distance) and **Malus’s Law/Polarisation** (for the filter).

### 1. The Inverse Square Law
The intensity $I$ of a point source of light follows the inverse square law: $I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$.
Given:
*   Initial intensity $I_1 = 120\text{ lm}$ at $r_1 = 0.5\text{ m}$.
*   We need the intensity $I_2$ at $r_2 = 3.0\text{ m}$ *before* it hits the filter.

Using the ratio:
$$\frac{I_2}{I_1} = \left(\frac{r_1}{r_2}\right)^2$$
$$I_2 = 120 \times \left(\frac{0.5}{3.0}\right)^2 = 120 \times \left(\frac{1}{6}\right)^2 = 120 \times \frac{1}{36}$$
$$I_2 = \frac{120}{36} \approx 3.333\text{ lm}$$

### 2. The Polarising Filter
When unpolarised light passes through an ideal polarising filter, the intensity of the transmitted light is exactly half of the incident intensity:
$$I_{\text{final}} = \frac{1}{2} I_2$$
$$I_{\text{final}} = \frac{3.333}{2} \approx 1.666\text{ lm}$$

Rounding to two significant figures, we get **$1.7\text{ lm}$**.

### Explanation Summary
1.  **Distance change:** Since the distance increased by a factor of $6$ ($3.0 / 0.5$), the intensity drops by a factor of $6^2 = 36$. Dividing $120$ by $36$ gives the intensity at the location of the filter.
2.  **Polarisation:** Passing through a polariser reduces the intensity of unpolarised light by exactly $50\%$. Dividing the resulting $3.33\dots$ by $2$ yields approximately $1.67$.
