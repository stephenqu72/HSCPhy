To solve this problem, we apply Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, which is given by the formula:

$$E = mc^2$$

Where:
*   $E$ is the energy released.
*   $m$ is the total mass being converted into energy.
*   $c$ is the speed of light in a vacuum ($3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$).

### Step-by-Step Explanation:

1.  **Identify the particles involved:** The question states that an electron and a positron annihilate. 
2.  **Determine the total mass ($m$):**
    *   The mass of an electron is approximately $9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$.
    *   A positron is the antiparticle of an electron and has the exact same mass: $9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$.
    *   Therefore, the total mass involved in the annihilation is the sum of both:
        $$m_{\text{total}} = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} + 9.1 \times 10^{-31} = 2 \times (9.1 \times 10^{-31}) \text{ kg}$$
3.  **Apply the formula:**
    *   Substitute the total mass and the speed of light ($c = 3.00 \times 10^8$) into the equation $E = mc^2$:
        $$E = (2 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)^2 \text{ J}$$

Comparing this result to the given options, it matches option **(C)**.

### Answer:
The correct option is **(C)**.
