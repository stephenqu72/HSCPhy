### Answer

**a)** The distance traveled is $2L_0$ (up to the mirror and back). Given the speed of light is $c$, the time $t_0$ is:
$$t_0 = \frac{2L_0}{c}$$

**b)** According to the second postulate of special relativity, the speed of light $c$ is constant for all inertial observers. As the clock moves to the right, the light must travel along a diagonal path to reach the mirror and return to the detector, which has also moved. Since the speed of light $c$ cannot change, and the diagonal path is longer than the straight vertical path ($2L_0$), the light must take a longer time to cover this greater distance.

**c)** Time dilation.

**d)** The Hafele-Keating experiment (1971). Caesium atomic clocks were placed on commercial airliners and flown around the world in opposite directions. When compared to stationary clocks on the ground, the moving clocks showed a measurable difference in elapsed time consistent with the predictions of special and general relativity. (Alternatively, the observation of atmospheric muons reaching the Earth's surface due to their increased lifetime at high velocities is also a valid response).

### How to solve this
*   **Part (a):** Use the fundamental definition of speed, $v = \frac{d}{t}$. Rearranging for time gives $t = \frac{d}{v}$. Here, the total distance is $2L_0$ and the speed is $c$.
*   **Part (b):** This is a conceptual reasoning task. Focus on the two pillars of Einstein’s Special Relativity: (1) The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames, and (2) The speed of light is constant ($c$). If the distance increases due to the relative motion, and $c$ remains constant, $t$ must increase ($t = \frac{d}{c}$).
*   **Part (c):** Recall the terminology for the phenomenon where moving clocks appear to run slower compared to a stationary observer.
*   **Part (d):** This requires knowledge of experimental physics. The Hafele-Keating experiment is the standard syllabus example for time dilation validation, but the observation of muons is a classic application in particle physics.
