### Answer
The spectrum provided is a typical stellar absorption spectrum. The factors affecting the shape and features of this spectrum include:

1.  **Temperature (Wien’s Law and Planck’s Law):** The overall curve (the "continuum") represents the blackbody radiation emitted by the star's photosphere. The peak of this curve is determined by the star's surface temperature. According to Wien's Law, $\lambda_{max} \propto 1/T$, meaning hotter stars have peaks shifted toward shorter (bluer) wavelengths, while cooler stars peak at longer (redder) wavelengths.
2.  **Chemical Composition (Absorption Lines):** The sharp "dips" or gaps in the spectrum are absorption lines. These are caused by specific elements in the cooler outer layers of the star (the stellar atmosphere) absorbing photons at characteristic wavelengths corresponding to the energy transitions of electrons in those atoms (e.g., Hydrogen Balmer lines).
3.  **Stellar Surface Gravity and Pressure:** The width of these absorption lines is influenced by pressure broadening. High-pressure environments (like those in main-sequence stars) result in broader absorption lines, while low-pressure environments (like giants) produce narrower, sharper lines.
