### Answer
The correct answer is **B) having power stations located much closer to the consumers.**

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the fundamental difference between Alternating Current (AC) and Direct Current (DC) regarding transmission.

1.  **The Role of Transformers:** The primary advantage of AC power is that it can be easily "stepped up" to very high voltages using transformers. This is crucial because power loss due to resistance in transmission lines is defined by the formula:
    $$P_{loss} = I^2 R$$
    By using a transformer to increase the voltage ($V$), we can decrease the current ($I$) required to transmit the same amount of power ($P = VI$). A lower current significantly reduces energy waste as heat in the transmission lines.

2.  **The DC Limitation:** Historically, DC voltage was very difficult to transform (step up or down). Because you cannot easily step up DC voltage, it must be transmitted at the same voltage at which it is generated. To keep $P_{loss}$ low without the ability to step up voltage, you would have to use extremely thick, expensive copper cables or transmit over very short distances.

3.  **The Consequence:** Because DC cannot be efficiently transmitted over long distances, a grid based on DC would require small, localized power stations located very close to the households or businesses they serve (which was exactly the design of the original Edison power systems). AC, by contrast, allows for long-distance transmission from distant, efficient, large-scale power plants.

    *   (C) is incorrect because needing more (not fewer) local stations would be the consequence of the distance limitation of DC.
