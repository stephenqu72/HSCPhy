### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the core findings and the methodology of the Geiger-Marsden (Rutherford) gold foil experiment:

1.  **Experimental Observation:** Rutherford's team observed that the vast majority of alpha particles passed straight through the thin gold foil with little to no deflection, indicating that the atom is mostly empty space. However, a small fraction of alpha particles were deflected at very large angles, and some even bounced directly backward. This was the key evidence that the atom contains a very small, dense, and positively charged nucleus that exerts a strong electrostatic force on the alpha particles.
2.  **Theoretical Calculation:** To estimate the size of this nucleus (the distance of closest approach, $d$), Rutherford utilized the **Principle of Conservation of Energy**. 

Specifically, he equated the initial kinetic energy ($KE$) of the alpha particle at a long distance from the nucleus to the electric potential energy ($PE$) at the point where the alpha particle momentarily stops before being repelled back:
$$KE_{initial} = PE_{final}$$
$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{k q_1 q_2}{d}$$

Where:
*   $m$ is the mass of the alpha particle.
*   $v$ is the velocity of the alpha particle.
*   $k$ is Coulomb's constant.
*   $q_1$ and $q_2$ are the charges of the alpha particle and the gold nucleus, respectively.
*   $d$ is the distance of closest approach (the estimate for the upper limit of the nuclear radius).

Since option (A) correctly identifies the experimental observations and the fundamental physical principle used to estimate the size, it is the only correct choice.
