As an HSC Physics teacher, here is how you should approach this classic question on the properties of cathode rays.

### Answer

**a) Observations:**
When the tube is in operation, a sharp, dark shadow in the shape of a Maltese cross will appear on the fluorescent screen at the end of the tube. The remainder of the glass screen will glow with a faint light (fluorescence) where it is struck by the cathode rays.

**b) Inferences:**
1. **Rectilinear propagation:** The sharp edges of the shadow indicate that cathode rays travel in straight lines, as they are blocked by the metal cross but do not "bend" around it.
2. **Particulate nature/Physical obstruction:** The formation of a shadow suggests that cathode rays are a stream of particles (or radiation) that possess momentum and can be physically obstructed by solid matter.

***

### How to solve this
1. **Identify the apparatus:** Recognize this as the "Maltese Cross tube," a historical experimental device used by J.J. Thomson and others to investigate the nature of cathode rays.
2. **Understand the mechanism:** Cathode rays are emitted from the cathode at high speed. When they strike the glass at the end of the tube, they cause fluorescence (glowing).
3. **Address part (a):** Focus on the *visual experience*. You are looking for two specific details: the "dark shadow" (caused by the metal obstructing the rays) and the "glowing background" (caused by the rays hitting the fluorescent coating).
4. **Address part (b):** Connect your observations to physics principles.
   * Since light is known to travel in straight lines and also produces shadows of opaque objects, the shadow in the tube allows us to infer that cathode rays also move in straight lines.
   * The fact that a shadow exists at all proves that the rays are not something that can simply pass through solid metal objects like a wave might diffract significantly, but rather behave as a stream of matter.
