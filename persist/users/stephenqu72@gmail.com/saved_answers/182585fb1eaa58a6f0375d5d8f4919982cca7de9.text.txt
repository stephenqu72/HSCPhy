### Suggested Response

**The Rutherford Model**
Rutherford proposed a "planetary model" where a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus is surrounded by electrons orbiting in mostly empty space. 
*   **Supporting Evidence:** The Geiger-Marsden (gold foil) experiment. The observation that most alpha particles passed through the foil undeflected, while a small fraction were deflected at large angles, provided evidence for a concentrated central positive charge.
*   **Limitations:** According to classical electromagnetic theory, an accelerating charge (the orbiting electron) must continuously radiate energy as electromagnetic waves. This loss of energy would cause the electron to spiral into the nucleus, making the atom unstable. Furthermore, the model could not explain the discrete emission spectra of gases.

**The Bohr Model**
Bohr retained the idea of a central nucleus but introduced the concept of "stationary states." He proposed that electrons exist in fixed, quantized orbits where they do not radiate energy.
*   **Supporting Evidence:** Bohr used the Rydberg formula and the concept of photon emission ($E = hf$) to explain the observed spectral lines of hydrogen. He postulated that electrons only emit or absorb energy when they transition between these stable energy levels.
*   **Limitations:** Bohr’s model was limited because it could only accurately predict the spectra of hydrogen and hydrogen-like ions. It could not explain the relative intensities of spectral lines, the hyperfine structure, or the Zeeman effect (splitting of lines in a magnetic field). It also failed to explain why electrons were restricted to these orbits, an issue later addressed by De Broglie’s matter-wave hypothesis.

3.  **Evidence/Limitations:** You must explicitly link the evidence to the model (e.g., mention the gold foil experiment for Rutherford and spectral lines for Bohr).
4.  **HSC Marking Criteria:** This is a "Compare" question. Ensure you mention both the similarities (nucleus, orbiting electrons) and the critical differences (quantization, stability).
