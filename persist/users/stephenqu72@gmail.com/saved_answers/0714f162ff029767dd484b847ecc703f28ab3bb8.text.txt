### Answers and Explanations

**a. What is the period of revolution of Skymuster II?**
**Answer:** $24$ hours (or $86,400$ s).
**Explanation:** By definition, a **geosynchronous** (specifically geostationary) orbit means the satellite's orbital period is synchronized with the Earth’s rotation. Thus, it completes one orbit in the same time it takes the Earth to rotate once on its axis.

**b. Why is Skymuster II not positioned directly above the Australian land mass?**
**Answer:** To be in a geostationary orbit, a satellite must orbit directly above the Earth's equator.
**Explanation:** Geostationary orbits are only stable if the satellite orbits in the plane of the equator. Since Australia is entirely in the Southern Hemisphere, a satellite positioned directly above the Australian land mass would require constant fuel expenditure to maintain its position (it would not be in a free-fall orbit). Therefore, it orbits above the equator, appearing at a northern angle to Australian ground stations.

**c. Use data about the Moon's orbit to determine the orbital radius of Skymuster II.**
**Answer:**
We use Kepler’s Third Law: $\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2} = \text{constant}$.
Given for the Moon: $r_m = 3.84 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$ and $T_m = 2.36 \times 10^6 \text{ s}$.
For Skymuster II: $T_s = 86,400 \text{ s}$.

Setting up the ratio:
$$\frac{r_s^3}{T_s^2} = \frac{r_m^3}{T_m^2}$$
$$r_s^3 = r_m^3 \times \left(\frac{T_s}{T_m}\right)^2$$
$$r_s^3 = (3.84 \times 10^8)^3 \times \left(\frac{86,400}{2.36 \times 10^6}\right)^2$$
$$r_s^3 \approx 5.66 \times 10^{25} \times 0.00134$$
$$r_s^3 \approx 7.58 \times 10^{22}$$
$$r_s \approx 4.23 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$$

**d. Calculate the total energy of the Skymuster II in its orbit.**
**Answer:**
The total energy of a satellite is given by $E = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$.
*Note:* The question does not provide the mass of the Earth ($M$) or the satellite ($m$). In an HSC context, if values are missing, you must express the answer in terms of these constants or use the standard value $GM = 3.986 \times 10^{14} \text{ m}^3\text{s}^{-2}$. 
Assuming $r = 4.23 \times 10^7 \text{ m}$ (from part c):
$$E = -\frac{GMm}{2(4.23 \times 10^7)} \approx -1.18 \times 10^{-8} \times GMm \text{ Joules}$$
*(If the student is expected to calculate a numerical value, the mass of the satellite would typically be provided in the stimulus).*
