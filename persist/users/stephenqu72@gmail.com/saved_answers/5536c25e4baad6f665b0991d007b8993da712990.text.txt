To solve this question, we must understand the historical context of the Rutherford-Geiger-Marsden experiment and the prevailing model of the atom at the time.

### Analysis of the Experiment
1.  **The Context:** Before this experiment, the "Plum Pudding" model (proposed by J.J. Thomson) was the accepted theory. It suggested that atoms consisted of a diffuse cloud of positive charge with electrons embedded within it.
2.  **The Hypothesis:** Based on the Plum Pudding model, scientists expected that the massive, fast-moving $\alpha$-particles would pass through the thin gold foil with little to no deflection, as the positive charge was thought to be too spread out to exert a strong electrostatic force. Occasional slight deflections were expected due to interactions with the light electrons.
3.  **The Observation:** Rutherford, Geiger, and Marsden were shocked to find that while *most* particles passed through undeflected, a small but significant fraction (approximately $0.013\%$) were deflected by large angles ($> 90^\circ$). This result famously led Rutherford to conclude that the atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at its center, contradicting the Plum Pudding model.
