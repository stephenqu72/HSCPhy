### Part (a)
To find the height difference between $P$ and $Q$, we use the law of conservation of energy. Since the track is frictionless, the initial gravitational potential energy at $P$ is converted into kinetic energy at $Q$.
*   At $P$: The ball is at rest, so $E_{total} = PE_P = mgh_P$.
*   At $Q$: The ball has kinetic energy, so $E_{total} = KE_Q = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.
*   Setting them equal: $mgh_P = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh_Q$.
*   The difference in height $h = h_P - h_Q$:
    $$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \implies h = \frac{v^2}{2g}$$

Using $v = 1.5\text{ m s}^{-1}$ and $g = 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$:
$$h = \frac{(1.5)^2}{2 \times 9.8} = \frac{2.25}{19.6} \approx 0.115\text{ m}$$
**The difference in height is approximately $0.115\text{ m}$ (or $11.5\text{ cm}$).**

### Part (b)
To find the height of $Q$ above the floor, we use the vertical component of projectile motion. We are given the time $t = 0.5\text{ s}$, the initial velocity $v = 1.5\text{ m s}^{-1}$, and the angle $\theta = 50^\circ$ below the horizontal.

1.  Find the initial vertical velocity component ($v_{y0}$):
    Since the ball is directed downwards at an angle of $50^\circ$ to the horizontal:
    $$v_{y0} = v \sin(50^\circ) = 1.5 \times \sin(50^\circ) \approx 1.5 \times 0.766 = 1.149\text{ m s}^{-1}$$
2.  Use the kinematic equation for displacement ($s_y$):
    $$s_y = v_{y0}t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
    *(Taking downwards as positive since the ball is moving downwards)*:
    $$s_y = (1.149 \times 0.5) + (0.5 \times 9.8 \times 0.5^2)$$
    $$s_y = 0.5745 + (4.9 \times 0.25) = 0.5745 + 1.225 = 1.7995\text{ m}$$

**The height of $Q$ above the floor is approximately $1.80\text{ m}$.**

### How to solve this:
1.  **For part (a):** Always look for "frictionless" in physics problems. It is a keyword signaling that you can use the Conservation of Energy ($PE_{initial} = KE_{final}$). Remember that mass cancels out!
2.  **For part (b):** This is a projectile motion problem. Always resolve the initial velocity into horizontal ($v \cos\theta$) and vertical ($v \sin\theta$) components first. Because the ball is moving downwards, define downwards as the positive direction to simplify your calculation and avoid sign errors.
