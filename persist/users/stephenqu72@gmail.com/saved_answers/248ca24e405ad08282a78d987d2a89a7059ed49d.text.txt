### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** The particle is **positively charged**.

**Explanation:** To determine the charge of a particle moving through a magnetic field, we use the **Right-Hand Rule**.
1. The magnetic field ($B$) points into the page (indicated by the 'X's).
2. The particle enters the field and curves toward the right. 
3. Point your right-hand fingers into the page.
4. Orient your thumb in the direction of the velocity vector at the point of entry (upward).
5. Your palm faces the direction of the magnetic force ($F_B$) on a *positive* charge. Since the particle curves to the right, the force is directed to the right, which matches the Right-Hand Rule for a positive charge.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
We are given:
- Velocity $v = 2.50 \times 10^4 \text{ m s}^{-1}$
- Magnetic field $B = 1.95 \times 10^{-6} \text{ T}$
- Radius $r = 5.0 \text{ cm} = 0.05 \text{ m}$

**Explanation:**
A charged particle moving perpendicular to a magnetic field experiences a centripetal force provided by the magnetic Lorentz force. We equate the two:
$$F_B = F_c$$
$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Rearrange to solve for the charge-to-mass ratio ($\frac{q}{m}$):
$$\frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br}$$

Now, substitute the given values:
$$\frac{q}{m} = \frac{2.50 \times 10^4}{(1.95 \times 10^{-6}) \times 0.05}$$
$$\frac{q}{m} = \frac{2.50 \times 10^4}{9.75 \times 10^{-8}}$$
$$\frac{q}{m} \approx 2.564 \times 10^{11} \text{ C kg}^{-1}$$

Rounding to two significant figures (consistent with the provided data):
$$\frac{q}{m} = 2.6 \times 10^{11} \text{ C kg}^{-1}$$
This confirms the required value.
