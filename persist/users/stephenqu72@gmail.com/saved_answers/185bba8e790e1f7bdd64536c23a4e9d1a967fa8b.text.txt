### Answer
The evolution of the atomic model illustrates how scientific knowledge is dynamic, relying on experimental data to challenge and refine established principles.

**1. Rutherford’s Model and the Gold Foil Experiment:**
Ernest Rutherford’s Geiger-Marsden experiment (alpha-particle scattering) provided experimental evidence that contradicted the prevailing "plum pudding" model. Observing that most alpha particles passed through gold foil while a small percentage were deflected at large angles led Rutherford to propose the nuclear model: a dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons. However, applying classical physics (Maxwell’s electromagnetic theory) revealed a fatal flaw: an accelerating electron orbiting the nucleus should lose energy via electromagnetic radiation and spiral into the nucleus, making atoms unstable.

**2. Bohr’s Model and Quantum Postulates:**
Niels Bohr addressed this contradiction by integrating experimental observations of atomic emission spectra (the discrete lines of the Balmer series) with Max Planck’s quantum hypothesis. Bohr proposed three postulates to resolve the instability:
*   Electrons exist in stationary "stable" orbits without radiating energy.
*   Transitions between these orbits result in the emission or absorption of photons with energy $E = hf$.
*   Angular momentum is quantized: $mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi}$.

**Conclusion:**
Scientists use experimental observations (e.g., scattering patterns or spectral lines) to test the validity of existing models. When these observations contradict established physical principles (e.g., classical electrodynamics), the model must be modified. Bohr’s work demonstrates that improvement often requires abandoning classical assumptions in favor of new physical paradigms to explain the empirical data.
