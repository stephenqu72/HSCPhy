### **Step-by-Step Explanation**

#### **1. Identify the Orbital Radii**
From the provided diagram, we can clearly see that satellite $A$ is orbiting at a larger distance from the center of the planet than satellite $B$. 
Let $r_A$ be the orbital radius of satellite $A$ and $r_B$ be the orbital radius of satellite $B$.
We established that:
$$r_A > r_B$$

#### **2. Comparing Gravitational Potential Energy ($U$)**
The formula for gravitational potential energy between two masses (the planet $M$ and a satellite $m$) is:
$$U = -\frac{G M m}{r}$$
Note that the value of $U$ is **negative**. In physics, when comparing negative numbers, a number closer to zero is "greater."

Since $r_A > r_B$, it follows that $\frac{1}{r_A} < \frac{1}{r_B}$. 
When we multiply by the negative constant $-GMm$:
$$-\frac{G M m}{r_A} > -\frac{G M m}{r_B}$$
Therefore:
$$U_A > U_B$$
*(Intuitively, as you move further away from a planet, the potential energy increases because it becomes "less negative" as you approach zero at infinity.)*

#### **3. Comparing Kinetic Energy ($K$)**
For an object in a stable circular orbit, the gravitational force provides the necessary centripetal force:
$$\frac{m v^2}{r} = \frac{G M m}{r^2}$$
By simplifying this, we can find an expression for orbital velocity squared ($v^2$):
$$v^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$$
The formula for kinetic energy is $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$. Substituting our expression for $v^2$:
$$K = \frac{G M m}{2r}$$
Since the radius $r$ is in the denominator, a larger radius results in lower kinetic energy. Because $r_A > r_B$, then:
$$\frac{G M m}{2r_A} < \frac{G M m}{2r_B}$$
Therefore:
$$K_A < K_B$$

### **Conclusion**
Combining our two findings:
1.  $U_A > U_B$
2.  $K_A < K_B$

This matches exactly with **Row A** in the table.
