### Answer

The prediction and maintenance of satellite orbits rely on the integration of Kepler’s Laws (describing the geometry and timing of motion) and the conservation of energy (describing the orbital state).

**1. Predicting Orbits using Kepler’s Laws:**
*   **Kepler’s First Law (Law of Ellipses):** Orbits are elliptical, with the Earth at one focus. By knowing the semi-major axis ($a$) and eccentricity ($e$), scientists can define the exact spatial path of the satellite.
*   **Kepler’s Second Law (Law of Equal Areas):** This relates the satellite's velocity to its position in the orbit. A satellite moves fastest at perigee and slowest at apogee. This allows controllers to predict where a satellite will be at any given time $t$.
*   **Kepler’s Third Law (Law of Harmonies):** The period $T$ is related to the radius of the orbit ($T^2 \propto r^3$). This is crucial for GPS satellites, which require a precise orbital period to maintain a ground track that ensures global coverage.

**2. Total Energy and Stability:**
The total energy $E$ of a satellite in a circular orbit is given by:
$$E = E_k + E_p = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{r} = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$
*   The negative sign indicates the satellite is in a **bound state** (gravity holds it in orbit). 
*   **Prediction:** If the total energy is known, the orbital radius is fixed. If energy is lost (e.g., due to atmospheric drag), $r$ must decrease to satisfy the conservation of energy, causing the satellite to "decay" into a lower orbit.

**3. Maintaining Orbits:**
*   **Orbital Manoeuvres:** To maintain an orbit, satellites carry onboard thrusters. If a satellite deviates from its predicted path due to perturbations (e.g., solar radiation pressure, Earth's non-spherical mass distribution), a change in kinetic energy ($E_k$) is required. 
*   **Station Keeping:** By firing thrusters, engineers change the satellite’s velocity. Increasing $E_k$ pushes the satellite into a higher potential energy state (higher altitude), effectively correcting the orbital decay caused by drag. 
*   **Precise Timing:** For GPS, the orbital path must be known to sub-meter precision to calculate the time-of-flight for signals. Constant monitoring and small burns ensure that $r$ and $T$ remain constant, keeping the satellite at the correct coordinate in space.
