### Explanation

This question tests your understanding of **electromagnetic induction** and **Lenz's Law**, specifically in the context of **eddy currents**. Here is the step-by-step physical breakdown:

1.  **Faraday's Law:** As the copper disc rotates through the region between the poles of the horseshoe magnet, segments of the disc experience a change in magnetic flux ($\Delta \Phi$). Because the disc is a conductor, this change in flux induces an electromotive force (EMF), leading to the circulation of induced currents within the disc known as **eddy currents**.
2.  **Lenz's Law:** Lenz's Law states that the direction of the induced current will be such that it creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in flux that produced it. 
3.  **Interaction (The Drag Force):** The induced eddy currents create their own magnetic fields. These fields interact with the external magnetic field of the horseshoe magnet. According to Lenz's Law, this interaction results in a force that opposes the motion of the disc. This is effectively a magnetic "drag" or "braking" force.
4.  **Why the other options are incorrect:**
    *   (A) Copper is non-ferromagnetic, so it is not significantly attracted to the magnet.
    *   (B) While the disc has an induced magnetic field, the primary mechanism of braking is the interaction of forces (Lorentz forces) acting on the moving charges, not just "becoming a magnet."
    *   (C) While currents are induced, they are not strictly "AC" in the sense of a circuit; they are circular loops of eddy currents, and the phrasing of this option is less precise than (D).

Therefore, option **(D)** provides the most accurate and scientific explanation for the observed electromagnetic braking effect.
