### Answer

**Part (a)**
A bright fringe is formed at point $P$ due to the constructive interference of light waves originating from slits $S_1$ and $S_2$. Because the distance from $S_2$ to $P$ is greater than the distance from $S_1$ to $P$, the two waves arrive at $P$ with a path difference equal to an integer multiple of the wavelength ($n\lambda$). When the waves are in phase (phase difference is zero or a multiple of $2\pi$), they superpose constructively, resulting in an increase in amplitude and intensity at that point.

**Part (b)**
To calculate the wavelength, we use the Young's Double Slit interference formula:
$$\Delta x = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$$
Where:
- $\Delta x = 1.26 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m}$ (the distance to the fringe)
- $L = 2.00 \text{ m}$ (distance to screen)
- $d = 4.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}$ (slit separation)

*Self-correction/Refinement:* By observing Figure 11b, point $P$ is the 6th bright fringe from the center (counting the fringes: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6). Thus, $n=6$.
The formula is $d \sin(\theta) = n\lambda$, or using the small angle approximation, $d \frac{\Delta x}{L} = n\lambda$.

$$\lambda = \frac{d \cdot \Delta x}{n \cdot L}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{(4.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}) \times (1.26 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m})}{6 \times 2.00 \text{ m}}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{5.04 \times 10^{-6}}{12}$$
$$\lambda = 4.2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$$
The wavelength is $420 \text{ nm}$.

### How to solve this type of problem

1.  **Understand the Physics:** Recognize that double-slit interference relies on the superposition principle. A bright fringe always requires constructive interference, which occurs when the path difference $\delta = S_2P - S_1P = n\lambda$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, \dots$.
2.  **Analyze the Diagram:** 
    *   Always check if the point $P$ is the *first* bright fringe or a higher order ($n > 1$). Counting the fringes in Figure 11b clearly shows $P$ is the 6th bright fringe ($n=6$).
    *   Identify your variables: $d$ (slit separation), $L$ (slit-to-screen distance), and $y$ (distance from central maximum).
3.  **Apply the formula:** Use the standard approximation formula $\lambda = \frac{yd}{nL}$. Ensure all units are in SI (meters) before calculating.
