### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) cathode rays have momentum.**

---

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must look at the physical implications of the observation provided:

1.  **The Physics of the Paddle-wheel:** A paddle-wheel only rotates if it is subjected to an external force that does work on it. In the context of physics, for a particle or a stream of particles to exert a force on a macroscopic object and set it into motion (rotational kinetic energy), the particles must transfer kinetic energy and momentum to the blades of the wheel.
2.  **Newtonian Mechanics:** According to Newton's laws, if an object (the paddle-wheel) accelerates from rest, there must be a net force acting on it. This force arises from the collisions of the "cathode rays" with the vanes of the paddle-wheel. This demonstrates that cathode rays possess **mass** and **velocity**, and therefore, they possess **momentum** ($p = mv$).
3.  **Evaluating the alternatives:**
    *   (B) Incorrect: Cathode rays are actually electrons, which are negatively charged. This experiment does not determine charge.
    *   (C) Incorrect: While photons carry momentum, this specific experimental setup was a foundational piece of evidence used by J.J. Thomson to prove that cathode rays consist of *material particles* (electrons) rather than electromagnetic radiation or pure energy.
    *   (D) Incorrect: This experiment provides no information regarding electric fields or deflection.

Therefore, the movement of the wheel serves as evidence for the corpuscular (particle-like) nature of cathode rays and their ability to transfer momentum.
