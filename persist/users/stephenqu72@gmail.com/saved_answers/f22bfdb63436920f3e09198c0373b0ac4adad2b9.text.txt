### Answer
Scientific theories are validated through empirical evidence, reproducibility, and the ability to make accurate predictions that distinguish them from previous models. In the case of Einstein’s theory of Special Relativity, validation occurred through both experimental observation and the reconciliation of conflicting physical principles.

**1. Experimental Evidence and Measurement:**
Special Relativity was validated by the experimental observation of time dilation. The classic example is the **Hafele-Keating experiment** (1971), where atomic clocks were flown on commercial airliners around the world. The clocks showed a time difference compared to ground-based clocks, consistent with the predictions of $t = \gamma t_0$ (time dilation). Furthermore, the observation of **muons** created in the upper atmosphere provides strong validation. Muons have a very short half-life and should decay before reaching the surface; however, due to their high velocity, time dilation increases their lifespan relative to an Earth-based observer, allowing them to be detected at sea level.

**2. Resolving Anomalies and Consistency:**
Einstein’s theory gained validity by resolving the conflict between Newtonian mechanics and Maxwell’s electromagnetism. The **Michelson-Morley experiment** failed to detect the "luminiferous aether," which was expected to provide a medium for light waves. By postulating that the speed of light $c$ is constant in all inertial frames, Einstein provided a theoretical framework that explained why the aether could not be detected. This demonstrated that scientific theories are often validated by their ability to provide a more consistent, unified explanation of natural phenomena than the theories they supersede.
