To determine the correct answer, we must apply the principles of the **Doppler Effect**, which describes the change in frequency or wavelength of a wave in relation to an observer who is moving relative to the wave source.

### Analysis of the Physics
The general formula for the Doppler effect for sound is given by:
$$f_{obs} = f_s \left( \frac{v \pm v_{obs}}{v \mp v_s} \right)$$
Where:
- $f_{obs}$ is the observed frequency.
- $f_s$ is the source frequency.
- $v$ is the speed of sound.
- $v_{obs}$ is the velocity of the observer.
- $v_s$ is the velocity of the source.

**Let's evaluate the options:**

*   **A (Incorrect):** When a source moves **towards** an observer, the wave fronts are "bunched up." This decreases the observed wavelength and **increases** the observed frequency.
*   **B (Incorrect):** When an observer moves **towards** a stationary source, the observer encounters wave fronts more frequently than if they were stationary, resulting in an **increase** in observed frequency.
*   **C (Correct):** As stated above, moving towards a stationary source means the observer intercepts more wave cycles per unit of time, which corresponds to an **increase in frequency**.
*   **D (Incorrect):** When an observer moves **away** from a source, the relative speed at which the wave fronts reach the observer is lower. This causes the observed frequency to decrease and the observed wavelength to appear **longer** (an increase in wavelength, not a decrease).

### Conclusion
When you move towards a stationary sound source, you effectively "run into" the sound waves at a higher rate, which your brain interprets as a higher pitch (frequency). Therefore, **Option C** is the correct description.
