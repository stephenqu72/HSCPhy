**Answer:**

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
Classical wave theory (the Rayleigh-Jeans Law) predicted that as wavelength decreases (frequency increases), the intensity of emitted radiation should approach infinity—an error known as the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe." Experimental curves like the one shown, which peak and then drop off at shorter wavelengths, contradict this wave model. 

Max Planck resolved this by proposing that energy is emitted in discrete "packets" or quanta. The energy of these quanta is proportional to the frequency, given by the equation:
$$E = hf$$
Where:
- $E$ is the energy of the photon
- $h$ is Planck's constant
- $f$ is the frequency of the light

Because higher-frequency (lower-wavelength) photons require more energy to be produced, the probability of them being emitted by a thermal source at a specific temperature decreases, causing the intensity to drop off at the UV end of the spectrum. This quantization of energy supports the particle model of light.
