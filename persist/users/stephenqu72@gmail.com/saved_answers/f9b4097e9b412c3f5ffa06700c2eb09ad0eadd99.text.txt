### Answer

**(a)**
The hydrogen atom possesses discrete energy levels ($n = 1, 2, 3, ...$). Emission lines are produced when an electron transitions from a higher energy level ($n_{high}$) to a lower energy level ($n_{low}$). 
- The **multiple lines** in the Balmer series occur because electrons can transition from any higher energy level ($n = 3, 4, 5, ...$) down to the $n = 2$ level. 
- **Other series exist** because electrons can also transition to different lower energy levels: transitions to $n = 1$ produce the Lyman series, and transitions to $n = 3$ produce the Paschen series.

**(b)**
The wavelength ($\lambda$) is given by the Rydberg formula:
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right)$$
For the Balmer series, $n_f = 2$. For the **longest wavelength**, we need the smallest energy transition, which is from $n_i = 3$ to $n_f = 2$.
Using $R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}$:
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times \left( \frac{1}{2^2} - \frac{1}{3^2} \right)$$
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times \left( \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{9} \right) = 1.097 \times 10^7 \times \left( \frac{5}{36} \right)$$
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} \approx 1,523,611 \text{ m}^{-1}$$
$$\lambda \approx 6.56 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m} \text{ (or } 656 \text{ nm)}$$

### How to solve this
1. **For (a):** Focus on the Bohr model of the atom. Keywords like "discrete energy levels," "electron transitions," and "final energy states" are essential. You must explain that the *series* is defined by the final state ($n_f$), while the *multiple lines* within a series are defined by the varying initial states ($n_i$).
2. **For (b):** This is a standard calculation question. 
   - Identify that "longest wavelength" corresponds to the "smallest energy change" (the smallest gap between energy levels).
   - Recognize that the Balmer series always ends at $n = 2$.
   - The transition $n=3 \rightarrow n=2$ is the smallest jump for this series.
   - Plug the values into the Rydberg equation and ensure you take the reciprocal at the end to solve for $\lambda$.
