### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the historical conflict between **Classical Physics** and **Experimental Observation** regarding blackbody radiation.

1.  **Classical Prediction (Rayleigh-Jeans Law):** Classical physicists used the equipartition theorem to predict how a blackbody would emit energy. Their theory predicted that the intensity of radiation would increase to infinity as the wavelength approached zero (or as frequency increased). This implies that a blackbody would emit infinite energy in the ultraviolet and higher frequency ranges.
2.  **Experimental Reality:** When physicists measured the actual intensity of light emitted by a blackbody at a set temperature (like the $5000\text{ K}$ curve shown in the graph), they found that the intensity actually peaks and then drops toward zero at very short wavelengths (the ultraviolet end of the spectrum).
3.  **The "Catastrophe":** The term "ultraviolet catastrophe" describes this fundamental disagreement between the classical theoretical prediction (which approaches infinity) and the physical measurements (which show a finite peak and drop off). As shown in the graph, the theoretical line continues to climb upwards while the experimental line curves down. The divergence becomes extreme as you move toward the shorter wavelengths (the ultraviolet region), which is why choice **C** is the correct description of this phenomenon.
