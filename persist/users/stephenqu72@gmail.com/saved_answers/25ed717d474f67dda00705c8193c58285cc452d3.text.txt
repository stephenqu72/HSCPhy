### Answer
The maximum brightness of the light globe is determined by the **power output** ($P_{out}$) of the generator, which is limited by the **rate of energy input** from the gravitational potential energy of the falling mass.

Factors affecting brightness include:
1.  **Mass ($m$) and Height ($h$):** The total energy available is $E = mgh$. Increasing the mass or the initial height increases the total energy available to the system.
2.  **Velocity of the mass ($v$):** The rate at which energy is delivered (power) depends on the velocity of the falling mass. Since $P = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = Fv = mgv$, a higher velocity translates to higher mechanical power input to the generator.
3.  **Efficiency ($\eta$):** The generator is not 100% efficient due to friction, heat loss, and internal resistance. The output power is $P_{out} = \eta \times P_{in}$.
4.  **Circuit Resistance ($R$):** The brightness (electrical power) depends on the voltage produced ($V$) and the resistance of the bulb ($R$). Power is given by $P = \frac{V^2}{R}$. The generator must induce a voltage high enough to overcome internal losses to effectively light the bulb.
