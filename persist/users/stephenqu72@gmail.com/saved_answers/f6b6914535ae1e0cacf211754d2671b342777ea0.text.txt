To solve this problem, we must apply Einstein’s photoelectric equation, which relates the stopping voltage ($V_s$), the work function of the metal ($\Phi$), and the frequency of incident light ($f$):

$$E_k = hf - \Phi$$

Since the maximum kinetic energy is also defined as $E_k = eV_s$, we can rearrange the equation into the form of a linear graph ($y = mx + c$):

$$V_s = \left(\frac{h}{e}\right)f - \frac{\Phi}{e}$$

### Analyzing the graph:
1.  **The y-intercept ($c$):** The y-intercept represents $-\frac{\Phi}{e}$. From the graph, the y-intercept is $-3.74\text{ V}$. Therefore:
    $$-\frac{\Phi}{e} = -3.74\text{ V} \implies \Phi = 3.74\text{ eV}$$
2.  **The x-intercept:** This represents the **threshold frequency** ($f_0$), where the stopping voltage is zero ($hf_0 = \Phi$).
3.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **(A) Incorrect:** The value $3.74\text{ eV}$ represents the work function, which is the energy required to remove an electron, not a voltage required to be applied to "release" them (in fact, applying a negative voltage relative to the anode is what *stops* them).
    *   **(B) Correct:** The threshold frequency $f_0$ is given by $f_0 = \frac{\Phi}{h}$. Using $\Phi = 3.74\text{ eV}$ and Planck's constant $h \approx 4.14 \times 10^{-15}\text{ eV}\cdot\text{s}$:
        $$f_0 = \frac{3.74\text{ eV}}{4.14 \times 10^{-15}\text{ eV}\cdot\text{s}} \approx 9.03 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$$
        This matches the statement that the threshold frequency is close to $9.0 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$.
    *   **(C) Incorrect:** Increasing the frequency increases the *kinetic energy* of the emitted photoelectrons, not the *number* of photoelectrons emitted. The number of photoelectrons depends on the *intensity* of the incident light.
    *   **(D) Incorrect:** The work function ($\Phi$) is a property specific to the metal used. Replacing zinc with copper changes the work function, and consequently, the threshold frequency.

**Correct Answer: (B)**
