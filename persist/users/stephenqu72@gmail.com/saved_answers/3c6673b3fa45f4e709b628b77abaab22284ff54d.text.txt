### Teacher's Solution and Explanation

#### Part a) Calculation of Wavelength

To find the wavelength of a photon when you are given its energy, we use the following two fundamental equations from quantum physics:
1. $E = hf$ (where $E$ is energy, $h$ is Planck's constant, and $f$ is frequency)
2. $c = f\lambda$ (where $c$ is the speed of light and $\lambda$ is wavelength)

By rearranging these, we get the combined formula:
$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \implies \lambda = \frac{hc}{E}$$

**Constants:**
* Planck's constant ($h$) $\approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
* Speed of light ($c$) $\approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
* Energy ($E$) = $9.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$

**Calculation:**
$$\lambda = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}) \times (3.00 \times 10^8)}{9.2 \times 10^{-19}}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{1.9878 \times 10^{-25}}{9.2 \times 10^{-19}}$$
$$\lambda \approx 2.16 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$$

#### Part b) Explanation of Energy Difference

When a photon strikes a metal surface to eject a photoelectron, it follows Einstein’s Photoelectric Equation:
$$E_{photon} = \Phi + E_{k(max)}$$

Where:
* $E_{photon}$ is the energy of the incident photon.
* $\Phi$ (Work Function) is the minimum energy required to liberate an electron from the surface of the metal.
* $E_{k(max)}$ is the maximum kinetic energy of the ejected photoelectron.

**Explanation:**
The photon's energy is not entirely transferred into the kinetic energy of the electron. A portion of the photon's energy is consumed as the **work function** ($\Phi$), which is the energy required to overcome the electrostatic forces holding the electron within the atom or metal lattice. Because this "cost" must be paid to liberate the electron, the remaining energy available to be converted into the photoelectron's kinetic energy is significantly less than the original energy of the absorbed photon.
