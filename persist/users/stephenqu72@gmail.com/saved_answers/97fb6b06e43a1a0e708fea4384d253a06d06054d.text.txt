### Part (a)
**Answer:**
Thomson's "plum pudding" model proposed that the atom consisted of a diffuse sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded throughout. This model was inconsistent with the Geiger-Marsden experiment because it predicted that alpha particles would pass through the gold foil with only minor deflections (as there was no concentrated region of positive charge or mass). The experiment, however, observed that a small fraction of alpha particles were deflected at large angles, and some were reflected backwards, indicating the presence of a very small, dense, and positively charged nucleus.

**How to solve it:**
1. **Identify the model:** Briefly define the "plum pudding" model (diffuse positive charge, distributed mass).
2. **Identify the observation:** State the key unexpected result from the experiment (large-angle backscattering).
3. **Link them:** Explain why the model failed. Because the positive charge in Thomson’s model was spread out, the electrostatic repulsion exerted on an alpha particle would be too weak to cause significant deflection.

### Part (b)
**Answer:**
James Chadwick discovered the neutron in 1932 by bombarding a beryllium target with alpha particles. This process produced a highly penetrating, electrically neutral radiation that could not be explained by known particles at the time. When this radiation struck a paraffin wax target, it ejected protons. By applying the laws of conservation of momentum and energy to the collisions, Chadwick determined that the radiation must consist of neutral particles with a mass approximately equal to that of a proton. This confirmed the existence of the neutron.

**How to solve it:**
1. **The Experiment:** Describe the setup (bombarding beryllium with alpha particles).
2. **The Observation:** Mention the resulting radiation was neutral (non-deflected by electric/magnetic fields) and highly penetrating.
3. **The Proof:** Explain the use of paraffin wax (recoiling protons) and how conservation laws were used to calculate that the particle had a mass roughly equal to $1 \, \text{amu}$, proving it was a distinct, neutral subatomic particle.
