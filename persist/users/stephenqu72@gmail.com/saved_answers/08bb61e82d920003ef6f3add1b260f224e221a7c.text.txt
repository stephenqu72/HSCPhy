### Explanation
To solve this problem, you must apply **Kepler’s Second Law of Planetary Motion**, also known as the **Law of Equal Areas**.

1.  **Understanding Kepler's Second Law:** Kepler’s Second Law states that a line segment joining a planet and its star sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. Mathematically, the rate at which area is swept is constant:
    $$\frac{dA}{dt} = \text{constant}$$

2.  **Analyzing the Given Information:** The problem explicitly states that "the shaded sections are of equal area." Because the areas swept out by the planet as it moves from $A$ to $B$ and from $C$ to $D$ are identical, the time taken to sweep these areas must also be equal.

3.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **A is incorrect:** The greatest orbital velocity occurs at the **perihelion** (the point closest to the star). Based on the diagram, point $C$ or $D$ is closer to the star than point $A$ or $B$, but the *greatest* velocity would be at the absolute closest point of the orbit, not necessarily at $D$.
    *   **B is incorrect:** Velocity is not constant in an elliptical orbit. Because the distance from the star changes, the orbital speed changes.
    *   **C is incorrect:** According to Kepler’s Second Law, equal areas are swept in equal times. Therefore, the times cannot be different.
    *   **D is correct:** Since the areas are equal, the time interval $\Delta t$ for the planet to travel from $A$ to $B$ must be equal to the time interval $\Delta t$ to travel from $C$ to $D$.
