### Answer and Explanation

This problem relates to orbital mechanics, specifically regarding the L1 Lagrange point.

#### (a) Calculate the radius of the Earth’s orbit, $R$

To find the radius of the Earth's orbit, we use Kepler's Third Law in the form derived from equating gravitational force to centripetal force:
$$\frac{GM_s}{R^2} = \frac{4\pi^2 R}{T_E^2} \implies R^3 = \frac{GM_s T_E^2}{4\pi^2}$$

**Given constants:**
- $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$
- $M_s = 2.0 \times 10^{30} \, \text{kg}$
- $T_E = 3.16 \times 10^7 \, \text{s}$

**Calculation:**
$$R^3 = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (2.0 \times 10^{30}) \times (3.16 \times 10^7)^2}{4\pi^2}$$
$$R^3 \approx 3.36 \times 10^{33} \, \text{m}^3$$
$$R \approx 1.50 \times 10^{11} \, \text{m}$$

#### (b) Identify the forces and the equation for distance $d$

**1. Identification of forces:**
There are two primary gravitational forces acting on the DSCOVR probe:
- The gravitational force exerted by the **Sun** pulling the probe toward the Sun.
- The gravitational force exerted by the **Earth** pulling the probe toward the Earth.

**2. Equation for $d$:**
The probe maintains the same orbital period as the Earth. This means its centripetal acceleration is provided by the net gravitational force of the Sun and the Earth.
Let $M_E$ be the mass of the Earth (which would be provided in a standard exam paper context if not known). The distance of the probe from the Sun is $(R - d)$.

The net gravitational force providing the centripetal force is:
$$\frac{G M_s m}{(R - d)^2} - \frac{G M_E m}{d^2} = m \omega^2 (R - d)$$

Since $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T_E}$, the equation is:
$$\frac{G M_s}{(R - d)^2} - \frac{G M_E}{d^2} = \left(\frac{2\pi}{T_E}\right)^2 (R - d)$$
