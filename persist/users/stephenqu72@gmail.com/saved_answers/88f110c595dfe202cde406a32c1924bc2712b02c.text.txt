### Solutions

**Part (a)**
The power dissipated in the light globe is given by $P = IV$. However, we aren't given the current in the globe directly. We know the power must be conserved (assuming an ideal transformer), so the power into the transformer equals the power out.
First, we need the current in the secondary coil ($I_s$). Since the transformer turns ratio is $N_p:N_s = 4:1$, the current ratio is $I_p:I_s = 1:4$. Given $I_p = 3.0\text{ A}$, then $I_s = 4 \times 3.0 = 12.0\text{ A}$.
$$P = I_s V_s = 12.0\text{ A} \times 4.0\text{ V} = 48\text{ W}$$

**Part (b)**
The voltage output of the power supply ($V_{in}$) must account for the voltage drop across the transmission lines ($V_{lines}$) and the input to the primary coil of the transformer ($V_p$).
1. Voltage drop on lines: $V_{lines} = I_p \times R_{total} = 3.0\text{ A} \times 8.0\text{ }\Omega = 24.0\text{ V}$.
2. Primary voltage: $\frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{N_p}{N_s} \implies V_p = 4 \times 4.0\text{ V} = 16.0\text{ V}$.
3. Supply voltage: $V_{supply} = V_{lines} + V_p = 24.0\text{ V} + 16.0\text{ V} = 40.0\text{ V}$.

**Part (c)**
The power loss in the lines is calculated using the current in the transmission lines and the total resistance of the lines:
$$P_{loss} = I_p^2 \times R_{total} = (3.0)^2 \times 8.0 = 9 \times 8.0 = 72\text{ W}$$

**Part (d)**
In the second experiment, the ratio is $8:1$. To keep the globe at $4.0\text{ V}$ ($48\text{ W}$), the current in the primary must change.
1. New primary current: $\frac{I_p'}{I_s} = \frac{N_s}{N_p} \implies I_p' = \frac{12.0}{8} = 1.5\text{ A}$.
2. Power loss: $P_{loss}' = (I_p')^2 \times R_{total} = (1.5)^2 \times 8.0 = 2.25 \times 8.0 = 18\text{ W}$.

**Part (e)**
1. **Minimizing Power Loss:** As shown in part (d), increasing voltage decreases current, which drastically reduces power loss ($P = I^2R$) since the loss is proportional to the square of the current.
2. **Efficiency/Cost:** Lower power loss means less energy is wasted as heat, allowing for the use of thinner (cheaper) transmission wires while maintaining high efficiency over long distances.

### How to solve it
1. **Identify the Transformer Ratio:** Always remember that $V_p/V_s = N_p/N_s$ and $I_p/I_s = N_s/N_p$. If you know the ratio, you can calculate the current or voltage at any point.
2. **Treat the transmission lines as a resistor:** The power supply sees the transmission lines and the transformer in series. Use Ohm's Law ($V = IR$) for the lines and $P = I^2R$ to find power dissipation.
3. **Conservation of Energy:** In an ideal transformer, $P_{in} = P_{out}$. This is your strongest tool when you are missing variables.
