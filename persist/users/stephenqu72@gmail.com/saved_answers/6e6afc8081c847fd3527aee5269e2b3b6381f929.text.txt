To solve these problems, we use Kepler’s Third Law of Planetary Motion, which states that for any object orbiting a central body, the square of the orbital period $T$ is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius $r$:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

### (a) Calculate the mass of Saturn
First, convert the period $T$ of Mimas into SI units (seconds):
$T = 0.942 \text{ days} \times 24 \times 3600 = 81388.8 \text{ s}$
Using the formula $M = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GT^2}$:
$$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2 \times (1.86 \times 10^8)^3}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times (81388.8)^2}$$
$$M \approx 5.73 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg}$$

### (b) Calculate the orbital period of Dione
Since both moons orbit the same central body (Saturn), we can use the ratio form of Kepler's Third Law:
$$\frac{r_M^3}{T_M^2} = \frac{r_D^3}{T_D^2}$$
Rearranging for $T_D$:
$$T_D = \sqrt{\frac{r_D^3 \times T_M^2}{r_M^3}} = T_M \times \sqrt{\left(\frac{r_D}{r_M}\right)^3}$$
$$T_D = 0.942 \times \sqrt{\left(\frac{3.78 \times 10^8}{1.86 \times 10^8}\right)^3} \approx 0.942 \times \sqrt{8.397} \approx 2.73 \text{ days}$$

### (c) Explain why the orbital velocity of Dione changes
While moons in circular orbits maintain a constant speed, in a real scenario, orbits are **elliptical** (Kepler’s First Law). According to **Kepler’s Second Law** (the law of equal areas), a satellite must sweep out equal areas in equal times. Consequently, as a moon approaches its **periapsis** (the closest point to Saturn), gravitational potential energy is converted into kinetic energy, causing the moon to speed up. Conversely, as it moves toward **apoapsis** (the furthest point), it loses kinetic energy and slows down.
