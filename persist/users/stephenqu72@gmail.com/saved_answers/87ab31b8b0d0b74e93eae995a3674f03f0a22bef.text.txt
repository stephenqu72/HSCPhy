### Part A: Explaining the Photoelectric Effect Current
**Answer:** Point $X$ represents the **stopping voltage** ($V_{stop}$). At this voltage, the electric field created by the power supply is strong enough to repel even the most energetic photoelectrons (those with maximum kinetic energy, $E_{K \text{ max}}$) from reaching the collector electrode. Since no electrons reach the collector, the circuit is effectively broken, and the photoelectric current drops to zero. Mathematically, this is expressed as:
$$E_{K \text{ max}} = q_e V_{stop}$$
where $q_e$ is the elementary charge.

**How to solve:**
1. Identify that the graph shows the relationship between retarding voltage and current.
2. Recall that $E_{K \text{ max}}$ depends on the frequency of incoming photons.
3. Link the voltage $V$ to the work done against the kinetic energy of the electrons. When the work done by the retarding potential equals the maximum kinetic energy, the electrons stop, resulting in zero current.

### Part B: Analyzing the Graph
**Answer:** To determine if the data points fit a straight line, follow these steps:
1. **Draw a line of best fit:** Using a ruler, draw a straight line that passes through the majority of the uncertainty bars. The line should balance the number of points above and below it, and it should intersect the uncertainty bars wherever possible.
2. **Assess the fit:** Check if the straight line passes through or touches all the error bars. If the line intersects every error bar, then a linear relationship is consistent with the experimental data.
3. **Calculate the intercept/slope (Optional):** Ensure the y-intercept aligns with the theoretical work function ($-W$ or $-\Phi$) and the slope represents Planck's constant ($h$) as per the Einstein photoelectric equation $E_{K \text{ max}} = hf - \Phi$.

**How to solve:**
- The relationship between $E_{K \text{ max}}$ and frequency $f$ is linear ($E_{K \text{ max}} = hf - \Phi$).
- The provided error bar shows the range of uncertainty for that specific data point. For the model to be valid, your "best fit" line must fall within the range of every uncertainty bar. If there is a point where no straight line can pass through its error bar, then the linear model may be incorrect.
