To solve for the total mechanical energy of the student at the highest point, we need to calculate the sum of the gravitational potential energy ($U_g$) and the kinetic energy ($K$).

### 1. Identifying the variables
*   Mass ($m$) = $55\text{ kg}$
*   Radius ($r$) = $15\text{ m}$
*   Period ($T$) = $45\text{ s}$
*   Acceleration due to gravity ($g$) $\approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$

### 2. Calculating Gravitational Potential Energy ($U_g$)
Taking the bottom of the Ferris wheel as the reference point ($h = 0$), the height at the top is the diameter of the wheel:
$$h = 2r = 30\text{ m}$$
$$U_g = mgh = 55 \times 9.8 \times 30 = 16,170\text{ J}$$

### 3. Calculating Kinetic Energy ($K$)
First, calculate the velocity ($v$) of the carriage:
$$v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} = \frac{2\pi \times 15}{45} = \frac{30\pi}{45} = \frac{2\pi}{3} \approx 2.094\text{ m/s}$$

Now, calculate kinetic energy:
$$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = 0.5 \times 55 \times (2.094)^2 \approx 120.7\text{ J}$$

### 4. Total Mechanical Energy ($E_{total}$)
$$E_{total} = U_g + K = 16,170 + 120.7 = 16,290.7\text{ J}$$

Rounding to appropriate significant figures (two, based on the provided data $15\text{ m}$ and $55\text{ kg}$), the total mechanical energy is approximately **$1.6 \times 10^4\text{ J}$**.

***

### Explanation of the approach:
1.  **Define the reference frame:** By setting the ground level of the ride as $h=0$, the highest point corresponds to a height of $2r$.
2.  **Calculate $U_g$:** Use the standard formula $mgh$.
3.  **Determine $v$:** Since the motion is uniform circular motion, we use the circumference ($2\pi r$) divided by the period ($T$) to find the linear velocity.
4.  **Calculate $K$:** Use the formula $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$.
5.  **Summation:** Adding these two components gives the total mechanical energy of the system at that specific height.
