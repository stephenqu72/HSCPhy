### Explanation
To understand this question, we must look at the mechanism of nuclear fission:

1.  **The Fission Process:** In both controlled and uncontrolled reactions, a heavy nucleus (like $^{235}U$) absorbs a neutron and splits, releasing a set amount of energy and approximately 2–3 additional neutrons. Because the physics of an individual fission event is identical in both scenarios, options (A) and (B) are incorrect.
2.  **The Chain Reaction:** The "control" in a nuclear chain reaction refers specifically to the **neutron population**. 
    *   In a **controlled reaction** (like in a nuclear power plant), control rods (usually made of boron or cadmium) are used to absorb excess neutrons. This ensures that exactly one neutron from each fission event goes on to trigger another fission event, maintaining a steady, constant rate of energy release.
    *   In an **uncontrolled reaction** (like in an atomic bomb), no such regulation exists. Each fission event triggers multiple subsequent events, leading to an exponential increase in the number of fission reactions occurring in a fraction of a second.
3.  **Conclusion:** The fundamental difference lies in how many subsequent fission reactions are permitted to occur. By regulating the number of available neutrons, we regulate the number of "subsequent fission reactions."

Therefore, option (C) correctly identifies that the rate of the chain reaction is defined by the number of subsequent events allowed to take place.
