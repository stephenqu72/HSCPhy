To determine the magnitude of the torque on the coil due to the magnetic field, we must first recognize that the system is in static equilibrium. 

### Step 1: Identify the forces providing torque
The system consists of a coil pivoted at the center. Two torques are acting on this system, keeping it stationary:
1. **Clockwise Torque ($\tau_g$):** Provided by the gravitational force acting on the $2.5\text{ kg}$ mass.
2. **Counter-clockwise Torque ($\tau_B$):** Provided by the magnetic force acting on the coil.

For the coil to be stationary, the sum of torques about the pivot must be zero:
$$\sum \tau = 0 \implies \tau_B = \tau_g$$

### Step 2: Calculate the gravitational torque ($\tau_g$)
The mass is $m = 2.5\text{ kg}$. The force of gravity is $F_g = mg = 2.5 \times 9.8 = 24.5\text{ N}$.
This force acts at the end of the coil. The distance from the pivot to the mass is half the width of the coil, $r = \frac{0.30\text{ m}}{2} = 0.15\text{ m}$.
The angle between the coil and the vertical is $50^\circ$. Therefore, the perpendicular distance (lever arm) is $d_\perp = r \cos(50^\circ)$.

$$\tau_g = F_g \times r \cos(50^\circ)$$
$$\tau_g = 24.5 \times 0.15 \times \cos(50^\circ)$$
$$\tau_g \approx 3.675 \times 0.6428 \approx 2.36\text{ N m}$$

Wait—let's re-examine the geometry. The weight acts at the end of the arm. The horizontal distance from the pivot to the line of action of the weight is $r \sin(50^\circ)$ if the coil is at $50^\circ$ to the horizontal. Looking at the diagram, the coil is at $50^\circ$ to the horizontal, so the horizontal displacement from the pivot is $0.15 \cos(50^\circ)$. Thus, $\tau_g = 24.5 \times 0.15 \cos(50^\circ) \approx 2.36\text{ N m}$.
