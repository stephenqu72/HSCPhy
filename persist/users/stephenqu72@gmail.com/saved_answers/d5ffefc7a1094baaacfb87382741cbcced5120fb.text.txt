### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** Kepler’s First Law states that planets (and moons) orbit in ellipses with the central body at one focus. Because the orbit is elliptical rather than perfectly circular, the distance between the moon and the planet varies as the moon moves along its orbital path.

**Explanation:** Always remember that "circular" is just a special, simplified case of an ellipse. In reality, most celestial orbits are eccentric, meaning the orbital radius fluctuates between periapsis (closest point) and apoapsis (farthest point).

#### Part (b)
**Step 1: Calculate the orbital period ($T$).**
The average orbital speed $v$ is given by $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$.
First, convert speed to standard SI units: $v = 2.50 \text{ km s}^{-1} = 2500 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.
Rearranging for $T$:
$$T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2 \pi (5.67 \times 10^8 \text{ m})}{2500 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$$
$$T \approx 1.425 \times 10^6 \text{ s}$$

**Step 2: Calculate the mass of the planet ($M$).**
We use the orbital velocity formula for a circular orbit: $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$. 
Squaring both sides gives $v^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$, so $M = \frac{v^2 r}{G}$.
Using $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2 \text{ kg}^{-2}$:
$$M = \frac{(2500)^2 \times (5.67 \times 10^8)}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}}$$
$$M = \frac{6.25 \times 10^6 \times 5.67 \times 10^8}{6.67 \times 10^{-11}}$$
$$M \approx 5.31 \times 10^{25} \text{ kg}$$

**Teacher's Tip:** When calculating mass in orbital mechanics, always ensure your radius is in meters and velocity is in meters per second to match the units of the Gravitational Constant $G$.
