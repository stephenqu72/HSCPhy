### Front
How can you distinguish between an **elastic** and an **inelastic** collision using kinetic energy ($KE$)?

### Back
- **Key idea:** In an **elastic** collision, total $KE$ is conserved. In an **inelastic** collision, total $KE$ decreases as it is converted into other forms (e.g., heat, sound, or deformation).
- **Formula / law / constant / figure:** No fixed constant or figure ($KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$).
- **When to use it:** When asked to classify a collision type or calculate energy loss in a system.
- **Common trap:** Assuming that because momentum is conserved, kinetic energy must also be conserved. Momentum is conserved in *both* types of collisions (in an isolated system), but $KE$ is only conserved in elastic ones.
