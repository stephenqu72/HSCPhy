### Explanation
To solve this, we need to recall the formal definition of electric field strength ($E$). 

Electric field strength at a point in space is defined as the electrostatic force ($F$) experienced by a stationary positive test charge ($q$) placed at that point, divided by the magnitude of that test charge. Mathematically, this is expressed as:

$$E = \frac{F}{q}$$

Now, let's look at why the other options are less precise or incorrect in an HSC physics context:

*   **A is incorrect** because it just says "force," which ignores the magnitude of the charge. A large charge would experience a much larger force in the same field than a small charge.
*   **B and C are partially correct** but incomplete. While "force per unit charge" is the standard shorthand, the rigorous definition specifically refers to the force experienced by a **positive test charge**. This convention ensures that the direction of the electric field vector is defined as the direction a positive charge would move.
*   **D is the most complete definition.** It specifies that we are looking at the force per unit charge as it relates specifically to a "positive test charge." In physics exams, examiners look for precision; defining the test charge as positive is crucial because it sets the standard for the direction of the field lines.

**Study Tip:** Always remember that the electric field is a vector quantity. By defining the field using a *positive* test charge, we ensure that the field vector points away from positive source charges and toward negative source charges.
