### Answer

The differences in the energy spectra arise due to the number of particles involved in the decay process and the associated conservation laws:

1.  **Alpha Decay (Discrete Energy):** Alpha decay is a two-body process. A parent nucleus decays into a daughter nucleus and an alpha particle. Because energy and momentum must be conserved in a two-body system, the available decay energy ($Q$-value) is shared between the daughter nucleus and the alpha particle in a fixed ratio. Consequently, alpha particles are emitted with a **discrete (single) energy**.
2.  **Beta Decay (Continuous Energy):** Beta decay is a three-body process. A neutron decays into a proton, an electron, and an antineutrino ($n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$). The total energy released ($Q$-value) is shared randomly among these three particles. Because the antineutrino carries away a variable amount of energy (ranging from zero to the maximum available energy), the remaining kinetic energy available to the electron varies, resulting in a **continuous energy spectrum**.
3.  **The Role of the Neutrino:** The "missing" energy in beta decay was historically problematic until Pauli proposed the existence of the neutrino (or antineutrino). The continuous distribution of electron energies is direct experimental evidence for the existence of the antineutrino, as it accounts for the conservation of energy, momentum, and angular momentum (spin) in the decay.

To secure full marks in an HSC Physics context:

*   **Apply Physics principles:** Explicitly state the "two-body" vs. "three-body" nature of the decays.
*   **Connect to conservation laws:** Mention the conservation of energy and momentum.
*   **Explain the "why":** For beta decay, you must explain that the antineutrino shares the energy, which is the reason the electron energy can vary.
*   **Terminology:** Use terms like "$Q$-value," "discrete," "continuous," and "three-body process" to show high-level scientific literacy.
