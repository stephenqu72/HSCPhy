### Explanation
To solve this question, you must understand the definition of a **fundamental particle** according to the Standard Model of particle physics. A fundamental particle is one that has no known substructure; it is not composed of smaller particles.

Let's evaluate the particles listed in each option:

*   **Option A:**
    *   **Tau-neutrino:** A fundamental lepton.
    *   **Strange Quark:** A fundamental quark.
    *   **Gluon:** A fundamental gauge boson (mediator of the strong nuclear force).
    *   **Electron:** A fundamental lepton.
    *   *Conclusion:* All particles listed here are fundamental.

*   **Option B:** Includes a **Proton**. Protons are composite particles made of three quarks (two up quarks and one down quark). Therefore, this is incorrect.

*   **Option C:** Includes a **Neutron**. Neutrons are composite particles made of three quarks (one up quark and two down quarks). Therefore, this is incorrect.

*   **Option D:** Includes an **Atom**. An atom is a composite structure containing a nucleus (protons and neutrons) and electrons. Therefore, this is incorrect.

**Summary for your HSC studies:**
Remember that the Standard Model categories for fundamental particles are:
1.  **Quarks** (Up, Down, Charm, Strange, Top, Bottom)
2.  **Leptons** (Electron, Muon, Tau, and their corresponding neutrinos)
3.  **Gauge Bosons** (Photon, Gluon, W and Z bosons)
4.  **Scalar Bosons** (Higgs Boson)

Anything else (like protons, neutrons, or atoms) is a composite particle.
