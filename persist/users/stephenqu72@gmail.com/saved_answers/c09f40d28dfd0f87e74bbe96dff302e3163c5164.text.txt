### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:**
As the comet moves from position $P$ (the aphelion) toward the Sun, the gravitational force exerted by the Sun acts at an acute angle to the comet's velocity, increasing its speed. The speed reaches a maximum at perihelion (closest to the Sun). As it moves away from the Sun toward $P$, the gravitational force acts at an obtuse angle to the velocity, causing the comet to decelerate until it reaches its minimum speed back at $P$. Throughout the motion, mechanical energy ($E = K + U$) is conserved. As potential energy $U$ decreases moving toward the Sun, kinetic energy $K$ must increase to keep total energy constant.

**Explanation:**

2. **Work/Energy Exchange:** Explain that as distance to the Sun decreases, gravitational potential energy decreases ($U = - \frac{G M m}{r}$ becomes more negative), requiring kinetic energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) to increase.
3. **Dynamics:** Alternatively, discuss the component of gravitational force parallel to the velocity vector which accelerates the object toward perihelion and decelerates it toward aphelion.

#### Part (b)
**Answer:**
For two stars of equal mass $m$ orbiting their common center of mass, each star moves in a circle of radius $r = \frac{x}{2}$. The centripetal force is provided by the gravitational force between them. The distance between the stars is $x$.

The gravitational force is:
$$F_g = \frac{G m^2}{x^2}$$

The centripetal force required for star $B$ to move in a circle of radius $\frac{x}{2}$ with speed $v$ is:
$$F_c = \frac{m v^2}{r} = \frac{m v^2}{x/2} = \frac{2 m v^2}{x}$$

Equating $F_g$ and $F_c$:
$$\frac{G m^2}{x^2} = \frac{2 m v^2}{x}$$

Solving for $v$:
$$\frac{G m}{x} = 2 v^2$$
$$v^2 = \frac{G m}{2x}$$
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{G m}{2x}}$$

**Explanation:**
This is a standard orbital mechanics problem. Because the masses are equal, they orbit a common center of mass located exactly at the midpoint between them. Therefore, the orbital radius for each star is half the separation distance ($x/2$). By setting the gravitational attraction equal to the required centripetal force for circular motion, you can isolate $v$.
