To solve this problem, we need to calculate the gravitational acceleration acting on a satellite at a specific altitude, which is equivalent to the centripetal acceleration required to keep the satellite in orbit.

### 1. Identify the given information
*   Mass of satellite ($m$): $500\text{ kg}$ (Note: The centripetal acceleration of an object in orbit is independent of its own mass).
*   Altitude ($h$): $800\text{ km} = 800 \times 10^3\text{ m} = 8 \times 10^5\text{ m}$.
*   Radius of Earth ($R_E$): Approximately $6.37 \times 10^6\text{ m}$ (standard HSC Physics constant).
*   Mass of Earth ($M$): Approximately $5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}$ (standard HSC Physics constant).
*   Gravitational constant ($G$): $6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ N m}^2\text{ kg}^{-2}$.

### 2. Determine the distance from the centre of the Earth
The total distance ($r$) from the centre of the Earth to the satellite is the sum of the Earth's radius and the altitude:
$$r = R_E + h = 6.37 \times 10^6\text{ m} + 0.8 \times 10^6\text{ m} = 7.17 \times 10^6\text{ m}$$

### 3. Calculate centripetal acceleration ($a_c$)
Since the only force providing the centripetal acceleration is gravity, we use Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation:
$$F_g = F_c$$
$$\frac{G M m}{r^2} = m a_c$$
Dividing both sides by $m$ gives the formula for gravitational field strength (acceleration):
$$a_c = \frac{G M}{r^2}$$

Now, substitute the values:
$$a_c = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24})}{(7.17 \times 10^6)^2}$$
$$a_c = \frac{3.982 \times 10^{14}}{5.14 \times 10^{13}}$$
$$a_c \approx 7.75\text{ m s}^{-2}$$

Rounding to two significant figures, we get $7.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$.

### 4. Conclusion
Comparing our result to the options provided:
(A) $3.6\text{ m s}^{-2}$
(B) $6.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$
(C) $7.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$
(D) $9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$

The correct option is **(C)**.
