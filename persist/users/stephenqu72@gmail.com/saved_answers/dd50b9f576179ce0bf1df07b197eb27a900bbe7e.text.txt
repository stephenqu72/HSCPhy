To solve this problem, we apply the principles of Special Relativity, specifically length contraction.

### Part (a): Calculation
**Formula:**
The length contraction formula is:
$$L_v = L_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$
Where:
- $L_0 = 456\text{ m}$ (rest length)
- $v = 0.95c$
- $L_v$ is the apparent length

**Calculation:**
$$L_v = 456 \sqrt{1 - \frac{(0.95c)^2}{c^2}}$$
$$L_v = 456 \sqrt{1 - 0.95^2}$$
$$L_v = 456 \sqrt{1 - 0.9025}$$
$$L_v = 456 \sqrt{0.0975}$$
$$L_v \approx 456 \times 0.31225$$
$$L_v \approx 142.38\text{ m}$$

**Answer:** The apparent length is approximately **$142.4\text{ metres}$**.

---

### Part (b): Explanation
To explain why the length changes, we must refer to Einstein’s postulates and the nature of space-time:

1.  **Constancy of the Speed of Light:** Einstein postulated that the speed of light ($c$) is constant for all inertial observers, regardless of their motion.
2.  **Relative Simultaneity:** Because $c$ is constant, measurements of time and distance cannot be absolute. If two events (measuring the front and back of the rocket) must be simultaneous in the observer's frame to determine length, the lack of absolute simultaneity between frames means the observer perceives a different distance than someone at rest with the rocket.
3.  **Space-Time Invariance:** Length contraction is a physical consequence of the geometry of space-time. As an object moves at relativistic speeds, the "distance" between the two ends of the object must contract to ensure that the speed of light remains consistent across different inertial reference frames. Essentially, length contraction "compensates" for time dilation to keep the physics consistent between moving observers.
