### **Solutions**

#### **a. Variables**
*   **Independent:** Mass of the load ($M$)
*   **Dependent:** Period of oscillation ($T$)
*   **Controlled:** The specific spring used (elastic constant $k$), the range of motion (amplitude), the environment (to keep air resistance/gravity constant).

#### **b. Minimising Effects**
*   **(i) Air resistance:** Perform the experiment in a vacuum chamber, or use a load with a very small surface area (aerodynamic shape) and oscillate at low speeds.
*   **(ii) Mass of the spring:** Use a spring with very low mass relative to the load, or use the theoretical correction formula: $T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{M + m/3}{k}}$ where $m$ is the mass of the spring.

#### **c. Corrected Table (Uncertainties)**
To find the uncertainty in the period $T$: If $T = \frac{t_{20}}{20}$, then the uncertainty $\Delta T = \frac{\Delta t_{20}}{20}$.

#### **d. Analysis**
The theoretical relationship for a spring-mass system is $T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{M}{k}}$. This implies $T^2 \propto M$.
To determine the relationship, you should plot $T^2$ on the y-axis and $M$ on the x-axis. A linear relationship (a straight line through the origin) would verify that $T \propto \sqrt{M}$.

#### **e. Improvement**
To better ascertain the relationship, the students should:
1.  **Increase the range of data points:** Use a wider variety of masses to see if the relationship holds over a greater interval.
2.  **Increase the number of oscillations:** Instead of 20, use 50 or 100 oscillations to reduce the relative uncertainty caused by human reaction time.

### **How to solve it**
1.  **Variables:** Always look for what the experimenter is changing (Independent) and what they are measuring as a result (Dependent).
2.  **Uncertainties:** When you divide or multiply a measurement by a constant, you must divide/multiply the absolute uncertainty by that same constant. 
3.  **Relationships:** When dealing with $T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{M}{k}}$, remember that identifying a non-linear relationship is easiest by "linearizing" the data (squaring both sides to get $T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{k}M$).
