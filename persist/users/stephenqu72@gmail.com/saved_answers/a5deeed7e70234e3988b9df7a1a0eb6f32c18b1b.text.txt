### Answer
The understanding of light shifted from a purely wave-based model to wave-particle duality due to experimental evidence that could not be explained by classical wave theory.

**1. The Evidence for Particle Properties:**
*   **The Photoelectric Effect:** Classical wave theory predicted that the intensity (amplitude) of light should determine the energy of ejected electrons. However, experiments (notably by Lenard and explained by Einstein in 1905) showed that the energy of ejected electrons depended solely on the *frequency* of the incident light, not its intensity. Furthermore, no electrons were ejected below a specific "threshold frequency" regardless of intensity.
*   **The Explanation:** Einstein proposed that light is quantized into discrete packets of energy called photons, where the energy of each photon is given by $E = hf$. This explains why frequency (energy per photon) determines electron kinetic energy, and why low-frequency light cannot eject electrons even at high intensities.

**2. The Evidence for Wave Properties:**
*   **Diffraction and Interference:** Young’s double-slit experiment (and subsequent experiments like Poisson’s spot/Arago spot) demonstrated that light exhibits interference patterns characteristic of waves. Maxwell’s equations further solidified light as an electromagnetic wave consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields.

**3. Development of Wave-Particle Duality:**
*   The wave model correctly predicted propagation phenomena (diffraction, interference, reflection).
*   The particle model (quantum theory) correctly predicted interaction phenomena (photoelectric effect, blackbody radiation).
*   Physicists concluded that light does not behave exclusively as one or the other, but rather manifests different properties depending on the nature of the interaction. This synthesis is wave-particle duality: light propagates as a wave but interacts with matter as a particle (quantized energy). This model was eventually extended to matter (de Broglie’s hypothesis), forming the foundation of quantum mechanics.
