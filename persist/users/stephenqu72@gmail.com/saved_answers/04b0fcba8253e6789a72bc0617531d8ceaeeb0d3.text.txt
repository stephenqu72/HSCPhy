### Answer
The correct answer is **(D)**.

### Explanation
To solve this, we must consider the physics of polarising materials (like Polaroid filters). 

1.  **Theoretical Ideal:** According to Malus’s Law, $I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$, where $I_0$ is the intensity of the light after passing through the first polariser and $\theta$ is the angle between the transmission axes. If the polarisers are aligned at $90^\circ$ (crossed), $\cos(90^\circ) = 0$, so the intensity $I$ should be zero.
2.  **Practical Reality:** In the real world, polarising films are not perfect. They consist of long-chain polymer molecules (often aligned by stretching) that absorb light components parallel to the molecular chains. 
3.  **Why light passes through:** No real-world polariser is a perfect "black-out" device. The small amount of light observed passing through at $90^\circ$ occurs because the "slits" or molecular gaps in the polarising material are not perfectly uniform or perfectly opaque. If the manufacturing process isn't perfect, or if the material structure is "too wide" (allowing microscopic leakage), some light components will not be fully absorbed, leading to **extinction leakage**. 
4.  **Eliminating other options:**
    *   (A) and (B) are incorrect because the state of the light source does not change the fact that an ideal polarising system at $90^\circ$ should block light. 
    *   (C) Monochromatic light is irrelevant to the polarisation efficiency of the material.
    *   (D) is the most scientifically accurate explanation in a high school physics context for why an experiment deviates from the ideal extinction predicted by Malus's Law.
