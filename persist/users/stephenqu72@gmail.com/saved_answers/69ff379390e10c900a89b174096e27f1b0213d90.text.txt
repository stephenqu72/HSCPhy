To answer this 8-mark question effectively, you must provide a structured, analytical response that links specific experimental evidence to the development of modern quantum theory. 

### How to solve this question:
1.  **Structure:** Use a clear paragraph for each of the three experiments. 
2.  **Analysis:** For each experiment, identify:
    *   The observation/phenomenon being investigated.
    *   The failure of classical (wave) physics to explain the results.
    *   The quantum model that emerged as a result.
3.  **Synthesis:** Conclude by summarizing how these pieces of evidence collectively shifted physics from a classical wave-based view of light to a quantum mechanical view (wave-particle duality).

---

### Sample Answer

**1. Blackbody Radiation (Planck/Wien):** 
Classical physics, specifically the Rayleigh-Jeans Law, predicted an "ultraviolet catastrophe"—that a blackbody would emit infinite energy at high frequencies. Experimental data showed a specific curve that dropped off at high frequencies. Max Planck resolved this by postulating that energy is quantized, where $E = nhf$. This was the first evidence that light-matter interaction occurs in discrete packets (quanta) rather than continuous waves.

**2. The Photoelectric Effect (Einstein):** 
Classical wave theory predicted that increasing the intensity of light would increase the kinetic energy of emitted electrons. However, experiments (notably by Lenard and Millikan) showed that the maximum kinetic energy depended only on the frequency of light, and below a "threshold frequency" $f_0$, no electrons were emitted. Einstein used Planck’s constant to propose light is made of particles called photons ($E=hf$), proving light exhibits particle-like properties in its interaction with matter.

**3. Atomic Emission Spectra (Bohr/Balmer):** 
Classical physics predicted that an orbiting electron should spiral into the nucleus while radiating energy continuously. Experimental observation of discrete spectral lines in hydrogen indicated that electrons exist in stable, quantized energy levels. Bohr’s model utilized this evidence to explain that light is emitted when an electron transitions between energy levels ($E_{photon} = \Delta E_{atom}$). This confirmed that energy states of matter are quantized, directly linked to the emission and absorption of photons.

**Conclusion:** Collectively, these experiments forced the paradigm shift from a purely classical wave theory of light to the quantum model. They established the principle of wave-particle duality, showing that light exhibits both wave characteristics (interference/diffraction) and particle characteristics (quantization of energy) when interacting with matter.
