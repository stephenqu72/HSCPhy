### Answer
The correct answer is **(D)**.

### Explanation
To understand why circular tracks are banked, we must look at the physics of a vehicle moving along a curved path. 

1.  **Requirement for Circular Motion:** For an object to move in a circle, a **centripetal force** ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$) must be directed toward the center of the circle.
2.  **Unbanked Tracks:** On a flat (horizontal) track, the only horizontal force available to provide this centripetal force is static **friction** between the tires and the road. Relying solely on friction is dangerous, especially in wet or icy conditions, as friction is limited and unreliable.
3.  **Banked Tracks:** When a track is banked at an angle $\theta$, the normal force ($N$) exerted by the track on the vehicle is no longer purely vertical. Instead, the normal force is tilted toward the center of the circle. This allows the horizontal component of the normal force ($N \sin \theta$) to provide all or part of the required centripetal force.
4.  **The Role of Friction:** By utilizing the component of the normal force to provide the necessary centripetal force, the vehicle no longer needs to rely as heavily on friction to stay on the track. Therefore, banking the track effectively **decreases the reliance on (or the need for) frictional force** to maintain the circular path.

**Evaluating the options:**
*   (A) and (B) are incorrect because the centripetal force required to turn a vehicle at a specific velocity and radius is determined by the laws of physics ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$) and does not change based on the track's design.
*   (C) is incorrect because the gravitational force ($mg$) is constant and independent of the track's banking.
*   (D) is the best explanation: by using the normal force to provide the centripetal component, we reduce the amount of friction required from the tires to prevent skidding.
