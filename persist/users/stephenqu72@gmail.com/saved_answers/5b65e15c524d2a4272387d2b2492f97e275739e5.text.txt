The correct answer is **A. Core temperature**.

### Explanation

To solve this problem, we need to consider what information is contained within a star's absorption spectrum (the spectrum of light formed as the star's radiation passes through its cooler outer atmosphere).

1.  **Chemical composition (B):** Each element absorbs light at specific, unique wavelengths (spectral lines). By identifying the patterns of these dark absorption lines, we can determine which elements are present in the star's atmosphere.
2.  **Atmospheric pressure (C):** The width and shape of spectral lines are affected by "pressure broadening." High-pressure environments cause more frequent collisions between atoms, which disrupts their energy levels and broadens the absorption lines. Astronomers measure this broadening to determine the pressure in the stellar photosphere.
3.  **Rotational velocity (D):** Rotation causes one side of the star to move toward Earth (blueshift) and the other to move away (redshift). This causes the spectral lines to broaden (or "smear out") due to the Doppler effect. By analyzing the extent of this broadening, we can calculate the star's rotational velocity.
4.  **Core temperature (A):** Absorption spectra only provide information about the star's **surface (photosphere)** and the gases moving through it. The core of a star is shielded by the dense, opaque layers of the star's interior. The light we observe has been processed through multiple scattering events and re-absorption, meaning all information about the specific thermal conditions of the core is lost before the light escapes the surface. While the core temperature influences the surface temperature and luminosity, it cannot be directly measured or uniquely determined simply by looking at the absorption lines of the light that reaches Earth.
