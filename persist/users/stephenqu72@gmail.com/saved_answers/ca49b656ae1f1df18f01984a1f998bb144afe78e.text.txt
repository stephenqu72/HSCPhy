This question tests your understanding of circular motion in a magnetic field, a core component of the HSC Physics Module 4: Electricity and Magnetism.

### (a) Derivation of the Period $T$
When a charged particle moves perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field, the magnetic force acts as the centripetal force.

1. Set the magnetic force equal to the centripetal force:
   $$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
2. Solve for the radius $r$:
   $$r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$
3. The period $T$ is the time taken to complete one full revolution (circumference $= 2\pi r$):
   $$T = \frac{2\pi r}{v}$$
4. Substitute the expression for $r$ into the period equation:
   $$T = \frac{2\pi (\frac{mv}{qB})}{v} = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}$$

---

### (b) Calculation
Given:
- Mass of an electron $m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}$
- Charge of an electron $q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}$
- Magnetic field $B = 2.0 \times 10^{-4}\text{ T}$

Using the formula derived in (a):
$$T = \frac{2\pi \times (9.11 \times 10^{-31})}{(1.60 \times 10^{-19}) \times (2.0 \times 10^{-4})}$$
$$T \approx 1.79 \times 10^{-7}\text{ s}$$

---

### (c) Drawing the Path
From the formula $T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}$, we see that the period $T$ is **independent of the velocity** but **inversely proportional to the magnetic field strength $B$**.

If the magnetic field strength $B$ is halved, the new period $T'$ becomes:
$$T' = \frac{2\pi m}{q(0.5B)} = 2T$$

**To draw the path:** The electron now completes only **half a revolution** in the original time $T$. Since the radius $r = \frac{mv}{qB}$ is inversely proportional to $B$, halving $B$ doubles the radius of the circle. You should draw a semi-circle starting from the point of entry, with a radius twice as large as the original circle, ending at a point "below" the starting position.
