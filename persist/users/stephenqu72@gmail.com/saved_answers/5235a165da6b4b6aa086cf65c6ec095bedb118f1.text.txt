### Answer Guide

**1. Newton’s Corpuscular Model:**
Newton proposed that light consists of tiny "corpuscles" (particles) emitted by light sources. This model successfully explained rectilinear propagation, reflection, and refraction (assuming light traveled faster in denser media). However, it could not explain diffraction or interference.

**2. Huygens’ Wave Model:**
Huygens proposed that light behaves as a longitudinal wave traveling through an "aether." This model explained reflection and refraction (correctly predicting light slows down in denser media) and successfully accounted for diffraction. It fell out of favor when Newton's reputation dominated, but was revived by **Young’s Double Slit Experiment**, which provided definitive evidence of interference, confirming the wave nature of light.

**3. Maxwell’s Theory of Electromagnetism:**
Maxwell mathematically unified electricity and magnetism, proposing that light is a transverse electromagnetic wave traveling at a constant speed ($c$). This model explained propagation through a vacuum without an aether and predicted the existence of the electromagnetic spectrum. It was confirmed by **Hertz’s experiments**, which produced and detected radio waves, showing they behaved like light.

**4. Planck and Einstein’s Particle Model:**
While Maxwell explained most phenomena, the "ultraviolet catastrophe" and the **Photoelectric Effect** could not be explained by wave theory. Planck proposed that energy is quantized ($E = hf$). Einstein extended this to light, proposing light consists of discrete "quanta" (photons). This explained why the kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons depends on frequency, not intensity, successfully establishing the wave-particle duality of light.

### How to solve this type of question:
1.  **Structure:** Use headings or clear paragraphs for each of the four models to ensure you address all parts of the prompt.
2.  **The "Analysis" Requirement:** Do not just list the models. You must explain *why* scientists moved from one to the next (e.g., "Huygens' model was accepted because Young's double-slit experiment provided evidence of interference that particles could not explain").
3.  **HSC Keyword (Analyse):** The verb "analyse" requires you to examine the relationship between the theories and the evidence. Always identify the specific experiment (e.g., Young’s, Hertz’s, Photoelectric Effect) that serves as the "turning point."
4.  **Mark Allocation:** With 9 marks available, aim to spend roughly 2 minutes per model, plus an extra minute for a concluding sentence summarizing the concept of wave-particle duality.
