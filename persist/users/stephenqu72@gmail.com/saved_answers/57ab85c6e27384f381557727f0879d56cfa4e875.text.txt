To solve this problem, we apply the principles of Special Relativity, specifically **time dilation** and **length contraction**.

First, calculate the Lorentz factor, $\gamma$, which is constant for this relative velocity $v = 0.995c$:
$$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.995^2}} \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.990025}} \approx \frac{1}{0.09987} \approx 10.0125$$

---

### (a) Calculate the half-life as measured by a stationary observer
Due to time dilation, a moving clock runs slow. The observed half-life $t$ is dilated relative to the proper half-life $t_0 = 2.2\ \mu\text{s}$:
$$t = \gamma t_0$$
$$t = 10.0125 \times 2.2\ \mu\text{s} \approx 22.03\ \mu\text{s}$$

---

### (b) Find the height of the mountain as measured by the muons
Due to length contraction, the distance measured by an observer moving relative to the object is shorter than the proper length $L_0 = 2627\ \text{m}$:
$$L = \frac{L_0}{\gamma}$$
$$L = \frac{2627\ \text{m}}{10.0125} \approx 262.37\ \text{m}$$

---

### (c) Explain why more muons reach the ground
In classical physics (ignoring relativity), we would expect the muons to decay rapidly because they only have a lifetime of $2.2\ \mu\text{s}$. However, Special Relativity provides two equivalent perspectives:
1. **Time Dilation (Earth frame):** Because the muons are traveling at high speeds, their internal "clocks" appear to run significantly slower to a stationary observer on Earth. This means they survive for a much longer time (approx $22\ \mu\text{s}$), allowing them to travel the full distance to the ground before decaying.
2. **Length Contraction (Muon frame):** In the reference frame of the muons, the atmosphere and the mountain are moving towards them at $0.995c$. Due to length contraction, the mountain appears significantly shorter (only $\approx 262\ \text{m}$ high). Consequently, the muons reach the ground within their own short proper lifetime.

Both perspectives confirm that more muons reach the ground than classical mechanics would predict.
