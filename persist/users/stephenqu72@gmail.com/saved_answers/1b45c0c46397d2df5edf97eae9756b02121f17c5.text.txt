The correct answer is **(D) a transverse wave.**

### Explanation

To solve this, you need to understand the fundamental difference between longitudinal and transverse waves:

1.  **Defining Polarisation:** Polarisation is the process of restricting the oscillations of a wave to a single plane. For a wave to be polarised, its oscillations must occur perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.
2.  **Transverse Waves:** In a transverse wave, the particle displacement (or the oscillation of the electric and magnetic fields in the case of electromagnetic waves) is perpendicular to the direction in which the wave travels. Because the oscillations have a specific orientation in 3D space, we can filter them to pass through only one plane. This is why light (an electromagnetic, transverse wave) can be polarised.
3.  **Longitudinal Waves:** In a longitudinal wave (like sound), the particle displacement is parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Because the vibration is already restricted to the axis of travel, there is no "side-to-side" or "up-and-down" component that can be filtered out by a polarising medium. Therefore, longitudinal waves cannot be polarised.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A)** While electromagnetic waves *are* transverse and *can* be polarised, not all polarisable waves are electromagnetic (e.g., transverse waves on a string can also be polarised). Thus, (D) is the more fundamental and correct condition.
*   **(B)** Longitudinal waves cannot be polarised, as their displacement is parallel to the direction of propagation.
*   **(C)** Mechanical waves can be either longitudinal (like sound) or transverse (like waves on a string). Since only the transverse variety can be polarised, "mechanical wave" is too broad and not necessarily true.
