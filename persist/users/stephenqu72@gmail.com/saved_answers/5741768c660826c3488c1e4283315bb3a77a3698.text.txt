To answer this question effectively, you must apply **Lenz's Law** and the principle of **Electromagnetic Induction**.

### Answer
When the north pole of the bar magnet is moved toward the aluminium ring, the ring will be observed to **swing away from the magnet**. Even when the magnet stops suddenly, the ring will momentarily maintain its displacement before eventually swinging back and coming to rest.

### Explanation (Step-by-Step)
To secure all 4 marks, your response should follow this logical structure:

1.  **Change in Magnetic Flux:** As the magnet moves toward the ring, the magnetic flux passing through the area of the aluminium ring increases.
2.  **Faraday's Law:** According to Faraday's Law, this changing magnetic flux induces an electromotive force (EMF), and because the aluminium ring is a conductor, it induces an electric current (eddy current) within the ring.
3.  **Lenz's Law (Direction of the Current):** Lenz's Law states that the direction of the induced current is such that it creates a magnetic field that opposes the change in flux that produced it. To oppose the approaching north pole, the face of the ring closest to the magnet will effectively become a **north pole**.
4.  **Interaction:** Since the face of the ring becomes a north pole and the approaching magnet is a north pole, a repulsive magnetic force is exerted between them. This force pushes the ring away from the magnet.

### Strategy for HSC Responses
*   **Keywords:** Ensure you use the terms *changing magnetic flux*, *induced EMF*, *induced current*, *Lenz's Law*, and *opposing the change*.
*   **The "Why":** Never just state the observation (that it moves). You must explicitly link the repulsive force to the creation of an induced magnetic pole in the ring.
*   **Conservation of Energy:** You can also mention that this is an application of the Law of Conservation of Energy; the work done to move the magnet against the repulsive force is the source of the electrical energy (the eddy currents) generated in the ring.
