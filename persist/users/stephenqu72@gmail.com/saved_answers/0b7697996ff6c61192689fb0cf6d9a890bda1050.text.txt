To determine the correct graph for the speed of a projectile, we must analyze how the horizontal and vertical components of velocity behave over time.

### Analysis
1.  **Horizontal Velocity ($v_x$):** In the absence of air resistance, there is no horizontal acceleration. Therefore, the horizontal component remains constant:
    $$v_x(t) = v \cos(\theta) = \text{constant}$$
2.  **Vertical Velocity ($v_y$):** The projectile is subject to constant gravitational acceleration $g$ acting downwards. The vertical component changes linearly with time:
    $$v_y(t) = v \sin(\theta) - gt$$
3.  **Total Speed ($S$):** The speed of the projectile at any time $t$ is the magnitude of the velocity vector:
    $$S(t) = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} = \sqrt{(v \cos \theta)^2 + (v \sin \theta - gt)^2}$$

### Solving the Problem
*   At launch ($t=0$), the speed is $v$.
*   At the peak of the trajectory, the vertical velocity $v_y$ is $0$. Therefore, the speed is at its **minimum**, and that minimum value is equal to the constant horizontal velocity $v_x = v \cos(\theta)$. 
*   Since $v \cos(\theta) > 0$ (assuming $\theta < 90^\circ$), the graph of speed cannot touch the $t$-axis (ruling out A and D).
*   The function $S(t) = \sqrt{C + (kt - b)^2}$ is a hyperbola-like curve, which is non-linear. Specifically, it is a concave-up curve that reaches a minimum value greater than zero at the peak of the flight.

Graph **(C)** is the only option that shows a smooth, concave-up curve where the minimum speed is greater than zero, consistent with the constant horizontal velocity component.

**Correct Answer: (C)**
