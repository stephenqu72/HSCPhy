### Part (a): Account for the similarities and differences of the two curves.

**Similarities:**
Both curves represent the continuous spectrum of electromagnetic radiation emitted by a hot, opaque body (a black-body). Both show a characteristic "hump" shape where intensity increases to a peak wavelength ($λ_{max}$) and then decreases. This follows Wien’s Law, which dictates that all objects above absolute zero emit thermal radiation.

**Differences:**
1.  **Presence of Absorption Lines:** Curve B (the star) displays distinct vertical dips, which are **Fraunhofer absorption lines**. These occur when the cooler outer atmosphere of the star absorbs specific wavelengths of light emitted by the hotter core, identifying the chemical composition of the star’s atmosphere. Curve A is a smooth, continuous curve, typical of an incandescent solid, lacking these discrete transitions.
2.  **Peak Wavelength ($λ_{max}$):** The peak wavelength for Curve A is approximately $700\text{ nm}$, whereas the peak wavelength for Curve B is roughly $400\text{--}450\text{ nm}$. This indicates that the star is significantly hotter than the body in Curve A, consistent with Wien's Displacement Law.

### Part (b): Calculate the surface temperature of the star.

To calculate the temperature, we use **Wien’s Displacement Law**:
$$\lambda_{max} T = 2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$$

**Step 1: Identify $\lambda_{max}$ from Curve B.**
Looking at the peak of the shaded region in Curve B, the intensity peaks between $400\text{ nm}$ and $500\text{ nm}$. A reasonable estimate for the peak is approximately $450\text{ nm} = 4.5 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$.

**Step 2: Substitute into the formula.**
$$T = \frac{2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}}{4.5 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}}$$
$$T \approx 6440 \text{ K}$$
