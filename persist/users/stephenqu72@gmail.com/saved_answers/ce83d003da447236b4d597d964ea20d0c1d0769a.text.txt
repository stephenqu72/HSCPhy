To solve this problem, we must consider both the magnetic force acting on the current-carrying rod and the gravitational force acting on the mass of the rod.

### 1. Calculate the Magnetic Force ($F_B$)
The magnetic force on a current-carrying conductor is given by the formula:
$$F_B = BIL\sin(\theta)$$
Where:
- $B = 10\text{ T}$ (magnetic field strength)
- $I = 2\text{ A}$ (current)
- $L = 1\text{ m}$ (length of the rod)
- $\theta = 90^\circ$ (the current is flowing perpendicular to the field lines, so $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$)

$$F_B = 10 \times 2 \times 1 \times 1 = 20\text{ N}$$

### 2. Calculate the Gravitational Force ($F_g$)
The weight of the rod is:
$$F_g = mg$$
Where $m = 0.1\text{ kg}$ (converting 100g to kg) and $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$.
$$F_g = 0.1 \times 9.8 = 0.98\text{ N (downwards)}$$

### 3. Analyze the Options
There seems to be a slight ambiguity in the question's intended direction for the magnetic force. If we assume the magnetic force is acting **downwards** (as implied by the options C and D involving "down"), we calculate the net force:
$$F_{net} = F_B + F_g$$
$$F_{net} = 20\text{ N} + 0.98\text{ N} = 20.98\text{ N (down)}$$
