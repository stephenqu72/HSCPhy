### Explanation
To solve this question, we need to recall the specific functions of the internal components of a nuclear fission reactor:

1.  **Moderators:** In a fission reaction, neutrons are released with very high kinetic energies (fast neutrons). However, the probability of these neutrons inducing further fission in a fuel nucleus (like $^{235}U$) is significantly higher if the neutrons have lower kinetic energy (thermal neutrons). The **moderator** (e.g., water, heavy water, or graphite) is designed to collide with these fast neutrons, causing them to lose kinetic energy through elastic scattering until they reach "thermal speeds." This increases the probability of sustaining a chain reaction. Therefore, the moderator **slows down fast neutrons**.

2.  **Control Rods:** These are made of materials that are excellent at capturing neutrons (such as boron, cadmium, or hafnium). By inserting or withdrawing these rods into the reactor core, the operators can control the number of neutrons available to cause further fission. When inserted, the control rods **absorb excess neutrons**, effectively slowing down or stopping the chain reaction. If the reaction needs to be increased, the rods are withdrawn to allow more neutrons to be available for fission.

**Analysis of the options:**
*   **Row A:** Correctly identifies that control rods absorb neutrons and moderators slow down neutrons.
*   **Row B:** Incorrect because moderators do not speed up neutrons; they slow them down.
*   **Row C:** Incorrect because moderators do not speed up neutrons.
*   **Row D:** Incorrect because, while the moderator part is correct, control rods are physically moved into the core to absorb neutrons, not just to change the "capture cross-section" of the entire system as a passive property. Absorption is their primary mechanism of control.

Therefore, **A** is the only scientifically accurate statement.
