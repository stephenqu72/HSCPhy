To solve this projectile motion problem, we break the initial velocity into vertical and horizontal components using trigonometry. Let $v = 25\text{ m s}^{-1}$ and $\theta = 39^\circ$. The acceleration due to gravity is $g = 9.8\text{ m s}^{-2}$.

### Part a: Show that time to highest point is $1.6\text{ s}$

1. **Calculate the vertical component of the initial velocity ($v_y$):**
   $$v_y = v \sin(\theta) = 25 \sin(39^\circ) \approx 15.73\text{ m s}^{-1}$$

2. **Use the kinematic equation for the vertical motion:**
   At the highest point, the final vertical velocity is $v_f = 0$. Using $v_f = v_i + at$:
   $$0 = v_y - gt$$
   $$t = \frac{v_y}{g} = \frac{15.73}{9.8} \approx 1.605\text{ s}$$

3. **Conclusion:**
   Rounding to two significant figures, $t = 1.6\text{ s}$.

---

### Part b: Calculate the range, $R$

1. **Find the total time of flight ($T$):**
   Since the projectile lands at the same height it was launched from, the total time is double the time to the highest point:
   $$T = 2 \times 1.605 = 3.21\text{ s}$$

2. **Calculate the horizontal component of velocity ($v_x$):**
   This remains constant throughout the flight:
   $$v_x = v \cos(\theta) = 25 \cos(39^\circ) \approx 19.43\text{ m s}^{-1}$$

3. **Calculate the range ($R$):**
   $$R = v_x \times T$$
   $$R = 19.43 \times 3.21 \approx 62.37\text{ m}$$
   Rounding to two significant figures (consistent with part a), $R \approx 62\text{ m}$.
