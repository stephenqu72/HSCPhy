### Answer
The correct answer is **(D) is older than our universe.**

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we look at the relationship between the Hubble constant ($H_0$) and the age of the universe ($t$). 

1.  **Understand Hubble's Law:** Hubble's Law states that the velocity ($v$) of a galaxy moving away from us is proportional to its distance ($d$) from us: 
    $$v = H_0 \times d$$
2.  **Relate to Age:** The Hubble constant $H_0$ represents the rate of expansion of the universe. The "Hubble time," which provides a first-order approximation for the age of the universe, is given by the reciprocal of the Hubble constant:
    $$t \approx \frac{1}{H_0}$$
3.  **Analyze the Inverse Relationship:** Because the age of the universe ($t$) is inversely proportional to the Hubble constant ($H_0$), if the Hubble constant in a hypothetical universe is smaller than it is in our universe, the value of $1/H_0$ must be larger. 
4.  **Conclusion:** A smaller rate of expansion (smaller $H_0$) implies that it has taken the universe longer to reach its current size. Therefore, a universe with a smaller Hubble constant is older than our universe.

*   (B) is incorrect because a smaller $H_0$ suggests a slower expansion, not a more compacted state.
