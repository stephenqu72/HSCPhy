### Answer
The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, you must recognize the historical significance of this experiment, which describes the discovery of the neutron by James Chadwick in 1932.

1.  **Analyze the context:** When alpha particles ($_{2}^{4}\text{He}$) strike a beryllium ($_{4}^{9}\text{Be}$) target, the reaction is: 
    $$_{2}^{4}\text{He} + _{4}^{9}\text{Be} \rightarrow _{6}^{12}\text{C} + _{0}^{1}\text{n}$$
    The "unknown radiation" produced is a stream of neutrons.

2.  **Evaluate the options against Gamma Radiation:**
    *   **A (Deflection by electric field):** Both neutrons and gamma rays are electrically neutral; neither is deflected by an electric field. This result would not distinguish between them.
    *   **B (Penetrating power):** Both neutrons and high-energy gamma rays possess high penetrating power. This property does not effectively differentiate the two.
    *   **C (Ejection of protons from paraffin):** This is the key piece of evidence. Gamma rays interact with electrons (via Compton scattering or the photoelectric effect), but they lack the mass to impart significant momentum to protons. Neutrons, having a mass roughly equal to that of a proton, can undergo elastic collisions with hydrogen nuclei (protons) in paraffin, "knocking" them out. Observing these high-speed protons was the definitive evidence used by Chadwick to prove the radiation consisted of massive, neutral particles (neutrons) rather than massless electromagnetic radiation (gamma rays).
    *   **D (Photoelectric effect):** Gamma rays produce the photoelectric effect, while neutrons do not. However, while this distinguishes the two, it is a negative result. Option C provides the *positive* physical evidence of the particle's interaction mechanism that confirmed its identity.
