### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Method:** 
To determine the gravitational acceleration $g$ without a stopwatch, we use a horizontal projection method.
1. Place the launcher at a known height $h$ above a level floor.
2. Launch the ball horizontally with the given initial velocity $u = 7.8\text{ m s}^{-1}$.
3. Measure the horizontal displacement (range) $R$ of the ball from the point directly below the launcher to where it hits the ground.
4. Using horizontal motion: $R = u \times t \implies t = \frac{R}{u}$.
5. Using vertical motion: $h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$.
6. Substitute $t$ into the vertical equation: $h = \frac{1}{2}g \left(\frac{R}{u}\right)^2$.
7. Rearrange to solve for $g$:
$$g = \frac{2h u^2}{R^2}$$

**Teacher's Tip:** When describing experimental methods, always mention that you measure the horizontal range $R$ and the vertical height $h$. Mentioning the specific substitution steps shows the marker you understand the derivation of the kinematic equations.

#### Part (b)
**Calculation:**
The effective gravity at the equator is the difference between the true gravitational pull ($g_0$) and the centripetal acceleration ($a_c$) required to keep an object rotating with the Earth. If the Earth stops rotating, the centripetal acceleration becomes zero. Therefore, the measured gravity would increase by the value of $a_c$.

1. Identify the formula for centripetal acceleration: $a_c = \omega^2 R_e$, where $\omega$ is angular velocity and $R_e$ is the Earth's radius.
2. $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$, where $T = 24 \text{ hours} = 24 \times 3600 \text{ s} = 86400 \text{ s}$.
3. $R_e \approx 6.37 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$.
4. Calculate $\omega$:
$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{86400} \approx 7.27 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad s}^{-1}$$
5. Calculate $a_c$:
$$a_c = (7.27 \times 10^{-5})^2 \times (6.37 \times 10^6) \approx 0.0337 \text{ m s}^{-2}$$

**Conclusion:** The measured gravitational acceleration would increase by approximately $0.034 \text{ m s}^{-2}$.
