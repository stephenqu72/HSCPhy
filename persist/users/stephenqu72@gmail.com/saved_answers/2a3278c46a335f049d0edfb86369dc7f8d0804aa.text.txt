### Answer and Explanation

**Part a) State Planck’s two fundamental postulates for black-body radiation.**

1.  **Quantization of Energy:** Atoms in a black-body radiator behave as oscillators that can only emit or absorb energy in discrete, quantized amounts (packets) known as quanta. The energy of these quanta is given by the equation $E = nhf$, where $n$ is an integer, $h$ is Planck’s constant, and $f$ is the frequency of the oscillator.
2.  **Energy Exchange:** The oscillators do not emit or absorb energy continuously; they change their energy states only by emitting or absorbing a single quantum of energy corresponding to the transition between allowed energy levels.

**Part b) Explain how this was able to explain the experimental data collected at high temperatures.**

Before Planck, classical physics (the Rayleigh-Jeans law) predicted that as the wavelength approached zero (the ultraviolet region), the intensity of radiation should approach infinity—a failure known as the "Ultraviolet Catastrophe."

Planck’s postulate resolved this by introducing the concept of quantization. Because oscillators can only emit discrete packets of energy, it becomes statistically highly improbable for oscillators to emit high-energy (high-frequency) radiation at lower temperatures. This "energy penalty" effectively cuts off the intensity curve at short wavelengths, forcing the intensity to drop to zero as wavelength decreases. This matches the experimental data shown in the provided graph, where the curves peak and then decline toward zero at short wavelengths, successfully eliminating the Ultraviolet Catastrophe and aligning theoretical models with physical observations.
