Hello! As an HSC physics teacher, I’m happy to help you break down this projectile motion problem. These questions are classic HSC fare—the key is to treat the horizontal and vertical motions as completely independent.

---

### Part (a)
To find the components of a velocity vector, we use trigonometry.
*   **Horizontal component ($v_x$):** $v_x = v \cos(\theta)$
*   **Vertical component ($v_y$):** $v_y = v \sin(\theta)$

**Calculation:**
*   $v_x = 14 \times \cos(25^{\circ}) \approx 12.69 \text{ m s}^{-1}$
*   $v_y = 14 \times \sin(25^{\circ}) \approx 5.92 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

---

### Part (b)
To see if the ball clears the fence, we need to find the height of the ball ($y$) at the exact moment it reaches the horizontal distance ($x = 12 \text{ m}$).

**Step 1: Find the time ($t$) it takes to reach the fence.**
Since horizontal velocity is constant:
$$x = v_x t \implies t = \frac{x}{v_x}$$
$$t = \frac{12}{12.69} \approx 0.946 \text{ s}$$

**Step 2: Calculate the vertical height ($y$) at that time.**
Using the displacement formula $y = v_{0y}t + \frac{1}{2}a_y t^2$, where $a_y = -9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$:
$$y = (5.92)(0.946) + 0.5(-9.8)(0.946)^2$$
$$y = 5.60 - 4.38$$
$$y = 1.22 \text{ m}$$

**Conclusion:**
The height of the ball at the fence is $1.22 \text{ m}$ (or $122 \text{ cm}$). Since $122 \text{ cm} > 95 \text{ cm}$, **the ball will clear the fence.**

---

### Teacher's Tip:
When solving projectile motion, always list your "suvat" variables for vertical motion separately from your horizontal motion. Remember that $a_x = 0$ and $a_y = -g$. Converting units (like $95 \text{ cm}$ to $0.95 \text{ m}$) is a common step where students lose easy marks—always ensure your units are consistent before calculating!
