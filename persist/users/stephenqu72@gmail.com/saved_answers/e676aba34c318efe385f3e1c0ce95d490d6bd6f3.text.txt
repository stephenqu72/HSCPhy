### Answer
The correct answer is **(D)**.

### Explanation
To distinguish between a controlled and an uncontrolled nuclear chain reaction, we must look at the neutron population and how it is managed:

1.  **Nuclear Fission Basics:** In a fission event, a heavy nucleus (like Uranium-235) absorbs a neutron, splits, and releases a certain amount of energy and several additional neutrons. These neutrons can then go on to strike other nuclei, continuing the chain reaction.
2.  **Controlled Reaction:** This occurs in nuclear reactors. Control rods made of neutron-absorbing materials (such as boron or cadmium) are inserted into the reactor core. These rods absorb excess neutrons, ensuring that, on average, exactly one neutron from each fission event triggers another fission. This maintains a steady, constant power output (the reaction rate is kept stable).
3.  **Uncontrolled Reaction:** This occurs in nuclear weapons (atomic bombs). There are no control mechanisms to absorb the excess neutrons. Consequently, almost all neutrons released trigger further fission events, causing an exponential increase in the number of fissions occurring per second. 

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A)** The number of neutrons released per fission is a property of the specific isotope being used (e.g., U-235), not the control method.
*   **(B)** The energy released per individual fission event is identical in both scenarios; it is a fundamental property of the specific nuclear reaction.
*   **(C)** The mass defect is a property of the nuclear reaction itself, which remains constant regardless of whether the overall chain reaction is controlled or uncontrolled.

Therefore, the defining difference is how the **rate of fission reactions** is managed—either kept steady (controlled) or allowed to increase exponentially (uncontrolled).
