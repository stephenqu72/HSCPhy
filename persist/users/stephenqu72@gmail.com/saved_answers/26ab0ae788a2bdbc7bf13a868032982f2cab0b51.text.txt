To solve this problem, we use Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Motion, which states that the square of the orbital period $T$ is directly proportional to the cube of the orbital radius $r$:
$$\frac{r^3}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{4\pi^2}$$

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Understand the Variables:**
*   Note that the mass of the satellite does not affect the orbital period (it cancels out in the derivation). Therefore, the $100\text{ kg}$ and $200\text{ kg}$ masses are irrelevant.
*   Let the first satellite have radius $r_1 = 6.671 \times 10^6\text{ m}$ and period $T_1$.
*   Let the second satellite have radius $r_2$ and period $T_2 = 2T_1$.

**2. Set up the ratio:**
Using the proportionality $\frac{r_1^3}{T_1^2} = \frac{r_2^3}{T_2^2}$, we can rearrange to find $r_2$:
$$\left( \frac{r_2}{r_1} \right)^3 = \left( \frac{T_2}{T_1} \right)^2$$
Substitute $T_2 = 2T_1$:
$$\left( \frac{r_2}{r_1} \right)^3 = (2)^2 = 4$$
$$r_2 = r_1 \times \sqrt[3]{4}$$
$$r_2 = (6.671 \times 10^6) \times 1.5874 \approx 10.589 \times 10^6\text{ m}$$

**3. Calculate the altitude:**
The radius $r$ is the distance from the centre of the Earth. Altitude $h$ is defined as $r - R_E$ (where $R_E \approx 6.371 \times 10^6\text{ m}$).
*Note: In many HSC questions, if the Earth's radius isn't provided, you may use the standard value $6.371 \times 10^6\text{ m}$ or simply leave the answer in terms of $r$. Assuming standard Earth radius:*
$$h = 10.589 \times 10^6 - 6.371 \times 10^6 = 4.218 \times 10^6\text{ m}$$

**Answer:**
The required orbital radius is approximately $1.059 \times 10^7\text{ m}$. Subtracting the Earth's radius, the altitude is approximately **$4.22 \times 10^6\text{ m}$**.
