To solve this problem, we use the de Broglie wavelength formula, which relates the wavelength ($\lambda$) of a particle to its momentum ($p$):

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$$

Since the particle is travelling at the speed of light ($c$), we use relativistic momentum $p = mc$. Thus:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{mc}$$

Rearranging to solve for the mass ($m$):

$$m = \frac{h}{\lambda c}$$

### Calculation:
Given:
- Planck's constant $h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$
- Speed of light $c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$
- Wavelength $\lambda = 1.319 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m}$

Substitute the values:

$$m = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{(1.319 \times 10^{-15})(3.00 \times 10^8)}$$
$$m = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{3.957 \times 10^{-7}}$$
$$m \approx 1.674 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$$

### Analysis:
We compare this mass to the known masses of the given particles:
- **Proton:** $\approx 1.673 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$
- **Neutron:** $\approx 1.675 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$
- **Electron:** $\approx 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
- **Helium nucleus:** $\approx 6.64 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$

The calculated mass is extremely close to the mass of a neutron. Therefore, the correct answer is **B. Neutron**.
