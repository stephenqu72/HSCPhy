To determine the constant centripetal force from the experimental data provided, you should follow a graphical analysis method. This approach linearizes the relationship between the variables, allowing you to use the gradient of a line of best fit to find the force.

### Step-by-Step Analysis

1.  **Identify the governing equation:**
    The formula for centripetal force ($F_c$) is:
    $$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
    Rearranging this to isolate the variables we have ($v^2$ and $r$), we get:
    $$v^2 = \left(\frac{F_c}{m}\right)r$$

2.  **Prepare the variables:**
    *   Convert the radius ($r$) from cm to SI units (meters) by dividing each value by 100.
    *   Convert the mass ($m$) from grams to kg: $100\text{ g} = 0.1\text{ kg}$.
    *   Calculate the square of each velocity measurement ($v^2$) to match the linear form $y = mx$.

3.  **Construct a graph:**
    *   Plot a graph with $v^2$ (in $\text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}$) on the vertical axis (y-axis) and the radius $r$ (in meters) on the horizontal axis (x-axis).
    *   Plot the six data points and draw a line of best fit. The line should pass through or near the origin $(0,0)$, as $v^2$ must be zero when $r$ is zero.

4.  **Determine the force:**
    *   Calculate the gradient (slope) of the line of best fit: $\text{gradient} = \frac{\Delta v^2}{\Delta r}$.
    *   From the equation $v^2 = (\frac{F_c}{m})r$, the gradient is equal to $\frac{F_c}{m}$.
    *   Therefore, solve for the centripetal force using: $F_c = \text{gradient} \times m$.

***

### Summary of reasoning
The goal of this analysis is to transform a non-linear relationship into a linear one. By plotting $v^2$ vs $r$, the constant proportionality $(\frac{F_c}{m})$ becomes the slope of the line, which minimizes experimental error by averaging the results across all data points rather than relying on a single calculation.
