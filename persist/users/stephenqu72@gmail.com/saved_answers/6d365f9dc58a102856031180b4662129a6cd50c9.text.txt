### Explanation
To understand why this is the correct answer, we must look at the nuclear physics governing $\beta^-$ decay.

1.  **The Process of $\beta^-$ Decay:** During beta-minus decay, a neutron inside the nucleus is converted into a proton, an electron (the $\beta^-$ particle), and an electron antineutrino. This can be represented by the following equation:
    $$n \rightarrow p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$
    Because a neutron is turning into a proton, the atomic number of the nucleus increases by $1$, while the mass number remains the same.

2.  **Why it occurs:** Nuclei are held together by the strong nuclear force, which must overcome the electrostatic repulsion between protons. For a nucleus to be stable, it requires a specific ratio of neutrons to protons. 
    *   If a nucleus is "neutron-rich" (has too many neutrons relative to the number of protons), it sits above the "band of stability." 
    *   To reach a more stable state, the nucleus converts a surplus neutron into a proton, thereby shifting its ratio toward the stable region. Thus, **too many neutrons** is the trigger condition for $\beta^-$ decay.
