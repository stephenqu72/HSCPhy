To answer this question effectively, you need to identify features of a star that can be deduced from its light spectrum, other than its chemical composition (absorption lines) and surface temperature (Wien's Law/peak wavelength).

### Answer
Spectra provide information about several other features of stars:

1.  **Radial Velocity:** By observing the Doppler shift of spectral lines, we can determine the speed and direction at which a star is moving relative to Earth. A shift toward longer wavelengths (redshift) indicates the star is moving away, while a shift toward shorter wavelengths (blueshift) indicates it is moving toward us.
2.  **Rotational Velocity:** The rotation of a star causes broadening of its spectral lines (Doppler broadening). As one side of the star rotates toward the observer and the other rotates away, the spectral lines are smeared out. The degree of this broadening indicates the star's rotational speed.
3.  **Density/Luminosity Class:** The pressure in a star’s atmosphere affects the width of its spectral lines (pressure broadening). High-density stars (like main-sequence stars) have broader spectral lines than low-density stars (like supergiants), allowing astronomers to determine the star's luminosity class.
