To achieve full marks on this 5-mark HSC Physics question, you must demonstrate a clear understanding of the physical processes behind energy release in both systems and relate them to the Principle of Conservation of Energy (or mass-energy equivalence).

### Answer

In traditional power plants (burning fossil fuels like coal), energy is released through **chemical reactions**. These reactions involve the rearrangement of electrons in the outer shells of atoms, breaking and forming molecular bonds. The energy released is relatively small, often measured in electron-volts (eV) per molecule. The law of conservation of energy applies here in the context of enthalpy changes; the total energy of the system remains constant as chemical potential energy is converted into thermal and kinetic energy.

In nuclear reactors, energy is released through **nuclear reactions** (fission). This involves the splitting of heavy nuclei, such as Uranium-235, into smaller daughter nuclei. During this process, there is a measurable "mass defect," where the mass of the products is slightly less than the mass of the reactants. This lost mass ($\Delta m$) is converted directly into energy according to Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence formula: 
$$E = \Delta m c^2$$
Because the speed of light ($c$) is a very large constant, even a tiny mass defect results in a massive release of energy, millions of times greater than chemical energy release per unit mass of fuel. In both cases, the total mass-energy of the universe is conserved; in the nuclear case, the equivalence of mass and energy is explicitly observed.

### How to solve this question:
1.  **Identify the Mechanism:** Start by distinguishing the source of energy. Chemical reactions involve electron shells (chemical potential energy); nuclear reactions involve the nucleus (binding energy).
2.  **Quantify the Difference:** Mention the scale. Nuclear energy is orders of magnitude more efficient than chemical energy.
3.  **Explain the Physics:** Reference the fundamental principles. Use the term "binding energy" for nuclear reactions and "chemical bonds" for traditional fuels.
4.  **Address the Constraint:** The question specifically asks to account for the "conservation of energy." You must explain that in chemical reactions, mass is conserved, whereas, in nuclear reactions, mass-energy equivalence must be invoked to satisfy the law of conservation of mass-energy.
5.  **Structure for 5 marks:** 
    *   1 mark: Chemical process (bond rearrangement).
    *   1 mark: Nuclear process (fission/mass defect).
    *   1 mark: Contrast the magnitude of energy released.
    *   2 marks: Clear explanation of how the Law of Conservation of Energy/Mass applies to both (highlighting mass-energy equivalence for the nuclear component).
