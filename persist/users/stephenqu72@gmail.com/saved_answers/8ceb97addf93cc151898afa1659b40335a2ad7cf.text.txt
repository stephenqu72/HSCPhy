To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of circular motion. First, we identify our variables and convert them into standard SI units:
*   Mass ($m$) = $750\text{ kg}$
*   Velocity ($v$) = $40\text{ km/h} = \frac{40}{3.6}\text{ m/s} \approx 11.11\text{ m/s}$
*   Radius ($r$) = $15.0\text{ m}$

---

### Part (a): Minimum frictional force
In a horizontal circular turn, the centripetal force ($F_c$) required to keep the car on the path is provided by the static friction ($f$) between the tyres and the road. The formula for centripetal force is:
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Substituting the values:
$$F_c = \frac{750 \times (11.11)^2}{15.0}$$
$$F_c = \frac{750 \times 123.46}{15.0} = 50 \times 123.46 = 6173\text{ N}$$

The minimum frictional force required is approximately **$6.17 \times 10^3\text{ N}$**.

---

### Part (b): Degree of banking
When there is no friction, the horizontal component of the normal force ($N$) must provide the centripetal force, while the vertical component balances the weight ($mg$). The relationship for an ideal banked track is given by:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$

Using $g \approx 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$:
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{(11.11)^2}{15.0 \times 9.8}$$
$$\tan(\theta) = \frac{123.46}{147} \approx 0.8398$$

To find the angle $\theta$:
$$\theta = \arctan(0.8398) \approx 40.0^\circ$$

The road needs to be banked at approximately **$40.0^\circ$**.

---

### Summary of method
1.  **Always convert units:** Physics formulas require SI units ($m/s$ for velocity).
2.  **Identify the provider of centripetal force:** In (a), it is friction; in (b), it is the horizontal component of the normal force.
3.  **Use trigonometry for banking:** The expression $\tan(\theta) = \frac{v^2}{rg}$ is a standard result derived from force vector diagrams that you should memorise for HSC Physics.
