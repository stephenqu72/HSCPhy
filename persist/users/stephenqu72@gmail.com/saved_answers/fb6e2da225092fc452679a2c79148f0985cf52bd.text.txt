### Solution and Explanation

#### a) Show that the momentum of the electrons is $p = 2.7 \times 10^{-22} \text{ kg ms}^{-1}$.

Since the electrons are traveling at $0.70c$, we must use the relativistic momentum formula:
$$p = \gamma m_e v = \frac{m_e v}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$
Given $v = 0.70c$:
$$p = \frac{(9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}) \times (0.70 \times 3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})}{\sqrt{1 - 0.70^2}}$$
$$p = \frac{1.913 \times 10^{-22}}{\sqrt{0.51}} \approx \frac{1.913 \times 10^{-22}}{0.714} \approx 2.68 \times 10^{-22} \text{ kg ms}^{-1}$$
Rounding to two significant figures gives $2.7 \times 10^{-22} \text{ kg ms}^{-1}$.

#### b) Describe how the data from this experiment provides qualitative evidence to support this proposal.

The experiment shows the formation of an interference pattern (multiple maxima and minima) when electrons pass through a double slit. Interference is a property exclusive to waves. Since electrons (which are particles) exhibit this behavior, it provides qualitative evidence that electrons possess wave-like characteristics, supporting the de Broglie hypothesis.

#### c) Explain how the data from this experiment provides quantitative evidence to support this proposal.

To provide quantitative evidence, we compare the de Broglie wavelength to the interference pattern measurements.
1.  **Calculate the de Broglie wavelength:**
    $$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}}{2.7 \times 10^{-22} \text{ kg ms}^{-1}} \approx 2.45 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m}$$
2.  **Use the double-slit interference formula:** $d \sin \theta = n \lambda$ (for maxima).
    From the graph, the 3rd order maximum is at $\theta \approx 3.3 \times 10^{-5}$ radians (using the $x$-axis scale $\times 10^{-5}$). 
    For small angles, $\sin \theta \approx \theta$.
    Using $d = 220 \text{ nm} = 220 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}$ and $n=3$:
    $$\lambda = \frac{d \sin \theta}{n} = \frac{220 \times 10^{-9} \times 3.3 \times 10^{-5}}{3} \approx 2.42 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m}$$
3.  **Conclusion:** The calculated wavelength from the interference pattern ($2.42 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m}$) matches the theoretical de Broglie wavelength ($2.45 \times 10^{-12} \text{ m}$) within experimental uncertainty, providing strong quantitative support for de Broglie’s proposal.
