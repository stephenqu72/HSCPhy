To solve this question, we must compare the experimental observations of the photoelectric effect with the predictions made by the classical wave model of light.

### Answer
The correct answer is **D**.

### Explanation

According to the classical wave theory of light:
1. **Intensity determines energy:** In the wave model, the energy of a light wave is proportional to its intensity (amplitude squared), not its frequency. Therefore, classical theory predicted that increasing the intensity of light would increase the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons, regardless of the frequency.
2. **Energy accumulation:** Classical theory predicted that if the light were weak (low intensity), it would take time for electrons to absorb enough energy to overcome the metal's work function. This contradicted Observation 3 (instantaneous emission).
3. **Frequency independence:** Classical theory suggested that light of *any* frequency should eventually cause emission if the intensity were high enough. This contradicted Observation 2 (threshold frequency).

Now let’s look at why **Observation 4** is consistent with the wave model:
* **Observation 4 states:** "at frequencies above the threshold frequency, the number of electrons emitted are determined only by the intensity of light."
* **Why it fits:** In the wave model, increasing the intensity of the light increases the magnitude of the oscillating electric field. A stronger electric field would oscillate the electrons in the metal with greater vigor, increasing the probability of "knocking" more electrons loose per second. Therefore, the wave model successfully predicts that increasing light intensity increases the current (number of electrons) at any frequency where emission occurs.

Observations 1, 2, and 3 are specifically the phenomena that the classical wave model **failed** to explain, leading to Einstein’s development of the particle (photon) model of light.
