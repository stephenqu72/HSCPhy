### Answer
When a current is passed through a motor, a magnetic force (torque) is produced, causing the motor to accelerate. As the coil rotates faster, the rate of change of magnetic flux through the coil increases, inducing a **back electromotive force (back EMF)** in the opposite direction to the supply voltage. 

According to Lenz’s Law, this back EMF opposes the supply voltage, causing the net current through the motor to decrease. As the motor reaches a specific speed, the back EMF becomes large enough that the net current (and thus the driving magnetic force/torque) decreases until it exactly balances the opposing frictional and load forces. At this point, the net force on the motor is zero, and the motor reaches a constant terminal velocity (maximum speed).

### How to solve this
To achieve full marks (4 marks) in an HSC Physics context, you must structure your answer by linking the electrical and mechanical properties:

1.  **Initiation of motion:** State that the supply current and magnetic field create a motor torque ($F = nBIl\sin\theta$) that causes acceleration.
2.  **The role of Back EMF:** Explain that as the motor rotates, it acts like a generator, inducing a back EMF ($\varepsilon$). This must be linked to Faraday's Law or Lenz's Law (the change in flux induces an EMF that opposes the supply).
3.  **The effect on current:** Explain that the effective voltage becomes $V_{net} = V_{supply} - \varepsilon$. As speed increases, $\varepsilon$ increases, causing the current $I$ to drop.
4.  **Equilibrium condition:** Conclude that the motor reaches maximum speed when the driving torque equals the resistive/load torques. At this steady state, there is no net torque, so there is no angular acceleration ($a=0$).

**Key Terms to include:** Back EMF, Lenz's Law, net current, torque balance, terminal speed.
