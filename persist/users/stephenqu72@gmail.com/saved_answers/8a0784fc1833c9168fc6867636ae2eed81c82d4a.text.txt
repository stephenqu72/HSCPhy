The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to understand the limitations of the classical wave model of light in explaining the photoelectric effect and which observations are actually consistent with that model.

1.  **The Wave Model's Prediction:** In the classical wave theory, light is a continuous wave. According to this model, increasing the **intensity** (amplitude) of the wave should deliver more energy to the surface of the metal per unit time. This should theoretically allow electrons to be ejected regardless of frequency (given enough time) and increase the kinetic energy of the ejected electrons.
2.  **Evaluating (A):** The wave model predicted that energy transfer depends on intensity, not frequency. The observation that there is a **threshold frequency** (below which no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity) completely contradicts the wave model.
3.  **Evaluating (B):** The wave model predicted that it would take time for the electrons to "soak up" enough energy from a low-intensity wave to escape. The observation that emission is **instantaneous** (regardless of how dim the light is) contradicted the wave model.
4.  **Evaluating (C):** Classical wave theory correctly predicts that increasing the intensity (brightness) of the light increases the energy delivered to the surface. Therefore, it would expect that more photons (in quantum terms) or a larger wave (in classical terms) would result in a greater number of electrons being ejected per second, leading to a higher **photocurrent**. Because the wave model *already* accounted for this, this observation did not challenge it.
5.  **Evaluating (D):** This refers to the concept of saturation current. While the wave model generally struggles with the mechanics of the photoelectric effect, the specific phenomenon of saturation current is a result of the experimental setup (all emitted electrons being collected), which introduces complexities not fully explained by simple wave theory as effectively as the photon model. However, (C) is the most direct and textbook example of a phenomenon where the classical wave model correctly anticipated the experimental result.
