To find the maximum height reached by the projectile, we need to analyze its vertical motion.

### Step 1: Identify the vertical component of the initial velocity
The projectile is launched with an initial velocity $v = 40\text{ m s}^{-1}$ at an angle $\theta = 60^\circ$ to the horizontal. The initial vertical component ($u_y$) is:
$$u_y = v \sin(\theta) = 40 \sin(60^\circ) = 40 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 20\sqrt{3} \approx 34.64\text{ m s}^{-1}$$

### Step 2: Determine the time to reach maximum height
The total time of flight is $T = 17.67\text{ s}$. Because the projectile lands at the same level from which it was launched, the path is symmetrical. The time to reach the maximum height ($t_{up}$) is exactly half of the total time:
$$t_{up} = \frac{T}{2} = \frac{17.67}{2} = 8.835\text{ s}$$

### Step 3: Calculate the maximum height
At the maximum height, the vertical velocity is $v_y = 0$. We can use the formula for displacement under constant acceleration:
$$\Delta y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2} a_y t^2$$
Since $v_y = u_y + a_y t$ at the peak ($0 = u_y + a_y t_{up}$), we can also use the simpler derived formula for maximum height:
$$h_{max} = \frac{u_y t_{up}}{2}$$
Substituting our values:
$$h_{max} = \frac{34.64\text{ m s}^{-1} \times 8.835\text{ s}}{2}$$
$$h_{max} \approx 153.0\text{ m}$$

The maximum height reached by the projectile is **$153.0\text{ m}$**.
