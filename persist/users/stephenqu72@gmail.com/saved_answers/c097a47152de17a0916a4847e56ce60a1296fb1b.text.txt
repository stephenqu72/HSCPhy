### Model Answer

**Features of Schrödinger’s Model:**
Erwin Schrödinger proposed the quantum mechanical model of the atom, which treats electrons as three-dimensional standing waves rather than point particles. Instead of defined orbits, Schrödinger introduced the concept of **orbitals**—mathematical regions of space where there is a high probability (often defined as 90-95%) of finding an electron. This is described by his wave equation, which calculates the energy levels and the spatial distribution of electrons based on quantum numbers.

**Contrast with Earlier Models:**
Schrödinger’s model contrasts significantly with the **Bohr model**. While Bohr proposed that electrons move in fixed, circular orbits at specific distances from the nucleus (like planets orbiting the sun), Schrödinger’s model rejects these fixed paths, asserting that it is impossible to know both the precise position and momentum of an electron simultaneously (Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle). Thus, the electron is viewed as a "cloud" of probability rather than a particle on a specific track.

### How to solve this question
1.  **Deconstruct the Verb:** The question uses the command term **"Outline."** This means you should provide a brief description of the main features; you do not need to perform an in-depth derivation of the wave equation.
2.  **The "Contrast" requirement:** The second half of the question asks to "contrast one feature." A contrast requires you to identify a specific aspect of the new model and show how it differs from a previous one. Always link it to a specific named model (Bohr is the best choice here).
3.  **Allocate your marks:**
    *   1 mark for the core concept of Schrödinger’s model (wave-like nature or orbitals).
    *   1 mark for defining orbitals (probability regions).
    *   1 mark for the comparison (contrasting orbits vs. probability clouds).
4.  **Scientific Accuracy:** Ensure you avoid saying electrons "move" in orbitals. Use terms like "probability distribution" or "electron density," as this reflects the quantum mechanical nature of the theory correctly.
