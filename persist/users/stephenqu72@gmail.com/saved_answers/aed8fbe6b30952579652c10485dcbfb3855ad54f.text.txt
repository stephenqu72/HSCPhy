### Answer
The correct answer is **B. Polarisation**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the nature of light and how it interacts with surfaces and materials:

1.  **Understanding Glare:** When unpolarized light (like light from the sun) strikes a horizontal surface (like a road or water), the reflected light becomes partially or fully **horizontally polarised**. This horizontal component is what we perceive as "glare," which is intense and uncomfortable for the eyes.
2.  **How Polarised Sunglasses Work:** Polarised lenses contain a special chemical filter consisting of long-chain molecules aligned in a specific direction (usually vertical). This filter acts like a set of shutters that only allows light waves oscillating in a specific plane to pass through.
3.  **Eliminating Glare:** Because the distracting reflected light (glare) is mostly horizontally polarised, and the sunglasses have vertical transmission axes, the lenses block the horizontal light waves. This significantly reduces the intensity of the reflection while still allowing most of the direct, non-glaring light to reach the eye.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A. Refraction:** This is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another. While light does refract through the lenses, it is not the phenomenon responsible for specifically reducing the glare from horizontal reflections.
*   **C. Diffraction:** This refers to the bending of light around obstacles or through apertures. It is not related to the reduction of reflected glare.
*   **D. Total internal reflection:** This occurs when light travels from a more optically dense medium to a less optically dense one at an angle greater than the critical angle. This is a property of light transmission within materials like fiber optics, not the mechanism for glare reduction in sunglasses.
