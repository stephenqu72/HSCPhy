### Answer

When current flows through the coil, the electromagnet creates a strong magnetic field in the region between the pole pieces. As the aluminum disc rotates through this magnetic field, the magnetic flux through specific regions of the disc changes over time. 

According to Faraday’s Law of Induction, this change in magnetic flux induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the conducting aluminum disc. Because aluminum is a conductor, this induced EMF causes circular currents, known as **eddy currents**, to flow within the disc. 

According to Lenz’s Law, these eddy currents generate their own magnetic field that opposes the change that caused them—in this case, the rotation of the disc. The interaction between the external magnetic field and the induced eddy currents creates a magnetic force that acts in the opposite direction to the disc's motion. This results in a braking torque that slows down the rotation of the disc (and thus the train's axle), converting the kinetic energy of the disc into thermal energy (heat) within the metal disc.

### How to solve this
To answer this type of HSC Physics question (typically found in the "Motors and Generators" or "Electromagnetism" modules), you need to link the physical principles in a logical chain of causality:

1.  **Identify the Source:** Start by explaining that the electromagnet creates a stationary magnetic field.
2.  **State the Induction Principle:** Note that as the disc moves, regions of the conductor experience a changing magnetic flux ($ \Delta\Phi / \Delta t $).
3.  **Introduce Eddy Currents:** Explain that this changing flux induces circulating currents in the conductor (eddy currents) due to Faraday's Law.
4.  **Apply Lenz’s Law:** This is the most crucial step. You must explicitly mention that the induced currents create a magnetic force that opposes the motion of the disc.
5.  **Summarize the Effect:** Conclude that the opposing force acts as a brake, slowing the disc while dissipating kinetic energy as heat.
