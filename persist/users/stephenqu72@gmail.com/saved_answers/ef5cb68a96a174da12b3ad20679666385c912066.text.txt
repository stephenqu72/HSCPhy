### Answer
The correct answer is **(B) Time dilation**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we need to recognize the classic "light clock" thought experiment proposed by Einstein to derive the effects of special relativity.

1.  **The Setup:** The diagram shows a light pulse being emitted from a source on the ceiling and reflecting off a mirror on the floor of a moving train. 
2.  **The Observer on the Train:** For an observer inside the train, the light pulse travels straight down and straight back up. The path length is simply $2 \times (\text{height of the carriage})$.
3.  **The Stationary Observer:** For an observer standing on the ground (outside the train), the train is moving at a velocity $v$. Because the train moves while the light is in transit, the light must follow a diagonal, "zig-zag" path to catch up with the floor and then the ceiling.
4.  **The Logic:**
    *   According to the second postulate of special relativity, the speed of light ($c$) is **constant** for all observers, regardless of their motion.
    *   Since the light path for the stationary observer is longer (the hypotenuse of the triangle formed by the train's motion) and the speed $c$ remains the same, it must take **more time** for the light to complete the round trip for the stationary observer compared to the observer on the train.
    *   This discrepancy, where a moving clock appears to run slower than a stationary one, is known as **time dilation**.

The other options are incorrect because:
*   (A) **Length contraction** deals with the shortening of objects in the direction of motion.
*   (C) **Mass dilation** (relativistic mass increase) is a consequence of high-speed motion but is not the primary purpose of the light clock thought experiment.
*   (D) **Energy/mass equivalency** ($E=mc^2$) relates mass to energy, which is not being demonstrated by this geometry-based light experiment.
