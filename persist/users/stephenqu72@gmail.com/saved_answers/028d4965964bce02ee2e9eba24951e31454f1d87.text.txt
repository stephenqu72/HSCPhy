### Suggested Answer

Diffraction is the phenomenon where waves spread out after passing through an aperture or around an obstacle of size comparable to their wavelength. Its observation has been fundamental in shifting our understanding of the nature of matter and light.

**1. The Wave Theory of Light:**
In the 19th century, Thomas Young’s Double Slit experiment demonstrated the diffraction and interference of light. This provided conclusive evidence that light behaves as a wave, challenging the Newtonian "corpuscular" (particle) theory. This cemented the classical wave model of light.

**2. The Crisis of Classical Physics:**
As physics progressed, the discovery of the photoelectric effect (where light acts as a particle/photon) created a conflict between the established wave theory and the emerging quantum theory. Scientists struggled to reconcile how light could be both a wave and a particle.

**3. The De Broglie Hypothesis:**
Louis de Broglie proposed the revolutionary idea that if waves (light) could act like particles, then particles (matter) could act like waves. He postulated that any moving particle has an associated wavelength:
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$$
where $\lambda$ is the wavelength, $h$ is Planck's constant, and $p$ is momentum.

**4. Experimental Verification (Davisson-Germer):**
The Davisson-Germer experiment provided the critical evidence needed to change the scientific consensus. By firing electrons at a crystalline nickel target, they observed a diffraction pattern similar to X-rays. Because diffraction is an exclusive property of waves, this proved that electrons exhibit wave-like behavior.

**Conclusion:**
The diffraction of matter particles confirmed the concept of **wave-particle duality**. This shifted the paradigm of physics from a strictly deterministic classical view to a probabilistic quantum mechanical view, forming the foundation of modern atomic physics and solid-state electronics (such as the electron microscope).

### How to solve this type of question:
1.  **Identify the "Big Picture":** The question asks about changing models and theories. This is a "Nature of Physics" type question, which requires you to discuss the **history** of science.
2.  **Chronology:** Start with the classical view (waves), introduce the conflict (photoelectric effect/duality), and finish with the resolution (matter waves).
3.  **Use Keywords:** Ensure you use specific terminology: *interference*, *double-slit*, *de Broglie wavelength*, *Davisson-Germer*, and *wave-particle duality*.
4.  **Mathematical Link:** Even in an explanation question, citing the formula $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$ demonstrates quantitative literacy and adds significant weight to your argument.
5.  **Impact:** Always conclude with how the new discovery changed the technological or theoretical landscape (e.g., the invention of the electron microscope).
