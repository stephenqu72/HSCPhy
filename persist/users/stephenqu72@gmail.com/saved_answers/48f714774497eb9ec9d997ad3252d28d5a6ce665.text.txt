To determine the correct answer, we must examine the historical contributions of James Clerk Maxwell regarding electromagnetic radiation (EMR).

### Answer
The correct answer is **(A)**.

### Explanation
To solve this, let's analyze each option based on historical physics:

*   **(B) Unified the fields of electricity, magnetism and light:** This is correct. Maxwell's equations showed that electric and magnetic fields travel through space as waves at a speed equal to the speed of light, mathematically proving that light is an electromagnetic wave.
*   **(C) Calculated the speed of light:** This is correct. By using the values of the permittivity of free space ($\epsilon_0$) and permeability of free space ($\mu_0$) in his equations, Maxwell calculated the speed of electromagnetic waves: $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$, which matched the known experimental speed of light.
*   **(D) Explain how EMR propagates:** This is correct. Maxwell proposed that a changing electric field creates a magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field creates an electric field. This self-sustaining cycle allows EMR to propagate through a vacuum.
*   **(A) Proved that EMR exists beyond the visible spectrum:** This is **incorrect** as a contribution of Maxwell. While Maxwell's theory *predicted* the existence of other forms of EMR (like radio waves), he did not experimentally prove their existence beyond the visible spectrum. That experimental proof was famously provided by **Heinrich Hertz** in 1887, who generated and detected radio waves in the laboratory.

Therefore, because the question asks for what was **not** a major contribution (specifically implying an experimental proof which he did not conduct), (A) is the correct choice.
