### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
To find the peak wavelength of the light emitted by an object at a specific temperature, we use **Wien’s Displacement Law**:

$$\lambda_{\text{max}} = \frac{b}{T}$$

Where:
*   $\lambda_{\text{max}}$ is the peak wavelength in meters ($m$).
*   $b$ is Wien’s displacement constant, approximately $2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$.
*   $T$ is the absolute temperature in Kelvin ($3200 \text{ K}$).

**Calculation:**
$$\lambda_{\text{max}} = \frac{2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}}{3200 \text{ K}}$$
$$\lambda_{\text{max}} = 9.05625 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$$

Rounding to significant figures provided in the question, the peak wavelength is approximately **$9.06 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$** (or $906 \text{ nm}$).

To outline two limitations of applying special relativity to this scenario, consider the fundamental postulates and scope of the theory:

1.  **Non-inertial Reference Frames:** Special relativity is strictly applicable only to **inertial reference frames** (frames moving at constant velocity). The capsule is undergoing rapid deceleration as it enters the atmosphere; therefore, it is in a non-inertial (accelerating) frame, which requires General Relativity for a full treatment of gravitation and acceleration.
2.  **Magnitude of Velocity:** While $12\,900 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ is very fast for a human-made object, it is approximately $0.004\%$ of the speed of light ($c$). At this velocity, the Lorentz factor ($\gamma$) is negligible ($\gamma \approx 1.0000000008$). Relativistic effects like time dilation or length contraction are mathematically insignificant compared to the classical Newtonian effects (like air resistance and heat generation) occurring during re-entry.
