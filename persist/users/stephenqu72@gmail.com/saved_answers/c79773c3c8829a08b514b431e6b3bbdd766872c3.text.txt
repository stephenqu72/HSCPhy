To solve this problem, we apply the **Law of Conservation of Mechanical Energy**. The total mechanical energy (kinetic energy + gravitational potential energy) at the launch point must equal the total mechanical energy at the point where the velocity is at its minimum ($v = 2000 \text{ ms}^{-1}$).

### 1. Identify the Physical Principles
The total energy $E$ at any point is given by:
$$E = K + U = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{r}$$

Since mechanical energy is conserved:
$$\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 - \frac{GMm}{r_i} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{GMm}{r_f}$$

For the velocity to "never fall below" a specific value as $r \to \infty$ (the point where gravitational potential energy approaches zero), we consider the limit where $r_f$ is very large and the kinetic energy remains $2000 \text{ ms}^{-1}$.
$$ \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 - \frac{GMm}{r_i} = \frac{1}{2}m(2000)^2 + 0 $$
*(Note: As the object moves to infinity, the gravitational potential energy term becomes 0).*

### 2. Constants and Values
*   $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}^3\text{kg}^{-1}\text{s}^{-2}$
*   $M = 5.97 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$
*   $r_i = 6371 \text{ km} = 6.371 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$
*   $v_f = 2000 \text{ ms}^{-1}$

### 3. Calculation
Cancel $m$ from both sides:
$$\frac{1}{2}v_i^2 - \frac{GM}{r_i} = \frac{1}{2}(2000)^2$$
$$v_i^2 = (2000)^2 + \frac{2GM}{r_i}$$

Substitute the values:
$$v_i^2 = (4 \times 10^6) + \frac{2 \times (6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24})}{6.371 \times 10^6}$$
$$v_i^2 = 4,000,000 + 125,058,075.6$$
$$v_i^2 = 129,058,075.6$$
$$v_i = \sqrt{129,058,075.6} \approx 11360 \text{ ms}^{-1}$$

Rounding to the closest provided option, we select **C**.

**Answer: C**
