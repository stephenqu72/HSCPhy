### Answer and Explanation

To solve this problem, we need to use the fundamental relationship between weight, mass, and gravitational acceleration:
$$W = m \times g$$

Where:
- $W$ is the weight in Newtons ($N$)
- $m$ is the mass in kilograms ($kg$)
- $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity in meters per second squared ($m/s^2$)

**Step 1: Find the mass of the astronaut.**
Since the mass of an object remains constant regardless of location, we can calculate the astronaut's mass using the data provided for Earth. On Earth, the acceleration due to gravity ($g_{earth}$) is approximately $9.8 \, m/s^2$.

$$m = \frac{W_{earth}}{g_{earth}}$$
$$m = \frac{686 \, N}{9.8 \, m/s^2} = 70 \, kg$$

**Step 2: Calculate the value of $g$ on the Moon.**
Now that we know the mass ($m = 70 \, kg$) and the weight on the Moon ($W_{moon} = 112 \, N$), we can rearrange the formula to solve for $g_{moon}$:

$$g_{moon} = \frac{W_{moon}}{m}$$
$$g_{moon} = \frac{112 \, N}{70 \, kg}$$
$$g_{moon} = 1.6 \, m/s^2$$

**Final Answer:** The value of $g$ on the Moon is $1.6 \, m/s^2$.
