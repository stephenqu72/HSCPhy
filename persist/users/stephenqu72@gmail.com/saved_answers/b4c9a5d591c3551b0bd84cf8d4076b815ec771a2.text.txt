### 1. Magnetic Flux ($\phi$)
The magnetic flux through a coil area $A$ in a magnetic field $B$ is defined by the formula:
$$\phi = BA \cos(\theta)$$
where $\theta$ is the angle between the normal to the coil and the magnetic field lines. 
- In the diagram, the coil is horizontal and the field lines are horizontal. This means the normal to the coil is vertical (perpendicular to the field), so $\theta = 90^\circ$. 
- However, standard graphing for a rotating motor typically sets $t=0$ at the position where the flux is maximum (coil vertical) or zero (coil horizontal). Looking at the options provided, the graphs show the flux starting at $0$ at $t=0$. This indicates that as the coil rotates from the horizontal position, the flux increases to a maximum, returns to zero, goes negative (due to the orientation of the area vector), and returns to zero. This follows a **sine wave** pattern.

### 2. Force on wire $WX$ ($F$)
The force on a current-carrying wire is given by:
$$F = BIL \sin(\theta)$$
where $\theta$ is the angle between the current and the magnetic field.
- As the coil rotates, the wire $WX$ remains parallel to the magnetic field lines (or moves through the field). Crucially, in a DC motor, the **split-ring commutator** reverses the current direction every half-turn.
- Because the commutator ensures the current in wire $WX$ always flows in the same direction relative to the magnetic field (e.g., always "into" or "out of" the page depending on the rotation side), the force $F$ does not oscillate between positive and negative; it remains a constant directional force (or a pulsed force if considering the instantaneous change at the gap).
- The correct representation for the force $F$ on wire $WX$ is a constant value (or a sequence of constant pulses) that does not cross the x-axis, distinguishing it from an AC generator.
