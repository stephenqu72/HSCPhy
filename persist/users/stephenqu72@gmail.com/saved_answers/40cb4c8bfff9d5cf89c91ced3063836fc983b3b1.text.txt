This is a classic Physics data analysis task involving Kepler’s Third Law. As an HSC teacher, here is how you should approach this problem to secure full marks.

### How to solve this question:

**Part (a): Plotting the graph**
1.  **Scale:** Looking at the data for $T^2$ (max 15.2) and $R^3$ (max 146), choose a scale that uses at least 75% of the provided graph paper. For the $x$-axis ($R^3$), 0 to 160 units (in $10^{24}\text{ m}^3$) works well. For the $y$-axis ($T^2$), 0 to 16 units (in $10^{10}\text{ s}^2$) works well.
2.  **Labeling:** Label the $x$-axis as "$R^3 \times 10^{24}\text{ (m}^3)$" and the $y$-axis as "$T^2 \times 10^{10}\text{ (s}^2$)".
3.  **Plotting & Line of Best Fit:** Plot each coordinate point $(R^3, T^2)$. Use a ruler to draw a single straight line of best fit that passes as close as possible to all points, ideally with an equal number of points on either side of the line. Do not simply "dot-to-dot" the points.

**Part (b): Calculating the gradient**
1.  Pick two points on your **line of best fit** that are far apart (do not use raw data points unless they happen to lie perfectly on your line). 
2.  Use the gradient formula: $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.
3.  Let's estimate using the data: If we use the range from Rhea $(146, 15.2)$ to Mimas $(6.4, 0.66)$, the gradient is approximately:
    $$m \approx \frac{15.2 - 0.66}{146 - 6.4} = \frac{14.54}{139.6} \approx 0.104 \times 10^{-14}\text{ s}^2/\text{m}^3$$
    *Note: Remember to account for the powers of 10! The units are $10^{10}/10^{24} = 10^{-14}$.*

**Part (c): Determining the mass of Saturn**
1.  Kepler’s Third Law states: $\frac{T^2}{R^3} = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$.
2.  Since we plotted $T^2$ versus $R^3$, the gradient of your graph is equal to $\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}$.
3.  Rearrange to solve for $M$: $M = \frac{4\pi^2}{G \times \text{gradient}}$.
4.  Substitute $G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ Nm}^2/\text{kg}^2$ and your calculated gradient:
    $$M = \frac{4 \times \pi^2}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (0.104 \times 10^{-14})} \approx 5.7 \times 10^{26}\text{ kg}$$
    *(Note: Using more precise line-fitting may yield results closer to Saturn’s true mass of $\approx 5.68 \times 10^{26}\text{ kg}$).*
