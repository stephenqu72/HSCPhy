To show that the graph is consistent with Wien’s displacement law, we must demonstrate that the product of the wavelength of peak intensity ($\lambda_{max}$) and the absolute temperature ($T$) remains constant.

### 1. Identify the Law
Wien’s displacement law is given by the formula:
$$\lambda_{max} T = b$$
where $b$ is Wien’s displacement constant, approximately $2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$.

### 2. Extract Data from the Graph
We can select two points from the graph to verify that the product $\lambda_{max} \times T$ is constant. Note that the wavelength on the y-axis is in micrometers ($\mu m$), so we must multiply by $10^{-6} \text{ m}$.

*   **Point 1:** At $T = 600 \text{ K}$, the curve intersects at $\lambda_{max} = 4.8 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}$ (reading from the grid lines).
*   **Point 2:** At $T = 1200 \text{ K}$, the curve intersects at $\lambda_{max} = 2.4 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}$.

### 3. Calculation and Verification
For Point 1:
$$\lambda_{max} T = (4.8 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}) \times (600 \text{ K}) = 2.88 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$$

For Point 2:
$$\lambda_{max} T = (2.4 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}) \times (1200 \text{ K}) = 2.88 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$$

### Conclusion
Since the calculated values for the constant $b$ ($2.88 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$) are very close to the standard value of $2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m K}$ and are consistent across different points on the curve, the graph demonstrates the inverse relationship defined by Wien’s law ($\lambda_{max} \propto 1/T$), thereby confirming its consistency.
