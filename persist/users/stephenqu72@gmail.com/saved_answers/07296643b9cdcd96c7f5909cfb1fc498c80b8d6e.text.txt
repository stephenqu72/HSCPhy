### Explanation
To solve this, we must analyze the motion of a projectile by separating it into two independent perpendicular components: horizontal ($x$) and vertical ($y$).

1.  **Horizontal Motion:** In ideal projectile motion (ignoring air resistance), there are no forces acting in the horizontal direction. According to Newton’s First Law, if there is no net force, there is no acceleration ($a_x = 0$). Therefore, the horizontal velocity remains constant throughout the flight.
2.  **Vertical Motion:** The projectile is subject to the force of gravity, which acts downwards. This creates a constant downward acceleration due to gravity ($g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$). Because the acceleration is constant, the vertical motion is classified as "uniformly accelerated motion."

**Evaluating the distractors:**
*   **(A)** Incorrect. The relationship between vertical and horizontal velocity depends entirely on the launch angle and time; one is not inherently "greater" than the other for all projectiles.
*   **(B)** Incorrect. While the acceleration is constant (gravity), the net velocity is the vector sum of the constant horizontal velocity and the changing vertical velocity. Since the vertical component changes, the net velocity changes in both magnitude and direction throughout the flight.
*   **(D)** Incorrect. This statement is the exact opposite of the physical reality of projectile motion; it incorrectly swaps the horizontal and vertical characteristics.

Therefore, **(C)** correctly identifies that the projectile experiences constant horizontal motion and uniformly accelerated vertical motion.
