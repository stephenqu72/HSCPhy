To solve this problem, we must apply the principles of orbital mechanics, specifically regarding Hohmann transfer orbits.

1.  **Understanding the Orbital Change:** The satellite starts in a low-energy orbit (Earth's orbit) and needs to move to a higher-energy orbit (Mars' orbit, which is further from the Sun). In physics, moving to an orbit with a larger radius requires the satellite to possess a higher **Total Mechanical Energy** ($E_{total}$).
    *   The total energy of an orbiting object is given by the equation:
        $$E_{total} = -\frac{GMm}{2r}$$
    *   As the orbital radius $r$ increases, the total energy becomes less negative (i.e., it increases).

2.  **Determining the Thrust Direction:**
    *   To increase the total energy of the satellite, work must be done on the system. By firing the thrusters in the **forwards** direction (in the direction of the satellite's current velocity), we increase the satellite's kinetic energy ($K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$).
    *   This "prograde" burn increases the satellite's velocity, allowing it to move away from the Sun into a higher, elliptical transfer orbit (the Hohmann transfer).

3.  **Conclusion:**
    *   **Thrust direction:** Forwards (prograde).
    *   **Total energy:** Increases (as the satellite moves to a higher potential energy state).
