To determine the correct answer, we must examine the specific scientific contributions of James Clerk Maxwell to the classical theory of electromagnetism.

### Answer
The correct answer is **B. The measurement of the speed of light.**

### Explanation
To solve this question, you need to differentiate between theoretical derivation and experimental measurement:

*   **A is a contribution:** Maxwell derived the wave equations for electromagnetic fields. From these equations, he calculated that the speed of these waves ($v$) is given by the formula $v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}}$. When he plugged in the values for the permeability of free space ($\mu_0$) and the permittivity of free space ($\epsilon_0$), he found the result was equal to the previously known speed of light.
*   **B is NOT a contribution:** While Maxwell *theoretically predicted* the speed of light using his equations, he did not experimentally *measure* it. The speed of light had been measured by others prior to Maxwell (most notably by Ole Rømer in the 17th century and Hippolyte Fizeau in 1849). Maxwell's genius was in showing that light is an electromagnetic wave, not in measuring its speed via an experiment.
*   **C is a contribution:** Maxwell’s most famous work, the publication of "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field" (1865), unified electricity, magnetism, and light into one set of four mathematical equations, now known as Maxwell's Equations.
*   **D is a contribution:** Maxwell predicted that changing electric and magnetic fields would propagate through space as waves. At the time of his prediction, electromagnetic waves (other than light) had not yet been experimentally produced or detected; this was later achieved by Heinrich Hertz in 1887.

Therefore, because Maxwell was a theoretical physicist who synthesized existing experimental data into a unified mathematical framework rather than performing the direct experimental measurement of the speed of light, option B is the correct choice.
