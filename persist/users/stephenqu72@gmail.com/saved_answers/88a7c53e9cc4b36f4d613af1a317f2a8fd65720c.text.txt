### Physics Analysis
1.  **Orbital Radius ($r$):** A geostationary orbit (GEO) is located at an altitude of approximately $35,786 \text{ km}$ above the Earth's surface, whereas a low-Earth orbit (LEO) is typically between $160 \text{ km}$ and $2,000 \text{ km}$. Therefore, the geostationary satellite has a **greater orbital radius**.
2.  **Orbital Period ($T$):** By Kepler's Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$), as the orbital radius increases, the period increases. A geostationary satellite takes 24 hours to orbit, while a LEO satellite takes approximately 90–120 minutes. Therefore, the geostationary satellite has a **longer orbital period**.
3.  **Orbital Speed ($v$):** The orbital velocity is given by $v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$. Since $v$ is inversely proportional to the square root of $r$, the satellite at a greater radius must travel at a **lower speed**.
4.  **Centripetal Force ($F_c$):** The centripetal force is provided by gravity: $F_c = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$. As the orbital radius $r$ increases, the gravitational force (and thus the centripetal force required to maintain the orbit) decreases. Therefore, the geostationary satellite experiences **less centripetal force**.

### Conclusion
Comparing these findings to the options:
*   Speed: Lower
*   Period: Longer

*   Centripetal Force: Less

This corresponds to option **(B)**.
