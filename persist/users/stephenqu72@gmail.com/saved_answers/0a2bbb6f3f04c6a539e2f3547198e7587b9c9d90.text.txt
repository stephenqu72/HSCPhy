To verify Malus’s Law, which states that the intensity of polarized light transmitted through a second polarizer (analyzer) is given by $I = I_0 \cos^2(\theta)$, follow the procedure below.

### Procedure
1. **Setup:** Place a laser (or light source) on a bench. Position a fixed polarizer (the polarizer) in front of the source to ensure the light is linearly polarized.
2. **Mounting:** Position a second polarizer (the analyzer) on a rotating mount equipped with an angular scale behind the first polarizer.
3. **Detection:** Place a light sensor or photodiode directly behind the analyzer to measure the transmitted light intensity.
4. **Data Collection:** Ensure the analyzer scale is set to $0^\circ$ (where intensity is maximized). Record the intensity $I$ as you rotate the analyzer in increments of $10^\circ$ through a full $180^\circ$.
5. **Analysis:** Plot a graph of $I$ (y-axis) against $\cos^2(\theta)$ (x-axis). If Malus’s Law holds, you should obtain a straight line passing through the origin with a gradient equal to the initial intensity $I_0$.

### Explanation for Success
To secure the full 5 marks, keep these tips in mind:
*   **Variable Identification:** You must clearly identify the independent variable (the angle $\theta$ of the analyzer) and the dependent variable (the light intensity $I$).
*   **Safety/Accuracy:** Mention ensuring the light source is stable and that background light is minimized (e.g., performing the experiment in a darkened room) to ensure the sensor readings are accurate.
*   **Mathematical Verification:** HSC markers look for how you intend to prove the law. Simply collecting data is not enough; you must describe the relationship to be tested—specifically that plotting $I$ vs $\cos^2(\theta)$ results in a linear relationship.
*   **Diagram:** A simple, labelled diagram is essential for high marks in experimental design questions.
