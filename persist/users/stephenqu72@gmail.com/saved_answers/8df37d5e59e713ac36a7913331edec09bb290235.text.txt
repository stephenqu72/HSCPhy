To solve this problem, we must first understand the physics of standing waves on a stretched string (fixed at both ends).

### Part a: Calculate the wavelength of the $150\text{ Hz}$ frequency.
For a string of length $L$ fixed at both ends, the resonant frequencies are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency ($f_1$): $f_n = n \times f_1$.
The difference between consecutive resonant frequencies is the fundamental frequency:
$$\Delta f = f_{n+1} - f_n = 200\text{ Hz} - 150\text{ Hz} = 50\text{ Hz}$$
So, $f_1 = 50\text{ Hz}$.
Since $f_n = n \times f_1$, the $150\text{ Hz}$ frequency corresponds to the 3rd harmonic ($n=3$).
The wavelength $\lambda$ for the $n$-th harmonic is given by $\lambda_n = \frac{2L}{n}$.
$$\lambda_3 = \frac{2 \times 0.48\text{ m}}{3} = \frac{0.96\text{ m}}{3} = 0.32\text{ m}$$

### Part b: Determine the wave speed of a $175\text{ Hz}$ transverse wave.
The wave speed $v$ on a string is constant regardless of frequency, determined by $v = f \times \lambda$.
Using the data from the $150\text{ Hz}$ wave:
$$v = f \times \lambda = 150\text{ Hz} \times 0.32\text{ m} = 48\text{ m s}^{-1}$$
Since the physical properties of the wire remain the same, a $175\text{ Hz}$ wave travels at the same speed: $48\text{ m s}^{-1}$.

### Part c: Estimate the wavelength of the sound wave produced.
The sound wave travels through air. We use the universal wave equation $v = f \times \lambda$, where $v$ is the speed of sound in air (approximately $340\text{ m s}^{-1}$).
$$\lambda_{\text{sound}} = \frac{v_{\text{air}}}{f} = \frac{340\text{ m s}^{-1}}{150\text{ Hz}} \approx 2.27\text{ m}$$

***

### Summary of Answers:
**a.** $0.32\text{ m}$  
**b.** $48\text{ m s}^{-1}$  
**c.** $2.27\text{ m}$ (or roughly $2.3\text{ m}$ depending on the accepted speed of sound)
