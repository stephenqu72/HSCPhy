### Explanation
To understand why transformers require AC (alternating current), you must refer to **Faraday’s Law of Induction**.

1.  **How a transformer works:** A transformer consists of two coils (primary and secondary) wound around a common iron core. When an AC current flows through the primary coil, it creates a magnetic field. Because AC constantly changes its direction and magnitude, the resulting magnetic flux, $\Phi$, through the iron core is also constantly changing.
2.  **Faraday's Law:** Faraday's Law states that an electromotive force (EMF) is induced in a conductor whenever there is a **change** in magnetic flux passing through it. This is expressed by:
    $$\varepsilon = -N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$
    Where $\varepsilon$ is the induced EMF, $N$ is the number of turns, and $\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$ is the rate of change of magnetic flux.
3.  **Why DC fails:** If you were to connect the primary coil to a steady DC source, the current would be constant. A constant current creates a constant magnetic field. Since the magnetic field is not changing ($\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} = 0$), there is no induced EMF in the secondary coil, and no power is transferred. (In fact, connecting a DC source to a primary coil often results in excessive current flow due to the lack of "back EMF" or impedance, which can overheat and damage the coils).

Therefore, option (C) is the only scientifically correct explanation.
