To solve this problem, we use the kinematic equations for motion under constant acceleration (gravity).

### Answer
The correct option is **A. $3t$**.

### Explanation

1.  **Identify the relevant formula:**
    For an object starting from rest ($u = 0$) under constant acceleration ($a = g$), the displacement $h$ in time $t$ is given by:
    $$h = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$
    Since $u = 0$, this simplifies to:
    $$h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

2.  **Establish the relationship:**
    From the formula above, we can see that $h \propto t^2$ (displacement is proportional to the square of the time taken).

3.  **Set up the ratio:**
    Let the initial distance be $h_1 = h$ at time $t_1 = t$.
    Let the new distance be $h_2 = 9h$ at an unknown time $t_2$.
    Using the proportionality $h \propto t^2$:
    $$\frac{h_2}{h_1} = \frac{t_2^2}{t_1^2}$$
    Substituting the known values:
    $$\frac{9h}{h} = \frac{t_2^2}{t^2}$$
    $$9 = \frac{t_2^2}{t^2}$$

4.  **Solve for $t_2$:**
    $$t_2^2 = 9t^2$$
    Taking the square root of both sides:
    $$t_2 = \sqrt{9t^2}$$
    $$t_2 = 3t$$

Thus, if the distance increases by a factor of 9, the time taken increases by a factor of $\sqrt{9} = 3$.
