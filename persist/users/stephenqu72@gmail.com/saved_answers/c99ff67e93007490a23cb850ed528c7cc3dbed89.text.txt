To solve this problem, we must analyze the physics of a vehicle on a banked track without friction.

### 1. The Physics Analysis
When a car moves around a banked curve of radius $r$ at a velocity $v$ without needing friction, the horizontal component of the normal force ($N$) must provide the necessary centripetal force.

*   **Vertical equilibrium:** The vertical component of the normal force balances the weight of the car:
    $$N \cos \theta = mg \implies N = \frac{mg}{\cos \theta}$$
*   **Horizontal motion:** The horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force:
    $$N \sin \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

By substituting the expression for $N$ into the second equation:
$$\left( \frac{mg}{\cos \theta} \right) \sin \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$mg \tan \theta = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
$$v^2 = rg \tan \theta$$
$$v = \sqrt{rg \tan \theta}$$

### 2. Interpreting the Graph
We are looking for the relationship between $v$ (on the vertical axis) and $\theta$ (on the horizontal axis). Since $r$ and $g$ are constant, we have $v \propto \sqrt{\tan \theta}$.

*   When $\theta = 0^\circ$, $\tan(0) = 0$, so $v = 0$.
*   As $\theta$ increases toward $90^\circ$, $\tan \theta$ increases at an accelerating rate (the slope of the tangent function gets steeper).
*   The square root function generally "flattens" a curve, but because the tangent function grows to infinity at $90^\circ$, the resultant graph of $v = \sqrt{rg \tan \theta}$ will show an upward curvature that starts at the origin and becomes increasingly steep.

*   The other graphs represent linear relationships, concave down curves, or S-curves, which do not fit the mathematical derivation.
