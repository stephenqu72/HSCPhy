To solve this problem, we will use the properties of radioactive decay, specifically the relationship between half-life ($t_{1/2}$) and the decay constant ($\lambda$).

### (a) Finding Half-Life and Decay Constant

**1. Determine the half-life ($t_{1/2}$) from the graph:**
The initial mass at $t = 0$ is $80\ \mu\text{g}$. The half-life is the time taken for the mass to reduce to half of its initial value, which is $80 / 2 = 40\ \mu\text{g}$. 
Looking at the graph, locate $40\ \mu\text{g}$ on the y-axis and trace across to the curve, then down to the x-axis. This corresponds to a time of $16\ \text{hours}$.
Therefore, $t_{1/2} = 16\ \text{h}$.

**2. Calculate the decay constant ($\lambda$):**
The relationship between half-life and the decay constant is given by the formula:
$$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(2)}{\lambda}$$
Rearranging to solve for $\lambda$:
$$\lambda = \frac{\ln(2)}{t_{1/2}}$$
$$\lambda = \frac{0.6931...}{16\ \text{h}} \approx 0.0433\ \text{h}^{-1}$$
Rounding to two significant figures as requested, $\lambda \approx 0.043\ \text{h}^{-1}$.

---

### (b) Calculating Time for 8 $\mu\text{g}$ Remaining

To find the time taken to reach $8\ \mu\text{g}$ from an initial $80\ \mu\text{g}$, we use the radioactive decay formula:
$$N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$

Where:
- $N(t) = 8\ \mu\text{g}$
- $N_0 = 80\ \mu\text{g}$
- $\lambda = 0.0433\ \text{h}^{-1}$ (using the unrounded value for accuracy)

Substituting the values:
$$8 = 80 e^{-0.0433t}$$
$$0.1 = e^{-0.0433t}$$
Take the natural logarithm of both sides:
$$\ln(0.1) = -0.0433t$$
$$t = \frac{\ln(0.1)}{-0.0433}$$
$$t \approx \frac{-2.3026}{-0.0433} \approx 53.18\ \text{hours}$$

Rounding to a reasonable precision, it takes approximately **53 hours**.

Our calculated 53 hours is consistent with this logic.)*
