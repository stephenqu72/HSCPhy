To answer this question effectively for an HSC Physics exam, you need to address the competition between the two primary fundamental forces acting within the nucleus: the **strong nuclear force** and the **electrostatic (Coulomb) force**.

### Answer
For a nucleus to be stable, the attractive strong nuclear force must be sufficient to overcome the repulsive electrostatic force between protons.

1.  **The Strong Nuclear Force:** This is a short-range, attractive force that acts between all nucleons (protons and neutrons). It effectively "glues" the nucleus together.
2.  **Electrostatic Repulsion:** Protons are positively charged and exert a long-range repulsive force on each other. As the number of protons ($Z$) increases, this repulsive force grows significantly.
3.  **The Role of Neutrons:** Neutrons provide additional "glue" via the strong nuclear force without adding any electrostatic repulsion. In larger nuclei, an excess of neutrons is required to increase the distance between protons and provide enough extra attractive force to offset the increased cumulative repulsion.
4.  **Range Limitations:** Because the strong nuclear force has a very short range, it cannot hold very large nuclei together indefinitely. As the atomic mass ($A$) increases, the ratio of protons to neutrons must shift (more neutrons relative to protons) to maintain stability. If this ratio is not met, the repulsive forces become too great for the short-range strong nuclear force to counteract, leading to instability.

### How to solve this question
To secure all 4 marks, structure your response as follows:
*   **Mark 1:** Identify the two opposing forces: the attractive strong nuclear force and the repulsive electrostatic force between protons.
*   **Mark 2:** Explicitly state that the strong nuclear force is short-range, while the electrostatic repulsion is long-range.
*   **Mark 3:** Explain that neutrons contribute to the attractive strong force without contributing to the repulsive electrostatic force.
*   **Mark 4:** Conclude that in heavier nuclei, extra neutrons are necessary to dilute the proton-proton repulsion and compensate for the limited reach of the strong nuclear force, hence the decrease in the proton-to-neutron ratio as $A$ increases.
