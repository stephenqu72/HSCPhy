To solve this problem, we use Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = mc^2$, where $E$ is the energy, $m$ is the mass, and $c$ is the speed of light ($c \approx 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$).

### Step-by-Step Solution:

**1. Calculate the total energy released in one minute:**
The power output is given in Watts ($W = J/s$). Since there are $60$ seconds in one minute, the total energy ($E$) released in one minute is:
$$E = P \times t$$
$$E = (3.85 \times 10^{28} \text{ J/s}) \times (60 \text{ s})$$
$$E = 2.31 \times 10^{30} \text{ J}$$

**2. Rearrange the mass-energy equivalence formula:**
To find the mass ($m$) lost, we rearrange $E = mc^2$ to solve for $m$:
$$m = \frac{E}{c^2}$$

**3. Substitute the values:**
Using $c = 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$:
$$m = \frac{2.31 \times 10^{30} \text{ J}}{(3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})^2}$$
$$m = \frac{2.31 \times 10^{30}}{9.00 \times 10^{16}}$$
$$m \approx 2.5666... \times 10^{13} \text{ kg}$$

Rounding to three significant figures, we get $2.57 \times 10^{13} \text{ kg}$.
