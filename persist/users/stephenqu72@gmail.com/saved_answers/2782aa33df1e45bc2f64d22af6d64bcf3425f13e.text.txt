### Answer
The spectra demonstrate a **redshift**, where the absorption lines of the distant galaxy are shifted toward the longer wavelength (red) end of the spectrum compared to the Sun’s stationary reference spectrum.

1.  **Hubble’s Law:** The shift toward longer wavelengths indicates that the distant galaxy is moving away from Earth. According to Hubble’s Law, $v = H_0 d$, the velocity of recession is proportional to the distance from the observer. This redshift provides the necessary observational evidence that galaxies are receding, consistent with the expansion of the universe.
2.  **Big Bang Theory:** The observation that almost all distant galaxies exhibit this redshift implies that the universe is expanding. By extrapolating this expansion backward in time, it suggests that the universe originated from an extremely hot, dense singularity. Thus, the redshifted spectra act as empirical evidence for an expanding universe, which is a fundamental prediction of the Big Bang theory.

### How to solve this
When approaching a "four-mark" question like this, follow these steps:

*   **Identify the phenomenon:** Observe that the dark lines in the distant galaxy spectrum appear to the right of the corresponding lines in the Sun’s spectrum. This is a "redshift."
*   **Link to Velocity:** Explain that this shift in wavelength ($\Delta \lambda$) is a Doppler effect for light, indicating the source is moving away.
*   **Connect to Hubble's Law:** Explicitly mention that Hubble's law states that velocity of recession is proportional to distance, and the spectra provide the experimental basis for this relationship.
*   **Connect to Big Bang:** Summarize the cosmological implication. If the universe is expanding now, it must have been smaller/denser in the past. Always conclude by explicitly linking the data back to the Big Bang theory to ensure you hit all parts of the question.
