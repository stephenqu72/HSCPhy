### Analysis of Orbital Properties:

1.  **Orbital Period:**
    *   **Near Earth Orbit (NEO):** These orbits are at a low altitude (typically $160 \text{ km}$ to $2,000 \text{ km}$). According to Kepler’s Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$), a smaller orbital radius results in a **short orbital period** (often around 90–120 minutes).
    *   **Geostationary Orbit (GEO):** These orbits are at a very high altitude (approx. $35,786 \text{ km}$). They have an orbital period of exactly 24 hours to match the Earth's rotation.

2.  **Altitude:**
    *   **NEO:** Low altitude.
    *   **GEO:** High altitude (the term "Large altitude" applies here).

3.  **Application (Mapping surface terrain):**
    *   **NEO:** Because these satellites are much closer to the Earth's surface, they provide high-resolution imagery and detailed data, making them ideal for mapping surface terrain, weather monitoring, and remote sensing.
    *   **GEO:** These are used primarily for communications and meteorological monitoring of wide areas, but because they are so far away, they are less suitable for high-resolution mapping of specific surface terrain compared to NEO satellites.
