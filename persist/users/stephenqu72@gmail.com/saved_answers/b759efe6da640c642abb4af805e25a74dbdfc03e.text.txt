To find the magnitude of the surface gravity ($g$) of a celestial body, we use the universal law of gravitation, specifically the formula derived from $F = ma$ and Newton's law of universal gravitation ($F = G \frac{Mm}{r^2}$):

$$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

### Given data:
*   Universal gravitational constant ($G$) $\approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$
*   Mass of Ganymede ($M$) $= 1.5 \times 10^{23} \, \text{kg}$
*   Radius of Ganymede ($r$) $= 2.6 \times 10^6 \, \text{m}$

### Calculation:
Substitute the values into the formula:

$$g = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (1.5 \times 10^{23})}{(2.6 \times 10^6)^2}$$

First, calculate the numerator:
$$(6.67 \times 1.5) \times 10^{(-11 + 23)} = 10.005 \times 10^{12}$$

Next, calculate the denominator:
$$(2.6)^2 \times (10^6)^2 = 6.76 \times 10^{12}$$

Finally, divide the two results:
$$g = \frac{10.005 \times 10^{12}}{6.76 \times 10^{12}} \approx 1.48 \, \text{m s}^{-2}$$

Rounding this to two significant figures, we get $1.5 \, \text{m s}^{-2}$.
