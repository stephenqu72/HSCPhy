This question assesses your understanding of quantum mechanics and the wave-particle duality of matter. To earn full marks (7 marks), you must provide evidence for both the particle nature and the wave nature of electrons, citing key experiments for each.

### Model Answer

The statement "electrons are particles; electrons are waves" reflects the principle of wave-particle duality, a cornerstone of quantum mechanics.

**1. Evidence for the Particle Nature of Electrons:**
*   **J.J. Thomson’s Cathode Ray Experiment:** By applying electric and magnetic fields to cathode rays, Thomson demonstrated that they were composed of negatively charged particles (electrons) with a measurable mass-to-charge ratio.
*   **Millikan’s Oil Drop Experiment:** By observing the motion of charged oil droplets in an electric field, Millikan proved that charge is quantized, supporting the idea that electrons are discrete, localized units of matter (particles).

**2. Evidence for the Wave Nature of Electrons:**
*   **Davisson-Germer Experiment:** This experiment demonstrated the diffraction of electrons when fired at a nickel crystal. The resulting interference pattern was analogous to X-ray diffraction, which can only be explained if electrons exhibit wave-like properties.
*   **De Broglie Hypothesis:** Louis de Broglie proposed that matter has a wavelength $\lambda$ related to its momentum $p$ by the equation $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$, where $h$ is Planck's constant. This theoretical framework provides the mathematical basis for the wave behavior observed in experiments like Davisson-Germer.

**Conclusion:**
Electrons exhibit particle-like behavior in interactions where they act as localized units with mass and charge. Conversely, they exhibit wave-like behavior, such as diffraction and interference, when they propagate through space. Neither model is complete in isolation; rather, the electron is a quantum object that manifests different properties depending on the experimental context.
