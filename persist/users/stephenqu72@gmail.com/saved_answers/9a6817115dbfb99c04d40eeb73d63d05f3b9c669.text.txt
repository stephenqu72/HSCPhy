### Explanation
1.  **Analyze the Spectrum:** The image provided shows an **absorption spectrum**. This is characterized by a continuous band of color (a continuous spectrum) interrupted by distinct black lines (absorption lines). These lines occur when light from a continuous source passes through a cooler gas, causing the atoms in that gas to absorb specific photons of light that correspond exactly to the energy transitions within the atoms (e.g., electronic transitions in hydrogen).
2.  **Evaluate the Options:**
    *   **(A) Very hot object:** A very hot, dense object produces a **continuous spectrum**, which appears as a solid rainbow of colors without discrete absorption or emission lines.
    *   **(B) Star:** While stars do exhibit absorption lines (the Fraunhofer lines) because light from the hot core passes through the cooler stellar atmosphere, the question asks for a spectrum *similar* to the one obtained *using hydrogen*. A hydrogen gas discharge tube is the standard laboratory method to demonstrate specific atomic spectral signatures.
    *   **(C) Gas discharge tube:** When a gas discharge tube containing hydrogen is excited by electricity, the electrons transition to higher energy levels and then drop back down, emitting light. If the light from a continuous source passes through this gas, the specific wavelengths absorbed by the hydrogen atoms will produce the exact dark lines shown in the image. This is the classic experimental setup to observe atomic absorption spectra.
    *   **(D) Fluorescent lamp:** A fluorescent lamp typically produces an **emission spectrum** (bright lines on a dark background) combined with a phosphor coating that creates a near-continuous spectrum. It does not produce the specific absorption pattern shown.

In the context of the HSC Physics curriculum, identifying the difference between continuous, emission, and absorption spectra is essential for understanding Bohr’s model of the atom and the nature of light.
