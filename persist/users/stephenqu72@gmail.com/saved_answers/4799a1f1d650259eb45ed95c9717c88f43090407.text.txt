To solve this problem, we must apply the photoelectric effect equation, which relates the energy of incoming photons, the work function of the material, and the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons.

### Step 1: Calculate the energy of the incident photon
The energy of a photon ($E_{photon}$) is given by the formula:
$$E_{photon} = hf$$
Where:
- $h = 4.14 \times 10^{-15} \text{ eV s}$ (Planck's constant)
- $f = 2 \times 10^{16} \text{ Hz}$ (Frequency)

$$E_{photon} = (4.14 \times 10^{-15}) \times (2 \times 10^{16}) = 82.8 \text{ eV}$$

### Step 2: Understand the Photoelectric Effect
The energy of the incident photon is used to overcome the work function ($\Phi$) of the material and provide kinetic energy ($E_k$) to the emitted electrons:
$$E_{photon} = \Phi + E_k$$
Rearranging to find the work function:
$$\Phi = E_{photon} - E_k$$

The problem states that the material emits photons (electrons) with an energy of $3.98 \text{ eV}$. In the context of photoelectric questions, this value refers to the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons ($E_k = 3.98 \text{ eV}$).

### Step 3: Calculate the required Work Function
