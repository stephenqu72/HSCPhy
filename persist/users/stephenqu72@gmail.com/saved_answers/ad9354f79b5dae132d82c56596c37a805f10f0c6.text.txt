### Answer
The correct answer is **(A) monochromatic**.

### Explanation
The image displays a classic **Young's double-slit interference pattern**. To produce this distinct pattern of bright and dark fringes, the light source must be **coherent**.

1.  **Monochromatic light:** For clear, sharp interference fringes, the light must have a single, well-defined wavelength. If the light contained multiple wavelengths (polychromatic), each color would form its own interference pattern with different fringe spacings (since fringe width $\Delta x = \frac{\lambda L}{d}$). This would cause the patterns to overlap and blur, resulting in a loss of distinct fringes.
2.  **Coherence:** While coherence is the primary requirement, being **monochromatic** is a prerequisite for temporal coherence, which ensures that the waves maintain a constant phase relationship over time. 
3.  **Why the others are incorrect:**
    *   **(B) Polarised:** Polarization affects the orientation of the electric field oscillations, but it is not a requirement for creating an interference pattern. Two-slit interference can occur with unpolarised light.
    *   **(C) Intense:** The intensity of the light affects the brightness of the fringes, but not the existence of the interference pattern itself. A dim monochromatic laser will still produce a sharp pattern.
    *   **(D) Longitudinal:** Light is a transverse wave. Longitudinal waves (like sound) can also create interference patterns, but light is fundamentally transverse, and stating it "must be longitudinal" is physically incorrect.

By selecting **(A)**, we satisfy the requirement for the interference maxima and minima to remain spatially distinct across the screen.
