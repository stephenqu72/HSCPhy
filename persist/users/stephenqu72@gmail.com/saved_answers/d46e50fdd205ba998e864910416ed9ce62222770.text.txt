### Sample Answer

In both nuclear fission and fusion, energy is released because the total mass of the products is less than the total mass of the reactants. This "missing" mass, known as the mass defect ($\Delta m$), is converted into energy ($E$) according to Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = \Delta m c^2$.

**1. Nuclear Fission:**
Fission involves the splitting of a heavy, unstable nucleus (like Uranium-235) into smaller, more stable nuclei. The product nuclei have a higher binding energy per nucleon than the original parent nucleus. Because the nucleons are more tightly bound in the products, they exist in a lower energy state. The difference in binding energy is released as kinetic energy of the products and gamma radiation.
*   **Example:** The neutron-induced fission of Uranium-235 into Barium-141 and Krypton-92.

**2. Nuclear Fusion:**
Fusion involves the joining of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus. The resulting nucleus is more stable and has a higher binding energy per nucleon than the individual reactants. Again, the mass defect resulting from this transition to a state of higher stability is released as energy.
*   **Example:** The fusion of Deuterium and Tritium nuclei to form Helium and a neutron, which occurs in the Sun and stars.
