### Answer and Explanation

#### Part a: Completing the Table

To solve this, we use the virial theorem for circular orbits, which states that for an object in a stable circular orbit:
1. The total mechanical energy is $E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}U$ (where $U$ is gravitational potential energy).
2. The kinetic energy is $K = -\frac{1}{2}U$.

**Initial position ($U = -4E$):**
*   $K = -\frac{1}{2}(-4E) = 2E$
*   $E_{total} = U + K = -4E + 2E = -2E$

**Final position ($U = -E$):**
*   $K = -\frac{1}{2}(-E) = 0.5E$
*   $E_{total} = U + K = -E + 0.5E = -0.5E$

| Position | Gravitational potential energy (J) | Orbital Kinetic Energy (J) | Total Energy (J) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Initial** | $-4E$ | $2E$ | $-2E$ |
| **Final** | $-E$ | $0.5E$ | $-0.5E$ |

---

#### Part b: Justification and Derivation

To justify why the kinetic energy changes, we equate the centripetal force required to keep the rocket in orbit to the gravitational force provided by the Earth:

$$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

Solving for $v^2$:
$$v^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$$

Since kinetic energy $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, we substitute $v^2$ into the formula:
$$K = \frac{GMm}{2r}$$

**Justification:**
The gravitational potential energy is defined as $U = -\frac{GMm}{r}$. 
Comparing this to the expression for kinetic energy ($K = \frac{GMm}{2r}$), we see that $K = -\frac{1}{2}U$.
When the rocket moves to a higher orbit, the radius $r$ increases. As $r$ increases, the magnitude of the potential energy $|U|$ decreases (it becomes less negative). Since $K$ is proportional to the inverse of the radius ($K \propto \frac{1}{r}$), increasing the radius results in a **decrease** in kinetic energy. This confirms our calculated values in part (a), where kinetic energy dropped from $2E$ to $0.5E$ as the rocket moved further away from Earth.
