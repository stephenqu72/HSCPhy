The correct answer is **C**.

### Explanation
To solve this problem, we must apply the concept of **Back Electromotive Force (back EMF)** in a DC motor.

1.  **The Physics Principle:** The current $I$ in a DC motor is determined by the potential difference between the supply voltage $V$ and the back EMF $\varepsilon$ generated by the motor's rotation. This is expressed by the formula:
    $$I = \frac{V - \varepsilon}{R}$$
    where $R$ is the internal resistance of the motor coils.
    *   Back EMF $\varepsilon$ is directly proportional to the rotational speed of the motor ($\varepsilon \propto \omega$).

2.  **Initial State:** When the motor is rotating at a constant speed with no load, it generates a back EMF that opposes the supply voltage, resulting in a steady, low current.

3.  **Increasing the Load:** As a load is placed on the motor and increased, the motor slows down. Because the speed decreases, the back EMF $\varepsilon$ also decreases.

4.  **Relationship between Speed and Current:**
    *   As $\varepsilon$ decreases, the numerator $(V - \varepsilon)$ in our equation $I = \frac{V - \varepsilon}{R}$ increases.
    *   Consequently, the current $I$ drawn by the motor must **increase** as the speed decreases.

5.  **Final State (Stall):** When the motor is "held still," its speed is zero. At this point, the back EMF becomes zero. The current reaches its maximum value (the stall current), determined solely by the supply voltage and the internal resistance ($I = \frac{V}{R}$). This results in a horizontal line at the peak current value.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **A** suggests a spike in current at the beginning, which would only happen during start-up, but the prompt states it is *initially* at a constant speed.
*   **B** represents an AC current, but this is a DC motor.
*   **D** shows current moving into negative values, which is physically incorrect for a standard DC motor circuit.

Graph **C** accurately depicts a steady initial current followed by a rise to a higher, constant stall current as the speed decreases to zero.
