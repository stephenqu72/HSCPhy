### Answer
J.J. Thomson determined the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$) of an electron by subjecting a beam of cathode rays (electrons) to perpendicular electric ($E$) and magnetic ($B$) fields. 

1.  **Balancing the fields:** Thomson first applied an electric field and magnetic field simultaneously such that the forces balanced, resulting in no deflection of the beam. At this point, the electric force ($F_e = qE$) equals the magnetic force ($F_b = qvB$). By equating these, he determined the velocity of the electrons: $v = E/B$.
2.  **Deflection:** He then switched off the electric field, leaving only the magnetic field. The magnetic force acted as a centripetal force, causing the electrons to deflect in a circular path with radius $r$. Using the formula $F_b = mv^2/r$, he substituted the velocity $v$ to solve for the charge-to-mass ratio: 
    $$\frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br} = \frac{E}{B^2r}$$
**Significance:** This discovery proved that cathode rays consisted of negatively charged particles (electrons) that were significantly lighter than the lightest known atom (hydrogen). This shattered the prevailing view that atoms were indivisible and provided the first evidence for subatomic particles.
