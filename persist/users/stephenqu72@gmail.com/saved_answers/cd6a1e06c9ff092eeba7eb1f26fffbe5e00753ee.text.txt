### Answer and Explanation

#### Part (a)
**Answer:** The new speed limit should be approximately $56.6 \text{ km h}^{-1}$ (or $40\sqrt{2} \text{ km h}^{-1}$).

**Explanation:**
1. The formula for centripetal acceleration ($a_c$) is:
   $$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
2. We are told the centripetal acceleration remains constant, so $a_c$ (original) = $a_c$ (new). This gives us:
   $$\frac{v_1^2}{r_1} = \frac{v_2^2}{r_2}$$
3. We are given $r_2 = 2r_1$ and $v_1 = 40 \text{ km h}^{-1}$. Substituting these into the equation:
   $$\frac{40^2}{r_1} = \frac{v_2^2}{2r_1}$$
4. The $r_1$ terms cancel out, leaving:
   $$v_2^2 = 2 \times 40^2$$
   $$v_2 = \sqrt{2 \times 1600} = \sqrt{3200}$$
   $$v_2 \approx 56.57 \text{ km h}^{-1}$$

#### Part (b)
**Answer:** No work is done because the gravitational force acting on the satellite acts perpendicular to the satellite's direction of motion (velocity).

**Explanation:**
1. In physics, the definition of work done is $W = Fs \cos(\theta)$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the force and the displacement.
2. For a satellite in a circular orbit, the gravitational force (the centripetal force) is directed towards the center of the Earth, while the displacement (the instantaneous velocity vector) is directed tangentially to the orbit.
3. Because the force and displacement are always perpendicular to each other ($90^\circ$), $\cos(90^\circ) = 0$. Therefore, the net work done on the satellite by gravity is zero, which is why the satellite’s speed remains constant.
