### Answer
The correct answer is **(C)**.

### Explanation
To solve this question, we must understand the definition of a **fundamental particle** within the context of the Standard Model of particle physics.

1.  **Defining Fundamental Particles:** A fundamental particle (or elementary particle) is defined as a particle that has no known substructure; it is not composed of any smaller parts. According to the Standard Model, these include quarks, leptons, and gauge bosons.
2.  **Evaluating the Proton:** Protons are classified as **hadrons**, specifically **baryons**. Through high-energy experiments (such as Deep Inelastic Scattering in particle accelerators), it has been determined that protons have an internal structure consisting of three valence quarks: two "up" quarks and one "down" quark ($uud$).
3.  **Why (C) is correct:** Because a proton is made of smaller components (quarks), it is considered a composite particle rather than a fundamental one. The fact that it can be "broken apart" (or, more accurately, that its internal structure can be probed and revealed) in high-energy particle collisions proves it is composite.

**Why the other options are incorrect:**
*   **(A):** Protons are currently considered stable particles in the Standard Model (though some Grand Unified Theories hypothesize proton decay, it has never been experimentally observed). Regardless, stability does not define whether a particle is fundamental.
*   **(B):** Many fundamental particles have charge. For example, quarks and electrons are fundamental particles and both possess electric charge.
*   **(D):** Protons are fermions (baryons), not bosons. Bosons are force-carrying particles (like the photon or Higgs boson), whereas protons are matter particles.
