To answer this question, we must understand the role of back EMF in a DC motor.

### Answer
The correct answer is **(C) Likely burn out.**

### Explanation
In a standard DC motor, the supply voltage ($V_{supply}$) is opposed by the back EMF ($\varepsilon$) generated by the motor's coils as they rotate within the magnetic field. According to Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law, the relationship is:
$$V_{supply} = I R + \varepsilon$$
Where $I$ is the current and $R$ is the internal resistance of the motor coils. Rearranging this for current gives:
$$I = \frac{V_{supply} - \varepsilon}{R}$$

1.  **The Role of Back EMF:** As a motor speeds up, the back EMF ($\varepsilon$) increases, which naturally reduces the current ($I$) drawn from the supply. This acts as a self-regulating mechanism to prevent excessive current.
2.  **The "No Back EMF" Scenario:** If a motor produces no back EMF ($\varepsilon = 0$), the equation becomes $I = \frac{V_{supply}}{R}$. 
3.  **The Consequence:** Because motor coils typically have very low internal resistance ($R$), the current $I$ will be extremely high—essentially reaching the "stall current" of the motor constantly. This excessive current will cause massive amounts of heat to be generated due to the Joule heating effect ($P = I^2 R$). 

This heat will quickly exceed the thermal limits of the insulation on the motor windings, causing the motor to melt or burn out. While the motor might start quickly, the sustained extreme current would make operation unsustainable, leading to immediate failure.
