### Answer
The correct answer is **(D)**.

### Explanation
This question refers to the famous **Geiger-Marsden experiment** (often called the Rutherford gold foil experiment). To determine the correct answer, we must analyze what the observation implies about the structure of the atom:

1.  **The Observation:** The vast majority of alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil with little to no deflection. This indicated that the atom is mostly empty space.
2.  **The Key Deduction:** The fact that only a tiny fraction (about 1 in 8000) of alpha particles were deflected at large angles or bounced back suggests that they encountered a very small, dense, and positively charged region of mass.
3.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   (A) While the nucleus does contain protons and neutrons, this experiment did not identify the composition of the nucleus itself; it only identified the existence of a dense, charged core.
    *   (B) This observation relates to the central mass, not the arrangement of electrons.
    *   (C) While true, the observation of "1 in 8000" specifically highlights the *size* and *density* of the core rather than just its position.
    *   (D) This is the most direct conclusion. If the nucleus occupied a large volume, the probability of deflection would be much higher. The extremely low frequency of large-angle scattering is direct evidence that the nucleus occupies a **very small fraction of the total volume of the atom**.

In HSC Physics, remember:
*   Most particles passing through = Atoms are mostly empty space.
*   Small number of particles bouncing back = The nucleus is very dense and occupies a very small volume.
