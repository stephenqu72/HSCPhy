To solve this problem, we must apply the physics of circular motion on a banked surface. In this scenario, the normal force $N$ from the tube's wall is providing the centripetal force required to keep the balls in their circular path.

### (a) Identify which ball has the greater velocity.

**Answer:** The **grey ball** has the greater velocity.

**Explanation:**
Looking at the free-body diagrams in Fig 1, the vertical component of the normal force must balance gravity ($N \cos\theta = mg$), and the horizontal component provides the centripetal force ($N \sin\theta = F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$). 
Dividing these equations gives:
$$\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{rg}$$
As the angle of inclination $\theta$ (or $\alpha$ in the diagram) increases, the value of $\tan\theta$ increases. Since $r$ and $g$ are constant, a larger angle corresponds to a larger $v$. Because $\alpha > \theta$, the grey ball is at a steeper angle, implying it must be traveling at a higher velocity to maintain its path without sliding down the tube.

### (b) Calculate the velocity of the grey ball if $\alpha = 25^\circ$ and the radius of its path is $6.0\text{ m}$.

**Explanation:**
To calculate the velocity, we use the derivation from part (a):
$$v = \sqrt{rg \tan\alpha}$$

**Step-by-step Calculation:**
1. Identify the variables:
   - $r = 6.0\text{ m}$
   - $g = 9.8\text{ m/s}^2$
   - $\alpha = 25^\circ$

2. Substitute the values into the formula:
   $$v = \sqrt{6.0 \times 9.8 \times \tan(25^\circ)}$$

3. Perform the calculation:
   - $\tan(25^\circ) \approx 0.4663$
   - $v = \sqrt{6.0 \times 9.8 \times 0.4663}$
   - $v = \sqrt{27.426}$
   - $v \approx 5.237\text{ m/s}$

**Final Answer:**
Rounding to two significant figures (matching the data provided), the velocity is $5.2\text{ m/s}$.
