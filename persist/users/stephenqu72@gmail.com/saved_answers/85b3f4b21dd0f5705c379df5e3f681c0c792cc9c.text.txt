### Answer

**(a)** Stars on the main sequence have all spectral classes (B through M) because they represent a wide range of masses. Despite this difference in mass and surface temperature, they are all in the same stage of stellar evolution: they are all currently **fusing hydrogen into helium in their cores**. This common energy-production mechanism is what defines their position along this specific band on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram.

**(b)** The luminosity ($L$) of a star is defined by the Stefan-Boltzmann law:
$$L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T^4$$
where $R$ is the radius, $\sigma$ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, and $T$ is the surface temperature. While giant stars have a relatively low surface temperature ($T$), their luminosity is high because they possess an extremely large radius ($R$). Because luminosity is proportional to the square of the radius ($R^2$), a massive increase in the star's surface area compensates for the lower temperature, resulting in a high total energy output.
