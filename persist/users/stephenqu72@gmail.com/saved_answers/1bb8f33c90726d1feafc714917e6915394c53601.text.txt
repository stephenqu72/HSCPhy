### Answer
The correct answer is **D. The height each was dropped from**.

### Explanation
To understand how to approach this, let's look at the scientific method as applied to a controlled experiment:

1.  **Identify the variables:** 
    *   **Independent Variable:** This is what you change on purpose to see the effect. In this experiment, you are testing Galileo’s statement about mass, so the **mass of the objects** is the independent variable.
    *   **Dependent Variable:** This is what you measure. In this case, it is the **time taken to fall** to the ground (which allows us to calculate the rate of acceleration, $a = \frac{2d}{t^2}$).
    *   **Controlled Variables:** These are factors that must be kept constant to ensure a fair test. If you change more than one thing (e.g., if you dropped one object from a higher point than the other), the results would be invalid because you wouldn't know if the difference in time was due to the mass or the different height.

2.  **Evaluating the options:**
    *   **A is incorrect** because the mass is the independent variable—it *must* change.
    *   **B is generally irrelevant** for simple free-fall experiments, provided they are released from rest.
    *   **C is the dependent variable**—it is the outcome you are measuring.
    *   **D is correct** because the kinematic equation $d = \frac{1}{2}at^2$ shows that the distance ($d$) is directly related to the time of fall ($t$). If you want to compare the acceleration ($a$) of two objects, you must ensure they both travel the exact same distance ($d$); otherwise, you cannot compare their acceleration rates fairly.
