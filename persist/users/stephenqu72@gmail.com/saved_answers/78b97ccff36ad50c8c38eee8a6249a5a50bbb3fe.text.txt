### Answer

Nuclear stability is determined by the balance between the attractive **strong nuclear force** and the repulsive **electrostatic force**.

1.  **The Forces Involved:**
    *   **Strong Nuclear Force:** This is an extremely short-range, attractive force that acts between all nucleons (protons and neutrons). It is much stronger than the electrostatic force at very short distances ($< 3 \times 10^{-15}$ m), effectively "gluing" the nucleus together.
    *   **Electrostatic (Coulombic) Force:** This is the long-range repulsive force between positively charged protons. As the number of protons increases, this repulsive force grows significantly.

2.  **Achieving Stability:**
    *   **Low atomic numbers ($Z < 20$):** Light nuclei achieve stability when the number of protons and neutrons is approximately equal ($N \approx Z$). In these small nuclei, the strong nuclear force is sufficient to overcome the repulsion between a few protons.
    *   **Higher atomic numbers ($Z > 20$):** As the number of protons increases, the cumulative repulsive force grows faster than the short-range strong nuclear force can compensate for. To maintain stability, these nuclei require an increasing ratio of neutrons to protons (up to $N:Z \approx 1.5:1$ for heavier stable elements). The additional neutrons provide extra "strong nuclear force" attraction without adding any electrostatic repulsion, effectively diluting the repulsion between protons.
    *   **Very heavy elements:** For elements with $Z > 82$ (lead), even the maximum ratio of neutrons cannot provide enough binding energy to overcome the massive electrostatic repulsion. These nuclei are inherently unstable and undergo radioactive decay (such as alpha emission) to reach a more stable configuration.

### How to solve this question
To score full marks (6/6) on this type of HSC Physics question, you must demonstrate a clear understanding of the competition between fundamental forces:

*   **Identify the key forces:** Explicitly name the strong nuclear force and the electrostatic repulsion. Explain the range and nature of both (e.g., strong force is short-range and attractive; electrostatic is long-range and repulsive).
*   **Explain the role of neutrons:** Don't just say "neutrons help." Explain that they provide the attractive strong nuclear force without adding charge-based repulsion.
*   **Structure by atomic size:** HSC examiners look for a progression from light to heavy elements.
    *   Explain the $N=Z$ rule for light elements.
    *   Explain the requirement for a neutron excess ($N>Z$) for heavier elements.
    *   Mention the limit of stability ($Z > 82$) to show comprehensive knowledge.
*   **Use Terminology:** Use terms like "binding energy," "nucleon," "Coulombic repulsion," and "short-range" to ensure you meet the band-6 criteria.
