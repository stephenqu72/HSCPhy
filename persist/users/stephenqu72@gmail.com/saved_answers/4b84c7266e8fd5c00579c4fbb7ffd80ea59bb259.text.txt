Because it is connected to the battery, the voltage across Coil $X$ will mirror the status of the switch: it will show a voltage (the EMF of the battery) while the switch is closed, and zero otherwise. This matches the "square-wave" pulse shapes seen in graphs **A** and **C**.

2.  **Coil Y (Secondary Circuit):** Coil $Y$ is an isolated circuit connected only to the data logger. According to Faraday’s Law, an induced EMF is generated only when there is a *change* in magnetic flux through the coil ($\mathcal{E} = -N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$).
    *   **At the instant the switch is closed:** The current in Coil $X$ increases rapidly, causing a rapid increase in magnetic flux through the iron core. This induces a sharp voltage spike in Coil $Y$.
    *   **While the switch is closed:** The current in Coil $X$ is constant. A steady current produces a steady magnetic flux. Since the flux is not changing ($\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} = 0$), no EMF is induced in Coil $Y$, so the voltage returns to zero.
    *   **At the instant the switch is opened:** The current in Coil $X$ drops rapidly to zero, causing a rapid decrease in magnetic flux. This induces another voltage spike in Coil $Y$, but in the opposite direction (due to Lenz's Law).

3.  **Comparing Options:**
    *   Graph $X$ must show a pulse for the duration the switch is closed.
