To find the kinetic energy of an electron given its de Broglie wavelength, we follow these steps:

### 1. Identify the relevant formulas
The de Broglie wavelength $\lambda$ is related to the momentum $p$ of the particle by:
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} \implies p = \frac{h}{\lambda}$$
where $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$).

The kinetic energy $E_k$ is related to momentum by:
$$E_k = \frac{p^2}{2m_e}$$
where $m_e$ is the mass of an electron ($9.109 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$).

### 2. Substitute and calculate
First, convert the wavelength to meters: $\lambda = 0.5 \text{ nm} = 0.5 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}$.

Now, substitute $p = \frac{h}{\lambda}$ into the kinetic energy equation:
$$E_k = \frac{(h/\lambda)^2}{2m_e} = \frac{h^2}{2m_e \lambda^2}$$

Plugging in the values:
$$E_k = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})^2}{2 \times (9.109 \times 10^{-31}) \times (0.5 \times 10^{-9})^2}$$
$$E_k = \frac{4.39 \times 10^{-67}}{4.55 \times 10^{-49}}$$
$$E_k \approx 9.65 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

### 3. Convert Joules to electronvolts (eV)
Since $1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$:
$$E_k (\text{in eV}) = \frac{9.65 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 6.02 \text{ eV}$$

Rounding to the nearest whole number, we get $6 \text{ eV}$.
