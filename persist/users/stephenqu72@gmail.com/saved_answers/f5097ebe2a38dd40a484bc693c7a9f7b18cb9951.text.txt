### Explanation

To solve this question, we need to compare the limitations of the Rutherford model with the specific postulates introduced by Niels Bohr:

1.  **The Rutherford Model:** Rutherford proposed that the atom consists of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons orbiting at various distances. However, according to classical electromagnetic theory, an accelerating charged particle (like an orbiting electron) should continuously lose energy as electromagnetic radiation. This would cause the electron to spiral into the nucleus, meaning the Rutherford atom should be unstable.
2.  **The Bohr Model as an Improvement:** Bohr addressed the stability issue by proposing that electrons exist in **quantised orbits**. He postulated that electrons can only occupy specific "stationary states" with fixed radii and fixed angular momentum. While an electron is in one of these stable orbits, it does not radiate energy. Energy is only emitted or absorbed when an electron transitions between these quantised energy levels.

**Evaluating the options:**
*   **A is incorrect:** This describes the Quantum Mechanical model (Schrödinger/Heisenberg), which came *after* Bohr. Bohr still envisioned electrons as particles in defined circular paths.
*   **B is incorrect:** This was a key feature of the Rutherford model itself, not an improvement made by Bohr.
*   **C is correct:** This is the defining postulate of the Bohr model that explains why the atom does not collapse.
*   **D is incorrect:** This refers to the basic concept of the nuclear atom, which was already established by Rutherford.
