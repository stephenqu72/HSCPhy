### Answer

In 1897, J.J. Thomson used a cathode ray tube (CRT) to determine the charge-to-mass ratio ($q/m$) of the electron.

1.  **Experimental Setup:** Thomson directed a beam of cathode rays (electrons) through a region containing crossed electric and magnetic fields.
2.  **Magnetic Deflection:** He first applied a magnetic field ($B$), which exerted a Lorentz force $F_B = qvB$ on the electrons, causing them to deflect.
3.  **Electric Balancing:** He then applied an electric field ($E$) perpendicular to the magnetic field. By adjusting the field strength, he created an electric force $F_E = qE$ that acted in the opposite direction to the magnetic force.
4.  **Determination of Velocity:** He adjusted the fields until the forces were balanced, meaning the electron beam traveled undeflected ($F_B = F_E$). By equating $qvB = qE$, he calculated the velocity of the electrons as $v = E/B$.
5.  **Circular Motion:** With the electric field turned off, the magnetic field caused the electrons to move in a circular path with radius $r$. Using the centripetal force formula $F_B = \frac{mv^2}{r}$, he equated this to the magnetic force $qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$. Rearranging this yields the charge-to-mass ratio:
$$\frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br}$$
By substituting the previously determined velocity ($v = E/B$) into this equation, Thomson was able to calculate the ratio $q/m$ for the electron.
