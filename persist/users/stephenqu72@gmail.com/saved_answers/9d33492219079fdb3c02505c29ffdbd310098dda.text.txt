### Explanation of how an AC Induction Motor generates torque

1.  **Rotating Magnetic Field (RMF):** When a three-phase AC supply is connected to the stator coils, it produces a rotating magnetic field that rotates at synchronous speed.
2.  **Electromagnetic Induction:** As the magnetic field lines from the stator sweep across the conductive bars of the squirrel cage rotor, they undergo a change in magnetic flux. According to **Faraday’s Law**, this induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the rotor bars.
3.  **Rotor Current:** Because the rotor bars form a closed circuit (short-circuited by end rings), this induced EMF causes a current to flow through the rotor bars.
4.  **Lenz’s Law and Force:** According to **Lenz’s Law**, the direction of this induced current creates its own magnetic field that opposes the change in the original magnetic field. This creates a force ($F = BIL$) acting on the rotor bars.
5.  **Torque Generation:** The force exerted on the current-carrying rotor bars in the presence of the stator's magnetic field creates a torque. The rotor is "dragged" along in the direction of the rotating magnetic field to minimize the relative motion between the field and the rotor.

### Answer Summary
The AC supply in the stator creates a rotating magnetic field. This field cuts the rotor bars, inducing an EMF and subsequently a current in the rotor (Faraday's Law). The interaction between this induced rotor current and the stator's magnetic field produces a Lorentz force on the rotor bars. By Lenz's Law, the rotor rotates to oppose the relative motion between the rotor and the magnetic field, generating the torque necessary for rotation.
