### Answer
The correct underlying physical principle for Kepler's Second Law is the **conservation of angular momentum**. 

However, looking at the provided options, there is a misunderstanding in the question's premise or the options provided. Kepler's Second Law states that a line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. Mathematically, this is expressed as:
$$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{L}{2m} = \text{constant}$$
where $L$ is the angular momentum and $m$ is the mass of the planet.

If this were a multiple-choice question from a standard exam, it is highly likely that there is a typo in the provided options. **None of the options A, B, C, or D describe the conservation of angular momentum.**

*   **A and D** describe energy conservation principles, which relate to the total mechanical energy of the orbit, not the area-sweeping rate.
*   **B** describes the consequence (the orbit is elliptical), not the physical "underpinning" or reason why the area is swept equally.
*   **C** refers to Kepler's Third Law ($T^2 \propto r^3$).

If you are forced to choose the "least wrong" answer, **B** is often identified in textbooks as the *context* in which the law applies (elliptical orbits), but it is physically incorrect to call it the "underpinning."
