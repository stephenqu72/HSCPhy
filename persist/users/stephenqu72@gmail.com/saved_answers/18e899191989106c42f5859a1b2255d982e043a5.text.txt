### Answer
A helium nucleus consists of two protons and two neutrons. When these nucleons bind together to form the nucleus, the total mass of the nucleus is less than the sum of the individual masses of the constituent particles. This difference in mass is known as the **mass defect** ($\Delta m$). This "missing" mass is converted into energy, specifically the **binding energy** ($E_b$), which is released during the formation of the nucleus. According to Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence principle, $E = \Delta m c^2$, this energy is released as the nucleons fall into a lower potential energy state created by the strong nuclear force. Consequently, the binding energy is also the energy required to disassemble the nucleus into its individual components.

### How to solve this
To achieve full marks (3 marks) for this question, you must demonstrate an understanding of the relationship between mass, energy, and nuclear stability:

1.  **Identify the components:** Explicitly state that a helium nucleus comprises two protons and two neutrons.
2.  **Define Mass Defect:** State that the sum of the masses of these individual particles is greater than the actual mass of the bound nucleus, and name this difference as the "mass defect."
3.  **Explain the energy conversion:** Reference Einstein’s mass-energy equivalence formula ($E = \Delta m c^2$) to explain that the mass defect represents the energy released (binding energy) when the strong nuclear force binds the nucleons together. 

**Teacher's Tip:** Always mention the **strong nuclear force** in these types of questions. It provides the context for why these particles "want" to be bound together and is essential for a high-quality HSC response.
