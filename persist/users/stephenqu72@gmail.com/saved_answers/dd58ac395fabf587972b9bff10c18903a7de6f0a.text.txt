### Answer and Explanation

**1. Reliability (Consistency of results)**
Both experiments are highly reliable because the students repeated each measurement 5 times and averaged the results. This reduces the effect of random error.

**2. Validity (Does it measure what it intends to measure?)**
*   **Experiment 1 (Free Fall):** This is highly valid. By dropping balls of different masses from different heights, the students are effectively isolating the variable of gravity, as $g$ is independent of mass in a vacuum (neglecting air resistance).
*   **Experiment 2 (Ramp):** This has lower validity for determining the *acceleration due to gravity* ($g$). Because the ball is rolling down a ramp, the motion is constrained. The acceleration is $a = g \sin(\theta)$. To find $g$, one must know the angle $\theta$ accurately and account for rotational kinetic energy, which complicates the calculation. If the goal is strictly to find $g$, Experiment 1 is more conceptually sound.

**3. Accuracy (Closeness to the true value)**
*   **Experiment 1:** This is prone to significant systematic error. Measuring the time for a free-falling object using a stopwatch involves high human reaction time error. Furthermore, air resistance will significantly affect the results, meaning the experimental value will likely be lower than the true value of $9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2$.
*   **Experiment 2:** This is more accurate in terms of measurement technique. Since the ball takes longer to roll down the ramp than to drop, the percentage uncertainty in the time measurement is reduced. However, there are systemic issues (e.g., friction between the ball and the ramp, rotational inertia), which would require complex corrections to reach the true value of $g$.

**Comparison Summary:**
Experiment 1 is more **valid** because it directly tests motion under gravity, but Experiment 2 is arguably better for reducing timing errors (improving measurement precision). Neither experiment will yield an "accurate" result for $g$ without sophisticated electronic timing gates and correction for air resistance or friction.
