### b) Explain how the student can measure the stopping voltage
To measure stopping voltage ($V_s$):
1. Set up the photoelectric circuit with a variable DC power supply connected in reverse bias (the collector plate is made negative relative to the emitter plate).
2. Shine light of a constant frequency onto the sodium surface.
3. Gradually increase the magnitude of the negative potential (retarding voltage) applied to the collector.
4. Monitor the current using a sensitive ammeter. The stopping voltage is reached when the current drops exactly to **zero**, indicating that even the most energetic photoelectrons are being repelled back to the emitter.

### c) Assess the accuracy of the student’s results
**Strategy:** Compare the theoretical gradient (Planck's constant) and the x-intercept (threshold frequency) with the experimental values.
1. **Gradient:** Use the line of best fit to calculate the gradient ($m = \Delta E_k / \Delta f$). According to $E_k = hf - \Phi$, the gradient should be $h \approx 6.63 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J s}$.
2. **x-intercept:** The x-intercept represents the threshold frequency ($f_0$). For sodium, $f_0$ is typically around $4.4 - 5.7 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$.
3. **Consistency:** Check if the given stopping voltage ($0.39\text{ V}$) is consistent with one of the data points. Since $E_k = qV_s$, then $E_k = (1.6 \times 10^{-19}\text{ C}) \times 0.39\text{ V} = 0.624 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$. This suggests the student's data may have significant errors, as this value does not align with the provided table (e.g., at $f = 5.64 \times 10^{14}\text{ Hz}$, $E_k$ is much higher than $0.624 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$).
4. **Conclusion:** State that the accuracy is poor if the experimental gradient deviates significantly from $6.63 \times 10^{-34}\text{ J s}$ or if the $E_k$ values do not align with the provided $V_s$.
