To solve this problem, we need to determine the mathematical relationship between the **range** ($\Delta x$) and the **time of flight** ($t$) for a projectile launched with a constant initial velocity ($v$) at varying angles ($\theta$).

### Step 1: Define the equations for projectile motion
For a projectile launched at an angle $\theta$ with initial velocity $v$:
1. The horizontal range is given by:
   $$\Delta x = (v \cos \theta) \cdot t$$
2. The vertical displacement for a flight that starts and ends at the same level is zero, given by:
   $$0 = (v \sin \theta) \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$
   Solving for $t$:
   $$t = \frac{2v \sin \theta}{g}$$

### Step 2: Express $\Delta x$ in terms of $t$
From the time equation, we can isolate $\sin \theta$:
$$\sin \theta = \frac{gt}{2v}$$
Using the trigonometric identity $\cos \theta = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta}$, we substitute the expression for $\sin \theta$:
$$\cos \theta = \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{gt}{2v}\right)^2}$$
Substitute $\cos \theta$ back into the range equation $\Delta x = v \cos \theta \cdot t$:
$$\Delta x = v \cdot t \cdot \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{gt}{2v}\right)^2}$$
$$\Delta x = \sqrt{v^2 t^2 - \frac{g^2 t^4}{4}}$$

### Step 3: Analyze the graph
*   At $t=0$, $\Delta x = 0$.
*   At the maximum time of flight (when $\theta = 90^\circ$), $t = \frac{2v}{g}$. Substituting this into our $\Delta x$ equation gives $\Delta x = 0$.
*   The function $\Delta x(t) = \sqrt{v^2 t^2 - \frac{g^2 t^4}{4}}$ represents an inverted parabolic-like curve that starts at the origin $(0,0)$ and returns to the $t$-axis at the maximum time of flight.

Looking at the options, **Graph (B)** shows this symmetric, curved relationship where the range starts at zero, increases to a maximum, and returns to zero as the launch angle approaches $90^\circ$ (where the horizontal range becomes zero).

### Correct Option
**The correct answer is (B).**
