### Explanation
To solve this, we must analyse the forces acting on a car on a banked track:

1.  **Understanding Circular Motion:** Any object moving in a circle—even at a constant speed—is **accelerating** because its velocity vector is constantly changing direction. According to Newton’s Second Law ($F = ma$), if there is an acceleration, there must be a **net force** acting on the object. This net force is directed toward the center of the circular path and is called the **centripetal force**.

2.  **Evaluating the Options:**
    *   **(A) is incorrect:** If all forces were balanced, the net force would be zero, meaning the car would travel in a straight line at a constant velocity (Newton’s First Law). Since the car is turning, the forces are unbalanced.
    *   **(B) is incorrect:** While a car *can* turn on a banked track without friction at a specific "design speed," it is not a requirement. Friction *can* and often does act on the car depending on its speed relative to the design speed. Therefore, stating there is "no" frictional force is not universally true.
    *   **(C) is incorrect:** This is a phrasing trap. The centripetal force is not an "extra" force added to the system; it is the **net result** of the components of the existing forces (the normal force and, if present, friction).
    *   **(D) is correct:** The horizontal component of the normal force (and any relevant friction) provides the net inward force required to maintain the circular path. We define this resultant net force as the **centripetal force**.

**Summary for your exams:** When an object moves in a circle, the net force acting on it is always directed toward the center of the circle, which provides the centripetal acceleration ($a_c = v^2/r$).
