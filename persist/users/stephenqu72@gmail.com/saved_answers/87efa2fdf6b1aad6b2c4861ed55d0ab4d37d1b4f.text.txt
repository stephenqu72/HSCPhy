To solve this problem, we apply the **Law of Conservation of Energy**, which states that the total mechanical energy in a closed system (ignoring air resistance and friction) remains constant.

### Part (a)
The ball falls a total vertical distance $h_{total} = 0.89 + 0.17 = 1.06\text{ m}$. All gravitational potential energy is converted into elastic potential energy at the point of maximum compression.

1. **Calculate gravitational potential energy ($U_g$):**
   $$U_g = mgh = (0.16)(9.8)(1.06) \approx 1.66208\text{ J}$$
2. **Set equal to elastic potential energy ($U_s$):**
   $$U_s = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$
   $$1.66208 = \frac{1}{2} \cdot k \cdot (0.17)^2$$
   $$k = \frac{1.66208 \cdot 2}{0.17^2} = \frac{3.32416}{0.0289} \approx 115\text{ N m}^{-1}$$

### Part (b)
At the lowest point, the ball experiences two forces: gravity acting downwards ($F_g = mg$) and the spring force acting upwards ($F_s = kx$).

1. **Calculate forces:**
   $$F_g = (0.16)(9.8) = 1.568\text{ N}$$
   $$F_s = kx = (115)(0.17) = 19.55\text{ N}$$
2. **Net force ($F_{net}$):**
   $$F_{net} = F_s - F_g = 19.55 - 1.568 = 17.982\text{ N}$$
   Rounding to significant figures, we get **$18\text{ N}$**.

### Part (c)
When the spring returns to its natural length, the ball has risen by $0.17\text{ m}$ from its lowest point. Its new height relative to the ground is $0.89\text{ m}$. We use conservation of energy: Total initial energy = Final kinetic energy + Final gravitational potential energy.

1. **Initial Energy:** $E_i = 1.66208\text{ J}$ (Calculated in Part a).
2. **Final Gravitational Potential Energy:** 
   $$U_{g,final} = mg(0.89) = (0.16)(9.8)(0.89) \approx 1.39552\text{ J}$$
3. **Conservation:**
   $$E_i = K_f + U_{g,final}$$
   $$1.66208 = K_f + 1.39552$$
   $$K_f = 1.66208 - 1.39552 = 0.26656\text{ J}$$
   Rounding appropriately, the kinetic energy is **$0.27\text{ J}$**.
