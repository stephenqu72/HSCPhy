### Answer

The investigation of cathode rays in the 1800s was fundamental to the discovery of the subatomic structure of the atom. Key experiments include:

1.  **Hertz and Lenard (Transmission through thin metal foils):** These scientists observed that cathode rays could pass through thin sheets of metal foil. This provided evidence that cathode rays were composed of particles much smaller than atoms, as they were able to penetrate the atomic lattice.
2.  **Hertz (Electric Field Deflection):** Initially, Hertz failed to observe the deflection of cathode rays in an electric field, leading him to argue they were waves. However, J.J. Thomson later proved that the cathode rays were indeed charged particles by refining the vacuum system, showing that the previous failure was due to ionization of residual gas.
3.  **J.J. Thomson (Magnetic and Electric Field Deflection):** By using crossed electric and magnetic fields, Thomson balanced the forces to measure the velocity of the rays and subsequently their charge-to-mass ratio ($\frac{q}{m}$). He found that this ratio was constant regardless of the cathode material or gas used, providing strong evidence that these were universal subatomic particles (electrons).
4.  **Jean Perrin (Direct Charge Measurement):** Perrin collected cathode rays in a Faraday cage (a "Faraday cup") and measured the negative charge directly, proving they were streams of negatively charged particles.
5.  **Crookes (Maltese Cross and Paddlewheel):** Crookes demonstrated that cathode rays travel in straight lines (casting a shadow of a Maltese cross) and possess momentum (spinning a paddlewheel). This supported the "particle" model, as waves would diffract around obstacles and typically do not transfer mechanical momentum in this way.

**Assessment:** Collectively, these experiments shifted the scientific consensus from the "atom as the indivisible unit" to the discovery of the electron. Thomson's $\frac{q}{m}$ experiment provided the most definitive quantitative evidence, proving the electron is a fundamental constituent of all matter.
