### Answer
The statement is true because of the principles of Special Relativity, specifically regarding the relationship between mass, energy, and velocity. 

As a particle with mass ($m > 0$) is accelerated toward the speed of light ($c$), its relativistic momentum and total energy increase. According to the formula for relativistic mass/energy:

$$E = \gamma mc^2 = \frac{mc^2}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

As the velocity ($v$) approaches the speed of light ($c$), the denominator $\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$ approaches zero. This causes the total energy required to further accelerate the particle to approach infinity. Since a particle accelerator can only provide a finite amount of energy to the particle, it is physically impossible to reach or exceed $c$. Therefore, the particle's velocity will always remain strictly less than $c$.
