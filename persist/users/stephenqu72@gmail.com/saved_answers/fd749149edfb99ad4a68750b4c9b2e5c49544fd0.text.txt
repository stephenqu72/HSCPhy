To answer this question effectively, you need to focus on how individual experimental results were synthesized to quantify the properties of the electron. A classic pairing for this topic is **J.J. Thomson** and **Robert Millikan**.

### Answer
J.J. Thomson and Robert Millikan provided foundational evidence that allowed for the precise determination of the electron's properties. 

1.  **J.J. Thomson (1897):** Through his cathode ray tube experiments, Thomson applied crossed electric and magnetic fields to deflect cathode rays. By balancing these forces, he was able to measure the **charge-to-mass ratio** ($\frac{q}{m}$) of the electron. He discovered that this ratio was constant regardless of the cathode material used, proving that electrons are fundamental subatomic particles.
2.  **Robert Millikan (1909):** In his oil-drop experiment, Millikan balanced the gravitational force on charged oil droplets with an upward electric force. By adjusting the electric field until the drops were suspended, he determined the elementary charge ($q$) of the electron.

**Combined Understanding:** 
Thomson's experiment provided the ratio $\frac{q}{m}$, while Millikan's experiment independently determined the absolute charge ($q$). By combining these two results—specifically by dividing the charge ($q$) by the charge-to-mass ratio ($\frac{q}{m}$)—scientists were able to calculate the **mass of the electron** ($m$) for the first time. This synthesis transformed the electron from a "corpuscle" with unknown characteristics into a defined physical entity with a measurable mass and charge.
